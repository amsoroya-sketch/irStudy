Why Physical Examination Matters

Critical for ICRP Success

  • Daily Requirement: You'll examine 5-15 patients daily
  • Supervisor Observation: Direct assessment of your examination technique
  • Clinical Competence: Physical exam findings guide diagnosis and management
  • Professional Image: Smooth, systematic exam = competent doctor
  • Patient Confidence: Thorough exam builds trust

Common Consequences of Weak Examination Skills

  • ‚ùå Missed clinical findings
  • ‚ùå Supervisor concern about competence
  • ‚ùå Takes too long (15-20 min when should be 5-10 min)
  • ‚ùå Looks disorganized and unprofessional
  • ‚ùå Patient discomfort from rough/uncertain technique
  • ‚ùå Lack of confidence shows

Common Problems & Solutions

Problem #1: No Systematic Approach

Symptoms:
  • Jumping around randomly
  • Forgetting steps
  • Having to re-examine

Solution:
  • Learn ONE systematic sequence for each examination
  • Practice the SAME sequence EVERY time
  • Never vary the order - muscle memory is key

Practice Method:
  • Write out the sequence
  • Perform examination while reading sequence
  • Perform examination while reciting sequence
  • Perform examination from memory
  • After 20 practices: Automatic

Problem #2: Rough/Uncomfortable Technique

Symptoms:
  • Patient winces or pulls away
  • Heavy-handed palpation
  • Not warming hands/stethoscope
  • Abrupt movements

Solution: The 3 W's:
  • Warm: Warm your hands and stethoscope
  • Watch: Watch patient's face for pain/discomfort
  • Warn: "I'm going to press on your tummy now"

Practice Method:
  • Practice on yourself first
  • Practice on family members - ask for feedback
  • Start gentle, gradually increase pressure
  • Always ask: "Does this hurt?"

Problem #3: Too Slow

Symptoms:
  • Taking 15-20 minutes per examination
  • Repeating steps
  • Uncertain about what to do next

Solution:
  • Timed practice - use stopwatch
  • Focused examination - examine relevant systems
  • Efficiency - smooth transitions between steps
  • Practice - speed comes with repetition

Time Targets:
  • Cardiovascular: 8-10 minutes
  • Respiratory: 6-8 minutes
  • Abdominal: 8-10 minutes
  • Neurological: 15-20 minutes (acceptable)
  • Focused MSK: 5-10 minutes per joint

Problem #4: Missing Findings

Symptoms:
  • Supervisor finds things you missed
  • Not examining thoroughly
  • Rushing through steps

Solution:
  • Systematic approach prevents misses
  • Never skip steps - even if they seem normal
  • Look, Listen, Feel - use all senses
  • Compare sides - always compare left and right
  • Specific technique - learn proper palpation, percussion, auscultation

Universal Examination Principles

The 5 Ps of Every Examination

1. PREPARATION

‚òê Wash hands (or use sanitizer)


‚òê Introduce yourself
‚òê Confirm patient identity
‚òê Explain what you'll do
‚òê Get consent
‚òê Ensure privacy (curtains/door)
‚òê Ensure adequate lighting
‚òê Position patient correctly
‚òê Expose area adequately (with dignity)
‚òê Warm hands and equipment

Script: 
"Hello, I'm [Name], Medical Support Officer. I'd like to examine your [system].


This will involve me looking, listening with my stethoscope, and gently pressing
on your [area]. It will take about 10 minutes. Is that okay?





Please could you [position instructions]?
I'll give you privacy to [adjust clothing].
Let me know if you feel any discomfort."

2. POSITION

Correct positioning is crucial!

| Examination | Patient Position | Bed Angle | |-------------|-----------------|-----------| | Cardiovascular | Semi-recumbent | 45 degrees | | Respiratory | Sitting up/45 degrees | 45-90 degrees | | Abdominal | Flat, arms by side | 0 degrees (flat) | | Neurological | Varies by test | Usually sitting | | Thyroid | Sitting up | 90 degrees |

Common Mistakes:
  • ‚ùå Examining cardiovascular with patient flat (miss JVP)
  • ‚ùå Examining abdomen with patient sitting (tense abdomen)
  • ‚ùå Wrong bed height (examiner uncomfortable)

3. PERMISSION

Before EVERY step: 
"I'm going to listen to your heart now, is that okay?"


"I'm going to press on your tummy, please tell me if it hurts"
"I'm going to check your pulses, this okay?"

Australian Communication Style:
  • Friendly but professional
  • Explain as you go
  • Check comfort regularly
  • "Is this okay?" not "Okay?"

4. PERFORM

The 4-Step Examination Sequence: 1. INSPECTION (Look)
  • General appearance
  • Specific signs
  • Asymmetry
  • Color changes

2. PALPATION (Feel)
  • Temperature
  • Texture
  • Lumps/masses
  • Tenderness
  • Pulses

3. PERCUSSION (Tap)
  • Resonance (lungs)
  • Dullness (fluid, solid organs)
  • Compare sides

4. AUSCULTATION (Listen)
  • Heart sounds
  • Breath sounds
  • Bowel sounds
  • Always use stethoscope properly

5. PRESENT

After examination: 
"Thank you [Name]. You can get dressed now/adjust your clothing.


I'll discuss the findings with [Supervisor]."

Then present findings to supervisor:
  • Systematic presentation
  • Positive findings
  • Relevant negative findings
  • Your interpretation (if confident)

CARDIOVASCULAR EXAMINATION

Complete Systematic Approach

PART 1: Introduction & Exposure (1 minute)

"Hello, I'm [Name]. I'd like to examine your heart and circulation.


This involves looking, feeling for your pulse, and listening with my
stethoscope. Is that alright?





Please could you remove your shirt/top and lie back at about 45 degrees?
I'll give you privacy."

Positioning:
  • 45-degree angle
  • Chest exposed (men: fully; women: preserve dignity with draping)
  • Arms by side, relaxed

PART 2: General Inspection (30 seconds)

From End of Bed:

‚òê General appearance: Comfortable at rest? Breathless? Distressed? ‚òê Obvious devices: Oxygen, monitors, IV lines ‚òê Breathing: Rate and effort

Around Patient:

‚òê Cyanosis: Central (tongue, lips) or peripheral (fingers, toes) ‚òê Pallor: Anemia ‚òê Malar flush: Mitral stenosis (rare)

PART 3: Hands & Arms (2 minutes)

HANDS: 
"May I look at your hands?"

‚òê Temperature: Feel temperature of hands (cold peripheries = poor perfusion) ‚òê Color: Pallor (anemia), cyanosis ‚òê Clubbing: Check nail bed angle - Normal: >160 degrees - Clubbing: <180 degrees (loss of angle) - Causes: Congenital heart disease, endocarditis ‚òê Splinter hemorrhages: Look under nails (endocarditis) ‚òê Osler's nodes: Painful nodules on finger pads (endocarditis) ‚òê Janeway lesions: Painless palmar/plantar macules (endocarditis)

PULSE - Radial: 
"I'm going to check your pulse"

‚òê Location: Radial artery at wrist ‚òê Technique: Use 2 fingers (index + middle), not thumb ‚òê Assess: - Rate: Count for 30 sec x 2 (or 15 sec x 4 if regular) - Rhythm: Regular or irregular? - Character: Volume/strength ‚òê Radio-radial delay: Check both wrists simultaneously ‚òê Radio-femoral delay: Check radial vs femoral (aortic coarctation)

Normal Values:
  • Rate: 60-100 bpm
  • Rhythm: Regular
  • Volume: Normal/strong

BLOOD PRESSURE: 
"I'm going to check your blood pressure"

‚òê Appropriate cuff size ‚òê Arm supported at heart level ‚òê Inflate 20-30 mmHg above palpated systolic ‚òê Deflate slowly (2-3 mmHg/second) ‚òê Record systolic (first sound) and diastolic (disappearance of sound)

PART 4: Face & Neck (2 minutes)

FACE:

‚òê Eyes: - Pallor (pull down lower lid - anemia) - Xanthelasma (cholesterol deposits) - Arcus senilis (white ring around cornea) ‚òê Mouth: - Central cyanosis (tongue, lips) - Dental hygiene (endocarditis risk) - High arched palate (Marfan syndrome)

NECK: Jugular Venous Pressure (JVP): 
"I'm going to look at the veins in your neck"

‚òê Position: Patient at 45 degrees ‚òê Technique: - Look for internal jugular vein pulsation - Between sternocleidomastoid muscle and clavicle - NOT visible normally (if visible >45¬∞ = raised JVP) ‚òê Measure: Vertical height above sternal angle - Normal: <3 cm above sternal angle - Raised: CCF, fluid overload, PE ‚òê Waveform: a wave (atrial contraction), v wave (venous filling)

Common Mistakes:
  • ‚ùå Confusing JVP with carotid pulse
  • ‚ùå Patient not at correct angle
  • ‚ùå Measuring from wrong landmark

How to Distinguish JVP from Carotid:

| JVP | Carotid | |-----|---------| | Not palpable | Palpable | | Two-part waveform | Single pulse | | Varies with position | Doesn't vary | | Varies with breathing | Doesn't vary | | Hepatojugular reflux | No change |

Carotid Pulse: 
"I'm going to feel the pulse in your neck"

‚òê Technique: Palpate gently, one side at a time (NEVER both simultaneously!) ‚òê Assess: - Rate & rhythm: Should match radial - Character: - Slow rising: Aortic stenosis - Bounding: Aortic regurgitation - Normal: Single, smooth upstroke

PART 5: Praecordium (Chest) - 3 minutes

INSPECTION:

‚òê Scars: Sternotomy, thoracotomy, pacemaker ‚òê Deformity: Pectus excavatum (sunken), pectus carinatum (pigeon chest) ‚òê Apex beat: Visible? (normally 5th intercostal space, mid-clavicular line) ‚òê Abnormal pulsations

PALPATION: 
"I'm going to feel your chest now"

‚òê Apex Beat: - Location: Normally 5th ICS, mid-clavicular line - Technique: Feel with fingertips - Character: - Normal: Gentle tap - Hyperdynamic: Strong/thrusting - Heaving: Sustained (LV hypertrophy) - Displacement: Laterally = cardiomegaly

‚òê Heaves & Thrills: - Heave: Palm over left sternal edge (RV hypertrophy) - Thrill: Palpable murmur (like cat purr) - Aortic: 2nd right ICS - Pulmonary: 2nd left ICS - Mitral: At apex

AUSCULTATION: 
"I'm going to listen to your heart now with my stethoscope"

Warm stethoscope first!

‚òê Technique: - Use diaphragm (high-pitched sounds) and bell (low-pitched) - Quiet environment - Patient breathes normally - Listen in 4 areas + extras

The 4 Main Areas:
  1. Aortic: 2nd intercostal space, right sternal edge
  2. Pulmonary: 2nd intercostal space, left sternal edge
  3. Tricuspid: 4th intercostal space, left sternal edge
  4. Mitral: 5th intercostal space, mid-clavicular line (apex)

Mnemonic: "All Physicians Take Money"
  • Aortic
  • Pulmonary
  • Tricuspid
  • Mitral

At Each Area:

‚òê Identify S1 (lub) and S2 (dub) ‚òê Listen for extra sounds (S3, S4, clicks) ‚òê Listen for murmurs - Timing: Systolic (between S1-S2) or Diastolic (between S2-S1) - Loudness: Grade 1-6 - Character: Blowing, harsh, rumbling - Radiation: To carotids, axilla

Additional Auscultation:

‚òê Left axilla: Mitral regurgitation radiation ‚òê Carotids: Aortic stenosis radiation ‚òê Lung bases: Pulmonary edema (crackles)

Special Maneuvers:

‚òê Left lateral position: Listen at apex with bell for mitral stenosis (diastolic murmur) ‚òê Sitting forward, exhale: Listen at left sternal edge for aortic regurgitation

PART 6: Peripheral & Completion (2 minutes)

PERIPHERAL PULSES: 
"I'm going to check the pulses in your legs"

‚òê Femoral: Midpoint of inguinal ligament ‚òê Popliteal: Behind knee (flex knee slightly) ‚òê Posterior tibial: Behind medial malleolus ‚òê Dorsalis pedis: Top of foot

Rate: Present/Absent, compare both sides PERIPHERAL EDEMA: 
"I'm going to check for swelling in your ankles"

‚òê Technique: Press firmly on anterior tibia for 5 seconds ‚òê Assessment: - Pitting edema: Depression remains - Non-pitting: Returns immediately - Grade: +1 to +4 ‚òê Distribution: Ankles, shins, sacrum (if bed-bound)

COMPLETE EXAMINATION: 
"To complete my examination, I would like to:

    

  • Measure the blood pressure
    • 

  • Perform fundoscopy (retinal examination)
    • 

  • Examine for hepatomegaly
    • 

  • Perform urinalysis
    • 

  • Request an ECG"
    •

RESPIRATORY EXAMINATION

Complete Systematic Approach

PART 1: Introduction (1 minute)

"Hello, I'm [Name]. I'd like to examine your chest and breathing.


This involves looking, feeling, tapping, and listening with my stethoscope.
Is that alright?





Please could you sit up and remove your top? I'll give you privacy."

Positioning:
  • Sitting at 45-90 degrees
  • Chest fully exposed (preserve dignity for women)

PART 2: General Inspection (30 seconds)

FROM END OF BED:

‚òê Respiratory distress? - Use of accessory muscles - Intercostal recession - Tracheal tug ‚òê Breathing pattern: - Rate (normal: 12-20/min) - Depth - Rhythm ‚òê Cyanosis: Central or peripheral ‚òê Cough: Productive or dry? ‚òê Sputum pot: Color and amount

AROUND PATIENT:

‚òê Oxygen: Type and flow rate ‚òê Inhalers: At bedside ‚òê Cachexia: Severe COPD, TB, cancer

PART 3: Hands (1 minute)

"May I look at your hands?"

‚òê Clubbing: (lung cancer, fibrosis, bronchiectasis) ‚òê Nicotine staining: Yellow fingers (smoker) ‚òê Peripheral cyanosis: Blue fingertips ‚òê CO2 retention flap: Hold arms outstretched, hands dorsiflexed - Flapping tremor = CO2 retention ‚òê Pulse: Rate and rhythm

PART 4: Face & Neck (1 minute)

FACE:

‚òê Central cyanosis: Tongue, lips (hypoxia) ‚òê Plethora: Red face (polycythemia) ‚òê Pursed lip breathing: COPD ‚òê Horner's syndrome: Ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis (Pancoast tumor)

NECK:

‚òê Trachea: 

"I'm going to feel your trachea, this may be slightly uncomfortable"
- Technique: Two fingers either side of trachea at sternal notch - Assess: Midline or deviated? - Deviation: Away from tension pneumothorax, toward collapse/fibrosis

‚òê Lymph nodes: - Cervical, supraclavicular - Lymphoma, TB, metastases

‚òê JVP: Raised in cor pulmonale

PART 5: Chest Examination - ANTERIOR (3 minutes)

INSPECTION:

‚òê Shape: - Barrel chest (COPD - increased AP diameter) - Pectus excavatum/carinatum ‚òê Scars: Surgery ‚òê Chest wall movement: Symmetrical? Reduced on one side?

PALPATION: 
"I'm going to feel your chest"

‚òê Chest expansion: - Technique: Hands on lower chest, thumbs meet at midline - Instruction: "Take a big breath in" - Assess: Symmetry and degree of expansion - Normal: 5-7 cm - Reduced: Fibrosis, effusion, pneumonia, pneumothorax

‚òê Tactile vocal fremitus: - Technique: Palm of hand on chest - Instruction: "Say ninety-nine" - Compare: Side to side - Increased: Consolidation - Decreased: Effusion, pneumothorax, obesity

PERCUSSION: 
"I'm going to tap on your chest"

‚òê Technique: - Place middle finger of non-dominant hand flat on chest (pleximeter) - Strike with middle finger of dominant hand (plexor) - Strike at DIP joint with finger tip - Quick wrist movement, bounce off - Compare side to side

‚òê Areas: Apex, upper, mid, lower zones bilaterally

‚òê Sounds: - Resonant: Normal air-filled lung - Hyperresonant: Pneumothorax, emphysema - Dull: Consolidation, collapse - Stony dull: Pleural effusion

AUSCULTATION: 
"I'm going to listen to your chest with my stethoscope"

Warm stethoscope first!

‚òê Technique: - Use diaphragm - Patient breathes through mouth (deeper) - "Take deep breaths in and out through your mouth" - Listen to full inspiration and expiration - Compare side to side - Top to bottom

‚òê Areas: Apex, upper, mid, lower zones bilaterally (at least 6 areas per side)

‚òê Breath Sounds: - Normal (vesicular): Soft, whooshing - Bronchial: Harsh, louder, gap between inspiration and expiration - Normal over trachea - Abnormal over lung = consolidation - Reduced/Absent: Effusion, pneumothorax, collapse

‚òê Added Sounds: - Wheeze: High-pitched, musical (asthma, COPD) - Expiratory = obstructive - Crackles (crepitations): - Fine: Late inspiratory (fibrosis, pulmonary edema) - Coarse: Early inspiratory (bronchiectasis, pneumonia) - Pleural rub: Creaking, leather-like (pleurisy)

‚òê Vocal resonance: - Instruction: "Say ninety-nine" - Normal: Muffled - Increased: Consolidation - Decreased: Effusion, pneumothorax

PART 6: Chest Examination - POSTERIOR (2 minutes)

"Please lean forward so I can examine your back"

Repeat all steps from anterior examination:

‚òê Inspection: Scars, symmetry ‚òê Palpation: Expansion, tactile fremitus ‚òê Percussion: Compare sides, all zones ‚òê Auscultation: Breath sounds, added sounds, vocal resonance

Important: Lower lobes are mostly posterior!

PART 7: Completion

"To complete my examination, I would like to:

    

  • Measure oxygen saturation
    • 

  • Perform peak flow or spirometry
    • 

  • Request a chest X-ray
    • 

  • Examine the sputum
    • 

  • Check temperature"
    •

ABDOMINAL EXAMINATION

Complete Systematic Approach

PART 1: Introduction (1 minute)

"Hello, I'm [Name]. I'd like to examine your tummy. This involves looking,


gently pressing, and listening. Is that okay?





Please could you lie flat and expose your abdomen from chest to groin?
I'll give you privacy."

Positioning:
  • Flat (0 degrees) - crucial!
  • Head on single pillow
  • Arms by side (relaxes abdominal muscles)
  • Expose nipples to pubis
  • Preserve dignity with towel/sheet

PART 2: General Inspection (30 seconds)

FROM END OF BED:

‚òê Comfort level: Pain? Distress? ‚òê Cachexia: Cancer, malabsorption ‚òê Jaundice: Yellow discoloration

PART 3: Hands (1 minute)

"May I look at your hands?"

‚òê Clubbing: Inflammatory bowel disease, liver disease ‚òê Palmar erythema: Red palms (chronic liver disease) ‚òê Dupuytren's contracture: Thickened palmar fascia (alcohol) ‚òê Koilonychia: Spoon-shaped nails (iron deficiency) ‚òê Leukonychia: White nails (hypoalbuminemia/liver disease) ‚òê Palmar pallor: Anemia

PART 4: Arms & Face (1 minute)

ARMS:

‚òê Asterixis (liver flap): 

"Hold your arms out with wrists cocked back"
- Flapping tremor = hepatic encephalopathy

‚òê Bruising: Clotting disorder (liver disease)

FACE:

‚òê Eyes: - Jaundice (scleral icterus) - look in natural light - Pallor (conjunctival - pull down lower lid) - Kayser-Fleischer rings (Wilson's disease - rare)

‚òê Mouth: 

"Open your mouth and stick out your tongue"
- Glossitis (smooth, red tongue - B12/folate deficiency) - Angular stomatitis (corners of mouth - iron/B12 deficiency) - Candidiasis (immunocompromised) - Ulcers (inflammatory bowel disease) - Fetor hepaticus (sweet smell - liver failure)

PART 5: Neck & Chest (30 seconds)

‚òê Lymph nodes: Virchow's node (left supraclavicular - gastric cancer) ‚òê Spider naevi: Small red lesions with central arteriole, radiating vessels - >5 = chronic liver disease - Normal: 0-2 (pregnancy) ‚òê Gynaecomastia: (chronic liver disease)

PART 6: Abdominal Inspection (1 minute)

"I'm going to look at your tummy"

POSITION: Squat to patient level, look tangentially across abdomen

‚òê Size & Shape: - Scaphoid (malnutrition) - Distended (5 F's: Fat, Fluid, Fetus, Flatus, Feces) - Asymmetry

‚òê Movement: - Respiratory movement - Peristalsis (visible waves - obstruction) - Pulsations (AAA)

‚òê Scars: - Midline laparotomy - Right upper quadrant (cholecystectomy) - Right lower quadrant (appendectomy) - Pfannenstiel (C-section) - Laparoscopic (small scars)

‚òê Striae: Purple (Cushing's), silver (pregnancy/weight loss) ‚òê Caput medusae: Dilated veins radiating from umbilicus (portal hypertension) ‚òê Hernias: Umbilical, incisional ‚òê Stomas: Ileostomy, colostomy ‚òê Masses: Visible lumps

PART 7: Palpation (3 minutes)

CRITICAL RULES:
  1. Always ask: "Do you have any pain? Where?"
  2. Examine painful area LAST
  3. Watch patient's face for pain/discomfort
  4. Warm your hands!
  5. Start gently, increase pressure gradually

"I'm going to feel your tummy now. Please tell me if anything hurts"

SUPERFICIAL PALPATION:

‚òê Technique: - Patient supine, relaxed - Examiner on right side - Use palmar surface of right hand - Fingers together, flat - Gentle, light pressure (indent 1-2 cm) - Systematic: all 9 regions

The 9 Regions:
  1. Right hypochondrium
  2. Epigastrium
  3. Left hypochondrium
  4. Right lumbar (flank)
  5. Umbilical
  6. Left lumbar (flank)
  7. Right iliac fossa
  8. Suprapubic
  9. Left iliac fossa

‚òê Assess: - Tenderness - Guarding (involuntary muscle contraction) - Masses - Hernias

DEEP PALPATION:

‚òê Technique: - Use two hands (bimanual) - Deeper pressure (indent 4-5 cm) - Same 9 regions

‚òê Assess: - Masses: Location, size, shape, consistency, mobility, tender? - Organomegaly (see below)

LIVER: 
"I'm going to feel for your liver"

‚òê Technique: - Start in right iliac fossa (below where liver should be) - Hand parallel to right costal margin - Instruction: "Take deep breaths in and out" - Feel for liver edge descending on inspiration - Move hand up 2 cm each breath - Palpate up to costal margin

‚òê Normal: Not palpable, or <2 cm below costal margin

‚òê If enlarged: - Measure: cm below costal margin - Assess: Smooth or nodular? Tender? Pulsatile?

SPLEEN: 
"I'm going to feel for your spleen"

‚òê Technique: - Start in right iliac fossa (spleen enlarges toward RIF) - Hand parallel to left costal margin - "Take deep breaths" - Feel for splenic edge on inspiration - Move hand up 2 cm each breath

‚òê Normal: Not palpable

‚òê If can't feel: Roll patient onto right side, repeat

‚òê If enlarged: - Can't get above it (vs kidney) - Moves with respiration - Notch palpable - Dull to percussion

KIDNEYS: 
"I'm going to feel for your kidneys - I'll need to lift your back slightly"

‚òê Technique (Ballot): - Bimanual palpation - One hand posteriorly in renal angle - Other hand anteriorly below costal margin - "Take deep breaths" - Push kidney anteriorly with posterior hand - Palpate with anterior hand (ballottable)

‚òê Normal: Not palpable

BLADDER:

‚òê Palpate: Suprapubic region ‚òê Normal: Not palpable ‚òê Enlarged: Dull to percussion, arises from pelvis, can't get below it

SPECIAL TESTS:

‚òê Rebound tenderness (peritonism): - Press slowly and deeply - Release quickly - Pain on release = peritonitis - BE GENTLE

‚òê Murphy's sign (cholecystitis): - Palpate right upper quadrant - "Take deep breath in" - Arrest of inspiration = positive (gallbladder pain)

‚òê Rovsing's sign (appendicitis): - Palpate left lower quadrant - Pain in right lower quadrant = positive

PART 8: Percussion (1 minute)

"I'm going to tap on your tummy"

‚òê Technique: Same as chest percussion

‚òê All 9 regions: - Resonant = gas-filled bowel (normal) - Dull = solid organ or mass

‚òê Liver: - Percuss from resonant (lung) to dull (liver) - Measure liver span (normally 12-15 cm)

‚òê Spleen: - Percuss in left upper quadrant - Traube's space (normally resonant) - Dull = splenomegaly

‚òê Bladder: - Suprapubic dullness = full bladder

ASCITES (Shifting Dullness):

If abdomen distended: ‚òê Percuss from midline (resonant) toward flank ‚òê Note where becomes dull ‚òê Keep finger on that spot ‚òê Roll patient toward you ‚òê Re-percuss same spot ‚òê If now resonant = shifting dullness (ascites)

PART 9: Auscultation (30 seconds)

"I'm going to listen to your tummy"

‚òê Technique: - Diaphragm of stethoscope - Listen in at least 2 areas - Listen for 30 seconds minimum

‚òê Bowel sounds: - Normal: Gurgling, 2-5/minute - Hyperactive: Tinkling, frequent (obstruction, diarrhea) - Absent: No sounds for 2 minutes (ileus, peritonitis)

‚òê Vascular bruits: - Renal arteries (renal artery stenosis) - Aorta (aneurysm)

PART 10: Completion

"To complete my examination, I would like to:

    

  • Examine external genitalia and perform digital rectal examination
    • 

  • Examine hernial orifices
    • 

  • Perform urinalysis
    • 

  • Check observations (temperature, BP)"
    •
