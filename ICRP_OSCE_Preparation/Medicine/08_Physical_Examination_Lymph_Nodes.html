Physical Examination: Lymph Nodes

OSCE Context: Lymph node examination is a common AMC Part 2 Clinical Examination station. You may be asked to "examine this patient's cervical lymph nodes" or "perform a full lymph node examination." The key is systematic examination of ALL regions (cervical, axillary, epitrochlear, inguinal) with accurate description using the 7 S's Framework. Time allocation: 6-8 minutes for complete examination.

LYMPH NODE EXAMINATION

CRITICAL: Lymph node examination MUST be systematic. Always examine ALL four major regions (cervical, axillary, epitrochlear, inguinal) even if only one area is clinically suspected. Describe findings using the 7 S's Framework - this is THE standard approach for describing any lump or lymph node in Australian medical practice.

1. PREPARATION 60 seconds

A. Hand Hygiene

Visible hand hygiene in front of patient (alcohol-based hand rub or soap and water for 20 seconds).

Say out loud: "I'm just going to wash my hands before I examine you."

B. Introduction

Word-for-word script:
"Good morning/afternoon. My name is Dr [Your Name], and I'm a medical officer here at [Hospital Name]. Before we begin, could you please tell me your full name and date of birth?"

Patient responds: "John Smith, 15th March 1965."

"Thank you, Mr Smith."

C. Confirm Task

Read the station instruction carefully. Common variations:

  • "Examine this patient's cervical lymph nodes" (focused examination)
  • "Perform a lymph node examination" (all regions required)
  • "Examine this patient who has noticed a lump in their neck" (cervical + associated examination)
  • "Examine this patient with generalised lymphadenopathy" (all regions + spleen/liver)

D. Check for Pain

Say out loud: "Before I begin, do you have any pain or tenderness in your neck, armpits, or groin area at the moment?"

If yes: "Please let me know if I cause you any discomfort during the examination, and I'll stop immediately."

E. Gather Equipment

Required: Measuring tape (for sizing lymph nodes), pen torch (for examining throat/tonsils), gloves (for inguinal examination).

F. Ensure Privacy

Close curtains, ensure door is closed, ask visitors to step outside temporarily.

2. POSITION

Patient Positioning: Differs by region being examined
  • Cervical/Supraclavicular: Patient sitting upright on edge of bed or chair, examiner standing in front or behind
  • Axillary: Patient sitting upright, arms relaxed by sides initially, then abducted for examination
  • Epitrochlear: Patient sitting, elbow flexed to 90 degrees
  • Inguinal: Patient lying supine, legs slightly abducted
Say out loud: "I'd like you to sit upright on the edge of the bed for me, please. We'll start by examining the lymph nodes in your neck, then I'll check your armpits and groin area. Is that comfortable for you?"

3. PERMISSION

A. Explain the Examination

Word-for-word script:
"Mr Smith, I'm going to examine the lymph glands throughout your body today. Lymph glands are small structures that are part of your immune system. I'll be feeling for them in your neck, armpits, elbows, and groin area. For the neck and armpit examination, I'll need you to remove your shirt. For the groin examination, I'll need to lower your trousers slightly, but I'll keep you covered with a sheet and only expose the area I'm examining. The examination shouldn't be painful, but please let me know immediately if you feel any discomfort. Is that okay with you?"

B. Obtain Verbal Consent

Wait for clear verbal consent: "Yes, that's fine" or "Yes, go ahead."

If patient hesitates: "Do you have any questions before we start?"

C. Chaperone Offer (MANDATORY for Inguinal Examination)

Say out loud: "For the groin examination, I can arrange for a nurse to be present as a chaperone if you'd prefer. Would you like me to do that?"

Document in notes: "Chaperone offered, patient accepted/declined" (AHPRA requirement).

D. Adequate Exposure

  • Cervical: Neck fully visible, remove scarf/necklaces
  • Axillary: Upper body exposed (offer towel for female patients for dignity)
  • Epitrochlear: Arms exposed to shoulders
  • Inguinal: Lower trousers to expose inguinal region, maintain dignity with draping
Say out loud: "I'll need you to remove your shirt for this examination. I'll provide you with a towel to maintain your privacy."

4. PERFORM THE EXAMINATION 6 minutes

Systematic Approach is MANDATORY: Always examine in the same sequence every time:
  1. General Inspection
  2. Cervical Lymph Nodes (9 groups)
  3. Axillary Lymph Nodes (5 groups)
  4. Epitrochlear Nodes
  5. Inguinal Lymph Nodes (2 groups)
  6. Associated Examination (drainage area, spleen, liver if generalised)

A. GENERAL INSPECTION 30 seconds

Say out loud: "I'm going to start by having a general look at you."

Stand at end of bed and observe:

  • Overall appearance: Well/unwell, comfortable/distressed, cachexia (malignancy), fever signs
  • Visible lymphadenopathy: Obvious neck masses, asymmetry
  • Skin changes: Scratch marks (lymphoma with pruritus), rashes (viral infection), pallor (anaemia), jaundice (lymphoma, haemolysis)
  • Nutritional status: Weight loss (malignancy, TB, HIV)
  • Respiratory pattern: Dyspnoea (mediastinal lymphadenopathy causing compression)
  • Signs of systemic disease:
    • Petechiae/bruising (leukaemia, thrombocytopaenia)
    • Herpes zoster scars (immunosuppression)
    • Oral candidiasis visible (immunosuppression)

B. CERVICAL LYMPH NODES 2-3 minutes

Technique: Examine from BEHIND the patient (standing position) using the pads of your fingers (index, middle, ring fingers of both hands). Use gentle, circular motions. Compare both sides simultaneously. Never examine from in front for cervical nodes (incorrect technique).
Say out loud: "I'm going to feel the glands in your neck now. I'll be standing behind you. Please relax your shoulders and let me know if any area is tender."

Systematic sequence - 9 cervical lymph node groups (examine in this order):

Mnemonic for cervical node sequence: "Some Students Totally Post Amazingly On Social Platforms"
Submental → Submandibular → Tonsillar → Pre-auricular → Post-auricular → Occipital → Anterior cervical → Supraclavicular → Posterior cervical chain
Node Group Location Palpation Technique What to Feel For
1. Submental Under chin, midline Hook fingers under chin, one hand, circular motion Single node in midline, mobile
2. Submandibular Under jaw, halfway between chin and angle Bimanual, hook fingers under mandible bilaterally Soft, mobile nodes; can feel submandibular gland (larger, smoother)
3. Tonsillar (Jugulodigastric) Angle of mandible Palpate at angle of jaw, press inward and upward Most commonly enlarged in tonsilitis
4. Pre-auricular In front of ear, on temporomandibular joint Circular motion over TMJ Small nodes, often reactive to scalp/face infections
5. Post-auricular (Mastoid) Behind ear, over mastoid process Circular motion over mastoid bone Reactive to ear infections, rubella
6. Occipital Back of head, base of skull Feel along occipital bone bilaterally Reactive to scalp infections, rubella
7. Anterior Cervical Chain Along anterior border of sternocleidomastoid muscle Run fingers along SCM from angle of jaw to clavicle Multiple small nodes may be palpable (normal if <1cm, soft, mobile)
8. Posterior Cervical Chain Along posterior border of sternocleidomastoid muscle Run fingers along posterior SCM from mastoid to clavicle Enlarged in Epstein-Barr virus, toxoplasmosis, lymphoma
9. Supraclavicular Above clavicle, in supraclavicular fossa Hook fingers over clavicle into supraclavicular fossa, ask patient to take deep breath (nodes become more prominent) CRITICAL: Virchow's node (left supraclavicular) = abdominal malignancy (gastric, pancreatic). Right supraclavicular = lung, oesophagus, mediastinum
RED FLAG: Supraclavicular lymphadenopathy is ALWAYS pathological in adults. It indicates malignancy until proven otherwise. Left supraclavicular node enlargement (Virchow's node or Troisier's sign) suggests intra-abdominal malignancy (stomach, pancreas, ovary, kidney, testis). Immediate further investigation required.
Say out loud as you examine each area:
"I'm feeling under your chin now... under your jaw... at the angle of your jaw... in front of your ears... behind your ears... at the back of your head... along the front of your neck... along the back of your neck... and now above your collar bones. Please take a deep breath for me."

C. AXILLARY LYMPH NODES 90 seconds

Technique: Support the patient's arm with your opposite hand (examining right axilla = support right arm with your left hand, palpate with right hand). This relaxes the pectoral muscles, allowing access to the axilla. Examine 5 groups systematically.
Say out loud: "Now I'm going to examine the lymph nodes in your armpits. I'll need to support your arm - please relax and let your arm go loose."

Systematic sequence - 5 axillary lymph node groups:

Node Group Location Palpation Technique
1. Central (Midaxillary) Centre of axilla, against rib cage Support patient's arm abducted to 45°, insert your fingers high into the apex of axilla, press against chest wall, then slide fingers downward
2. Anterior (Pectoral) Against lateral border of pectoralis major muscle Palpate along anterior axillary fold (edge of pectoral muscle) from top to bottom
3. Posterior (Subscapular) Against lateral border of scapula Palpate along posterior axillary fold (latissimus dorsi muscle) from top to bottom
4. Lateral (Humeral) Along upper humerus, medial to biceps Palpate along upper arm against the humeral shaft, medial to the biceps muscle
5. Apical (Infraclavicular) Apex of axilla, below clavicle Patient's arm fully relaxed, press fingers deep into apex of axilla toward clavicle
Say out loud as you examine:
"I'm examining the centre of your armpit... the front wall... the back wall... along your upper arm... and deep at the top of your armpit. Now I'll examine the other side."

Repeat for the opposite axilla.

Common Error: Not supporting the patient's arm properly. If the patient's arm is tense or unsupported, pectoral muscles contract, making it impossible to palpate axillary nodes. Always support the arm and ask the patient to relax completely.

D. EPITROCHLEAR LYMPH NODES 30 seconds

Location: Approximately 3 cm above the medial epicondyle of the humerus, in the groove between biceps and triceps muscles.
Say out loud: "Now I'm going to check the lymph nodes at your elbows. Please bend your arms to 90 degrees."

Technique:

  1. Patient sitting, arms relaxed
  2. Support patient's elbow with your hand, flex elbow to 90 degrees
  3. With other hand, palpate 3 cm above medial epicondyle (bony prominence on inner elbow)
  4. Use fingertips to feel in the groove between biceps and triceps
  5. Compare both sides

Clinical significance: Epitrochlear lymphadenopathy is uncommon but significant. Causes include:

  • Hand/forearm infections
  • Lymphoma
  • Secondary syphilis (bilateral epitrochlear lymphadenopathy is classic)
  • Sarcoidosis
RED FLAG: Bilateral epitrochlear lymphadenopathy is pathological (normal nodes should not be palpable). Consider syphilis, lymphoma, or sarcoidosis.

E. INGUINAL LYMPH NODES 90 seconds

CHAPERONE MANDATORY: For inguinal examination, a chaperone MUST be offered (NSW Health/AHPRA standard). Document whether accepted or declined. If examining opposite gender without chaperone (patient declined), ensure clear documentation of patient consent.
Say out loud: "Now I need to examine the lymph nodes in your groin area. I'll need you to lie down and lower your trousers/underwear slightly. I'll keep you covered with a sheet. Is that alright?"

Patient positioning: Supine (lying flat), legs slightly abducted (spread apart)

Exposure: Lower trousers/underwear to expose inguinal region. Use sheet to cover genitalia for dignity. Only expose one side at a time.

Systematic sequence - 2 inguinal lymph node groups:

Node Group Location Palpation Technique Drainage Area
Horizontal (Superior) Along inguinal ligament (parallel to groin crease) Palpate from ASIS (anterior superior iliac spine) to pubic tubercle, just below inguinal ligament Lower abdominal wall, buttock, external genitalia (superficial), perineum, perianal area
Vertical (Inferior) Along proximal great saphenous vein (medial thigh) Palpate 2-3 cm below inguinal ligament, medial to femoral artery, along line of saphenous vein Superficial lower limb (foot, leg, thigh)
Say out loud as you examine:
"I'm feeling along your groin crease now... and down your upper inner thigh. Now I'll examine the other side."

Repeat for the opposite side.

Normal Findings: Small (<1 cm), soft, mobile inguinal lymph nodes are often palpable in normal individuals (common due to minor foot/leg trauma, minor infections). Pathological features: >1.5 cm, hard, fixed, tender, or rapidly enlarging.

F. DESCRIBING LYMPH NODES - THE 7 S's FRAMEWORK Apply to ANY palpable node

CRITICAL - EXAM ESSENTIAL: The 7 S's Framework is THE standard Australian approach for describing ANY lump, swelling, or lymph node. You MUST use this systematic framework in your presentation. Examiners expect this structure. Failure to use the 7 S's will lose marks.

When you palpate a lymph node, systematically assess and document using the 7 S's:

S Feature What to Assess Clinical Significance
1. Site/Location Anatomical region and specific location Which lymph node group? (e.g., "left anterior cervical chain, mid-level") Drainage area determines differential diagnosis
2. Size Measure in centimeters (cm) Length × width × depth. Use measuring tape or estimate with finger breadths (1 finger = ~1.5cm) • <1 cm = usually benign
• 1-2 cm = intermediate concern
• >2 cm = higher suspicion for malignancy
• >3 cm = very concerning
3. Shape Configuration Round, oval, irregular, lobulated • Round/oval = typical benign
• Irregular/lobulated = concerning for malignancy or matted nodes
4. Surface Texture of surface Smooth vs nodular/irregular • Smooth = benign reactive node, lymphoma
• Nodular/irregular = metastatic carcinoma
5. Consistency Firmness Soft, firm, hard/stony hard, rubbery • Soft = reactive/infectious
• Firm = chronic inflammation, early malignancy
• Hard/stony = metastatic carcinoma
• Rubbery = lymphoma (classic "rubbery" consistency)
6. Surrounding tissues (Mobility/Fixation) Mobile vs fixed to underlying/overlying structures Try to move node in two planes. Is skin moveable over it? Is it fixed to deeper structures? • Mobile in all directions = benign (reactive, infection)
• Fixed to skin = skin invasion (advanced malignancy)
• Fixed to deeper structures = advanced malignancy, extracapsular spread
• Matted (nodes stuck together) = TB, metastatic disease, lymphoma
7. Sensation (Tenderness) Tender vs non-tender Ask patient: "Is this tender?" Watch patient's face for grimace • Tender = acute inflammation, infection, rapid enlargement
• Non-tender = chronic process, malignancy (lymphoma, metastases)
Note: Malignant nodes are typically NON-tender (important red flag)
Mnemonic for 7 S's: "Some Students Seem Super Confident Surrounding Subjects"
Site → Size → Shape → Surface → Consistency → Surrounding tissues → Sensation
Example of using 7 S's during examination (say out loud):
"I can feel a lymph node here. Let me assess it properly. It's about 2 cm in size, round in shape, with a smooth surface. The consistency is firm, it's mobile in all directions and not fixed to surrounding tissues, and you're telling me it's not tender. Is that correct?"

[Patient confirms]

"Thank you."

G. ASSOCIATED EXAMINATION 1-2 minutes if indicated

If lymphadenopathy is detected, examine the drainage area:

Lymph Node Enlargement Examine This Area Looking For
Cervical nodes Scalp, ears, oral cavity, throat, thyroid Skin infections, dental abscess, tonsillitis, pharyngitis, thyroid mass, oral malignancy
Axillary nodes Breast, upper limb, chest wall Breast mass (malignancy), skin infections on arm, chest wall lesions
Epitrochlear nodes Hand and forearm Hand infections, wounds, rashes, skin lesions
Inguinal nodes Lower limb, genitalia, perineum, anus Foot/leg ulcers, cellulitis, fungal infections, STIs, anal/genital malignancy
Say out loud: "I've found some enlarged lymph nodes in your [region]. I'd like to examine [drainage area] to look for a cause. Is that okay?"

If GENERALISED lymphadenopathy (≥2 non-contiguous regions):

  • Examine spleen: Splenomegaly suggests lymphoma, leukaemia, infectious mononucleosis, portal hypertension
  • Examine liver: Hepatomegaly suggests lymphoma, leukaemia, metastatic disease
  • Look for systemic signs: Pallor (anaemia from bone marrow infiltration), petechiae (thrombocytopaenia), jaundice (haemolysis, liver involvement)
Say out loud (if completing examination):
"To complete my examination, I would:
  • Examine the patient's spleen and liver
  • Perform a full skin examination looking for rashes or lesions
  • Examine the oral cavity and throat
  • Check for hepatomegaly and splenomegaly
  • Request blood tests including full blood count, film, and LDH
  • Consider imaging such as ultrasound or CT scan of the affected area
  • Arrange for biopsy of the lymph node if malignancy is suspected"

5. PRESENT FINDINGS 60-90 seconds

Presentation Structure:
  1. Opening statement (patient identity, what you examined)
  2. General inspection findings
  3. Systematic regional findings (cervical → axillary → epitrochlear → inguinal)
  4. Describe any palpable lymph nodes using 7 S's Framework
  5. Summary statement with clinical correlation
  6. Differential diagnosis (brief)
  7. Further investigations/management

Example Presentation 1: Normal Examination

"This is Mr John Smith, a 45-year-old gentleman. I have performed a complete lymph node examination.

On general inspection, Mr Smith appears well and comfortable at rest. There is no visible lymphadenopathy, cachexia, pallor, or jaundice.

On systematic examination of the lymph node regions:
• Cervical: No palpable cervical, supraclavicular, submandibular, or submental lymphadenopathy
• Axillary: No palpable axillary lymphadenopathy bilaterally
• Epitrochlear: No palpable epitrochlear nodes
• Inguinal: Small, soft, mobile inguinal lymph nodes palpable bilaterally, less than 1 cm, consistent with normal variant

In summary, this is a normal lymph node examination with no pathological lymphadenopathy detected.

I would document these findings in the medical record and reassure the patient."

Example Presentation 2: Pathological Lymphadenopathy (using 7 S's)

"This is Mrs Sarah Jones, a 62-year-old lady presenting with a neck lump. I have performed a focused cervical lymph node examination.

On general inspection, Mrs Jones appears well-nourished but has visible left-sided neck swelling. There is no cachexia, pallor, or respiratory distress.

On systematic examination of the cervical lymph nodes, I have identified an abnormal lymph node in the left posterior cervical chain, which I will describe using the 7 S's framework:

1. Site: Left posterior cervical chain, mid-level, approximately 4 cm below the mastoid process
2. Size: Approximately 3 cm × 2 cm × 2 cm
3. Shape: Oval, regular margins
4. Surface: Smooth
5. Consistency: Rubbery firm
6. Surrounding tissues: Mobile in all directions, not fixed to skin or deeper structures, no matting with adjacent nodes
7. Sensation: Non-tender

I also note multiple smaller nodes (approximately 1-1.5 cm) in the right posterior cervical chain with similar characteristics.

The remainder of the examination is unremarkable: no palpable supraclavicular, axillary, epitrochlear, or inguinal lymphadenopathy.

In summary, this patient has bilateral posterior cervical lymphadenopathy, predominantly left-sided, with a 3 cm rubbery, mobile, non-tender lymph node. The features are concerning and the differential diagnosis includes:
• Lymphoma (most likely given rubbery consistency, size >2 cm, non-tender, bilateral involvement)
• Infectious mononucleosis (though typically tender)
• Other haematological malignancy

To complete my assessment, I would:
• Examine the spleen and liver for organomegaly
• Perform a full skin examination
• Request urgent blood tests: FBC, film, LDH, EBV serology
• Arrange urgent ultrasound of the neck
• Refer for excision biopsy or core biopsy of the lymph node for histopathological diagnosis
• Consider CT chest/abdomen/pelvis staging if lymphoma confirmed

This case requires urgent haematology referral."

CRITICAL CLINICAL PEARL: Virchow's node (left supraclavicular lymphadenopathy) = intra-abdominal malignancy until proven otherwise. This is because the thoracic duct drains abdominal lymphatics and enters the venous system at the left subclavian vein. This sign is also called Troisier's sign. Immediate investigation with CT abdomen/pelvis and upper GI endoscopy required.

THE 7 S's DESCRIPTION FRAMEWORK - DETAILED GUIDE

EXAM CRITICAL: The 7 S's framework is used to describe ANY lump, not just lymph nodes. This includes thyroid nodules, breast lumps, skin lesions, hernias, testicular lumps, etc. Mastering this framework is essential for AMC Part 2 Clinical Examination success.
S Feature How to Examine Example Normal Example Pathological
1 Site/Location • Anatomical region
• Side (left/right)
• Specific landmarks
• Relation to other structures 		"Small soft node in the right anterior cervical chain, mid-level, 5 cm below the angle of mandible" "Left supraclavicular node, 2 cm above the left clavicle, medial third" (Virchow's node)
2 Size • Measure with tape or estimate
• Length × width × depth in cm
• Compare to familiar objects if needed (pea = 0.5cm, marble = 1cm, cherry = 2cm, walnut = 3cm, golf ball = 4cm) 		"Approximately 0.8 cm × 0.5 cm" (normal small reactive node) "3.5 cm × 2.5 cm × 2 cm" (pathological - any node >2 cm concerning)
3 Shape • Round, oval, irregular
• Regular vs irregular margins
• Lobulated (multiple nodules) 		"Oval with smooth, regular margins" "Irregular shape with lobulated surface" (suggests malignancy or matted nodes)
4 Surface • Run fingers over surface
• Smooth vs nodular vs bosselated
• Even vs uneven 		"Smooth, even surface" "Nodular, irregular surface with areas of firmness" (metastatic carcinoma)
5 Consistency • Palpate firmness
• Soft (like lips)
• Firm (like nose tip)
• Hard (like forehead bone)
• Rubbery (like rubber ball)
• Fluctuant (fluid-filled) 		"Soft, compressible consistency" (reactive/inflammatory node) Hard/Stony: "Hard, non-compressible consistency" (metastatic carcinoma)
Rubbery: "Rubbery firm consistency" (lymphoma - classic)
Fluctuant: "Fluctuant consistency suggesting abscess" (TB with necrosis)
6 Surrounding tissues (Mobility/Fixation) • Try to move lump in two perpendicular planes
• Check skin mobility over lump
• Check if fixed to deeper structures (ask patient to tense muscles)
• Check for matting (stuck to adjacent nodes) 		"Mobile in all directions, skin freely mobile over it, not fixed to deeper structures" (benign reactive node) Skin fixation: "Fixed to overlying skin which is tethered" (advanced malignancy with skin invasion)
Deep fixation: "Fixed to deeper structures, does not move with muscle contraction" (advanced malignancy)
Matted: "Multiple nodes matted together, forming conglomerate mass" (TB, metastases, lymphoma)
7 Sensation (Tenderness) • Ask: "Is this tender?"
• Watch patient's face (grimace = pain)
• Compare to surrounding area
• Grade: non-tender, mildly tender, moderately tender, very tender 		"Mildly tender to palpation" (acute reactive lymphadenopathy from infection) Non-tender: "Non-tender to palpation despite significant size" (MALIGNANCY - key red flag)
Very tender: "Very tender with acute inflammation signs" (acute bacterial lymphadenitis, abscess)
KEY CLINICAL CORRELATION:
Red Flags for Malignancy (using 7 S's):
  • Size: >2 cm (higher concern), >3 cm (very concerning)
  • Shape: Irregular margins
  • Surface: Nodular, uneven
  • Consistency: Hard/stony (metastatic carcinoma) OR rubbery (lymphoma)
  • Surrounding: Fixed to skin or deeper structures, matted nodes
  • Sensation: NON-TENDER (most malignant nodes are painless - critical red flag!)
Combination of ≥3 red flags → urgent investigation (biopsy, imaging) required

COMMON IMG MISTAKES ❌

Learn from these common errors: These mistakes cost marks in OSCE examinations and can lead to missed diagnoses in clinical practice.
  • ❌ Not examining from BEHIND for cervical nodes: Examining cervical lymph nodes from in front of the patient is incorrect technique. You must stand behind the patient and use both hands simultaneously. This allows proper bimanual palpation and comparison of both sides. Examining from the front makes it difficult to palpate posterior chain and occipital nodes.
  • ❌ Not supporting the arm during axillary examination: If you don't support the patient's arm properly, their pectoral muscles remain tense, making axillary node palpation impossible. Always support the arm at the wrist/forearm with your opposite hand, abduct to 45 degrees, and ask patient to relax completely. Common error: Asking patient to "hold your arm up" - this tenses muscles.
  • ❌ Skipping supraclavicular examination: This is a CRITICAL error. Supraclavicular nodes are often pathological, especially Virchow's node (left supraclavicular) which indicates intra-abdominal malignancy. You MUST specifically examine the supraclavicular fossa by hooking fingers over the clavicle. Ask patient to take a deep breath (makes nodes more prominent). Never skip this step.
  • ❌ Not using the 7 S's framework to describe findings: Simply saying "there's a lump in the neck" is inadequate. You MUST systematically describe using all 7 S's: Site, Size (measured in cm), Shape, Surface, Consistency, Surrounding tissues (mobile vs fixed), Sensation (tender vs non-tender). This is the expected Australian standard and examiners will mark you down if you don't use it.
  • ❌ Forgetting to offer a chaperone for inguinal examination: This is an AHPRA professional standard violation. You MUST offer a chaperone before any inguinal examination, regardless of patient gender. Document whether accepted or declined. In OSCE: State clearly "I would offer a chaperone for this examination." Failure to do so = automatic fail in some stations.
  • ❌ Assuming non-tender = benign: This is dangerous reverse logic. MALIGNANT lymph nodes are typically NON-TENDER. Tender nodes suggest acute inflammation/infection (benign reactive process). A large, hard, non-tender, fixed lymph node is HIGHLY suspicious for malignancy and requires urgent investigation. Never reassure a patient that "it's probably nothing" just because it's not tender.
  • ❌ Not examining the drainage area: If you find enlarged lymph nodes, you MUST examine the area they drain. Example: Cervical lymphadenopathy → examine scalp, oral cavity, throat, thyroid. Axillary nodes → examine breast and upper limb. This demonstrates clinical reasoning and may identify the primary pathology. In OSCE, state: "To complete my examination, I would examine [drainage area]."
  • ❌ Missing generalised lymphadenopathy: Only examining one region when the patient has generalised lymphadenopathy leads to missed diagnosis. Always examine ALL four major regions (cervical, axillary, epitrochlear, inguinal). Generalised lymphadenopathy (≥2 non-contiguous regions) has different differential diagnosis (lymphoma, leukaemia, HIV, sarcoidosis) compared to localised.
  • ❌ Incorrect measurement technique: Guessing size without measuring or using non-specific terms like "big" or "small" is inadequate. Use a measuring tape or estimate accurately using finger breadths (1 finger ≈ 1.5 cm). Describe in centimeters (e.g., "2.5 cm × 2 cm"). Size is critical for risk stratification: <1 cm usually benign, 1-2 cm intermediate, >2 cm concerning, >3 cm very concerning.
  • ❌ Not checking epitrochlear nodes: Many candidates forget to examine epitrochlear lymph nodes (above medial epicondyle of elbow). While less commonly enlarged, bilateral epitrochlear lymphadenopathy is pathological and suggests secondary syphilis, lymphoma, or sarcoidosis. Complete examination includes all regions. In OSCE, demonstrate you know where epitrochlear nodes are located.
  • ❌ Poor hand hygiene practice: Not performing visible hand hygiene before examination or wearing gloves for inguinal examination without washing hands first. Always wash hands in front of patient before starting. For inguinal examination: wash hands, then put on gloves. This is infection control basics and AHPRA standard. In OSCE, verbalize: "I'm washing my hands now" even if using hand rub.
  • ❌ Not recognizing Virchow's node (Troisier's sign): Left supraclavicular lymphadenopathy is almost always pathological and suggests intra-abdominal malignancy (gastric, pancreatic, ovarian, renal, testicular). This is called Virchow's node or Troisier's sign. If you find this, you MUST state the significance and recommend urgent investigation (CT abdomen/pelvis, upper GI endoscopy). Failure to recognize this clinical sign suggests knowledge gap.

EXAMINATION MNEMONICS

1. The 7 S's Framework Mnemonic

"Some Students Seem Super Confident Surrounding Subjects"
  • Site/Location
  • Size
  • Shape
  • Surface
  • Consistency (the "C" sounds like "S")
  • Surrounding tissues (mobility/fixation)
  • Sensation (tenderness)

2. Cervical Lymph Node Sequence Mnemonic

"Some Students Totally Post Amazingly On Social Platforms"
  • Submental
  • Submandibular
  • Tonsillar
  • Pre-auricular
  • Post-Auricular
  • Occipital
  • Anterior cervical (the "S" is for the SCM muscle - examine along anterior border)
  • Supraclavicular
  • Posterior cervical chain

3. Axillary Node Groups Mnemonic

"Can Anyone Please Look Apically?"
  • Central (midaxillary)
  • Anterior (pectoral)
  • Posterior (subscapular)
  • Lateral (humeral)
  • Apical

4. Causes of Generalised Lymphadenopathy Mnemonic

"LYMPH"
  • Lymphoma (Hodgkin's and Non-Hodgkin's)
  • LYmphatic leukaemia (CLL, ALL)
  • Mononucleosis (EBV) and other infections (CMV, toxoplasmosis, HIV)
  • Persistent generalised lymphadenopathy (HIV/AIDS)
  • Haematological (leukaemia, myeloproliferative disorders)

Also remember: Sarcoidosis, TB, autoimmune diseases (SLE, RA)

5. Red Flags for Malignant Lymph Nodes

"HARD FIX"
  • Hard consistency (stony hard = metastatic carcinoma; rubbery = lymphoma)
  • Abnormal size (>2 cm concerning, >3 cm very concerning)
  • Rapidly enlarging
  • Don't hurt (non-tender = malignancy more likely than tender)
  • Fixed to surrounding structures (skin or deep fixation)
  • Irregular shape/surface (Irregular)
  • eXtra symptoms (B symptoms: fever, night sweats, weight loss)
EXAM TIP: Practice the systematic sequence until it becomes automatic. In the OSCE, nerves can make you forget steps. Having a reliable mnemonic-based sequence ensures you cover all regions systematically, even under pressure. Examiners specifically watch for systematic approach.
