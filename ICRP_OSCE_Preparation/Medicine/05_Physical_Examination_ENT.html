Opening Statement (30 seconds)

Scenario: You are asked to examine the ear, nose, and throat of a 45-year-old patient presenting with recurrent ear pain and hearing loss.

Differential thinking cue - What to consider from the start:

As I approach this ENT examination, I'm immediately considering three broad categories:

  1. Infection: Otitis externa, otitis media, chronic suppurative otitis media (CSOM), tonsillitis, pharyngitis
  2. Mechanical obstruction: Wax impaction, foreign body, nasal polyps, deviated septum, Eustachian tube dysfunction
  3. Neoplastic/structural: Acoustic neuroma, nasopharyngeal carcinoma, cholesteatoma, perforated tympanic membrane

The pattern of symptoms (unilateral vs bilateral, acute vs chronic, associated symptoms) will guide my examination focus and differential ranking.

What you say - word for word:

The 5 Ps Framework for ENT Examination

1. POSITION (Patient Positioning)

What you say: "Please sit comfortably in this chair with your back supported. I'll need you to turn your head in different directions as we go through the examination. Could you please remove any earrings or hearing aids for me?"

Positioning requirements:

  • Patient seated upright in examination chair or on edge of bed
  • Examiner standing or seated at same level for ear examination
  • Good lighting essential (use otoscope light or examination lamp)
  • Remove hearing aids, earrings, glasses if examining ears
  • Ensure patient's hair is away from ears

5. PRESENTATION (General Inspection)

What you do (mental checklist while greeting patient):

From the end of the bed/across the room:

  • General appearance: Well/unwell, comfortable/distressed, febrile appearance
  • Facial asymmetry: Facial nerve palsy (Bell's palsy), stroke, parotid swelling
  • Obvious abnormalities: Hearing aid, cochlear implant, obvious ear discharge
  • Speech: Nasal quality (nasopharyngeal obstruction), hoarse voice (laryngeal pathology), muffled "hot potato" voice (peritonsillar abscess)
  • Breathing pattern: Mouth breathing (nasal obstruction), stridor (upper airway obstruction)
  • Swellings: Thyroid enlargement, cervical lymphadenopathy, parotid swelling

Differential thinking at presentation:

  • Febrile + toxic appearance: Think acute infection (mastoiditis, quinsy, epiglottitis)
  • Unilateral facial weakness: Bell's palsy vs stroke vs Ramsay Hunt syndrome
  • Stridor: EMERGENCY - upper airway obstruction (foreign body, epiglottitis, angioedema)
  • Hearing aids bilaterally: Presbycusis, noise-induced hearing loss

Systematic Examination Steps (4-5 minutes)

EXAMINATION SEQUENCE: Ears ‚Üí Nose ‚Üí Throat ‚Üí Neck

Always examine BOTH sides for comparison, starting with the unaffected/less painful side.

1. EAR EXAMINATION (2.5 minutes total)

A. External Ear Inspection (30 seconds)

What you say: "I'm going to start by looking at the outside of your ears. I'll examine the right ear first, then the left."

What you look for (inspect both ears):

  • Pinna (auricle):
    • Size and shape: Microtia (small ear), prominent ears, cauliflower ear (trauma)
    • Colour: Erythema (infection, inflammation), pallor, cyanosis
    • Skin changes: Eczema, psoriasis, seborrhoeic dermatitis, tophi (gout), skin cancer
    • Swelling: Haematoma, perichondritis, cellulitis
    • Discharge from meatus: Purulent (infection), clear (CSF leak post-trauma), bloody
  • Pre-auricular area: Sinuses, skin tags, swelling
  • Post-auricular area: Scars (mastoidectomy), swelling (mastoiditis), erythema
  • Tragus: Note position for tenderness test

Differential thinking - External ear findings:

  • Red, swollen, tender pinna: Otitis externa (swimmer's ear), perichondritis, cellulitis
  • Vesicles on pinna + facial palsy: Ramsay Hunt syndrome (herpes zoster oticus) - urgent antiviral needed
  • Post-auricular swelling + tenderness: Acute mastoiditis (complication of otitis media) - surgical emergency
  • Clear discharge from ear after head injury: CSF otorrhoea - basal skull fracture

B. Palpation of External Ear (30 seconds)

What you say: "Now I'm going to gently feel around your ear. Please tell me if anything is tender."

What you do (palpate both ears):

  1. Tragus tenderness test: Gently press on tragus (cartilaginous projection anterior to ear canal)
    • Positive (tender) = Otitis externa
    • Negative (not tender) = More likely otitis media
  2. Pinna traction test: Gently pull pinna upward and outward
    • Positive (painful) = Otitis externa
    • Negative = More likely otitis media
  3. Mastoid tenderness: Palpate mastoid process (bony prominence behind ear)
    • Tender + swollen = Acute mastoiditis
  4. Pre-auricular lymph nodes: Palpate anterior to tragus
    • Enlarged = Infection, malignancy

üö® RED FLAG: Severe post-auricular swelling, tenderness, and fever = ACUTE MASTOIDITIS. This is a surgical emergency. Immediate ENT referral and IV antibiotics required.

C. Otoscopy (1.5 minutes - most important part)

What you say: "Now I'm going to look inside your ear using this otoscope. I'll pull your ear back slightly to straighten the ear canal so I can see the eardrum. You'll feel some gentle pressure but it shouldn't hurt. Please keep your head very still and let me know if it's painful."

Otoscopy technique (CRITICAL for AMC OSCE):

  1. Select appropriate speculum: Largest that fits comfortably (usually 4-5mm for adults)
  2. Hold otoscope correctly:
    • RIGHT hand for RIGHT ear, LEFT hand for LEFT ear
    • Hold like a pen, rest hand on patient's cheek/head (stabilizes scope, prevents injury if patient moves)
  3. Straighten the ear canal:
    • Adults: Pull pinna UP and BACK (posterosuperiorly)
    • Children: Pull pinna DOWN and BACK (posteroinferiorly) - different anatomy
  4. Insert speculum gently: Only enter outer 1/3 of canal (cartilaginous part), never force into bony canal (painful)
  5. Systematic inspection:
    • First: External auditory canal (EAC) walls
    • Then: Tympanic membrane (eardrum)

What you look for in External Auditory Canal (EAC):

  • Wax (cerumen): Colour (yellow-brown normal, black = impacted), amount, impacted blocking view
  • Discharge: Purulent (infection), serous (CSOM), bloody
  • Foreign body: Beads, insects (common in children)
  • Canal walls: Erythema and swelling (otitis externa), eczema, fungal debris (otomycosis), polyps
  • Exostoses: Bony lumps in canal (surfer's ear)

What you look for in Tympanic Membrane (TM):

Differential thinking - Otoscopy findings:

  • Attic perforation with white debris (keratin): Cholesteatoma ‚Üí urgent ENT referral (can erode ossicles, mastoid, facial nerve, skull base)
  • Central TM perforation with scanty discharge: CSOM ‚Üí topical antibiotics, keep ear dry, ENT referral
  • Blue TM post head injury: Haemotympanum from basal skull fracture ‚Üí CT head, neurosurgical assessment

  • Haemotympanum post-trauma = Basal skull fracture ‚Üí CT head
  • Bulging TM + severe pain + fever in child = AOM with imminent perforation ‚Üí Antibiotics

D. Hearing Assessment (if time permits - 30 seconds)

What you say: "I'd like to do a quick hearing test. I'm going to whisper some numbers, and I want you to repeat them back to me."

Whisper test (screening for hearing loss):

  1. Stand 60cm (arm's length) behind patient
  2. Ask patient to occlude opposite ear (press tragus in)
  3. Exhale fully, then whisper a combination of numbers/letters (e.g., "3-F-9")
  4. Patient should repeat correctly if hearing normal
  5. Repeat on other side

If abnormal: Proceed to Rinne and Weber tests to differentiate conductive vs sensorineural hearing loss.

Rinne Test (512 Hz tuning fork):

What you say: "I'm going to use this tuning fork to test your hearing in two ways. First, I'll place it on the bone behind your ear, then near your ear. Tell me where it sounds louder."

  1. Strike tuning fork (512 Hz) on your elbow/knee
  2. Place base on mastoid process (bone conduction - BC)
  3. When patient can no longer hear it, move fork to 2cm from ear canal (air conduction - AC)
  4. Ask: "Can you still hear this?"

Interpretation:

  • Rinne positive (normal): AC > BC (patient hears fork at ear canal after mastoid stops) - Normal or sensorineural hearing loss
  • Rinne negative (abnormal): BC > AC (patient doesn't hear fork at ear canal) - Conductive hearing loss (wax, OME, ossicle damage, perforation)

Weber Test (512 Hz tuning fork):

What you say: "Now I'll place the tuning fork on your forehead. Tell me if you hear it more in one ear or equally in both."

  1. Strike tuning fork
  2. Place base on centre of forehead or top of head (midline)
  3. Ask: "Do you hear this in the middle, or more in one ear?"

Interpretation:

  • Weber central (normal): Sound heard equally both ears - Normal hearing or symmetrical hearing loss
  • Weber lateralizes to affected ear: Conductive hearing loss (that ear) - sound lateralizes to ear with conductive loss
  • Weber lateralizes to normal ear: Sensorineural hearing loss (opposite ear) - sound lateralizes away from SNHL

Differential thinking - Hearing tests:

Finding Type of Hearing Loss Common Causes
Rinne negative (BC > AC), Weber lateralizes to affected ear Conductive hearing loss Wax impaction, OME (glue ear), CSOM, otosclerosis, ossicular chain disruption
Rinne positive (AC > BC), Weber lateralizes to normal ear Sensorineural hearing loss Presbycusis (age-related), noise-induced, M√©ni√®re's disease, acoustic neuroma, ototoxic drugs
Rinne positive both sides, Weber central, but reduced hearing Bilateral sensorineural loss Presbycusis, bilateral noise exposure, genetic

2. NOSE EXAMINATION (1 minute)

A. External Nose Inspection (15 seconds)

What you say: "Now I'm going to examine your nose. First I'll look at the outside, then I'll look inside your nostrils using this light."

What you look for (inspect external nose):

  • Shape and symmetry: Deviation (trauma, previous surgery), saddle nose deformity (Wegener's granulomatosis, cocaine use)
  • Skin changes: Erythema, telangiectasia, rhinophyma (bulbous red nose - rosacea, chronic alcohol use)
  • Swelling: Nasal polyps visible at nostrils, furuncle, cellulitis
  • Discharge: Clear (rhinitis, CSF leak), purulent (sinusitis), bloody (epistaxis, trauma, malignancy)
  • Scars: Previous rhinoplasty, trauma repair

B. Palpation of Nose and Sinuses (15 seconds)

What you say: "I'm going to gently feel your nose and the areas around your eyes and cheeks. Let me know if anything is tender."

What you do:

  1. Palpate nasal bridge: Tenderness (fracture), crepitus (air in tissues)
  2. Frontal sinuses: Press above medial aspect of eyebrows
    • Tenderness = Frontal sinusitis
  3. Maxillary sinuses: Press on cheeks below eyes (over maxilla)
    • Tenderness = Maxillary sinusitis (most common)
  4. Transillumination (rarely done): Shine light in mouth to check sinus opacification

C. Anterior Rhinoscopy (30 seconds)

What you say: "I'm going to tilt your head back slightly and shine this light into your nostrils to examine inside your nose. This won't hurt."

Technique:

  1. Tilt patient's head back slightly (or examine with patient sitting, you standing)
  2. Use pen torch or otoscope light (without speculum) or nasal speculum
  3. Lift tip of nose gently with your thumb
  4. Inspect each nostril systematically

What you look for (both nostrils):

  • Nasal septum: Midline or deviated, perforation (cocaine use, Wegener's, previous surgery)
  • Nasal mucosa: Colour (red = inflammation, pale/boggy = allergic rhinitis), congestion
  • Turbinates: Swollen/enlarged (chronic rhinitis), normal (small, pink)
  • Polyps: Smooth, grey/translucent masses (bilateral polyps = chronic rhinosinusitis, asthma, aspirin sensitivity)
  • Discharge: Clear (viral URTI, allergic rhinitis), purulent (bacterial sinusitis), bloody
  • Foreign body: Common in children (beads, food, toy parts) - unilateral purulent discharge

Differential thinking - Nasal findings:

  • Bilateral nasal polyps + asthma history: Samter's triad (asthma, nasal polyps, aspirin sensitivity) ‚Üí avoid NSAIDs
  • Unilateral purulent discharge in child: Nasal foreign body until proven otherwise ‚Üí removal required
  • Septal perforation + saddle nose: Cocaine use, Wegener's granulomatosis (GPA), previous surgery
  • Maxillary sinus tenderness + purulent nasal discharge >10 days: Acute bacterial rhinosinusitis ‚Üí consider antibiotics (amoxicillin)

3. THROAT EXAMINATION (45 seconds)

Oropharyngeal Examination

What you say: "Now I need to look inside your mouth and throat. Please open your mouth wide and stick your tongue out. I'm going to use this tongue depressor to get a better view. Try to relax and breathe through your mouth. This might make you feel like gagging - that's completely normal."

Technique:

  1. Use pen torch for illumination
  2. Ask patient to open mouth wide and say "Ahhh" (lowers tongue, elevates soft palate)
  3. Use wooden tongue depressor to gently depress middle-posterior 1/3 of tongue (not too far back - triggers gag reflex)
  4. Systematic inspection of all structures

    • vs Infectious mononucleosis (EBV - "glandular fever") ‚Üí DO NOT give amoxicillin (causes rash)

  • White patches on tongue/buccal mucosa that scrape off: Oral candidiasis (thrush) ‚Üí immunosuppression (HIV, steroids, diabetes)
  • Smooth red tongue (atrophic glossitis): B12 deficiency, folate deficiency, iron deficiency

  • Asymmetric tonsil enlargement (especially if painless): Tonsillar lymphoma or squamous cell carcinoma ‚Üí urgent ENT biopsy

4. NECK EXAMINATION (1.5 minutes)

A. Inspection (10 seconds)

What you say: "Now I need to examine your neck. Please sit upright and keep your head in a neutral position."

What you look for:

  • Obvious masses: Thyroid enlargement (goitre), lymphadenopathy, salivary gland swelling
  • Scars: Previous thyroid surgery, lymph node biopsy, neck dissection
  • Asymmetry: Unilateral swelling
  • Skin changes: Erythema, sinus openings

B. Palpation of Cervical Lymph Nodes (1 minute)

What you say: "I'm going to feel the lymph nodes in your neck now. Please relax your neck muscles and let me know if any area is tender."

Technique (CRITICAL for AMC OSCE):

  • Stand behind patient (or beside if examining from front)
  • Use pads of fingers (2nd, 3rd, 4th fingers) of both hands
  • Gentle circular/rolling motion against underlying structures
  • Systematic approach: Follow anatomical chain
  • Compare both sides simultaneously

Systematic lymph node examination (order matters):

  1. Submental: Under chin, midline (bimanual palpation)
  2. Submandibular: Under jaw, angle of mandible bilaterally
  3. Pre-auricular: Anterior to tragus
  4. Post-auricular (mastoid): Behind ear, over mastoid process
  5. Occipital: Base of skull posteriorly
  6. Anterior cervical chain: Along anterior border of sternocleidomastoid (superficial to SCM)
  7. Posterior cervical chain: Along posterior border of SCM and trapezius
  8. Supraclavicular: Above clavicles bilaterally (left = Virchow's node - GI malignancy)
  9. Deep cervical (jugular chain): Deep to SCM, along internal jugular vein

If lymph nodes palpable, describe using 7 S's:

  • Site: Which nodal group (e.g., right submandibular)
  • Size: In cm (e.g., 2cm x 1cm)
  • Shape: Round, oval, irregular
  • Surface: Smooth or irregular
  • Consistency: Soft (infection), firm (lymphoma), hard (malignancy)
  • Mobility: Mobile (infection/lymphoma) vs fixed (malignant infiltration)
  • Tenderness: Tender (infection, inflammation) vs non-tender (malignancy)

Differential thinking - Lymphadenopathy:

Lymph Node Features Most Likely Cause Action
Multiple, small (<1cm), mobile, tender, bilateral Viral URTI, infectious mononucleosis (glandular fever) Conservative management, monospot test if IM suspected
Single, large (>2cm), tender, unilateral submandibular Bacterial cervical lymphadenitis (Staph/Strep) Oral antibiotics (flucloxacillin), consider ultrasound if abscess
Multiple, rubbery, non-tender, >2cm, matted Lymphoma (Hodgkin's or Non-Hodgkin's) Urgent haematology referral, lymph node biopsy
Single, hard, non-tender, fixed, irregular, >2cm Metastatic malignancy (head/neck SCC, lung, breast, GI) Urgent ENT/oncology referral, staging CT, biopsy
Left supraclavicular (Virchow's node), hard, fixed GI malignancy (gastric, pancreatic, colorectal metastases) Urgent gastroenterology referral, CT chest/abdomen/pelvis
Generalized lymphadenopathy (>2 non-contiguous sites) Systemic infection (HIV, TB, toxoplasmosis), lymphoma, leukaemia Full blood count, film, HIV/TB testing, haematology referral

C. Thyroid Examination (if indicated - 30 seconds)

What you say: "Now I'm going to examine your thyroid gland at the front of your neck. I'll ask you to swallow some water - this helps me feel the gland moving."

Quick thyroid exam (if time allows):

  1. Inspection: Ask patient to swallow (thyroid moves up with swallowing)
  2. Palpation from behind: Feel for thyroid lobes on either side of trachea, ask patient to swallow
  3. Percuss over sternum: Dullness = retrosternal goitre
  4. Auscultate: If enlarged thyroid, listen for bruit (thyrotoxicosis - Graves' disease)

Top 3 Differentials (Ranked with Justification)

Based on examination findings, here are the TOP 3 differentials for common ENT presentations:

Scenario A: Ear Pain + Hearing Loss

  1. 1st: Otitis Media with Effusion (OME - "Glue Ear")
    • Justification: Retracted tympanic membrane, amber fluid level visible, reduced TM mobility on pneumatic otoscopy, Rinne negative (conductive hearing loss), Weber lateralizes to affected ear
    • Management: Watchful waiting for 3 months (80% resolve spontaneously), hearing assessment, consider grommet insertion if persistent >3 months or bilateral with developmental delay
    • Why ranked 1st: Most common cause of conductive hearing loss in children and adults post-URTI
  2. 2nd: Chronic Suppurative Otitis Media (CSOM)
    • Justification: Central TM perforation, scanty mucopurulent discharge, conductive hearing loss on tuning fork tests, history of recurrent ear infections
    • Management: Keep ear dry, topical antibiotic ear drops (ciprofloxacin), ENT referral for possible tympanoplasty (surgical TM repair)
    • Why ranked 2nd: Common in adults with childhood ear infection history, requires specialist management
  3. 3rd: Cholesteatoma
    • Justification: Attic (marginal) perforation, white keratin debris visible, foul-smelling discharge, conductive hearing loss, erosion of ossicles possible
    • Management: URGENT ENT referral for surgical excision (can erode into mastoid, facial nerve, inner ear, brain)
    • Why ranked 3rd: Less common but MUST NOT MISS - dangerous complication of chronic ear disease

Scenario B: Sore Throat + Fever

  1. 1st: Acute Bacterial Tonsillitis (Streptococcal Pharyngitis)
    • Justification: Bilateral tonsillar enlargement (Grade 2-3), white/yellow exudate on tonsils, tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy, fever >38¬∞C, no cough (Centor score ‚â•3)
    • Management: Phenoxymethylpenicillin 500mg QID for 10 days, analgesia, throat swab for culture, advise return if worsening
    • Why ranked 1st: Most common bacterial cause requiring antibiotics, prevents rheumatic fever complications
  2. 2nd: Infectious Mononucleosis (Glandular Fever - EBV)
    • Justification: Massive bilateral tonsillar enlargement, thick white exudate, palatal petechiae, generalized lymphadenopathy (cervical + axillary + inguinal), splenomegaly, young adult (15-25 years), prolonged fatigue
    • Management: Supportive care, avoid contact sports (splenic rupture risk), DO NOT give amoxicillin (causes rash), monospot test/EBV serology, advise 4-6 week recovery
    • Why ranked 2nd: Common in young adults, important to differentiate from strep throat (different management)
  3. 3rd: Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy)
    • Justification: UNILATERAL tonsillar swelling (asymmetric), uvula deviation AWAY from affected side, trismus (difficulty opening mouth), "hot potato" voice, drooling, toxic appearance
    • Management: URGENT ENT referral for incision and drainage, IV benzylpenicillin + metronidazole, IV fluids, analgesia, admit for observation
    • Why ranked 3rd: Less common but SURGICAL EMERGENCY - can compromise airway

Scenario C: Nasal Obstruction + Facial Pain

  1. 1st: Acute Bacterial Rhinosinusitis (Maxillary Sinusitis)
    • Justification: Maxillary sinus tenderness on palpation, purulent nasal discharge (unilateral or bilateral), nasal congestion, facial pain/pressure worse on bending forward, symptoms >10 days or worsening after initial improvement ("double sickening"), fever
    • Management: Amoxicillin 500mg TDS for 5-7 days (if severe/not improving), intranasal corticosteroids (fluticasone), saline nasal irrigation, analgesia, advise return if no improvement in 48 hours
    • Why ranked 1st: Most common cause of persistent nasal symptoms >10 days post-URTI
  2. 2nd: Chronic Rhinosinusitis with Nasal Polyps
    • Justification: Bilateral smooth grey/translucent polyps visible on anterior rhinoscopy, chronic nasal obstruction (>12 weeks), reduced sense of smell (anosmia), post-nasal drip, history of asthma, aspirin sensitivity possible (Samter's triad)
    • Management: Intranasal corticosteroids (mometasone/fluticasone) long-term, saline irrigation, oral corticosteroids if severe, ENT referral for polypectomy if medical management fails, avoid aspirin/NSAIDs if aspirin-sensitive
    • Why ranked 2nd: Common chronic condition, associated with asthma, requires long-term management
  3. 3rd: Nasal Foreign Body (in children)
    • Justification: UNILATERAL purulent foul-smelling nasal discharge in child, history of nasal manipulation/play, foreign body visible on anterior rhinoscopy (bead, food, toy part)
    • Management: ENT removal (may need sedation if child uncooperative), do NOT attempt to remove if object pushed posteriorly (aspiration risk), educate parents about supervision
    • Why ranked 3rd: Less common in adults, but MUST NOT MISS in paediatric patients with unilateral nasal symptoms

Critical Errors that Fail AMC OSCE Stations

1. Otoscopy Technique Errors ‚ùå

  • ‚ùå WRONG: Inserting otoscope speculum too deeply into ear canal (painful, can damage TM)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: Insert speculum only into outer 1/3 of ear canal (cartilaginous portion), rest examining hand on patient's cheek to stabilize and prevent sudden movements
  • ‚ùå WRONG: Pulling child's pinna UP and BACK like an adult
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: In children <3 years, pull pinna DOWN and BACK (different ear canal anatomy - more horizontal in children)
  • ‚ùå WRONG: Not comparing both ears (missing unilateral pathology)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: ALWAYS examine both ears for comparison, document "normal left ear, abnormal right ear" to show systematic approach
  • ‚ùå WRONG: Describing TM as "normal" without identifying specific landmarks (cone of light, handle of malleus, umbo)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: "The tympanic membrane is pearly grey and translucent. I can see the cone of light at the 5 o'clock position, the handle of malleus is visible running from superior to central, and the membrane is in a neutral position - not bulging or retracted."

2. Hearing Test Interpretation Errors ‚ùå

  • ‚ùå WRONG: Confusing Rinne and Weber test interpretation
    • Saying "Rinne negative = sensorineural loss" (INCORRECT - it's conductive loss)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT:
    • Rinne NEGATIVE = Conductive loss (BC > AC - bone conduction better than air conduction)
    • Weber lateralizes TO affected ear = Conductive loss (sound lateralizes to ear with conductive problem)
    • Weber lateralizes AWAY from affected ear = Sensorineural loss (sound lateralizes to normal ear)
  • ‚ùå WRONG: Using 128 Hz tuning fork for hearing tests (this is for vibration sense testing in neurological exam)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: Use 512 Hz tuning fork for Rinne and Weber tests (optimal frequency for detecting hearing loss)

3. Throat Examination Safety Errors ‚ùå

  • ‚ùå WRONG: Forcing tongue depressor too far back on tongue (triggers severe gag reflex, patient vomits)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: Place tongue depressor on middle-posterior 1/3 of tongue, avoid touching posterior pharyngeal wall, ask patient to breathe through mouth and relax
  • ‚ùå WRONG: In suspected epiglottitis, attempting to examine throat with tongue depressor (can precipitate complete airway obstruction - FATAL ERROR)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: If patient has severe sore throat + stridor + drooling + tripod position (leaning forward) + toxic appearance ‚Üí DO NOT EXAMINE THROAT. Call anaesthetics/ENT immediately. Diagnosis is EPIGLOTTITIS - urgent airway management needed.
  • ‚ùå WRONG: Giving amoxicillin to patient with infectious mononucleosis (causes widespread maculopapular rash in 90% of cases)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: If tonsillar exudate + generalized lymphadenopathy + splenomegaly + young adult ‚Üí suspect EBV (glandular fever). Do monospot test BEFORE prescribing antibiotics. If positive, supportive care only, avoid amoxicillin/ampicillin.

4. Lymph Node Examination Errors ‚ùå

  • ‚ùå WRONG: Palpating lymph nodes from the front of patient (awkward, misses posterior chains)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: Stand BEHIND patient for cervical lymph node examination, allows better access to all nodal groups and bilateral comparison
  • ‚ùå WRONG: Not examining supraclavicular lymph nodes (missing Virchow's node - gastric cancer metastases)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: ALWAYS palpate supraclavicular fossae (ask patient to shrug shoulders to relax neck, palpate above clavicles). LEFT supraclavicular node (Virchow's node) = GI malignancy metastases.
  • ‚ùå WRONG: Describing lymph nodes as just "enlarged" without characterization
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: Use 7 S's: Site, Size (cm), Shape, Surface, Consistency (soft/firm/hard), Mobility (mobile/fixed), Tenderness (tender/non-tender). Example: "There is a 2cm x 1cm, hard, non-tender, fixed right-sided level II cervical lymph node, concerning for metastatic disease."

5. Nasal Examination Errors ‚ùå

  • ‚ùå WRONG: Not recognizing nasal foreign body in child with unilateral purulent discharge (assuming it's sinusitis and prescribing antibiotics)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: Unilateral nasal discharge in child = foreign body until proven otherwise. Perform anterior rhinoscopy, look for beads, food, toy parts. ENT removal required.
  • ‚ùå WRONG: Diagnosing bacterial sinusitis in patient with viral URTI symptoms <10 days (over-prescribing antibiotics)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: Acute bacterial rhinosinusitis diagnosis requires: symptoms >10 days OR worsening after initial improvement OR severe symptoms (fever >39¬∞C + purulent discharge + facial pain). If <10 days with improving symptoms = viral URTI, supportive care only.

6. Cultural and Communication Errors ‚ùå

  • ‚ùå WRONG: Using medical jargon: "I'm going to perform otoscopy to visualize your tympanic membrane"
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: "I'm going to look inside your ear using this lighted instrument to check your eardrum"
  • ‚ùå WRONG: Not warning patient about gag reflex before throat examination (patient panics and becomes uncooperative)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: "When I examine your throat with this tongue depressor, you might feel like gagging - that's completely normal. Try to relax and breathe through your mouth."

7. Differential Diagnosis Errors ‚ùå

  • ‚ùå WRONG: Presenting unranked list of 10 possible diagnoses without justification
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: Present TOP 3 differentials ranked by likelihood, with specific examination findings justifying each. Example: "Most likely is OME because of retracted TM, amber fluid, and conductive hearing loss. Second is CSOM given the central perforation..."
  • ‚ùå WRONG: Missing red flag diagnoses: cholesteatoma, quinsy, epiglottitis, malignancy
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: Always consider dangerous diagnoses:
    • Attic perforation + white debris = Cholesteatoma ‚Üí urgent ENT
    • Unilateral tonsil swelling + uvula deviation = Quinsy ‚Üí urgent ENT drainage
    • Stridor + drooling + tripod position = Epiglottitis ‚Üí DO NOT examine throat, call anaesthetics
    • Hard, fixed, non-tender lymph node = Malignancy ‚Üí urgent biopsy

8. Australian Context Errors ‚ùå

  • ‚ùå WRONG: Saying "I'll refer to an ENT specialist" (American terminology)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: "I'll refer to the ENT surgeons" or "I'll make an urgent ENT referral" (Australian NHS/hospital terminology)
  • ‚ùå WRONG: Using American drug names: "I'll prescribe Augmentin for sinusitis"
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: Use generic names in Australia: "I'll prescribe amoxicillin-clavulanate" or just "amoxicillin 500mg three times daily for acute bacterial rhinosinusitis"
  • ‚ùå WRONG: Spelling errors in written notes: "pediatric ENT examination" (American spelling)
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: Australian spelling: "paediatric ENT examination", "oesophagus", "colour"

9. Time Management Errors ‚ùå

  • ‚ùå WRONG: Spending 4 minutes on otoscopy alone, rushing through throat/neck examination, no time for presentation
  • ‚úÖ CORRECT: Allocate time appropriately:
    • Introduction + 5 Ps: 1.5 minutes
    • Ear examination: 2.5 minutes
    • Nose examination: 1 minute
    • Throat examination: 45 seconds
    • Neck examination: 1.5 minutes
    • Completion + presentation: 1 minute
    • TOTAL: 8 minutes

10. OSCE Station Failure Points ‚ùå

  • ‚ùå AUTOMATIC FAIL: Not washing hands before/after examination (infection control breach)
  • ‚ùå AUTOMATIC FAIL: Not obtaining consent before examination (AHPRA violation)
  • ‚ùå AUTOMATIC FAIL: Causing patient pain/distress without stopping (patient safety concern)
  • ‚ùå AUTOMATIC FAIL: Missing red flag diagnosis (cholesteatoma, quinsy, epiglottitis, malignancy)
  • ‚ùå AUTOMATIC FAIL: Examining throat in suspected epiglottitis (life-threatening error)

Key Take-Home Messages

  • ‚úì 5 Ps Framework is mandatory: Position, Permission, Pain, Preparation, Presentation
  • ‚úì Always examine BOTH sides: Comparison is critical for identifying unilateral pathology
  • ‚úì Otoscopy technique matters: Correct pinna traction (UP-BACK adults, DOWN-BACK children), stabilize hand, shallow insertion
  • ‚úì Rinne/Weber interpretation: Rinne negative = conductive loss, Weber lateralizes TO affected ear = conductive loss
  • ‚úì Don't miss red flags: Cholesteatoma (attic perforation), quinsy (unilateral swelling), epiglottitis (DO NOT examine throat), malignant lymph nodes
  • ‚úì Differential thinking throughout: From initial observation to final presentation, link findings to ranked differentials
  • ‚úì Time-conscious examination: 8 minutes total, allocate time systematically
  • ‚úì Australian context: Use Australian spelling, terminology, and referral pathways (ENT surgeons, GP, Emergency Department)
  • ‚úì Communication in plain English: No jargon, warn about gag reflex, explain each step
  • ‚úì Top 3 differentials with justification: Not just a list - rank by likelihood with specific examination findings supporting each
