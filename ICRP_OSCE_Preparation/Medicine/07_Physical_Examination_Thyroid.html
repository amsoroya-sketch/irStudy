THYROID EXAMINATION

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL FOR AMC OSCE:
  • Always examine from BEHIND the patient (standing position)
  • MUST provide water for swallowing test (key diagnostic feature)
  • Assess for both anatomical abnormality AND functional status
  • Eye examination is MANDATORY if any signs of hyperthyroidism
  • Never forget lymph node examination (thyroid cancer screening)

1. PREPARATION (60 seconds)

WORD-FOR-WORD INTRODUCTION SCRIPT:

[Perform visible hand hygiene with alcohol gel]

"Good morning/afternoon. My name is Dr [Your Name], and I'm one of the doctors here at [Hospital Name]. Before we begin, can you please confirm your full name and date of birth for me?"

[Patient confirms identity]

"Thank you. Today I'd like to examine your thyroid gland, which is in your neck. This will involve me looking at your neck and face, feeling your neck from behind, and checking some other areas to assess your thyroid function. I'll need you to take a sip of water during the examination. The examination shouldn't be painful, but please let me know immediately if you feel any discomfort."

"Is that okay with you? Do you have any questions before we start?"

[Patient agrees]

"Before we begin, do you have any pain or tenderness in your neck at the moment?"
üí° Equipment Checklist:
  • ‚úÖ Stethoscope (for auscultation of thyroid bruit)
  • ‚úÖ Glass of water (essential for swallowing test)
  • ‚úÖ Pen torch (for eye examination - Graves' disease)
  • ‚úÖ Tendon hammer (for reflex assessment)
  • ‚úÖ Hand hygiene gel visible to patient

2. POSITION

Patient Positioning:

  • Sitting upright on chair or edge of bed (NOT lying down)
  • Patient should be comfortable and relaxed
  • Neck should be in neutral position (not extended or flexed)
  • Good lighting essential - ensure you can see neck clearly

Your Position:

  • Initially stand in FRONT for inspection
  • Move BEHIND patient for palpation (key technique)
  • Return to FRONT for percussion, auscultation, and functional assessment
Say out loud: "I'm going to position you sitting upright for this examination. This helps me assess your thyroid gland properly. Is this position comfortable for you?"

3. PERMISSION

CONSENT AND EXPOSURE SCRIPT:

"I'll need to see and examine your neck, so I'll need you to remove any scarves, necklaces, or high collars. I'll also need to examine your hands, eyes, and chest area. For the chest, I'll need to expose the upper chest to check your heart rate and look for any signs. I'll provide a sheet to maintain your privacy."

[For female patients]
"I may need to briefly examine the front of your chest. Would you like a female chaperone present during the examination?"

"Is all of that okay with you?"

Required Exposure:

  • Neck fully exposed (remove scarves, ties, necklaces, high collars)
  • Upper chest visible (to assess for thyroidectomy scars extending below neck)
  • Hands visible (for tremor assessment)
  • Arms accessible (for reflex testing)
  • Face clearly visible (for eye signs, facial features)

4. PERFORM THE EXAMINATION (6 minutes)

A. GENERAL INSPECTION (30 seconds - from end of bed/standing in front)

Say out loud: "I'm going to start by observing you from here, looking for any signs that might relate to your thyroid function."

Observe for Signs of Thyroid Dysfunction:

HYPERTHYROIDISM (Thyrotoxicosis) Signs:

  • General appearance: Anxious, fidgety, restless, weight loss, sweating
  • Eyes: Exophthalmos (bulging eyes), lid retraction, stare
  • Skin: Warm, moist, flushed
  • Hair: Fine, thin hair
  • Body habitus: Thin, wasted (despite good appetite)
  • Behaviour: Hyperkinetic movements, tremor visible at rest

HYPOTHYROIDISM (Myxoedema) Signs:

  • General appearance: Lethargic, slow movements, cold intolerance
  • Face: Puffy, coarse features, periorbital oedema, loss of lateral eyebrows
  • Skin: Dry, pale, yellowish (carotenaemia), cool to touch
  • Hair: Coarse, brittle, thinning
  • Body habitus: Overweight, generalised non-pitting oedema
  • Voice: Deep, husky voice (listen when taking history)

B. INSPECTION OF THE NECK (60 seconds - standing in front of patient)

Say out loud: "Now I'm going to examine your neck. First, I'll just look at it carefully from the front."

Inspect the neck for:

  • Goitre (thyroid enlargement): Visible swelling in anterior neck
    • Symmetrical enlargement (diffuse goitre)
    • Asymmetrical enlargement (nodular goitre)
    • Single nodule (thyroid nodule, possible malignancy)
  • Scars:
    • Transverse collar scar (previous thyroidectomy)
    • Small scars (previous fine needle aspiration)
    • Vertical midline scar (previous thyroglossal cyst removal)
  • Skin changes: Erythema, dilated veins (if massive goitre), pretibial myxoedema scars
  • Asymmetry: One lobe larger than other
  • Position: Thyroid should be at level of thyroid cartilage (Adam's apple)
üî¥ CRITICAL TESTS - DO NOT FORGET:

TEST 1: SWALLOWING TEST (Diagnostic for thyroid mass)

Say out loud: "Now I'd like you to take a sip of water and hold it in your mouth. When I say 'swallow,' please swallow the water. This helps me see if any lump is attached to your thyroid gland."
  • Hand patient glass of water
  • Ask patient to take sip and hold in mouth
  • Watch neck carefully
  • Say "Swallow" and observe
  • Positive sign: Thyroid mass moves UPWARD with swallowing (attached to thyroid)
    • Thyroid is attached to trachea via pretracheal fascia
    • Trachea moves up with swallowing ‚Üí thyroid moves with it
  • Differential: Lymph nodes do NOT move with swallowing

TEST 2: TONGUE PROTRUSION TEST (for thyroglossal cyst)

Say out loud: "Now, please stick your tongue out as far as you can."
  • Ask patient to protrude tongue fully
  • Watch for midline neck mass movement
  • Positive sign: Midline neck mass moves UPWARD with tongue protrusion
    • Indicates thyroglossal cyst (connected to tongue via thyroglossal duct remnant)
    • Thyroid gland itself does NOT move with tongue protrusion

C. PALPATION (2-3 minutes - STAND BEHIND THE PATIENT)

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL TECHNIQUE: You MUST stand behind the patient for thyroid palpation. This is the standard technique taught in Talley & O'Connor and expected in Australian OSCE examinations. Standing in front is INCORRECT.
Say out loud: "Now I'm going to stand behind you and gently feel your thyroid gland. Please let me know if you feel any tenderness or discomfort."

STEP-BY-STEP PALPATION TECHNIQUE:

  1. Position yourself:
    • Stand directly behind seated patient
    • Patient's neck should be slightly flexed (relaxes strap muscles)
    • Ask patient to relax neck and shoulders
  2. Locate anatomical landmarks:
    • Thyroid cartilage (Adam's apple) - your index/middle fingers should rest here
    • Cricoid cartilage - immediately below thyroid cartilage
    • Thyroid isthmus - lies across trachea at level of 2nd-4th tracheal rings (just below cricoid)
    • Lateral lobes - lie lateral to trachea, deep to sternocleidomastoid muscles
  3. Palpate the isthmus:
    • Place thumbs on back of neck for support
    • Use pads of index and middle fingers of both hands
    • Place fingers on midline just below cricoid cartilage
    • Feel for thyroid isthmus crossing trachea
    • Ask patient to swallow - isthmus moves up with swallowing
  4. Palpate the lateral lobes:
    • Move fingers laterally from isthmus
    • Palpate each lobe separately:
      • Right lobe: Use left hand to displace trachea slightly to right, palpate with right hand
      • Left lobe: Use right hand to displace trachea slightly to left, palpate with left hand
    • Feel deep to sternocleidomastoid muscle
    • Assess from isthmus upward toward thyroid cartilage
    • Ask patient to swallow during palpation - lobes move upward
  5. Assess the following characteristics:
    • Size:
      • Normal: Not palpable or just barely palpable
      • Enlarged: Grade 1-4 goitre classification
      • Grade 1: Palpable but not visible
      • Grade 2: Palpable and visible with neck extended
      • Grade 3: Visible with neck in normal position
      • Grade 4: Massive goitre visible from distance
    • Symmetry: Both lobes equal or one larger?
    • Consistency:
      • Soft (normal, or diffuse hyperplasia)
      • Firm (nodules, chronic thyroiditis)
      • Hard (malignancy, Riedel's thyroiditis - rare)
    • Nodules:
      • Single or multiple?
      • Size, shape, consistency of each nodule
      • Smooth or irregular surface?
    • Tenderness: Suggests acute/subacute thyroiditis
    • Mobility:
      • Mobile with swallowing = attached to thyroid (normal or benign)
      • Fixed, immobile = concerning for malignant infiltration
    • Tracheal position: Central or deviated by large goitre?
üí° PALPATION TIPS:
  • Use finger PADS, not tips (more sensitive)
  • Gentle pressure - thyroid is superficial
  • Always palpate during swallowing - diagnostic feature
  • If you can't feel thyroid, ask patient to extend neck slightly
  • Warm your hands first - cold hands make patient tense neck muscles

PALPATE LYMPH NODES (Thyroid cancer screening):

Say out loud: "Now I'm going to feel the lymph nodes in your neck."

Systematically palpate cervical lymph node chains (still standing behind patient):

  • Submental: Under chin, midline
  • Submandibular: Under jaw line, bilateral
  • Jugular chain (deep cervical): Along sternocleidomastoid, anterior and posterior
  • Posterior cervical: Behind sternocleidomastoid
  • Supraclavicular: Above clavicles (Virchow's node)
  • Assess: Size, consistency, mobility, tenderness
    • Hard, fixed, non-tender = concerning for metastatic thyroid cancer
    • Soft, mobile, tender = reactive/infectious

D. PERCUSSION (30 seconds - stand in front of patient)

Say out loud: "I'm going to tap on your chest now to check if the thyroid extends behind your breastbone."

PERCUSSION FOR RETROSTERNAL EXTENSION:

  • Percuss from neck down over upper sternum
  • Normal: Resonant over chest
  • Retrosternal goitre: Dull percussion note over upper sternum (mass extends below)
  • If large goitre present, also check for:
    • Tracheal deviation (percuss to see if trachea deviated)
    • Superior mediastinal obstruction signs

PEMBERTON'S SIGN (for retrosternal goitre):

Say out loud: "I'd like you to raise both arms above your head and hold them there for one minute. Let me know if you feel any symptoms."
  • Ask patient to raise both arms straight up above head
  • Hold position for 60 seconds
  • Positive sign (retrosternal goitre causing thoracic inlet obstruction):
    • Facial plethora (redness, flushing)
    • Facial/neck vein distension
    • Inspiratory stridor
    • Dizziness, shortness of breath
  • Mechanism: Arms raised compress thoracic inlet, obstructing venous return if large goitre present

E. AUSCULTATION (30 seconds)

Say out loud: "Now I'm going to listen over your thyroid gland with my stethoscope."

AUSCULTATE FOR THYROID BRUIT:

  • Use diaphragm of stethoscope
  • Auscultate over each thyroid lobe
  • Ask patient to hold breath briefly (eliminates transmitted breath sounds)
  • Thyroid bruit: Soft, whooshing systolic sound
    • Indicates increased vascularity of thyroid
    • Heard in Graves' disease (autoimmune hyperthyroidism)
    • Also possible in toxic multinodular goitre
  • Differential: Carotid bruit (from carotid artery stenosis)
    • Carotid bruit heard lateral to thyroid, over carotid artery
    • Radiates to neck, not localised to thyroid

F. FUNCTIONAL ASSESSMENT (2-3 minutes - ESSENTIAL)

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: Thyroid examination is NOT complete without functional assessment. You must assess for signs of hyperthyroidism OR hypothyroidism. This is what separates good candidates from excellent candidates in OSCE.

ASSESS FOR HYPERTHYROIDISM:

1. HANDS:

Say out loud: "Please hold your hands out in front of you with your fingers spread apart."
  • Tremor: Fine tremor of outstretched hands
    • Place piece of paper on back of hands to amplify tremor
    • Hyperthyroid tremor: Fine, rapid (8-12 Hz)
  • Palmar erythema: Warm, moist, sweaty palms (increased metabolism)
  • Onycholysis: Separation of nail from nail bed (thyroid acropachy - rare)
  • Thyroid acropachy: Clubbing + periosteal new bone formation (very rare, Graves')

2. PULSE:

Say out loud: "I'm going to check your pulse now."
  • Palpate radial pulse for full 15 seconds (x4 = rate per minute)
  • Hyperthyroidism:
    • Tachycardia (HR >90-100 bpm at rest)
    • Irregular pulse (atrial fibrillation - AF common in hyperthyroidism, especially elderly)
    • Bounding pulse (increased cardiac output)

3. EYES (CRITICAL FOR GRAVES' DISEASE):

‚ö†Ô∏è MUST DO: Eye examination is MANDATORY if any suspicion of hyperthyroidism. Graves' ophthalmopathy is a sight-threatening complication. Examiners expect you to check eyes systematically.
Say out loud: "I'd like to examine your eyes now to check for signs related to thyroid disease."

Systematic Eye Examination (Use Mnemonic: RELIEF):

  • R - Retraction (Lid Retraction):
    • Look at patient from front, eye level
    • Upper eyelid should normally just touch upper border of iris
    • Lid retraction: Sclera visible above iris (white showing above)
    • Causes "staring" appearance
    • Due to sympathetic overactivity (increased M√ºller's muscle tone)
  • E - Exophthalmos (Proptosis):
    • Stand behind seated patient, look down from above at eyes
    • Normal: Cannot see cornea protruding beyond malar eminence
    • Exophthalmos: Eyeballs bulge forward, visible from above
    • Due to retro-orbital inflammation and oedema (Graves' ophthalmopathy)
    • Can measure with exophthalmometer (if available) - normal <20mm
  • L - Lid Lag (von Graefe's Sign):
    • Ask patient to follow your finger with eyes only (keep head still)
    • Move finger slowly from up to down (superior to inferior)
    • Watch upper eyelid movement
    • Positive lid lag: Upper lid lags behind downward eye movement, sclera visible above iris
    • Due to sympathetic overactivity
  • I - Impaired eye movements (Ophthalmoplegia):
    • Ask patient to follow your finger in H-pattern (test all directions)
    • Ask "Any double vision?"
    • Graves' ophthalmopathy: Extraocular muscle inflammation
      • Inferior rectus most commonly affected (difficulty looking up)
      • Medial rectus second most common (difficulty looking laterally)
    • Diplopia (double vision) in affected direction
  • E - Erythema (Periorbital Oedema and Conjunctival Injection):
    • Look for puffy, swollen eyelids (periorbital oedema)
    • Look for red, inflamed conjunctiva (chemosis)
    • Due to inflammation in Graves' ophthalmopathy
  • F - Failure of convergence:
    • Ask patient to look at your finger as you bring it toward their nose
    • Normal: Both eyes converge (turn inward)
    • Failure of convergence: Eyes cannot maintain convergence, one drifts outward
    • Less common sign
üí° GRAVES' OPHTHALMOPATHY:
  • Present in 25-50% of Graves' disease patients
  • Can occur before, during, or after hyperthyroidism develops
  • More severe in smokers
  • Sight-threatening if severe (corneal exposure, optic nerve compression)
  • Requires urgent ophthalmology referral if vision affected

4. PRETIBIAL MYXOEDEMA (Graves' Disease):

Say out loud: "I'd like to check your shins for a skin condition that can occur with thyroid disease."
  • Examine lower legs, specifically anterior shin area
  • Pretibial myxoedema: Raised, thickened, non-pitting oedema of skin
    • Orange-peel appearance (peau d'orange)
    • Violaceous (purple-red) discolouration
    • Usually bilateral over shins
    • Rare - only 1-2% of Graves' patients
    • Associated with severe Graves' ophthalmopathy and thyroid acropachy

5. REFLEXES (Deep Tendon Reflexes):

Say out loud: "I'm going to test your reflexes now. This helps assess your thyroid function."
  • Test ankle reflex (Achilles tendon) - most sensitive for thyroid function
  • Ask patient to kneel on chair with feet hanging free, OR lie down with foot dorsiflexed
  • Strike Achilles tendon with tendon hammer
  • Hyperthyroidism: BRISK reflexes (hyperreflexia)
    • Fast contraction and relaxation
    • Exaggerated response
  • Hypothyroidism: SLOW-RELAXING reflexes (hung-up reflexes)
    • Normal contraction but DELAYED relaxation phase
    • "Hung-up" ankle jerk - pathognomonic for hypothyroidism

ASSESS FOR HYPOTHYROIDISM:

  • General: Slow, lethargic movements, cold peripheries
  • Skin: Dry, cold, pale/yellowish, non-pitting oedema (myxoedema)
  • Hair: Coarse, brittle, loss of lateral third of eyebrows (Queen Anne's sign)
  • Face: Puffy face, periorbital oedema, coarse features, macroglossia (large tongue)
  • Voice: Deep, hoarse voice (listen when patient speaks)
  • Pulse: Bradycardia (HR <60 bpm)
  • Reflexes: Slow-relaxing (delayed relaxation phase)
  • Mental state: Slow thinking, poor memory, depression
‚úÖ TO COMPLETE THE EXAMINATION:

Say out loud: "To complete my examination of the thyroid, I would like to:"
  • "Measure vital signs including blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate, and temperature"
  • "Perform cardiovascular examination, particularly auscultation for atrial fibrillation and heart failure"
  • "Examine the lower legs for pretibial myxoedema and peripheral oedema"
  • "Request thyroid function tests: TSH, free T4, free T3"
  • "Request thyroid antibodies: anti-TPO, anti-thyroglobulin, TSH receptor antibodies if Graves' suspected"
  • "Arrange thyroid ultrasound if nodule or goitre detected"
  • "Arrange fine needle aspiration (FNA) if suspicious thyroid nodule"
  • "Refer to ophthalmology if significant Graves' ophthalmopathy detected"

5. PRESENT FINDINGS (60-90 seconds)

üí° PRESENTATION STRUCTURE: Follow GIST format
  • General inspection findings
  • Inspection of neck findings
  • Specific examination findings (palpation, percussion, auscultation)
  • Thyroid function assessment
EXAMPLE PRESENTATION 1: GRAVES' DISEASE (HYPERTHYROIDISM)

"This is Mrs Thompson, a 35-year-old woman who appears anxious and mildly distressed. On general inspection, she appears thin with a flushed complexion and visible tremor at rest.

On examination of the thyroid gland, there is a symmetrical, smooth, diffuse enlargement of the thyroid, approximately Grade 2 goitre. The goitre moves upward with swallowing and is non-tender. There is no palpable tracheal deviation, and no cervical lymphadenopathy is detected. On auscultation, a thyroid bruit is audible bilaterally, suggesting increased vascularity.

Functional assessment reveals multiple signs of hyperthyroidism:
- Fine tremor of outstretched hands with warm, moist palms
- Tachycardia with heart rate of 110 beats per minute, regular rhythm
- Brisk reflexes bilaterally

Eye examination reveals features of Graves' ophthalmopathy:
- Bilateral lid retraction with visible sclera above iris
- Mild bilateral exophthalmos visible from above
- Positive lid lag on downward gaze
- No ophthalmoplegia or diplopia
- No periorbital oedema or conjunctival injection

There is no pretibial myxoedema.

In summary, this patient has clinical features consistent with Graves' disease with thyrotoxicosis and mild Graves' ophthalmopathy. I would request thyroid function tests (TSH, free T4, free T3), TSH receptor antibodies, and arrange urgent endocrinology review. I would also refer to ophthalmology for assessment of the eye disease and discuss anti-thyroid medication or radioiodine therapy with the endocrine team."
EXAMPLE PRESENTATION 2: HYPOTHYROIDISM

"This is Mr Chen, a 58-year-old man who appears lethargic with slow movements and speech. On general inspection, he has a puffy face with periorbital oedema, coarse facial features, thinning hair, and loss of the lateral third of both eyebrows. His skin appears dry and pale.

On examination of the thyroid gland, there is a firm, symmetrical, diffuse enlargement consistent with Grade 2 goitre. The gland moves normally with swallowing, is non-tender, and there is no tracheal deviation. No cervical lymphadenopathy is palpable. No thyroid bruit is audible.

Functional assessment reveals signs of hypothyroidism:
- Bradycardia with heart rate of 58 beats per minute
- Slow-relaxing ankle jerks bilaterally with prolonged relaxation phase
- Cool, dry skin with non-pitting oedema of hands
- Deep, husky voice noted during history taking

No eye signs of Graves' disease are present. No pretibial myxoedema.

In summary, this patient has clinical features consistent with primary hypothyroidism, likely Hashimoto's thyroiditis given the goitre. I would request thyroid function tests (TSH will be elevated, free T4 low), anti-TPO antibodies, and lipid profile. I would discuss commencing levothyroxine replacement therapy with the patient and arrange follow-up in 6-8 weeks to recheck thyroid function."
EXAMPLE PRESENTATION 3: THYROID NODULE

"This is Ms Patel, a 42-year-old woman who appears clinically euthyroid with no obvious signs of thyroid dysfunction.

On examination of the neck, there is a visible 3cm solitary nodule in the right lobe of the thyroid. The nodule is firm, smooth, non-tender, and moves upward with swallowing. The left lobe appears normal in size. There is no tracheal deviation. Cervical lymph node examination reveals a 1.5cm firm, non-tender lymph node in the right jugular chain.

There is no thyroid bruit audible. No retrosternal extension on percussion.

Functional assessment shows no signs of hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism:
- Normal pulse rate at 72 bpm, regular
- No tremor
- Normal reflexes
- No eye signs

In summary, this patient has a solitary thyroid nodule with associated cervical lymphadenopathy, which raises concern for possible thyroid malignancy. The firm consistency and lymph node involvement are concerning features. I would urgently request:
- Thyroid function tests (likely normal)
- Thyroid ultrasound to characterise the nodule
- Fine needle aspiration (FNA) biopsy of both the nodule and lymph node
- Urgent referral to endocrine surgery for assessment

Red flags for malignancy include: solitary nodule, firm consistency, associated lymphadenopathy, and the patient's age group (thyroid cancer more common in 30-50 years)."

COMMON IMG MISTAKES ‚ùå

‚ö†Ô∏è AVOID THESE CRITICAL ERRORS IN OSCE:
  • ‚ùå MISTAKE 1: Examining from the front only
    • Why it's wrong: Standard technique requires standing BEHIND patient for thyroid palpation (Talley & O'Connor method, Australian standard)
    • ‚úÖ Correct approach: Always move behind patient for palpation. Stand in front for inspection only
    • Impact: Immediate mark deduction - examiner expects proper technique
  • ‚ùå MISTAKE 2: Not testing swallowing movement
    • Why it's wrong: Swallowing test is THE diagnostic manoeuvre to confirm thyroid origin of neck lump
    • ‚úÖ Correct approach: ALWAYS give patient water and ask to swallow during both inspection AND palpation. Say "This helps confirm the lump is from the thyroid"
    • Impact: Fails to distinguish thyroid mass from lymph node or other neck mass - critical diagnostic error
  • ‚ùå MISTAKE 3: Skipping the eye examination
    • Why it's wrong: Graves' ophthalmopathy is sight-threatening and MUST be assessed in any hyperthyroid patient
    • ‚úÖ Correct approach: Systematically examine eyes using RELIEF mnemonic if ANY signs of hyperthyroidism. Say out loud "I need to check your eyes for thyroid-related changes"
    • Impact: Misses sight-threatening complication, fails to complete examination, loses significant marks
  • ‚ùå MISTAKE 4: Not assessing thyroid function clinically
    • Why it's wrong: Thyroid exam is NOT just palpating the gland - you must assess functional status (hyper/hypo/euthyroid)
    • ‚úÖ Correct approach: ALWAYS check: pulse rate, tremor, reflexes (especially ankle jerk for slow relaxation). State "I need to assess thyroid function by examining..."
    • Impact: Incomplete examination, fails to demonstrate clinical reasoning, loses major marks
  • ‚ùå MISTAKE 5: Forgetting lymph node examination
    • Why it's wrong: Cervical lymphadenopathy is key sign of thyroid cancer metastasis
    • ‚úÖ Correct approach: Systematically palpate all cervical lymph node chains while standing behind patient. State "I'm checking for lymph node enlargement which could indicate cancer"
    • Impact: Misses thyroid cancer diagnosis, major patient safety concern
  • ‚ùå MISTAKE 6: Not testing for retrosternal extension
    • Why it's wrong: Retrosternal goitre can cause superior vena cava obstruction and airway compression
    • ‚úÖ Correct approach: Percuss over upper sternum for dullness. Perform Pemberton's sign (raise arms above head for 1 minute). State "I need to check if the goitre extends behind the breastbone"
    • Impact: Misses potentially life-threatening complication, incomplete examination
  • ‚ùå MISTAKE 7: Incorrect presentation sequence
    • Why it's wrong: Examiners expect structured presentation following examination sequence
    • ‚úÖ Correct approach: Present as GIST - General inspection ‚Üí Inspection of neck ‚Üí Specific examination ‚Üí Thyroid function. Always end with "In summary..." and differential diagnosis
    • Impact: Appears disorganized, examiner questions your clinical reasoning
  • ‚ùå MISTAKE 8: Using wrong terminology
    • Why it's wrong: Australian examiners expect Australian terminology and spelling
    • ‚úÖ Correct approach: Say "goitre" (not goiter), "oedema" (not edema), "refer to endocrinology" (not endocrine), "GP" (not PCP), "thyroid function tests" (not thyroid panel)
    • Impact: Marks you as unfamiliar with Australian medical practice
üí° IMG-SPECIFIC TIPS:
  • Practice the "from behind" palpation technique extensively - feels unnatural initially but is standard Australian method
  • Always carry water for the patient in OSCE - don't expect examiner to provide it
  • Don't rush functional assessment - it's what separates competent from excellent candidates
  • If you're not confident with eye examination, PRACTICE IT. Graves' ophthalmopathy questions are common in Australian OSCEs
  • Learn to identify "red flags" for thyroid cancer: hard consistency, fixed mass, lymphadenopathy, rapid growth, hoarse voice

EXAMINATION MNEMONICS

üéØ MNEMONIC 1: THYROID Examination Sequence

T - Talk to patient (Introduction, consent, pain check)
H - Hands (for tremor, then position behind patient)
Y - look at paYtient's neck (Inspection - goitre, scars)
R - Request swallow (Swallowing test with water)
O - tOngue protrusion (Tongue test for thyroglossal cyst)
I - palpItion (Palpate from behind - size, consistency, nodules, lymph nodes)
D - Discern function (Percussion, Auscultation, Functional assessment - pulse, reflexes, eyes)

üéØ MNEMONIC 2: RELIEF (Graves' Eye Signs)

R - Retraction (Lid retraction - sclera visible above iris)
E - Exophthalmos (Proptosis - eyeball bulges forward)
L - Lid lag (von Graefe's sign - lid lags behind on downward gaze)
I - Impaired eye movements (Ophthalmoplegia - restricted eye movements, diplopia)
E - Erythema (Periorbital oedema, conjunctival injection/chemosis)
F - Failure of convergence (Eyes cannot maintain convergence)

üéØ MNEMONIC 3: GOITRE (Causes of Thyroid Enlargement)

G - Graves' disease (Autoimmune hyperthyroidism)
O - nOdules (Multinodular goitre, toxic adenoma)
I - Iodine deficiency (Simple colloid goitre - rare in Australia)
T - Thyroiditis (Hashimoto's, De Quervain's, Riedel's)
R - Red flags for malignancy (Thyroid cancer - papillary, follicular, medullary, anaplastic)
E - Endemic goitre (Geographical areas with iodine deficiency - rare in Australia due to iodized salt)

üéØ MNEMONIC 4: CANCER (Red Flags for Thyroid Malignancy)

C - Consistency HARD (Rock-hard nodule)
A - Adhered/fixed (Fixed to surrounding structures, immobile)
N - Nodes (Cervical lymphadenopathy)
C - Changed voice (Hoarse voice - recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement)
E - Extremes of age (<20 years or >60 years higher risk)
R - Rapid growth (Quick enlargement over weeks to months)

üéØ MNEMONIC 5: HYPER (Signs of Hyperthyroidism)

H - Hot, sweaty, heat intolerance
Y - Youthful hyperactivity (Anxious, restless, hyperkinetic)
P - Pounding heart (Tachycardia, palpitations, AF)
E - Eyes (Graves' ophthalmopathy - RELIEF signs)
R - Rapid reflexes (Brisk, hyperactive reflexes) + Tremor

üéØ MNEMONIC 6: SLOW (Signs of Hypothyroidism)

S - Slow everything (Bradycardia, slow reflexes, slow thinking, slow movements)
L - Lethargic, cold intolerance
O - Oedema (Periorbital oedema, non-pitting myxoedema)
W - Weight gain, coarse skin, hoarse voice, loss of lateral eyebrows
