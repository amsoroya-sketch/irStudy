Headache: Differential Diagnosis and Management

ICRP OSCE Preparation - Medicine Module (Neurology)

Target Audience

This guide is for International Medical Graduates (IMGs) preparing for the AMC Clinical OOSE, specifically:

  • IMGs requiring systematic differential-driven approach to headache presentations
  • Candidates needing recognition of life-threatening headache causes
  • Doctors preparing for ICRP placement in Australian ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY Departments
  • Medical professionals needing frameworks for primary vs secondary headache differentiation
  • Clinicians requiring Australian investigation and management protocols (eTG 2024)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  1. Differentiate primary headaches (tension, migraine, cluster) from secondary headaches (life-threatening causes)
  2. Recognize üö® RED FLAGs requiring URGENT investigation (SAH, meningitis, temporal arteritis, raised ICP)
  3. Generate differential diagnoses based on headache characteristics and associated features
  4. Investigate headache appropriately (when to image, LP timing, URGENT bloods)
  5. Manage primary headaches according to Australian guidelines (eTG 2024, PBS)
  6. Identify emergencies requiring immediate intervention
  7. Avoid common IMG mistakes in headache assessment
  8. Perform confidently in 8-minute OSCE stations for headache presentations

Dr. Amir Soufi's Approach Applied to Headache

Core Principle: üö® RED FLAGS FIRST ‚Üí Primary vs Secondary ‚Üí CHARACTER-DRIVEN DIFFERENTIAL

Traditional Approach (Less Effective):
  • Complete generic headache history
  • Consider all causes equally
  • Delayed recognition of emergencies

Dr. Amir Soufi's Differential-Driven Approach:
  1. üö® RED FLAGS IMMEDIATELY (Life-Threatening Causes - Rule Out FIRST):

- "WORST HEADACHE OF LIFE" (thunderclap) ‚Üí SAH until proven otherwise - Sudden onset (<1 minute to peak) ‚Üí SAH, vascular (dissection, reversible vasoconstriction syndrome) - Fever + headache + neck stiffness ‚Üí Meningitis/encephalitis - Age >50 + new headache + jaw claudication ‚Üí Temporal arteritis (GCA) - Papilledema, confusion, focal neurology ‚Üí Raised ICP (tumor, abscess, venous sinus thrombosis) - Headache worsening over weeks ‚Üí Space-occupying lesion, chronic subdural

  1. PRIMARY vs SECONDARY HEADACHE:

- Primary: Migraine, tension-type, cluster (no underlying pathology) - Secondary: Underlying cause (infection, vascular, trauma, tumor, medication) - üö® RED FLAGs ‚Üí Always think SECONDARY first

  1. CHARACTER-DRIVEN DIFFERENTIAL (if no üö® RED FLAGs):

- Unilateral, pulsating, 4-72 hours, photophobia/phonophobia, aura ‚Üí Migraine - Bilateral, pressing/tightening, 30min-7 days, mild-moderate ‚Üí Tension-type headache - Unilateral orbital/supraorbital, 15-180 min, autonomic features ‚Üí Cluster headache

  1. ASSOCIATED FEATURES REFINE:

- Visual aura ‚Üí Migraine with aura - Lacrimation, rhinorrhea ‚Üí Cluster, trigeminal autonomic cephalalgias - Fever ‚Üí Meningitis, encephalitis, sinusitis - Focal neurology ‚Üí Stroke, tumor, SAH

  1. TEMPORAL PATTERN:

- Sudden onset (<1 min) ‚Üí Vascular (SAH, dissection) - Subacute worsening (days-weeks) ‚Üí Tumor, abscess, chronic subdural, temporal arteritis - Recurrent episodic (years) ‚Üí Migraine, cluster - Chronic daily ‚Üí Medication overuse headache, chronic tension-type

  1. TARGETED INVESTIGATION:

- üö® RED FLAGs ‚Üí CT brain URGENTLY (SAH, mass, bleed) - Suspected SAH with normal CT ‚Üí LP (xanthochromia) - Suspected temporal arteritis ‚Üí ESR/CRP + temporal artery biopsy - Fever + meningism ‚Üí Blood cultures + LP (after CT if concern for raised ICP)

Example:
  • Presentation: 68-year-old woman, new-onset headache for 3 weeks (gradual worsening), jaw pain when chewing, scalp tenderness, visual disturbance
  • Immediate thought: üö® RED FLAG - Age >50, new headache, jaw claudication, visual symptoms ‚Üí TEMPORAL ARTERITIS (GCA) until proven otherwise
  • Immediate actions: URGENT ESR + CRP (markedly elevated), ophthalmology review (risk of irreversible blindness), start high-dose steroids IMMEDIATELY (prednisolone 60mg), arrange temporal artery biopsy (within 1-2 weeks, can do after starting steroids)
  • Not migraine/tension - üö® RED FLAGs present

1. üö® RED FLAGS IN HEADACHE ("SNOOP4" Mnemonic)

üö® RED FLAGs indicate SECONDARY headache (underlying pathology) - require URGENT investigation

SNOOP4 Mnemonic

S - Systemic symptoms/signs:
  • Fever ‚Üí Meningitis, encephalitis, abscess, sinusitis
  • Weight loss ‚Üí Malignancy, temporal arteritis
  • Cancer history ‚Üí Brain metastases
  • HIV/immunosuppression ‚Üí Opportunistic infections (toxoplasmosis, cryptococcal meningitis)

N - Neurological symptoms/signs:
  • Focal neurology: Weakness, numbness, visual field defect, diplopia, ataxia ‚Üí Stroke, tumor, SAH
  • Confusion, altered consciousness ‚Üí Encephalitis, meningitis, raised ICP
  • Seizures ‚Üí Tumor, encephalitis, SAH
  • Papilledema (swollen optic disc) ‚Üí Raised ICP

O - Onset sudden (Thunderclap headache):
  • <1 minute to peak intensity ‚Üí SAH, vascular (dissection, RCVS, pituitary apoplexy)
  • "Worst headache of my life" ‚Üí SAH until proven otherwise

O - Onset after age 50:
  • New headache in patient >50 years ‚Üí Higher risk of secondary causes (temporal arteritis, tumor, subdural)

P - Pattern change:
  • Progressive worsening over days-weeks ‚Üí Tumor, abscess, chronic subdural, temporal arteritis
  • Change in established headache pattern ‚Üí Investigate

P - Positional:
  • Worse lying flat, better upright ‚Üí Raised ICP (tumor, idiopathic intracranial hypertension)
  • Worse upright, better lying flat ‚Üí Low CSF pressure (post-LP headache, CSF leak)

P - Precipitated by Valsalva (cough, sneeze, strain):
  • Cough headache ‚Üí Posterior fossa lesion, Chiari malformation

P - Papilledema, Pulsatile tinnitus:
  • Papilledema ‚Üí Raised ICP
  • Pulsatile tinnitus ‚Üí Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (young obese women), venous sinus thrombosis

Specific üö® RED FLAG Patterns (Recognize IMMEDIATELY)

1. Thunderclap Headache (Sudden Onset <1 Minute to Peak)

"WORST HEADACHE OF LIFE" ‚Üí SAH UNTIL PROVEN OTHERWISE Causes:
  1. Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH) (25% of thunderclap headaches):

- Classic: Sudden severe occipital headache ("explosion in head", "hit by bat"), neck stiffness, photophobia, nausea/vomiting, LOC (50%) - Investigations: URGENT non-contrast CT brain (sens 95% if done <6 hours, decreases with time) ‚Üí If CT negative: LP 12 hours after onset (xanthochromia - yellow CSF from bilirubin breakdown) - Management: Neurosurgery URGENT (clipping or coiling of aneurysm), nimodipine (prevent vasospasm), BP control

  1. Arterial Dissection (carotid, vertebral):

- Headache/neck pain + Horner's syndrome (carotid) or posterior circulation stroke (vertebral) - Investigations: CT/MR angiography - Management: Anticoagulation, neurology

  1. Reversible Cerebral Vasoconstriction Syndrome (RCVS):

- Thunderclap headache (recurrent), triggers (exertion, Valsalva, sexual activity), associated with postpartum, drugs (SSRIs, triptans, cannabis) - Investigations: CT angiography (multifocal stenosis - "string of beads"), normal LP - Management: Nimodipine, avoid triggers

  1. Pituitary Apoplexy:

- Thunderclap headache + visual field defect (bitemporal hemianopia) + ophthalmoplegia + hypopituitarism - Investigations: CT/MRI brain (hemorrhage into pituitary) - Management: Endocrinology, neurosurgery, steroids

  1. Cerebral Venous Sinus Thrombosis:

- Thunderclap or progressive headache, seizures, focal neurology, risk factors (OCP, pregnancy, thrombophilia) - Investigations: CT/MR venography - Management: Anticoagulation (even if hemorrhagic infarction)

2. Headache + Fever + Neck Stiffness

MENINGITIS/ENCEPHALITIS UNTIL PROVEN OTHERWISE Bacterial Meningitis:
  • Classic triad (only 44% have all three): Fever, headache, neck stiffness
  • Additional: Photophobia, altered consciousness, seizures, petechial rash (meningococcal)
  • Investigations:

- Blood cultures (before antibiotics) - CT brain (if concern for raised ICP: papilledema, focal neurology, GCS <12, immunosuppressed) BEFORE LP - LP: Opening pressure, CSF analysis (WCC, protein, glucose, Gram stain, culture) - Bacterial: Elevated WCC (neutrophils >1000), elevated protein, low glucose (<50% of serum)

  • Management:

- Do NOT delay antibiotics waiting for LP or CT - Empirical antibiotics: Ceftriaxone 2g IV 12-hourly (eTG 2024) + dexamethasone 10mg IV QID (before/with first antibiotic dose - improves outcomes in pneumococcal) - If >50 years or immunosuppressed: Add ampicillin (for Listeria coverage)

Viral Meningitis:
  • Less severe, no altered consciousness typically
  • CSF: Elevated WCC (lymphocytes <1000), normal glucose, mildly elevated protein
  • Management: Supportive (usually self-limiting)

Encephalitis:
  • Headache, fever, altered consciousness, seizures, focal neurology
  • HSV encephalitis: Temporal lobe involvement, personality change
  • Investigations: LP (lymphocytic pleocytosis, elevated protein, RBCs if HSV), MRI (temporal lobe changes), EEG
  • Management: Aciclovir 10mg/kg IV TDS (empirical if suspected - do NOT delay)

3. Age >50 + New Headache + Jaw Claudication

TEMPORAL ARTERITIS (GIANT CELL ARTERITIS - GCA) UNTIL PROVEN OTHERWISE Epidemiology (CRITICAL FOR DIAGNOSIS):
  • Age >50 years (RARE under age 50 - virtually never <40)
  • Most common in age >70 years (peak incidence 70-80 years)
  • If patient <50 years old with suspected GCA symptoms ‚Üí consider alternative diagnosis
Presentation:
  • New-onset persistent headache (temporal, throbbing, different from previous headaches)
  • Jaw claudication (pain when chewing - HIGHLY SPECIFIC for GCA, 95% specific)
  • Scalp tenderness (combing hair painful, can't rest head on pillow)
  • Visual symptoms (transient monocular visual loss - amaurosis fugax, diplopia) - üö® WARNING SIGN OF IMPENDING PERMANENT BLINDNESS
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica symptoms (shoulder/hip girdle pain and stiffness, worse in morning) - associated in 50% of cases
  • Temporal artery: Tender, thickened, reduced/absent pulsation, nodular
  • Systemic symptoms: Fever, weight loss, malaise, night sweats

Investigations:
  • ESR + CRP: Markedly elevated (ESR typically >50, often >100)
  • FBC: Anemia of chronic disease, elevated platelets
  • Temporal artery biopsy: GOLD STANDARD (skip lesions - need >1cm segment), can do up to 2 weeks after starting steroids

Management (üö® TIME-CRITICAL TO PREVENT BLINDNESS):

IF HIGH CLINICAL SUSPICION (Age >50 + New Headache + ANY: Jaw Claudication, Visual Symptoms, Temporal Artery Tenderness, Elevated ESR/CRP):

üö® START HIGH-DOSE PREDNISOLONE 40-60mg DAILY IMMEDIATELY (SAME DAY, BEFORE TEMPORAL ARTERY BIOPSY)

RATIONALE: Prevents irreversible blindness from anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (AION). Once vision lost = permanent. Treatment within hours to days of visual symptoms can prevent blindness.

CRITICAL FACT: Starting steroids DOES NOT affect temporal artery biopsy histology for first 7-14 days. DO NOT delay treatment awaiting biopsy.

Treatment Protocol:
  • Step 1 (SAME DAY as clinical suspicion):
    • If visual symptoms present (amaurosis fugax, diplopia, vision loss): Methylprednisolone 500mg-1g IV daily x 3 days, then prednisolone 60mg PO daily
    • If NO visual symptoms but high suspicion: Prednisolone 40-60mg PO daily (start SAME DAY)
    • Send URGENT ESR + CRP before starting steroids (if possible - but don't delay treatment)
  • Step 2 (Same day): URGENT ophthalmology review (assess visual acuity, fundoscopy for AION, formal visual fields)
  • Step 3 (Within 7 days): Arrange temporal artery biopsy (can be done up to 7-14 days after starting steroids without affecting histology)
  • Step 4 (Long-term): Slow steroid taper over 12-24 months, monitor ESR/CRP for disease activity, bone protection (calcium 1.5g daily, vitamin D 800 IU daily, bisphosphonate)

üö® CRITICAL WARNINGS:
  • DO NOT wait for temporal artery biopsy results before starting treatment - blindness can occur while waiting
  • DO NOT wait for ophthalmology review before starting steroids - start steroids, then arrange review
  • Anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (AION) = IRREVERSIBLE - once optic nerve infarcted, vision permanently lost
  • Transient visual symptoms (amaurosis fugax) = WARNING that permanent vision loss imminent (hours to days)

4. Headache + Papilledema / Focal Neurology / Confusion

RAISED INTRACRANIAL PRESSURE ‚Üí URGENT CT BRAIN Causes:
  1. Brain tumor (primary or metastases)
  2. Brain abscess
  3. Chronic subdural hematoma (elderly, trauma weeks-months prior, anticoagulation)
  4. Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (young obese women, papilledema, pulsatile tinnitus, visual obscurations)
  5. Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis
  6. Hydrocephalus

Presentation:
  • Headache: Progressive (worse over days-weeks), worse in morning/lying flat, worse with Valsalva
  • Papilledema: Swollen optic disc (fundoscopy)
  • Focal neurology: Weakness, sensory loss, visual field defect
  • Confusion, altered consciousness, seizures
  • "Cushing's triad" (if severe raised ICP): Hypertension, bradycardia, irregular breathing

Investigation: URGENT CT brain (non-contrast initially) Management: Depends on cause (neurosurgery, oncology, antibiotics for abscess)

2. PRIMARY HEADACHES

Primary headaches: No underlying structural/systemic cause (benign but can be disabling) Three main types:
  1. Tension-type headache (most common)
  2. Migraine (second most common)
  3. Cluster headache (less common, most severe)

Tension-Type Headache (TTH)

Most common primary headache (>70% of population at some point)

Diagnostic Criteria (IHS - International Headache Society)

At least 10 episodes with: A. Headache duration: 30 minutes to 7 days B. At least 2 of:
  1. Bilateral location
  2. Pressing/tightening quality (non-pulsating) - "band around head"
  3. Mild-moderate intensity (doesn't prohibit activities)
  4. NOT aggravated by routine physical activity (walking, climbing stairs)

C. Both of:
  1. NO nausea or vomiting (may have anorexia)
  2. NO more than one of photophobia or phonophobia

**D. Not better accounted for by another diagnosis

Subtypes

  • Episodic TTH: <15 days/month
  • Chronic TTH: ‚â•15 days/month for >3 months

Management (eTG 2024)

Acute treatment (episodic):
  • Simple analgesia:

- Paracetamol 1g PO (can repeat 4-6 hourly, max 4g/day) - NSAIDs: Ibuprofen 400mg PO, aspirin 900mg

  • Avoid combination analgesics (codeine, caffeine) - risk of medication overuse headache
  • Limit to <15 days per month (risk of transformation to chronic TTH)

Preventive treatment (if frequent, disabling):
  • Indications: >2 headaches per week, significantly affecting quality of life
  • First-line: Amitriptyline 10-25mg nocte, titrate to 50-75mg (tricyclic antidepressant)
  • Alternatives: Mirtazapine, venlafaxine
  • Non-pharmacological: Relaxation, stress management, CBT, physiotherapy (neck muscle tension), acupuncture

Chronic TTH:
  • Preventive medication as above
  • Address medication overuse if present (stop all analgesia - withdrawal headache for 2-4 weeks, then improve)
  • Multidisciplinary approach (psychology, physiotherapy)

Migraine

Second most common primary headache, affects 15% of population (F:M = 3:1)

Diagnostic Criteria (IHS)

Migraine WITHOUT Aura (75%): At least 5 attacks with: A. Headache duration: 4-72 hours (untreated) B. At least 2 of:
  1. Unilateral location (can be bilateral, especially children)
  2. Pulsating quality (throbbing)
  3. Moderate-severe intensity (prohibits/inhibits activities)
  4. Aggravated by routine physical activity (or causes avoidance)

C. During headache, at least 1 of:
  1. Nausea and/or vomiting
  2. Photophobia AND phonophobia (both required)

**D. Not better accounted for by another diagnosis

Migraine WITH Aura (25%): At least 2 attacks with reversible aura symptoms:
  • Visual aura (most common): Scintillating scotoma (zigzag lines, fortification spectra), blind spots, flashing lights

- Develops over ‚â•5 minutes, lasts 5-60 minutes

  • Sensory aura: Unilateral tingling/numbness (marching from hand up arm to face)
  • Speech/language aura: Dysphasia
  • Motor aura (rare - "hemiplegic migraine"): Unilateral weakness

Aura characteristics:
  • At least one aura symptom spreads gradually over ‚â•5 minutes
  • Each symptom lasts 5-60 minutes
  • Fully reversible
  • Headache follows aura within 60 minutes (can overlap or occur without headache)

CRITICAL: First presentation of aura >40 years or aura with motor weakness ‚Üí Investigate (CT/MRI) to exclude stroke/TIA

Migraine Triggers (Common)

  • Hormonal: Menstruation (decline in estrogen), OCP, pregnancy (often improve), menopause
  • Dietary: Alcohol (red wine), aged cheese, chocolate, caffeine withdrawal, MSG
  • Environmental: Bright lights, strong smells, weather changes
  • Lifestyle: Stress, sleep disturbance (too little or too much), missed meals, dehydration
  • Medications: Nitrates, phosphodiesterase inhibitors

Management (eTG 2024, PBS)

Acute Treatment (Abortive): Mild-moderate migraine:
  • Simple analgesia + antiemetic:

- Aspirin 900mg or Ibuprofen 400-600mg or Naproxen 500-1000mg - + Metoclopramide 10mg PO (prokinetic - improves gastric emptying and analgesic absorption, antiemetic)

  • Take early in attack (within 1 hour of onset - more effective)

Moderate-severe migraine:
  • Triptans (5-HT1B/1D agonists - vasoconstriction, block pain pathways):

- First-line: Sumatriptan 50-100mg PO (PBS listed) - can repeat once after 2 hours if partial response - Alternatives: Rizatriptan 10mg, eletriptan 40mg, zolmitriptan 2.5mg - Nasal: Sumatriptan nasal spray 20mg (if vomiting) - Subcutaneous: Sumatriptan 6mg SC (fastest onset - 10-15 min, for severe/refractory)

  • Contraindications to triptans: Ischemic heart disease, stroke, uncontrolled hypertension, hemiplegic migraine, basilar migraine, pregnancy
  • Side effects: Chest tightness (usually benign), flushing, tingling, dizziness
  • Can combine triptan + NSAID (additive effect)

Status migrainosus (migraine >72 hours):
  • IV fluids, antiemetics (metoclopramide 10mg IV, ondansetron 8mg IV)
  • IV aspirin 1g or IV prochlorperazine 10mg
  • Consider IV magnesium sulfate 1-2g over 15 min (some evidence)

Medication overuse headache (MOH):
  • Using acute migraine medications >10 days/month (triptans) or >15 days/month (simple analgesics)
  • Results in chronic daily headache
  • Management: Withdraw all acute medications for 2 months (withdrawal headache for 2-4 weeks, then improve), start preventive

Preventive Treatment: Indications:
  • ‚â•4 migraine days per month affecting quality of life
  • Failure/contraindication to acute treatment
  • Medication overuse headache
  • Patient preference

Principles:
  • Trial medication for 2-3 months at therapeutic dose before declaring failure
  • Goal: Reduce frequency by 50%

First-line options (PBS listed for migraine prophylaxis):
  1. Propranolol 40-160mg daily (beta-blocker):

- Most evidence, effective - Contraindications: Asthma, heart block, hypotension, depression - Side effects: Fatigue, hypotension, bradycardia, nightmares, sexual dysfunction

  1. Topiramate 50-100mg daily (anticonvulsant):

- Effective, especially if comorbid epilepsy or obesity (weight loss side effect) - Contraindications: Pregnancy (teratogenic) - Side effects: Cognitive impairment ("dopamax"), paresthesias, kidney stones, glaucoma, weight loss

  1. Amitriptyline 25-75mg nocte (tricyclic antidepressant):

- Effective, especially if comorbid tension headache, insomnia, depression - Side effects: Dry mouth, constipation, drowsiness, weight gain

  1. Candesartan 16mg daily (ARB - angiotensin receptor blocker):

- Effective, well-tolerated - Contraindications: Pregnancy, renal artery stenosis - Side effects: Dizziness, hypotension

Second-line:
  • Pizotifen 0.5-3mg daily (serotonin antagonist) - older agent, weight gain
  • Verapamil 240-480mg daily (calcium channel blocker)

New agents (expensive, specialist-initiated):
  • CGRP monoclonal antibodies: Erenumab, fremanezumab, galcanezumab (SC injection monthly) - very effective, expensive, PBS-restricted to specialist neurologists
  • Botulinum toxin A (Botox): For chronic migraine (‚â•15 headache days/month), injections every 12 weeks

Non-pharmacological:
  • Trigger avoidance (diary to identify triggers)
  • Regular sleep, meals, hydration
  • Stress management, relaxation techniques, CBT
  • Acupuncture (some evidence)

Migraine in Specific Populations

Women of childbearing age:
  • OCP: Can worsen migraine, contraindicated if migraine with aura (increased stroke risk)
  • Pregnancy: Migraine often improves (especially 2nd/3rd trimester)

- Acute treatment: Paracetamol (safe), metoclopramide (safe) - Avoid triptans (limited data, Category B3) - Avoid NSAIDs in 3rd trimester (premature closure of ductus arteriosus) - Preventive: Propranolol (safe), avoid topiramate (teratogenic)

Menstrual migraine:
  • Migraine predictably 2 days before to 3 days after menstruation
  • Treatment: NSAIDs continuously peri-menstrually, short-term triptan prophylaxis (frovatriptan 2.5mg BD for 6 days peri-menstrually)

Cluster Headache

Rare (0.1% population), most severe primary headache ("suicide headache"), M:F = 3:1, age 20-40s

Diagnostic Criteria (IHS)

At least 5 attacks with: A. Severe/very severe unilateral orbital, supraorbital, and/or temporal pain B. Headache duration: 15-180 minutes (untreated) C. At least ONE of following ipsilateral (same side as headache) autonomic features:
  1. Conjunctival injection (red eye) and/or lacrimation (tearing)
  2. Nasal congestion and/or rhinorrhea (runny nose)
  3. Eyelid edema
  4. Forehead/facial sweating
  5. Miosis (constricted pupil) and/or ptosis (drooping eyelid) - partial Horner's syndrome
  6. Restlessness or agitation (patient paces, cannot lie still - unlike migraine)

D. Frequency: 1 attack every other day to 8 attacks per day (during cluster period)

**E. Not better accounted for by another diagnosis

Subtypes

Episodic cluster headache (80%):
  • Cluster periods (bouts): Last weeks-months, then remission for months-years
  • Attacks occur same time each day (often night - "alarm clock headache")
  • Seasonal pattern common (spring, autumn)

Chronic cluster headache (20%):
  • No remission, or remission <3 months, for >1 year

Clinical Pearls

  • Excruciating unilateral pain (orbital/temple)
  • Short duration (15-180 min, average 45-90 min)
  • Frequent attacks (1-8 per day during cluster)
  • Same time daily (circadian rhythm - often 1-2am)
  • Autonomic features (red, teary eye, runny nose on affected side)
  • Restlessness (patient paces, rocks, agitated - CANNOT lie still)
  • Male predominance, smokers
  • Alcohol triggers attacks during cluster period (avoid alcohol during bout)

Management (eTG 2024)

Acute Treatment (Abortive):
  1. Subcutaneous sumatriptan 6mg - MOST EFFECTIVE (works in 10-15 min)

- PBS listed for cluster headache - Can repeat once after 1 hour if needed (max 2 doses/24 hours)

  1. High-flow 100% oxygen 12-15 L/min via non-rebreather mask x 15-20 minutes

- Very effective, no contraindications, no side effects - First-line (especially if triptan contraindicated)

  1. Intranasal zolmitriptan 5-10mg (faster than oral triptan)

Preventive Treatment (Prophylaxis during cluster period): First-line: Verapamil 240-960mg daily (calcium channel blocker)
  • Most effective preventive, drug of choice
  • Start 240mg daily, titrate up by 80mg every 2 weeks (typical dose 360-480mg)
  • ECG monitoring (risk of heart block at high doses - check ECG before starting, after each dose increase)
  • Side effects: Constipation, ankle edema, bradycardia, heart block

Alternative/Additional:
  • Short-term corticosteroids (bridge until verapamil takes effect):

- Prednisolone 60-80mg daily x 5 days, then rapid taper over 10 days - Effective quickly, use as bridge therapy

  • Lithium 400-800mg daily (effective but requires monitoring - renal, TSH, lithium levels)
  • Topiramate 100-200mg daily

Transitional therapy (prevent attacks while starting verapamil):
  • Greater occipital nerve block (local anesthetic + steroid injection)
  • Prednisolone as above

Refractory chronic cluster:
  • Neurology referral
  • Consider deep brain stimulation, nerve stimulation

3. SECONDARY HEADACHES (Dangerous Causes)

Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH)

Covered in üö® RED FLAGs section above Key Points:
  • Thunderclap headache ("worst headache of life", <1 min to peak)
  • Investigations: CT brain (95% sensitive <6 hours) ‚Üí LP if CT negative (xanthochromia)
  • Management: Neurosurgery (clip/coil aneurysm), nimodipine, BP control

Meningitis / Encephalitis

Covered in üö® RED FLAGs section above Key Points:
  • Fever + headache + neck stiffness (triad)
  • Do NOT delay antibiotics (ceftriaxone + dexamethasone)
  • CT before LP if raised ICP concern
  • Aciclovir empirically if encephalitis suspected

Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis)

Covered in üö® RED FLAGs section above Key Points:
  • Age >50, jaw claudication, visual symptoms ‚Üí GCA
  • START STEROIDS IMMEDIATELY (don't wait for biopsy - risk blindness)
  • ESR/CRP elevated, temporal artery biopsy

Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)

AKA Pseudotumor Cerebri Epidemiology: Young obese women (90%) Presentation:
  • Headache: Worse morning/lying flat, worse with Valsalva
  • Visual symptoms: Transient visual obscurations (seconds, precipitated by postural change), diplopia (6th nerve palsy), visual field defect (enlarged blind spot, peripheral constriction)
  • Pulsatile tinnitus (whooshing sound in ear)
  • Papilledema (bilateral) - hallmark

Investigations:
  • CT/MRI brain: Normal parenchyma (excludes tumor), may show "empty sella", slit-like ventricles
  • MR venography: Exclude venous sinus thrombosis
  • LP: Elevated opening pressure (>25 cm H2O), normal CSF composition
  • Ophthalmology: Formal visual fields, optical coherence tomography (papilledema assessment)

Management:
  • Weight loss (most important - even 5-10% can improve)
  • Acetazolamide 500mg-2g daily (carbonic anhydrase inhibitor - reduces CSF production)
  • Repeated LPs (therapeutic CSF drainage)
  • Neurology/ophthalmology referral
  • Surgery if refractory and vision threatened: Optic nerve sheath fenestration, VP shunt, venous sinus stenting

Complications: Permanent visual loss if untreated (optic atrophy from chronic papilledema)

Brain Tumor

Presentation:
  • Headache: Progressive over weeks-months, worse morning/lying flat/Valsalva
  • Focal neurology: Weakness, sensory loss, visual field defect (depends on location)
  • Seizures (new-onset, especially focal)
  • Cognitive/personality change
  • Papilledema (if raised ICP)

üö® RED FLAGs: New headache >50 years, progressive headache, focal neurology, seizures, papilledema Investigations: CT/MRI brain (contrast) Management: Neurosurgery, oncology (surgery, radiotherapy, chemotherapy depending on type)

Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)

AKA Rebound Headache, Analgesic Overuse Headache Definition: Chronic headache (‚â•15 days/month) caused by regular overuse of acute headache medications Criteria:
  • Headache ‚â•15 days/month in patient with pre-existing primary headache
  • Regular overuse of acute headache medication for >3 months:

- Triptans/opioids/combination analgesics: ‚â•10 days/month - Simple analgesics (paracetamol, NSAIDs, aspirin): ‚â•15 days/month

5. COMMON IMG MISTAKES

  1. Not recognizing thunderclap headache as SAH:

- "Worst headache of life" = SAH until proven otherwise - URGENT CT, then LP if CT negative

  1. Delaying antibiotics in suspected meningitis:

- Do NOT delay antibiotics for LP or CT - Give ceftriaxone + dexamethasone immediately if meningitis suspected - Can do LP after (antibiotics don't sterilize CSF for 4-6 hours, culture still useful)

  1. Not treating temporal arteritis URGENTly:

- START STEROIDS IMMEDIATELY on clinical suspicion (don't wait for biopsy) - Risk of irreversible blindness if delayed

  1. Missing medication overuse headache:

- Ask about frequency of acute medication use - If ‚â•10 days/month (triptans) or ‚â•15 days/month (simple analgesics) ‚Üí MOH - Management: Withdraw all acute medications (withdrawal headache, then improve)

  1. Over-investigating primary headaches:

- Typical migraine/tension/cluster with no üö® RED FLAGs = do NOT need CT/MRI - Imaging low yield, reassure patient

  1. Not asking about aura in migraine:

- Migraine with aura + OCP = increased stroke risk ‚Üí contraindicated - First aura >40 years ‚Üí image (exclude stroke/TIA)

  1. Treating cluster headache like migraine:

- Cluster = short duration (15-180 min) - oral meds too slow - Need subcutaneous sumatriptan or high-flow oxygen - Preventive: Verapamil (not propranolol/amitriptyline)

  1. Not checking fundoscopy in new headache:

- Papilledema = raised ICP ‚Üí URGENT investigation - Always do fundoscopy in new headache, especially if üö® RED FLAGs

  1. CT brain without LP in suspected SAH:

- CT negative does NOT exclude SAH (sensitivity decreases with time) - If CT negative and high suspicion ‚Üí LP (xanthochromia)

  1. Using opioids for primary headaches:

- Avoid opioids (codeine, morphine, oxycodone) for migraine/tension/cluster - High risk of medication overuse headache, dependence - Not effective, not evidence-based
