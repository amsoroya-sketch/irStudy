Physical Examination: Peripheral Vascular System

OSCE Context:

Station: 8-minute peripheral vascular examination (AMC Clinical Exam format)

Task: "Please examine this patient's peripheral vascular system and present your findings"

Common Scenarios: Intermittent claudication, critical limb ischaemia, diabetic foot assessment, suspected DVT, chronic venous insufficiency

Young Hospital Context: Elderly patients with diabetes, peripheral arterial disease (PAD), post-amputation assessments common in rural NSW

PERIPHERAL VASCULAR EXAMINATION

‚è±Ô∏è TIMING BREAKDOWN (Total: 8 minutes)
  • Preparation: 60 seconds
  • Position & Permission: 30 seconds
  • General Inspection: 30 seconds
  • Upper Limbs: 60 seconds
  • Lower Limbs: 3 minutes
  • Special Tests: 60 seconds
  • Presentation: 60-90 seconds

1. PREPARATION ‚è±Ô∏è 60 seconds

WORD-FOR-WORD INTRODUCTION SCRIPT: [Perform visible hand hygiene with alcohol gel]

"Good morning/afternoon. My name is Dr [Your Name]. I'm a medical officer here at [Hospital Name]. Before we start, can you please tell me your full name and date of birth?"

[Patient confirms identity]

"Thank you, Mr/Ms [Surname]. I understand you've been having some problems with your legs/circulation. I'd like to examine your arms and legs today to check your blood flow. This will involve me looking at your skin, feeling for pulses, and checking the colour and temperature of your limbs. I'll need you to remove your socks and shoes, and roll up your sleeves and trouser legs. Is that alright with you?"

[Wait for verbal consent]

"Before we start, do you have any pain anywhere at the moment?"

[If yes: "Where is the pain? I'll be gentle during the examination and will let you know before I examine that area."]

Equipment to Gather:

  • Hand-held Doppler ultrasound (if available for ABPI measurement)
  • Tape measure (for calf circumference in suspected DVT)
  • Pillow for leg elevation
  • Stethoscope (for auscultating bruits)
  • Pen torch (for capillary refill assessment)

Privacy and Dignity:

  • Close curtains/door
  • Ensure adequate but not excessive exposure (arms from elbows, legs from knees - or fully expose if instructed)
  • Offer blanket to cover areas not being examined
  • Chaperone not typically required unless examining groin pulses in opposite gender patient

2. POSITION ‚è±Ô∏è 15 seconds

Patient Positioning:

  • Initial position: Patient lying supine (flat) on examination couch, one pillow under head
  • Arms: Resting comfortably by sides, accessible for pulse examination
  • Legs: Straight, uncrossed, fully visible from hips to toes
  • Adequate exposure: "From fingers to shoulders" for arms; "from toes to groin" for legs
  • Note: You will need to sit/stand patient up for Buerger's test later
üí° POSITIONING TIP: For suspected venous disease, you may need to examine patient standing (varicose veins). For arterial disease, supine position is standard. In OSCE, follow examiner instructions: "Examine as you would in clinical practice" means include positional changes.

3. PERMISSION ‚è±Ô∏è 15 seconds

CONSENT SCRIPT: "I'm going to examine your arms first, then your legs. I'll be checking your skin, feeling for pulses in your wrists, arms, groin, and feet, and checking the temperature and colour. Some parts might feel a bit uncomfortable, particularly when I press on your pulses, but it shouldn't be painful. If anything hurts, please let me know straight away. Is that okay?"

[Wait for verbal "yes"]

If examining groin pulses in opposite gender:
"I'll need to feel for pulses in your groin area. I can arrange for a nurse to be present if you'd prefer. Would you like that?"

Exposure Check:

  • Sleeves rolled up above elbows
  • Trouser legs rolled up above knees (or shorts/gown worn)
  • Socks and shoes removed
  • Ensure patient is warm (cold room affects peripheral circulation findings)

4. PERFORM THE EXAMINATION ‚è±Ô∏è 6 minutes

A. GENERAL INSPECTION ‚è±Ô∏è 30 seconds

"I'm going to start by having a look at you from the end of the bed."

End-of-Bed Observation:

  • General appearance: Comfortable at rest vs. in pain, mobility aids (walking stick, crutches, wheelchair)
  • Colour: Pale (anaemia), cyanosed (peripheral vascular disease)
  • Body habitus: Obesity (vascular risk factor), cachexia (severe PAD, malignancy)
  • Obvious abnormalities: Amputations (below-knee, above-knee), visible ulcers, bandages, compression stockings
  • Walking aids visible: Crutches, walking frame (intermittent claudication limiting mobility)

Environmental Clues:

  • Medications on bedside table: Aspirin, clopidogrel, statins (vascular disease)
  • Diabetic equipment: Blood glucose monitor, insulin pens (diabetic foot risk)
  • Compression stockings: Venous insufficiency
  • Cigarettes/smell of smoke: Smoking history (major PAD risk factor)

B. UPPER LIMB EXAMINATION ‚è±Ô∏è 60 seconds

"I'm going to examine your hands and arms now."

HANDS - Inspection (both hands simultaneously):

  • Colour:
    • Normal pink vs. pale (anaemia, arterial insufficiency)
    • Cyanosis (peripheral vascular disease, Raynaud's phenomenon)
    • Mottling (livedo reticularis - vasculitis, cholesterol emboli)
    • Nicotine staining (yellow/brown fingers - smoking history)
  • Temperature: "I'm going to feel if your hands are warm."
    • Use back of your hand to compare both hands and compare to your own
    • Cold peripheries: Poor perfusion, arterial disease, shock
    • Warm peripheries with bounding pulses: Sepsis, hyperthyroidism, anaemia
  • Capillary refill time (CRT): "I'm going to press on your fingernail."
    • Press on nail bed for 5 seconds, release, count seconds until pink colour returns
    • Normal: <2 seconds
    • Prolonged (>2 seconds): Poor perfusion, shock, arterial insufficiency
    • Perform on at least 2 fingers (compare sides)
  • Nail changes:
    • Splinter haemorrhages (endocarditis, vasculitis)
    • Clubbing (rare in isolated vascular disease, think lung/heart pathology)
  • Fingertip changes:
    • Ulceration, necrosis, gangrene (critical limb ischaemia, Buerger's disease)
    • Sclerodactyly (scleroderma - associated vascular disease)

RADIAL PULSE - Palpation:

"I'm going to feel the pulse at your wrist now."

  • Technique: Use index and middle fingers, palpate lateral aspect of wrist (radial side)
  • Assess BOTH radial pulses:
    • Presence: Present vs. absent
    • Rate: Count for 15 seconds x 4 (or 30 seconds if irregular)
    • Rhythm: Regular vs. irregular (AF - increased stroke/embolic risk)
    • Character: Normal, bounding, thready
    • Volume: Normal, weak, strong
    • Radio-radial delay: Palpate both simultaneously - any delay? (subclavian stenosis, aortic coarctation, aortic dissection)

BRACHIAL PULSE - Palpation:

"I'm going to feel the pulse in your elbow."

  • Location: Medial to biceps tendon in antecubital fossa
  • Palpate bilaterally, compare
  • Absent brachial pulse: Arterial occlusion, thoracic outlet syndrome

BLOOD PRESSURE:

"I would measure blood pressure in both arms."

  • In OSCE: Usually state you would do this (time constraints)
  • Significance: Difference >20 mmHg between arms ‚Üí subclavian stenosis, aortic dissection

ARMS - Further Inspection:

  • Scars: Arteriovenous fistula (AVF) for dialysis, previous vascular surgery
  • AV fistula assessment (if present):
    • Look: Prominent veins, surgical scar
    • Feel: Palpable thrill (buzzing sensation)
    • Listen: Auscultate for bruit (continuous machinery murmur)

C. LOWER LIMB EXAMINATION ‚è±Ô∏è 3 minutes

"I'm going to examine your legs now, starting by looking at the skin."

INSPECTION - Both Legs (Compare Side-to-Side):

1. Colour Changes:

  • Pallor: Pale, white foot/leg (arterial insufficiency)
    • Elevation pallor: Leg goes white when elevated (Buerger's test - see below)
    • Dependent rubor: Dusky red/purple when dependent (chronic ischaemia)
  • Cyanosis: Blue/purple discolouration (severe ischaemia, venous congestion)
  • Gangrene: Black, necrotic tissue (critical limb ischaemia)
    • Dry gangrene: Black, mummified (arterial occlusion)
    • Wet gangrene: Black with purulent discharge (infection + ischaemia)
  • Venous changes:
    • Haemosiderin deposition: Brown pigmentation (chronic venous insufficiency)
    • Lipodermatosclerosis: Hard, thickened skin on lower leg (venous disease)
    • Varicose eczema: Red, scaly, itchy skin (venous hypertension)

2. Skin and Tissue Changes:

  • Hair loss: Loss of leg/foot hair (chronic arterial insufficiency)
  • Shiny, atrophic skin: Thin, shiny skin (chronic ischaemia)
  • Muscle wasting: Thin, wasted calf muscles (disuse, chronic ischaemia)
  • Oedema: "I'm going to press on your shin."
    • Press over tibia for 5 seconds, assess for pitting
    • Unilateral oedema: DVT, cellulitis, chronic venous insufficiency
    • Bilateral oedema: Heart failure, renal disease, hypoalbuminaemia

3. Ulcers (CRITICAL - Describe Location, Size, Appearance):

Feature Arterial Ulcer Venous Ulcer Neuropathic (Diabetic) Ulcer
Location Toes, heel, lateral malleolus, pressure points Medial malleolus (gaiter area) Plantar surface, pressure points (metatarsal heads), between toes
Appearance Punched-out, deep, necrotic base Shallow, irregular edges, slough Deep, painless, surrounded by callus
Pain Very painful (worse at night, elevation) Mild to moderate pain, aching Painless (loss of sensation)
Surrounding skin Pale, shiny, hairless, cold Brown (haemosiderin), eczema, warm Callus formation, normal colour, warm
Pulses Reduced or absent Present May be present or reduced (co-existent PAD common)

4. Varicose Veins:

  • Inspection: Dilated, tortuous superficial veins (usually medial leg)
  • Note distribution: Long saphenous (medial), short saphenous (posterior/lateral)
  • May need to examine standing (veins more prominent when standing)
  • Associated signs: Oedema, skin changes, venous ulcers

5. Scars:

  • Surgical scars:
    • Longitudinal medial leg scar: Long saphenous vein harvest (CABG) or varicose vein surgery
    • Groin scar: Femoral endarterectomy, embolectomy, vascular bypass
    • Below-knee/above-knee amputation
  • Traumatic scars: Previous injury (may have damaged vessels)

6. Nail Changes:

  • Thickened, dystrophic nails: Chronic ischaemia, fungal infection (common in diabetics)
  • Absent nails: Previous trauma, severe ischaemia

PALPATION - Lower Limbs:

1. Temperature:

"I'm going to feel the temperature of your legs."

  • Technique: Use back of hand, compare:
    • Left vs. right (asymmetry)
    • Proximal vs. distal (temperature gradient)
  • Cold foot/leg: Arterial insufficiency (acute or chronic)
  • Warm foot/leg: Cellulitis, DVT (may be warm), normal perfusion
  • Sharp demarcation (warm to cold): Level of arterial occlusion

2. Capillary Refill Time:

"I'm going to check the circulation to your toes."

  • Press on big toe nail bed for 5 seconds, release, count
  • Normal: <2 seconds
  • Prolonged: Arterial insufficiency, shock

3. Peripheral Pulses - Systematic Palpation (CRITICAL SKILL):

üö® PULSE EXAMINATION TECHNIQUE:
  • Use index and middle fingers (not thumb - you'll feel your own pulse)
  • Palpate both sides for each pulse (compare presence, volume)
  • Grade: 0 (absent), 1+ (diminished), 2+ (normal), 3+ (increased)
  • In OSCE: Say out loud "Femoral pulse present", "Dorsalis pedis absent on right"

A. FEMORAL PULSE:

"I'm going to feel for the pulse in your groin."

  • Anatomical location: Mid-inguinal point (halfway between ASIS and pubic symphysis)
  • Technique:
    • Press firmly (deep pulse)
    • Palpate just below inguinal ligament
    • Lateral to femoral vein (remember: NAVY - Nerve, Artery, Vein, Y-fronts from lateral to medial)
  • Absent femoral pulse: Aorto-iliac occlusion, femoral artery occlusion
  • Auscultation: If examining for renovascular disease or AAA, auscultate for femoral bruit

B. POPLITEAL PULSE (Often difficult to palpate even when present):

"I'm going to feel for the pulse behind your knee - this can be a bit uncomfortable."

  • Technique:
    • Patient supine, knee slightly flexed (place pillow under knee or flex to 30¬∞)
    • Place both hands around knee, fingers in popliteal fossa
    • Press deeply (pulse is deep to tibial nerve and popliteal vein)
    • Alternative: Patient prone, knee flexed to 90¬∞, palpate fossa
  • Often impalpable in normal patients (obesity, deep location)
  • Easily palpable, prominent pulsation: Popliteal aneurysm (urgent vascular referral)

C. POSTERIOR TIBIAL PULSE:

"I'm going to feel for the pulse behind your ankle bone."

  • Anatomical location: Posterior and inferior to medial malleolus
  • Technique:
    • Curl fingers behind and below medial malleolus
    • Pulse between malleolus and Achilles tendon
    • Easier to feel with foot dorsiflexed
  • Absent: Peripheral arterial disease, acute limb ischaemia

D. DORSALIS PEDIS PULSE:

"I'm going to feel for the pulse on top of your foot."

  • Anatomical location: Dorsum of foot, lateral to extensor hallucis longus tendon
  • Technique:
    • Ask patient to dorsiflex big toe (makes tendon prominent)
    • Palpate lateral to tendon, between 1st and 2nd metatarsals
    • Move fingers around if not immediately felt (anatomical variation common)
  • Absent in 10% of normal population (anatomical variant)
  • If absent, check for alternative: Arcuate artery (more lateral on dorsum of foot)
üí° PULSE PALPATION TIP:

If you cannot feel a pulse, DON'T say "absent" immediately:

  1. Try different finger positions (anatomical variation)
  2. Ensure your fingers are in correct anatomical location
  3. Use Doppler ultrasound to confirm (if available)
  4. Document as "not palpable" rather than "absent" unless confirmed absent on Doppler

In OSCE: Say "I'm having difficulty feeling this pulse, I would use a hand-held Doppler to confirm its presence."

MEASUREMENT - If DVT Suspected:

"I would measure the calf circumference to compare both legs."

  • Technique: Measure circumference 10 cm below tibial tuberosity (mark with pen to ensure same level both sides)
  • Significance: Difference >3 cm suggests DVT (or other cause of unilateral swelling)
  • Wells Score for DVT: Calf swelling >3cm is +1 point

D. SPECIAL TESTS ‚è±Ô∏è 60 seconds

1. BUERGER'S TEST (Arterial Insufficiency):

"I'm going to do a special test now to check the blood supply to your legs. I'll need you to lie back and lift your legs up."

BUERGER'S TEST - Step by Step:
  1. Elevation Phase:
    • Patient supine, both legs elevated to 45¬∞ (or as high as patient can tolerate - some say 15-30¬∞ sufficient)
    • Hold for 60 seconds (or until colour change occurs)
    • Observe feet for colour change
    • Positive sign (Buerger's angle): Foot goes pale/white (inadequate arterial blood flow against gravity)
    • Note the angle at which pallor occurs (lower angle = worse ischaemia)
  2. Dependency Phase:
    • Ask patient to sit up, legs hanging over edge of bed
    • Observe for 2-3 minutes
    • Positive sign (dependent rubor): Foot becomes dusky red/purple (reactive hyperaemia, chronic ischaemia)
    • Normal: Foot returns to normal pink colour within 10 seconds
    • Delayed venous filling: Veins on dorsum of foot take >15 seconds to refill (arterial insufficiency)

Interpretation:

  • Positive Buerger's test: Significant peripheral arterial disease
  • Negative test: Does not exclude arterial disease (only detects severe disease)

2. ANKLE-BRACHIAL PRESSURE INDEX (ABPI):

"I would perform an ankle-brachial pressure index measurement using a Doppler ultrasound."

  • In OSCE: Usually just state you would do this (time-consuming, requires equipment)
  • Equipment needed: Hand-held Doppler, blood pressure cuff, gel
  • Technique (if required to demonstrate):
    1. Measure systolic BP in brachial artery (both arms, use higher value)
    2. Apply BP cuff to ankle (above malleoli)
    3. Use Doppler to locate dorsalis pedis pulse
    4. Inflate cuff until Doppler signal disappears
    5. Slowly deflate, note pressure when signal returns (ankle systolic BP)
    6. Repeat for posterior tibial artery
    7. Use higher of the two ankle pressures
    8. Calculate: ABPI = Ankle systolic BP √∑ Brachial systolic BP
  • Interpretation:
    ABPI Value Interpretation Clinical Significance
    >1.3 Non-compressible vessels Calcified arteries (diabetes, CKD) - unreliable, use toe pressures instead
    1.0 - 1.3 Normal No significant arterial disease
    0.9 - 1.0 Acceptable/borderline Mild disease or normal (exercise testing may help)
    0.5 - 0.9 Moderate PAD Intermittent claudication likely
    <0.5 Severe PAD Critical limb ischaemia, rest pain, tissue loss

3. AUSCULTATION FOR BRUITS:

"I would listen over the major arteries for bruits."

  • Sites to auscultate:
    • Femoral arteries: Groin (femoral stenosis)
    • Abdominal aorta: Epigastrium (AAA, aortic stenosis)
    • Renal arteries: Either side of umbilicus (renal artery stenosis - renovascular hypertension)
  • Bruit: Whooshing sound (turbulent blood flow through stenosis)
  • Absence of bruit: Does not exclude stenosis (complete occlusion is silent)

4. EXAMINATION FOR ABDOMINAL AORTIC ANEURYSM (AAA):

"I would examine the abdomen for an aortic aneurysm."

  • Palpation: Place hands either side of midline, above umbilicus, feel for pulsatile, expansile mass
  • Expansile: Pushes fingers apart laterally (true aneurysm) vs. transmitted pulsation (just lifts fingers up)
  • Auscultation: Listen for bruit over AAA
  • Significance: >3 cm diameter = aneurysm; >5.5 cm = surgical referral

To Complete the Examination:

COMPLETION STATEMENT: "To complete my peripheral vascular examination, I would:
  • Measure the ankle-brachial pressure index (ABPI) using a hand-held Doppler
  • Examine for varicose veins with the patient standing
  • Perform a full cardiovascular examination including blood pressure in both arms
  • Examine the abdomen for an abdominal aortic aneurysm
  • Assess sensation in the feet if diabetic neuropathy suspected (10g monofilament test)
  • Inspect any ulcers more closely and measure dimensions
  • Review cardiovascular risk factors: check lipid profile, HbA1c, and arrange arterial Doppler ultrasound if indicated
Thank you Mr/Ms [Name], you can put your socks back on now."

5. PRESENT FINDINGS ‚è±Ô∏è 60-90 seconds

STRUCTURED PRESENTATION FORMAT:

Opening Statement:
"This is Mr/Ms [Name], a [age]-year-old [gentleman/lady] who I have examined for peripheral vascular disease."

General Inspection:
"On general inspection, the patient appears [comfortable/in pain], [well/unwell], with [no obvious mobility aids / walking stick present]. I noted [no amputations / below-knee amputation on the left]."

Upper Limb Findings:
"Examination of the upper limbs reveals [normal/pale/cyanosed] hands with [normal/prolonged] capillary refill time of [<2 seconds / 3 seconds]. Radial and brachial pulses are [palpable bilaterally and equal / reduced on the left]. There is [no radio-radial delay / radio-radial delay present]. Blood pressure would be measured in both arms to assess for subclavian stenosis."

Lower Limb Findings:
"On inspection of the lower limbs, the skin is [normal / hairless and atrophic / showing brown haemosiderin deposition]. There is [no/unilateral/bilateral] oedema. The right/left foot appears [pale/cyanosed/normal in colour]. I can see [no ulcers / a [X] cm arterial ulcer on the lateral aspect of the right foot with a necrotic base]."

"On palpation, the right/left foot is [cold/warm] compared to the left/right. Capillary refill time is [normal/<2 seconds / prolonged at 4 seconds] in the toes."

Pulse Findings (CRITICAL - Be Specific):
"Femoral pulses are [palpable bilaterally / absent on the right / present and equal].
Popliteal pulses are [not palpable bilaterally - which can be normal / palpable and enlarged on the left suggesting aneurysm].
Posterior tibial pulses are [palpable bilaterally / absent on the right].
Dorsalis pedis pulses are [palpable bilaterally / absent on the left / not palpable but confirmed present on Doppler]."

Special Tests:
"Buerger's test was [positive / negative]. On elevation, the right foot became pale at 20 degrees (positive), and on dependency showed rubor at 90 seconds, with delayed venous filling of 25 seconds, consistent with significant arterial insufficiency."

Summary and Diagnosis:
"In summary, this patient has clinical signs of [peripheral arterial disease affecting the right lower limb / chronic venous insufficiency / critical limb ischaemia with absent pulses and tissue loss]."

Further Investigations/Management:
"I would measure the ankle-brachial pressure index, arrange arterial Doppler ultrasound imaging, and refer to the vascular surgery team for further assessment. I would also assess cardiovascular risk factors and optimize medical management with antiplatelet therapy, statin, and smoking cessation advice."
üí° PRESENTATION TIPS:
  • Be precise about which limb is affected (don't say "the leg" - say "the right leg")
  • For each pulse, explicitly state present/absent and which side
  • Describe ulcers with location, size, depth, and type (arterial/venous/neuropathic)
  • Link findings together: "Absent pulses + cold foot + pallor on elevation = arterial insufficiency"
  • State likely diagnosis clearly: "This is consistent with peripheral arterial disease" not "There might be some vascular issues"

COMMON IMG MISTAKES ‚ùå

üö® CRITICAL ERRORS THAT FAIL OSCES:
  • ‚ùå Not comparing both sides: You MUST examine both legs/arms and explicitly compare findings. Saying "pulse present" without specifying which side = incomplete examination. Say "Radial pulses palpable bilaterally and equal."
  • ‚ùå Using your thumb to palpate pulses: Common mistake! Your thumb has its own pulse ‚Üí you'll feel your own pulse instead of patient's. Always use index and middle fingers.
  • ‚ùå Saying "all pulses present" without actually palpating them: Examiners watch your hands. You must physically palpate femoral, popliteal, posterior tibial, and dorsalis pedis on BOTH legs. Skipping popliteal is common - don't do it!
  • ‚ùå Incorrect pulse locations:
    • Dorsalis pedis: Students often palpate too medially (over tendon instead of lateral to it)
    • Posterior tibial: Too far posterior (should be behind AND below medial malleolus)
    • Femoral: Too medial (over vein) or too lateral (over nerve) - mid-inguinal point!
  • ‚ùå Not assessing temperature correctly: Use the BACK of your hand (more sensitive to temperature). Compare left vs right, proximal vs distal. Many students skip this completely.
  • ‚ùå Forgetting to expose adequately: "Can I see your legs?" with patient in jeans = automatic fail. Must have full exposure "from toes to groin" - ask patient to remove socks/shoes and roll up trousers BEFORE starting.
  • ‚ùå Not performing Buerger's test: This is THE special test for arterial disease. If you don't do it, examiner assumes you don't know how. Practice: Elevate ‚Üí observe for pallor ‚Üí sit up ‚Üí observe for rubor.
  • ‚ùå Confusing arterial and venous ulcers:
    • Arterial: Painful, toes/heel, punched-out, absent pulses
    • Venous: Mild pain, medial malleolus, shallow, pulses present
    • Neuropathic: Painless, plantar surface, callus around
    Get this wrong and you fail the diagnosis.
  • ‚ùå Not checking capillary refill time (CRT): Essential basic observation. Press nail bed 5 seconds, count seconds to pink return. Normal <2 sec. Many students forget entirely.
  • ‚ùå Poor communication during examination: Silent examination = poor marks. You MUST say out loud what you're doing: "I'm going to feel your pulse now." This demonstrates professionalism and patient-centeredness (AMC marking criteria).
  • ‚ùå Not looking for scars: Previous vascular surgery = crucial clinical context. Look for:
    • Long saphenous vein harvest scar (medial leg) - CABG
    • Groin scars (femoral surgery)
    • Amputations
  • ‚ùå Incorrect ABPI interpretation:
    • Students often think <0.9 is normal - it's NOT. Normal is 1.0-1.3
    • Forgetting that >1.3 is ABNORMAL (calcified vessels in diabetes) not "really good circulation"
    • Not knowing when to use toe pressures instead (diabetes, CKD with vessel calcification)
  • ‚ùå Missing the diagnosis of acute limb ischaemia: If examiner gives you a patient with:
    • Sudden onset leg pain
    • Absent pulses
    • Pale, cold leg
    • Paraesthesia
    This is a SURGICAL EMERGENCY (6 Ps). You must say "This patient needs URGENT vascular surgery review within 6 hours" or you fail on safety grounds.
  • ‚ùå Not examining for AAA when asked for "peripheral vascular exam": In a complete peripheral vascular examination, you MUST offer to examine abdomen for AAA. Say: "I would complete my examination by palpating for an abdominal aortic aneurysm."
  • ‚ùå Rough examination technique causing pain: Especially when:
    • Palpating popliteal pulse (press too hard)
    • Testing for pitting oedema (press too long/hard on tender leg)
    • Performing Buerger's test on painful ischaemic leg (elevate gently!)
    Watch patient's face. If they wince, you're being too rough = poor patient care.
  • ‚ùå Cultural mistake: Not asking permission to examine groin: In Australian OSCE, examining femoral pulses in opposite gender patient WITHOUT offering chaperone = automatic fail (AHPRA professional standards). Always say: "I need to examine pulses in your groin. I can arrange for a nurse to be present if you'd prefer."

EXAMINATION MNEMONICS

üéØ "6 Ps" of ACUTE LIMB ISCHAEMIA (Emergency):
  • Pain (sudden onset, severe)
  • Pallor (white/pale leg)
  • Pulseless (absent distal pulses)
  • Paraesthesia (altered sensation, numbness, tingling)
  • Paralysis (inability to move toes/foot - late sign, muscle death imminent)
  • Perishingly cold (cold to touch)

Clinical Significance: If you see 4+ of these ‚Üí IMMEDIATE vascular surgery referral (within 6 hours to save limb)

üéØ "ABCDEF" for Chronic PAD Assessment:
  • Atherosclerotic risk factors (diabetes, smoking, hypertension, hyperlipidaemia)
  • Buerger's test (elevation pallor, dependent rubor)
  • Colour (pale, cyanosed, rubor)
  • Distal pulses (absent or reduced)
  • Examination findings (cold, hairless, atrophic skin, ulcers, gangrene)
  • Fontaine classification (I = asymptomatic, II = claudication, III = rest pain, IV = tissue loss)
üéØ Pulse Palpation Sequence (Cephalad to Caudad):
  1. Radial
  2. Brachial
  3. Femoral
  4. Popliteal
  5. Posterior tibial
  6. Dorsalis pedis

Mnemonic: "Really Big Feet Push Past Doors" (R-B-F-P-P-D)

üéØ Differentiating Ulcer Types - "Location, Location, Location":
Ulcer Type Location Mnemonic Additional Features
Arterial "Toes and Heel" (pressure points) Deep, Dry, Dead tissue (3 Ds)
Venous "Medial Malleolus" (gaiter area) Shallow, Slough, Surrounding skin changes (3 Ss)
Neuropathic "Plantar Pressure points" (metatarsal heads) Painless, Punched-out, Perimeter callus (3 Ps)
üö® RED FLAGS - Immediate Escalation Required:
  • 6 Ps present: Acute limb ischaemia ‚Üí emergency vascular surgery (within 6 hours)
  • Pulsatile popliteal mass: Popliteal aneurysm ‚Üí urgent vascular referral (high risk limb loss)
  • Unilateral leg swelling + tenderness + warmth: DVT ‚Üí immediate anticoagulation, assess for PE
  • Diabetic foot ulcer + purulent discharge + fever: Infected diabetic foot ‚Üí urgent admission, IV antibiotics, surgical debridement
  • Tender, pulsatile abdominal mass: Symptomatic AAA (pre-rupture) ‚Üí immediate vascular surgery, CT angiogram
  • Gangrene + systemic sepsis: Wet gangrene ‚Üí urgent surgical debridement/amputation + IV antibiotics
  • Rest pain not relieved by analgesia: Critical limb ischaemia ‚Üí urgent vascular assessment for revascularization
üí° YOUNG HOSPITAL CONTEXT (Rural NSW):

Common Presentations:

  • Elderly diabetic patients with foot ulcers: High prevalence in rural areas, often delayed presentation
  • Smokers with claudication: Rural patients may walk long distances (farms, properties) ‚Üí claudication impacts daily life significantly
  • Post-amputation patients: High rates of diabetes ‚Üí frequent below-knee amputations
  • Limited vascular surgery access: Complex cases transferred to Wagga Base or Canberra ‚Üí early recognition critical
  • Co-morbidities common: Diabetes + CKD + IHD (calcified vessels, unreliable ABPI ‚Üí use toe pressures)

Clinical Priorities for ICRP:

  • Recognize critical limb ischaemia early (rural patients present late)
  • Accurate diabetic foot assessment (prevent amputation)
  • Know when to transfer urgently (acute limb ischaemia, infected diabetic foot)
  • Optimize medical management (antiplatelet, statin, diabetes control) - prevents progression
