MUSCULOSKELETAL EXAMINATION

Note: The musculoskeletal examination can be either a GALS screening examination (3-4 minutes) or a detailed regional examination (6-8 minutes). In Australian OSCE settings, you should be prepared for both. Always clarify with the examiner: "Would you like me to perform a screening GALS examination or a detailed examination of a specific region?"
Australian OSCE Context: GALS (Gait, Arms, Legs, Spine) screening is standard in Australian medical education and AMC clinical examinations. If screening is positive, proceed to detailed regional examination. This guide covers both approaches.

1. PREPARATION (60 seconds)

Word-for-word script:

[Perform visible hand hygiene with alcohol gel]

"Good morning/afternoon. My name is Dr [Your Name], and I'm a medical officer here at [Hospital Name]. Before we begin, can you please confirm your full name and date of birth for me?"

[Patient confirms identity]

"Thank you. Today I'd like to examine your joints and muscles. Before we start, I need to ask: are you experiencing any pain in your joints at the moment? Which areas are affected?"

[Patient responds - note pain locations]

"I understand. During the examination, I'll be looking at your joints, feeling them gently, and asking you to perform some movements. I'll try to be as gentle as possible, but please let me know immediately if anything I do causes you discomfort or pain. Is that okay with you?"

[Obtain verbal consent]

"For this examination, I'll need you to be in your undergarments or loose clothing. I can provide you with a gown, and I'll only expose the areas I'm examining. I can also arrange for a chaperone to be present if you'd like. Would you prefer that?"

[Address chaperone preference]
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Safety Point: Always ask about pain BEFORE moving any joint. If the patient reports acute severe pain, swelling, or recent trauma, inform the examiner immediately as this may require modification of the examination or urgent imaging.

Equipment needed:

  • Goniometer (for measuring joint angles - optional in OSCE)
  • Tape measure (for limb length discrepancy)
  • Tendon hammer (for reflexes if neurological component)
  • Examination couch
  • Adequate lighting

2. POSITION

Positioning varies by examination type:

For GALS Screening:

  • Patient standing initially (for gait and general inspection)
  • Then sitting on edge of examination couch (for upper limb)
  • Then lying supine (for lower limb examination)
  • Adequate exposure: Patient in undergarments or loose shorts and singlet

For Regional Examinations:

  • Hands/Wrists: Patient sitting, hands resting on pillow on examination table
  • Elbows: Patient sitting, arms by sides then extended
  • Shoulders: Patient sitting upright, upper body exposed
  • Spine: Patient standing for inspection and movement, then lying for palpation
  • Hips: Patient lying supine on examination couch
  • Knees: Patient lying supine, then sitting with legs over edge of couch
  • Feet/Ankles: Patient lying supine, then standing for gait
Exposure Guidelines: "From underwear to visibility" - patients should be adequately exposed to see joint contours, muscle bulk, and skin changes, but maintain dignity with strategic draping. For male patients examining lower limbs: shorts. For female patients: shorts and sports bra or gown with strategic exposure.

3. PERMISSION

Specific permission script:

"I'm going to examine your joints now. This involves looking at them, gently feeling for any swelling or warmth, and then asking you to move them through their range of motion. Some movements might feel a bit uncomfortable if you have arthritis or inflammation, but they shouldn't be painful. If anything hurts, please tell me immediately and I'll stop. Is that alright with you?"

[Await explicit consent]

"Thank you. Let's begin."

Ongoing consent throughout examination:

  • "I'm going to move your hip now - let me know if this hurts"
  • "I'm going to press on your knee - tell me if this is tender"
  • "Can you try this movement for me? Stop if it's painful"

4. PERFORM (6 minutes for detailed exam, 3-4 minutes for GALS)

A. GALS SCREENING EXAMINATION (3-4 minutes)

GALS Mnemonic:
G - Gait (watch patient walk)
A - Arms (upper limb screening)
L - Legs (lower limb screening)
S - Spine (spinal movements)

Purpose: Rapid screening examination to detect locomotor abnormalities. Sensitivity >90% for detecting significant joint disease. If abnormalities detected, proceed to detailed regional examination.

GALS Screening Questions (ask while observing at rest):
  1. "Do you have any pain or stiffness in your muscles, joints or back?"
  2. "Can you dress yourself completely without any difficulty?"
  3. "Can you walk up and down stairs without any difficulty?"
Screening Interpretation: If all three questions answered "NO pain/stiffness" and "YES can dress/walk stairs", and GALS examination normal ‚Üí musculoskeletal system likely normal. Any positive response or abnormal finding ‚Üí detailed regional examination required.
GALS Examination Sequence:

1. GAIT (G) - Patient Standing and Walking

Say aloud: "I'm observing the patient's gait. I'm looking for symmetry, stride length, arm swing, and any signs of pain or limp."

Inspection from Behind:

  • Observe patient standing: posture, spinal alignment, pelvic level, muscle bulk
  • "Please walk to the end of the room and back" - observe gait
  • Normal gait: symmetrical, heel-toe pattern, reciprocal arm swing
  • Abnormal: antalgic (pain), Trendelenburg (hip disease), high-stepping (foot drop)

2. ARMS (A) - Upper Limb Screening

Say aloud: "I'm now examining the upper limbs, looking at the hands, arms, and shoulders for any deformity, swelling, or restriction of movement."

Inspection (Patient Standing, Arms by Sides):

  • Hands: swelling, deformity, muscle wasting
  • Arms: muscle bulk, carrying angle, scars
  • Shoulders: symmetry, muscle wasting

Screening Movements (Demonstrate, then ask patient to copy):

  1. Hands: "Put your hands out in front, palms down" (inspect dorsum)
  2. "Now turn them over" (inspect palms)
  3. "Make a tight fist with both hands" (tests grip and MCP joints)
  4. "Now open your hands wide and spread your fingers apart" (tests finger extension/abduction)
  5. Squeeze test: "I'm going to gently squeeze across your knuckles - tell me if this is tender" (squeeze across MCPs - tests for synovitis)
  6. Wrists: "Put your hands together like you're praying, and lift your elbows up" (tests wrist extension - prayer sign)
  7. "Now point your fingers down and put the backs of your hands together" (tests wrist flexion - reverse prayer sign)
  8. Elbows: "Bend your elbows fully and touch your shoulders" (tests elbow flexion)
  9. "Now straighten your arms out completely" (tests elbow extension)
  10. Shoulders: "Put your hands behind your head and push your elbows back" (tests shoulder abduction and external rotation)
  11. "Now reach behind your back and try to touch your shoulder blades" (tests shoulder internal rotation)

3. LEGS (L) - Lower Limb Screening

Say aloud: "The patient is now lying supine. I'm examining the lower limbs for any deformity, swelling, or restricted movement."

Inspection (Patient Supine):

  • Leg alignment, muscle bulk (quadriceps wasting?), swelling
  • Knees: varus/valgus deformity, effusion, muscle wasting
  • Feet: deformity, swelling, toe alignment

Screening Movements:

  1. Hips: "I'm going to move your hip" - passively flex hip and knee to 90¬∞ (observes hip flexion, patient's face for pain)
  2. Internally rotate hip while flexed (tests hip internal rotation)
  3. Knees: "Bend your knee up towards your chest" (tests active knee flexion)
  4. Palpate for knee effusion: "I'm going to feel for any fluid in your knee" (patellar tap test or bulge test)
  5. Ankles/Feet: "Point your toes down towards the floor" (tests ankle plantarflexion)
  6. "Now pull your toes up towards your nose" (tests ankle dorsiflexion)
  7. "I'm going to squeeze across your toes - tell me if this hurts" (squeeze across metatarsals - tests for MTP synovitis)

4. SPINE (S) - Spinal Movements

Say aloud: "I'm now examining the spine, looking at posture and testing movements in all directions."

Inspection (Patient Standing, Viewed from Behind):

  • Spinal alignment: scoliosis, kyphosis, lordosis
  • Shoulder and pelvic level
  • Muscle bulk and symmetry

Screening Movements (Patient Standing):

  1. Cervical Spine: "Bend your head forward and try to touch your chin to your chest" (flexion)
  2. "Look up at the ceiling" (extension)
  3. "Turn your head to look over your right shoulder, then your left" (rotation)
  4. "Tilt your ear towards your right shoulder, then your left" (lateral flexion)
  5. Lumbar Spine: "Bend forward and try to touch your toes, keeping your knees straight" (flexion - observe lumbar spine flattening)
  6. "Stand up straight and lean backwards" (extension)
  7. "Slide your hand down the side of your leg towards your knee, first right side, then left" (lateral flexion)

Palpation (if indicated by screening findings):

  • Palpate spinous processes for tenderness
  • Palpate paravertebral muscles for spasm
  • Palpate sacroiliac joints
GALS Examination Complete Statement: "To complete my GALS screening examination, I would test muscle power and reflexes if any neurological abnormality was suspected, measure blood pressure, and examine for extra-articular manifestations of rheumatic disease such as skin rashes, nail changes, eye inflammation, and lymphadenopathy."

B. DETAILED HANDS AND WRISTS EXAMINATION (Look, Feel, Move)

Clinical Context: Hand examination is critical for diagnosing rheumatoid arthritis, osteoarthritis, psoriatic arthritis, and gout. This is a HIGH-YIELD OSCE station in Australian exams.

Patient Position: Sitting comfortably, hands resting on pillow on examination table in front of them, examiner sitting opposite.

LOOK (Systematic Inspection - 60-90 seconds)
Say aloud: "I'm looking at both hands from the dorsal and palmar aspects, comparing both sides, looking for skin changes, swelling, deformity, and muscle wasting."

General Inspection:

  • Ask patient to place hands palms down on pillow (inspect dorsum first)
  • Then ask to turn palms up (inspect palmar aspect)
  • Compare both hands - symmetry of deformity suggests inflammatory arthritis

Skin:

  • Colour: erythema (inflammation), pallor (anaemia)
  • Rashes: psoriatic plaques (psoriatic arthritis)
  • Scars: surgical (joint replacement, tendon repair)
  • Rheumatoid nodules: firm subcutaneous nodules over extensor surfaces (elbows, fingers)
  • Gouty tophi: chalky deposits over joints, ear helix
  • Calcinosis: calcium deposits (systemic sclerosis)

Nails:

  • Psoriatic changes: pitting, onycholysis, oil-drop discolouration
  • Nail fold infarcts: vasculitis (SLE, rheumatoid arthritis)
  • Splinter haemorrhages: infective endocarditis, vasculitis

Joints (assess each joint from DIP to wrist):

  • DIP joints (Distal Interphalangeal):
    • Heberden's nodes (OA): bony swellings at DIP joints
    • DIP involvement rare in RA (if present, think psoriatic arthritis or OA)
  • PIP joints (Proximal Interphalangeal):
    • Bouchard's nodes (OA): bony swellings at PIP joints
    • Fusiform swelling (RA): soft tissue swelling giving "sausage finger" appearance
  • MCP joints (Metacarpophalangeal):
    • Swelling: boggy (synovitis in RA), bony (OA less common at MCP)
    • Subluxation: MCPs deviate ulnarly in advanced RA
  • Thumb:
    • CMC joint (base of thumb): "squared" appearance in OA
    • Z-thumb deformity (RA): hyperextension of IP, flexion of MCP
  • Wrist:
    • Dorsal swelling: rheumatoid synovitis
    • Ganglia: cystic swellings (benign)
    • Volar (palmar) swelling: assess for carpal tunnel syndrome

Deformities (specific to RA):

  • Ulnar deviation: MCPs deviate towards ulnar side (little finger)
  • Swan neck deformity: Hyperextension of PIP, flexion of DIP
  • Boutonni√®re deformity: Flexion of PIP, hyperextension of DIP
  • Z-thumb: Hyperextension of IP joint, flexion of MCP joint
  • Trigger finger: Finger stuck in flexion, clicks when extended

Muscle Wasting:

  • Thenar eminence (thumb base): median nerve palsy, carpal tunnel syndrome
  • Hypothenar eminence (little finger side): ulnar nerve palsy
  • Dorsal interossei (guttering between metacarpals): disuse atrophy in arthritis, ulnar nerve palsy
FEEL (Palpation - 60-90 seconds)
Say aloud: "I'm now going to feel the joints, checking for warmth, swelling, and tenderness. I'll be as gentle as possible - please tell me if anything hurts."

Temperature:

  • Use back of your hand to compare temperature
  • Feel both hands, then compare affected vs unaffected joints
  • Warmth suggests active inflammation (synovitis, infection, crystal arthropathy)

Systematic Palpation (each joint individually):

  1. DIP joints: Gently squeeze between your thumb and index finger - "Does this hurt?"
  2. PIP joints: Palpate each joint - feel for bony enlargement (OA) vs soft swelling (synovitis)
  3. MCP joints:
    • Palpate each MCP joint individually
    • MCP squeeze test: Gently compress across all MCPs simultaneously (2nd-5th) - "Does this hurt?"
      • Positive test (tenderness) = active synovitis (RA, other inflammatory arthritis)
      • High sensitivity for detecting early inflammatory arthritis
  4. First CMC joint (thumb base): Palpate with thumb and index finger - tender in OA
  5. Wrist joint:
    • Palpate radiocarpal joint (just distal to radius)
    • Feel for boggy swelling (synovitis)
    • Palpate ulnar styloid (often tender in RA)

Special Palpation Tests:

  • Anatomical snuffbox: Palpate between EPL and EPB tendons (scaphoid fracture tenderness)
  • Phalen's test: (See Move section - technically a movement test but assesses median nerve compression)
  • Tinel's sign: Tap over median nerve at wrist (carpal tunnel) - tingling in median nerve distribution = positive
MOVE (Active and Passive Movements - 90-120 seconds)
Say aloud: "I'm now going to assess the range of movement of the joints. I'll ask you to move your fingers and wrists, then I'll move them gently myself to check the full range."

Active Movements (Patient performs, you observe):

  1. Finger Extension: "Straighten your fingers out as far as they'll go" (tests extensor tendons, joint extension)
  2. Finger Flexion: "Make a tight fist" (tests flexor tendons, joint flexion)
  3. MCP Flexion: "Keep your fingers straight and bend them down from your knuckles" (isolates MCP movement)
  4. Thumb Opposition: "Touch your thumb to the tip of each finger in turn" (tests thumb CMC joint, median nerve function)
  5. Finger Abduction: "Spread your fingers wide apart" (tests dorsal interossei, ulnar nerve)
  6. Finger Adduction: "Now bring them back together" (tests palmar interossei, ulnar nerve)
  7. Wrist Extension: "Bend your wrist back, pointing your fingers to the ceiling" (normal 70-90¬∞)
  8. Wrist Flexion: "Now bend your wrist forward, pointing your fingers to the floor" (normal 80-90¬∞)
  9. Wrist Radial Deviation: "Move your wrist towards your thumb side" (normal 20¬∞)
  10. Wrist Ulnar Deviation: "Now towards your little finger side" (normal 30-40¬∞)

Passive Movements (You perform, assess range and end-feel):

  • Gently repeat each movement while supporting the joint
  • Compare passive with active range (if passive > active, suggests muscle/tendon problem; if both equally restricted, suggests joint problem)
  • Assess "end-feel":
    • Soft/springy = normal
    • Hard/bony = OA, bony block
    • Empty = pain stops movement before mechanical block (infection, fracture)

Functional Assessment (Power and Precision):

  1. Power Grip: "Squeeze my fingers as hard as you can" (tests grip strength - compare both sides)
  2. Precision Pinch (Key Pinch): "Hold this piece of paper between your thumb and side of your index finger - don't let me pull it away" (tests thumb adduction, ulnar nerve)
  3. Tip Pinch: "Hold this paper between your thumb and index finger tips" (tests median nerve, FDP function)

Special Tests for Wrist:

  • Phalen's Test (Carpal Tunnel Syndrome):
    • "Put the backs of your hands together, with your wrists fully flexed, and hold for 60 seconds"
    • Positive = tingling/numbness in median nerve distribution (thumb, index, middle, radial half of ring finger)
    • Sensitivity ~50-70%, specificity ~70-80%
  • Tinel's Test (Carpal Tunnel):
    • Tap over median nerve at wrist crease
    • Positive = tingling in median nerve distribution
  • Finkelstein's Test (De Quervain's Tenosynovitis):
    • "Tuck your thumb into your palm and close your fingers over it. Now bend your wrist down towards your little finger"
    • Positive = pain over radial styloid (thumb side of wrist)
To Complete Hand Examination:
"To complete my examination of the hands, I would:
  • Examine the elbows and shoulders (rheumatoid arthritis is polyarticular)
  • Perform a full neurological examination of the upper limbs if weakness or sensory symptoms are present
  • Look for extra-articular manifestations of rheumatoid arthritis: rheumatoid nodules, lung fibrosis, eye involvement
  • Request hand X-rays (AP and lateral) to assess for joint space narrowing, erosions, and deformity
  • Check inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) and rheumatoid factor/anti-CCP antibodies if inflammatory arthritis suspected
"

C. ELBOWS EXAMINATION (Look, Feel, Move)

Patient Position: Sitting or standing, arms initially by sides, then extended for examination.

LOOK
Say aloud: "I'm inspecting both elbows, comparing both sides, looking for swelling, deformity, and any skin changes."
  • Skin: Psoriatic plaques, rheumatoid nodules (over extensor surface), scars
  • Swelling: Olecranon bursitis (swelling over back of elbow), joint effusion
  • Deformity:
    • Carrying angle (normal 5-15¬∞ valgus in extension - "angle away from body")
    • Fixed flexion deformity (cannot fully extend - RA, OA, previous trauma)
    • Cubitus varus (decreased angle - malunited fracture)
    • Cubitus valgus (increased angle - can cause ulnar nerve compression)
  • Muscle Wasting: Triceps, biceps (disuse atrophy in chronic arthritis)
FEEL
Say aloud: "I'm going to feel around the elbow joint, checking for warmth, swelling, and tenderness."
  • Temperature: Use back of hand - compare both elbows
  • Bony Landmarks: Palpate medial epicondyle, lateral epicondyle, olecranon (form triangle when elbow flexed 90¬∞)
  • Olecranon Bursa: Palpate over olecranon for boggy swelling (bursitis - can be inflammatory or septic)
  • Joint Line: Palpate radiohumeral joint (lateral) for tenderness/effusion
  • Tenderness:
    • Lateral epicondyle - tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis)
    • Medial epicondyle - golfer's elbow (medial epicondylitis)
  • Rheumatoid Nodules: Firm, non-tender, subcutaneous - over extensor surface
MOVE
Say aloud: "I'm going to test the movements of your elbow. Please let me know if you experience any pain."

Active Movements:

  • Flexion: "Bend your elbow and try to touch your shoulder" (normal 150¬∞)
  • Extension: "Now straighten your arm completely" (normal 0¬∞ or 5-10¬∞ hyperextension)
  • Pronation: "Hold your arms out with elbows bent 90¬∞. Now turn your palms face down" (normal 80-90¬∞)
  • Supination: "Now turn your palms face up" (normal 90¬∞)

Passive Movements: Gently repeat each movement, supporting arm

Special Tests:

  • Test for Tennis Elbow:
    • "Keep your elbow straight, make a fist, and bend your wrist back. Now resist me as I try to push your hand down"
    • Positive = pain over lateral epicondyle (extensor tendon origin)
  • Test for Golfer's Elbow:
    • "Keep your elbow straight, make a fist, and bend your wrist forward. Now resist me as I try to pull your hand back"
    • Positive = pain over medial epicondyle (flexor tendon origin)

D. SHOULDERS EXAMINATION (Look, Feel, Move)

Patient Position: Sitting upright on edge of examination couch, upper body exposed (offer gown with back opening for female patients).

LOOK
Say aloud: "I'm inspecting both shoulders from the front, sides, and back, comparing both sides for symmetry, muscle bulk, and any deformity."

Inspection (from front, sides, and back):

  • Symmetry: Shoulder heights equal? (dropped shoulder in rotator cuff tear, brachial plexus injury)
  • Muscle Wasting:
    • Deltoid (axillary nerve injury, disuse)
    • Supraspinatus/infraspinatus (rotator cuff tear - seen from behind)
    • Pectoralis major (anterior - rare)
  • Scars: Surgical (arthroscopy portals, open repair, shoulder replacement)
  • Swelling: Joint effusion (rare - deep joint), acromioclavicular joint swelling
  • Deformity:
    • Anterior dislocation: "squared-off" shoulder, palpable humeral head anteriorly
    • AC joint separation: prominent distal clavicle ("step deformity")
    • Clavicle fracture: visible deformity, swelling
FEEL
Say aloud: "I'm going to palpate around the shoulder, feeling for tenderness and any structural abnormalities."

Systematic Palpation (from medial to lateral):

  1. Sternoclavicular Joint: Palpate medial end of clavicle - tenderness (arthritis, infection rare)
  2. Clavicle: Palpate full length - tenderness, step deformity (fracture)
  3. Acromioclavicular (AC) Joint: Palpate joint at lateral end of clavicle - tender (AC joint arthritis, separation)
  4. Acromion: Palpate bony point of shoulder
  5. Subacromial Space: Palpate just below acromion - tender (subacromial bursitis, impingement)
  6. Greater Tuberosity: Palpate lateral to acromion - rotator cuff insertion (tender in tendinopathy)
  7. Bicipital Groove: Palpate anterior shoulder, between greater and lesser tuberosities - tender (biceps tendinitis)
MOVE
Say aloud: "I'm now going to test shoulder movements. I'll ask you to perform certain movements, then I'll move your arm myself to check the passive range."

Active Movements (demonstrate each, then ask patient to copy):

  1. Abduction: "Lift your arms out to the sides and reach for the ceiling" (normal 180¬∞)
    • 0-90¬∞ = glenohumeral joint
    • 90-180¬∞ = scapulothoracic movement
    • Painful arc (60-120¬∞) = subacromial impingement/bursitis
    • Cannot initiate abduction = rotator cuff tear (supraspinatus)
  2. Forward Flexion: "Lift your arms straight up in front of you" (normal 180¬∞)
  3. Extension: "Move your arms backwards behind you" (normal 50-60¬∞)
  4. External Rotation: "Keep your elbows by your sides, bent to 90¬∞, then move your hands out to the sides" (normal 60-70¬∞)
  5. Internal Rotation: "Reach behind your back and try to touch your shoulder blades - how high can you reach?" (assess by vertebral level reached - normal: T6-T8)
    • Limited internal rotation = adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder), glenohumeral arthritis

Passive Movements:

  • Gently repeat each movement while supporting the arm
  • If passive ROM > active ROM = suggests muscle/tendon problem (rotator cuff tear)
  • If both equally restricted = suggests joint problem (frozen shoulder, arthritis)

Special Tests:

  1. Rotator Cuff Tests:
    • Supraspinatus Test (Empty Can Test):
      • "Hold your arms out in front at shoulder height, thumbs pointing down like you're emptying a can. Now resist me as I push down"
      • Positive = weakness or pain (supraspinatus tear/tendinopathy)
    • External Rotation (Infraspinatus/Teres Minor):
      • "Keep your elbows tucked by your sides, bent 90¬∞, and push your hands outwards against my resistance"
      • Weakness = infraspinatus/teres minor tear
    • Internal Rotation (Subscapularis - Lift Off Test):
      • "Place your hand behind your back. Now push your hand away from your back against my resistance"
      • Cannot perform = subscapularis tear
  2. Impingement Tests:
    • Neer's Test: Stabilise scapula, passively flex shoulder to maximum ‚Üí pain = impingement
    • Hawkins-Kennedy Test: Flex shoulder 90¬∞, flex elbow 90¬∞, then internally rotate shoulder ‚Üí pain = impingement
  3. Apprehension Test (Anterior Instability/Dislocation):
    • Patient supine or sitting, arm abducted 90¬∞, elbow flexed 90¬∞
    • Gently externally rotate shoulder
    • Positive = patient feels shoulder will dislocate (apprehension, resists further movement)
  4. AC Joint Stress Test:
    • Adduct arm across body (hand to opposite shoulder)
    • Positive = pain at AC joint (AC joint arthritis)

E. SPINE EXAMINATION (Cervical, Thoracic, Lumbar)

Patient Position: Standing initially (for inspection and movement), then lying prone or sitting for palpation.

Red Flags for Back Pain (Ask First): Trauma, fever, night pain, weight loss, history of cancer, age >50 or <20 years, neurological symptoms (weakness, bowel/bladder dysfunction, saddle anaesthesia) ‚Üí These require urgent investigation and careful examination.
LOOK (Patient Standing, Examined from Behind, Side, and Front)
Say aloud: "I'm inspecting the spine from behind, the side, and the front, looking at the normal curves, any deformity, and muscle bulk."

From Behind:

  • Skin: Scars (surgical), midline dimples (spina bifida occulta), hairy patches, caf√©-au-lait spots
  • Spinal Alignment:
    • Straight vertical line from occiput to natal cleft? (normal)
    • Scoliosis: Lateral curvature (C-shape or S-shape)
      • Structural vs functional (functional disappears when sitting/forward flexion)
  • Shoulder Level: Equal height? (unequal ‚Üí scoliosis, leg length discrepancy)
  • Pelvic Level: Iliac crests at same height? (unequal ‚Üí scoliosis, leg length discrepancy)
  • Muscle Bulk: Paravertebral muscles - wasting (chronic back pain, disuse), asymmetry

From Side:

  • Cervical Lordosis: Normal inward curve (loss ‚Üí muscle spasm, disc disease)
  • Thoracic Kyphosis: Normal outward curve (excessive ‚Üí osteoporosis, Scheuermann's disease)
  • Lumbar Lordosis: Normal inward curve (loss ‚Üí muscle spasm, "flattened" in acute disc prolapse; excessive ‚Üí pregnancy, obesity)
FEEL (Palpation)
Say aloud: "I'm going to feel along your spine, checking for any tender areas or muscle spasm. Please tell me if anything hurts."

Systematic Palpation (patient standing or sitting):

  1. Spinous Processes:
    • Palpate each spinous process from C7 (most prominent at base of neck) down to sacrum
    • Tenderness = fracture, infection, inflammation
    • Step deformity = spondylolisthesis (vertebra slipped forward)
  2. Paravertebral Muscles:
    • Palpate either side of spinous processes
    • Muscle spasm = protective response to pain (disc prolapse, facet joint pain)
  3. Sacroiliac Joints:
    • Palpate over posterior superior iliac spines (dimples of Venus)
    • Tenderness = sacroiliitis (ankylosing spondylitis, psoriatic arthritis)
MOVE
Say aloud: "I'm now going to assess spinal movements. I'll test the neck, mid-back, and lower back separately."

CERVICAL SPINE (Patient Sitting or Standing):

  1. Flexion: "Bend your head forward and try to touch your chin to your chest" (normal 50-60¬∞)
  2. Extension: "Now look up at the ceiling" (normal 50-75¬∞)
  3. Lateral Flexion: "Tilt your right ear towards your right shoulder, then left ear to left shoulder" (normal 40-45¬∞ each side)
  4. Rotation: "Turn your head to look over your right shoulder, then your left" (normal 70-90¬∞ each side)

THORACIC SPINE:

  • Primarily rotation (flexion/extension difficult to isolate)
  • Rotation: "Cross your arms over your chest, and twist your upper body to the right, then to the left" (normal ~40¬∞ each side)
  • Limited in ankylosing spondylitis

LUMBAR SPINE (Patient Standing):

  1. Flexion: "Keep your knees straight and bend forward to try to touch your toes"
    • Observe: lumbar lordosis should flatten/reverse (if remains ‚Üí muscle spasm, disc disease)
    • Measure: Schober's Test (more accurate):
      • Mark skin 10cm above and 5cm below a point level with posterior superior iliac spines
      • Ask patient to flex forward maximally
      • Re-measure distance between marks
      • Normal: increases from 15cm to >20cm (>5cm increase)
      • Reduced in ankylosing spondylitis (<5cm increase)
  2. Extension: "Stand up straight and lean backwards" (normal 20-30¬∞) - support patient as may lose balance
  3. Lateral Flexion: "Keep your legs straight and slide your hand down the side of your leg towards your knee, first right then left" (normal 20-30¬∞ each side)

Special Tests:

  1. Straight Leg Raise (SLR) - for Lumbar Disc Prolapse:
    • Patient supine, leg straight
    • Lift leg by heel, keeping knee straight
    • Positive = pain radiating down leg (sciatica) at <70¬∞ elevation
    • Pain in back only = not positive (tight hamstrings)
    • Dorsiflexing foot at point of pain exacerbates it (Bragard's test)
  2. Femoral Stretch Test (L2-L4 Nerve Roots):
    • Patient prone or lying on side
    • Flex knee, then extend hip (pull thigh backwards)
    • Positive = pain in anterior thigh (femoral nerve stretch)
  3. Sacroiliac Joint Tests:
    • FABER Test: Flexion, Abduction, External Rotation
      • Patient supine, place foot of affected side on opposite knee (figure-of-4 position)
      • Press down on flexed knee
      • Positive = pain in SI joint or groin (hip pathology)
    • Gaenslen's Test: Patient at edge of couch, pull one knee to chest, let other leg hang off edge ‚Üí pain = SI joint pathology
Neurological Examination (if radiculopathy suspected):
  • Test myotomes (muscle power): L2-S1 dermatomes
  • Test sensation: L2-S1 dermatomes
  • Reflexes: Knee (L3/4), ankle (S1)

F. HIPS EXAMINATION (Look, Feel, Move)

Patient Position: Lying supine on examination couch, wearing shorts/underwear.

Note: Hip pathology often presents as groin pain, but can also present as buttock pain, thigh pain, or referred knee pain. Always examine the hip if knee pain is present but knee examination normal.
LOOK
Say aloud: "I'm inspecting both lower limbs, comparing both sides for any asymmetry, muscle wasting, scars, or deformity."

General Inspection (Patient Supine, Legs Straight):

  • Posture: Legs in neutral position? Or externally rotated (hip fracture, OA)?
  • Scars:
    • Lateral (hip replacement - posterior approach)
    • Anterior (hip replacement - anterior approach, hip arthroscopy)
    • Groin (vascular surgery, femoral hernia repair)
  • Swelling: Rarely visible (deep joint)
  • Muscle Wasting:
    • Quadriceps (vastus medialis especially - atrophy from disuse)
    • Gluteal muscles (assess from behind when patient standing - atrophy in chronic hip disease)
  • Leg Length: Apparent shortening (pelvis tilted), true shortening (actual bone length difference)
FEEL
Say aloud: "I'm going to feel around your hip joint for any tenderness."
  • Temperature: Use back of hand (increased warmth = septic arthritis - rare)
  • Greater Trochanter: Palpate lateral prominence - tender (trochanteric bursitis)
  • Anterior Hip: Palpate below inguinal ligament, lateral to femoral pulse - tender (hip joint pathology, psoas bursitis)
MOVE
Say aloud: "I'm now going to move your hip through its range of motion. Please tell me if you experience any pain."

Active Movements (ask patient to perform):

  1. Flexion: "Bring your knee up towards your chest as far as it will go" (normal 120¬∞ with knee bent)
  2. Abduction: "Keep your leg straight and move it out to the side" (normal 45-50¬∞)
  3. Adduction: "Bring your leg across the midline" (normal 20-30¬∞)

Passive Movements (examiner performs):

  1. Flexion: Support thigh and leg, flex hip with knee bent (normal 120¬∞)
  2. Internal Rotation: Flex hip and knee to 90¬∞, then rotate foot outwards (hip rotates internally) - normal 30-40¬∞
    • Most sensitive test for hip OA - first movement to be lost
  3. External Rotation: Flex hip and knee to 90¬∞, rotate foot inwards (hip rotates externally) - normal 40-50¬∞
  4. Abduction: Stabilise pelvis with hand on opposite anterior superior iliac spine, abduct leg (normal 45-50¬∞)
  5. Adduction: Lift opposite leg, adduct affected leg underneath (normal 20-30¬∞)
  6. Extension: Patient prone or lying on side, extend hip backwards (normal 10-15¬∞) - or assessed during gait

Special Tests:

  1. Thomas Test (Fixed Flexion Deformity):
    • Patient supine, fully flex opposite (normal) hip and knee to chest (flattens lumbar lordosis)
    • Observe affected leg: if knee lifts off couch ‚Üí fixed flexion deformity present
    • Positive in hip OA, hip flexor contracture
  2. Trendelenburg Test (Hip Abductor Weakness - Gluteus Medius):
    • Patient standing, examiner behind
    • "Stand on your right leg, and lift your left leg off the ground" (examiner observes pelvis)
    • Normal: pelvis on lifted side stays level or rises (supported by gluteus medius of standing leg)
    • Positive Trendelenburg: pelvis on lifted side drops (abductor weakness on standing leg)
    • Repeat on other side
    • Causes: Hip OA, developmental dysplasia, superior gluteal nerve palsy, hip replacement with abductor damage
  3. Trendelenburg Gait:
    • Observe patient walking
    • Positive: trunk lurches towards affected side with each step (compensates for abductor weakness)
  4. Leg Length Measurement:
    • Apparent Leg Length: Measure from umbilicus to medial malleolus (includes pelvic tilt)
      • Different = pelvic tilt (scoliosis, abduction/adduction deformity)
    • True Leg Length: Measure from anterior superior iliac spine (ASIS) to medial malleolus (excludes pelvic tilt)
      • Different = actual bone length difference (fracture, developmental dysplasia)

G. KNEES EXAMINATION (Look, Feel, Move)

Patient Position: Supine on couch, both legs fully exposed, then sitting with legs over edge.

LOOK
Say aloud: "I'm inspecting both knees, comparing both sides for any swelling, deformity, muscle wasting, or scars."

Inspection (Patient Supine, Knees Extended):

  • Skin: Scars (arthroscopy portals, knee replacement, previous surgery), erythema (infection, gout)
  • Swelling:
    • Generalised (knee effusion - intra-articular)
    • Localised:
      • Pre-patellar bursitis (swelling over kneecap - "housemaid's knee")
      • Infra-patellar bursitis (below kneecap - "clergyman's knee")
      • Baker's cyst (popliteal fossa - see from behind)
  • Deformity:
    • Varus deformity: Bow-legged (medial compartment OA)
    • Valgus deformity: Knock-kneed (lateral compartment OA, rheumatoid arthritis)
    • Fixed flexion deformity: Cannot fully extend knee
  • Muscle Wasting: Quadriceps (especially vastus medialis obliquus - VMO) - occurs rapidly with knee pathology

Inspection from Behind (Patient Standing):

  • Popliteal fossa swelling (Baker's cyst)
  • Calf swelling (DVT if unilateral, ruptured Baker's cyst)
FEEL (Patient Supine)
Say aloud: "I'm going to feel around your knee, checking for warmth, any fluid, and tender areas."

Temperature: Back of hand - compare both knees (warmth = active synovitis, infection)

Effusion Tests (Testing for Fluid in Joint):

  1. Patellar Tap Test (Moderate to Large Effusion):
    • Empty suprapatellar pouch: Slide hand down thigh from 15cm above patella to top of patella (pushes fluid from pouch into joint)
    • With other hand, press sharply on patella with fingertips
    • Positive = patella "taps" against femur (bounces back) - indicates fluid between patella and femur
    • Negative = patella already resting on femur (no fluid, or very tense effusion)
  2. Bulge Test (Small Effusion):
    • Stroke fluid from medial side of knee up towards suprapatellar pouch
    • Then stroke down lateral side of knee
    • Positive = bulge of fluid appears on medial side (fluid moved from lateral to medial)
    • More sensitive than patellar tap for small effusions

Palpation for Tenderness:

  • Joint Line: Palpate medial and lateral joint lines (gaps between femur and tibia)
    • Tenderness = meniscal tear, OA
    • Medial more common (medial meniscus tear, medial compartment OA)
  • Tibial Tubercle: Palpate bony prominence below kneecap - tender (Osgood-Schlatter disease in adolescents, patellar tendonitis)
  • Patella: Palpate around edges - tender (patellofemoral syndrome)
  • Popliteal Fossa: Patient prone or knee flexed - palpate for Baker's cyst
MOVE
Say aloud: "I'm now going to test the movements and stability of your knee."

Active Movements:

  • Flexion: "Bend your knee and bring your heel towards your buttock" (normal 135-150¬∞)
  • Extension: "Straighten your leg completely" (normal 0¬∞ or 5-10¬∞ hyperextension)
    • Fixed flexion deformity = cannot reach 0¬∞ (OA, effusion, meniscal tear)

Passive Movements: Gently repeat flexion and extension

Special Tests:

1. Ligament Stability Tests (Patient Supine, Knee Flexed 90¬∞):

  • Anterior Drawer Test (ACL - Anterior Cruciate Ligament):
    • Sit on patient's foot to stabilise, grasp top of tibia with both hands
    • Pull tibia forward (towards you)
    • Positive = excessive forward movement of tibia on femur (ACL tear)
    • Compare with normal side
  • Posterior Drawer Test (PCL - Posterior Cruciate Ligament):
    • Same position, push tibia backwards
    • Positive = excessive backward movement (PCL tear - rare)
  • Lachman Test (ACL - More Sensitive than Anterior Drawer):
    • Knee flexed only 20-30¬∞ (not 90¬∞)
    • Stabilise femur with one hand, pull tibia forward with other
    • Positive = excessive anterior translation, soft end-feel (ACL tear)
  • Collateral Ligament Tests:
    • Medial (MCL): Knee in full extension (or slight flexion), apply valgus stress (push knee medially, ankle laterally)
      • Positive = excessive medial opening, pain (MCL tear)
    • Lateral (LCL): Apply varus stress (push knee laterally, ankle medially)
      • Positive = excessive lateral opening (LCL tear - rare)

2. Meniscal Tests:

  • McMurray's Test (Meniscal Tear):
    • Patient supine, fully flex knee
    • Hold heel with one hand, palpate joint line with other
    • For medial meniscus: Externally rotate tibia, then extend knee while applying valgus stress
    • For lateral meniscus: Internally rotate tibia, then extend knee while applying varus stress
    • Positive = palpable/audible click, pain (meniscal tear)
  • Apley's Grind Test:
    • Patient prone, knee flexed 90¬∞
    • Apply downward pressure on heel while rotating tibia
    • Positive = pain (meniscal tear)

3. Patellofemoral Tests:

  • Patellar Apprehension Test:
    • Knee extended, push patella laterally
    • Positive = patient apprehensive, resists (history of patellar dislocation)
  • Patellar Grind Test:
    • Press patella down into femur, ask patient to contract quadriceps
    • Positive = pain (patellofemoral syndrome, chondromalacia patellae)

H. FEET AND ANKLES EXAMINATION (Look, Feel, Move)

Patient Position: Supine initially, then standing for gait assessment.

LOOK
Say aloud: "I'm inspecting both feet and ankles, comparing both sides for deformity, swelling, and any skin changes."

Inspection (Examine from Top, Medial, Lateral, and Plantar Aspects):

  • Skin:
    • Calluses (abnormal pressure points - deformity, abnormal gait)
    • Ulcers (diabetic foot, peripheral vascular disease)
    • Scars (bunion surgery, ankle surgery)
    • Colour: Rubor (red - dependent in peripheral vascular disease), pallor
  • Nails: Psoriatic changes, fungal infection (onychomycosis)
  • Deformities:
    • Hallux Valgus (Bunion): Big toe deviates laterally, bunion forms over medial MTP joint
    • Hallux Rigidus: OA of 1st MTP joint - limited dorsiflexion
    • Claw Toes: Hyperextension of MTP, flexion of IP joints (RA, neurological disease)
    • Hammer Toes: Flexion of PIP joint, DIP extended
    • Pes Planus (Flat Feet): Loss of medial longitudinal arch
    • Pes Cavus (High Arched Feet): Exaggerated medial arch (neurological disease - Charcot-Marie-Tooth)
  • Swelling:
    • Ankle effusion (arthritis, trauma)
    • MTP swelling (RA typically involves MTP joints)
    • Generalised pedal oedema (cardiac failure, venous insufficiency)
  • Muscle Wasting: Small muscles of foot (neurological disease, chronic arthritis)
FEEL
Say aloud: "I'm going to feel your feet and ankles for any warmth or tenderness."
  • Temperature: Back of hand - warmth (active inflammation, infection, gout)
  • MTP Joints: Squeeze across MTPs - "Does this hurt?" (synovitis in RA)
    • Very sensitive test for inflammatory arthritis in feet
  • Ankle Joint: Palpate medial and lateral to tendons - feel for effusion, tenderness
  • Achilles Tendon: Palpate full length - tender (Achilles tendinopathy), nodular (xanthoma, rheumatoid nodule)
  • Plantar Fascia: Palpate heel - tender (plantar fasciitis)
  • Pulses:
    • Dorsalis pedis (dorsum of foot, lateral to extensor hallucis longus tendon)
    • Posterior tibial (behind medial malleolus)
    • Absent = peripheral vascular disease
MOVE
Say aloud: "I'm going to test the movements of your ankle and foot."

Ankle Movements:

  • Plantarflexion: "Point your toes down" (normal 40-50¬∞)
  • Dorsiflexion: "Pull your toes up towards you" (normal 15-20¬∞)

Subtalar Joint (Hindfoot) Movements:

  • Inversion: Rotate heel medially (normal 20-30¬∞)
  • Eversion: Rotate heel laterally (normal 10-20¬∞)

Midfoot Movements:

  • Inversion/Eversion: Stabilise heel, rotate forefoot

Toe Movements:

  • MTP Flexion/Extension: Test each toe, especially 1st MTP (hallux rigidus if limited)

Special Tests:

  • Simmond's Test (Achilles Tendon Rupture):
    • Patient prone or kneeling on chair, feet hanging over edge
    • Squeeze calf muscle
    • Normal = foot plantarflexes
    • Positive (no plantarflexion) = Achilles tendon rupture
  • Anterior Drawer Test (Ankle Instability):
    • Stabilise tibia, pull heel forward
    • Positive = excessive anterior movement (lateral ligament tear)
Gait Assessment:
  • Observe patient walking normally
  • Heel-toe pattern normal?
  • Antalgic gait (limping due to pain)
  • Foot drop (high-stepping gait - common peroneal nerve palsy)
To Complete Musculoskeletal Examination:
"To complete my musculoskeletal examination, I would:
  • Examine all other joint regions not yet assessed (full GALS screening if only performed regional examination)
  • Perform a focused neurological examination if weakness, sensory symptoms, or reflexes abnormalities are suspected
  • Assess function: Ask patient to perform specific tasks (grip objects, write, walk, climb stairs, dress)
  • Look for extra-articular manifestations of rheumatic disease:
    • Skin: Psoriatic plaques, photosensitive rash (SLE), scleroderma changes
    • Eyes: Dry eyes (Sj√∂gren's), uveitis (ankylosing spondylitis), conjunctivitis (reactive arthritis)
    • Lungs: Pulmonary fibrosis (RA, systemic sclerosis)
    • Heart: Pericarditis, valve disease (RA, SLE)
  • Request appropriate investigations:
    • X-rays of affected joints (erosions, joint space narrowing, osteophytes)
    • Blood tests: FBC, CRP, ESR, rheumatoid factor, anti-CCP, ANA, uric acid
    • Joint aspiration if effusion present (cell count, crystals, culture)
"

5. PRESENT (60-90 seconds)

Structured Presentation Format:

Example 1: Rheumatoid Arthritis
"This is Mrs. Jones, a 58-year-old woman who is alert and comfortable at rest. On examination of the musculoskeletal system, specifically examining the hands:

General Inspection: There is symmetrical deformity affecting both hands, consistent with inflammatory polyarthropathy.

Skin and Nails: No psoriatic plaques, nails normal. No rheumatoid nodules visible.

Joints: The MCP and PIP joints are swollen bilaterally with fusiform swelling. There is ulnar deviation at the MCP joints. I note swan neck deformities of the index and middle fingers bilaterally, and a boutonni√®re deformity of the left ring finger. The DIP joints are spared. The wrists show dorsal swelling.

Palpation: The joints are warm to touch. MCP squeeze test is positive bilaterally, indicating active synovitis. There is tenderness over the wrists and PIP joints.

Movement: There is restriction of flexion and extension at the MCPs and PIPs. Grip strength is reduced bilaterally. Wrist extension is limited to 45 degrees bilaterally (normal 70-90 degrees).

Function: She demonstrates difficulty with fine motor tasks - unable to oppose thumb to little finger on either hand.

Summary: In summary, this patient has symmetrical inflammatory polyarthropathy affecting the small joints of the hands with characteristic rheumatoid deformities, active synovitis, and functional impairment, consistent with active rheumatoid arthritis.

Further Assessment: To complete my examination, I would assess for extra-articular manifestations including rheumatoid nodules, pulmonary fibrosis, and eye involvement. I would request hand X-rays to assess for erosive changes, and check inflammatory markers, rheumatoid factor, and anti-CCP antibodies."

Example 2: Hip Osteoarthritis
"This is Mr. Smith, a 72-year-old gentleman who has an antalgic gait favouring his right leg. On examination of the right hip:

Look: The right leg is held in slight external rotation and appears shortened compared to the left. There is wasting of the right quadriceps muscle. I can see a lateral scar consistent with previous hip surgery.

Feel: There is no warmth over the hip. Tenderness is elicited on palpation of the anterior hip joint.

Move: Active and passive movements are globally restricted and painful. Internal rotation is particularly limited at 10 degrees (normal 30-40 degrees). Flexion is limited to 90 degrees (normal 120 degrees) with pain at the extremes of movement.

Special Tests: Thomas test is positive, demonstrating a 20-degree fixed flexion deformity. Trendelenburg test is positive on the right - when standing on the right leg, the left side of the pelvis drops, indicating right hip abductor weakness.

Summary: In summary, this gentleman has clinical features consistent with severe right hip osteoarthritis, including restricted range of movement (particularly internal rotation), fixed flexion deformity, antalgic gait, and positive Trendelenburg test. The surgical scar may indicate previous hip arthroscopy or other intervention.

Further Assessment: I would complete examination of the left hip for comparison, assess leg length discrepancy formally, examine the lumbar spine as hip pathology can present with back pain, and request anteroposterior pelvis and lateral hip X-rays to assess for joint space narrowing, osteophyte formation, and subchondral sclerosis."
Key Presentation Points:
  • Always present symmetrical findings (both sides compared)
  • Use anatomically precise language ("MCP joints" not "knuckles")
  • Distinguish active vs passive ROM limitations
  • Link clinical findings to likely diagnosis
  • Always state what you would do to complete the examination
  • Mention relevant investigations (X-rays, blood tests)

COMMON IMG MISTAKES ‚ùå

Learn from these common errors to excel in your OSCE:
  • ‚ùå Not asking about pain location BEFORE starting examination
    • ‚úÖ ALWAYS ask "Where is your pain?" first, then examine that area LAST (examine unaffected areas first to establish technique and avoid causing pain early)
    • Risk: Causing severe pain at start ‚Üí patient withdraws consent, examination incomplete, OSCE fail
  • ‚ùå Inadequate exposure
    • ‚úÖ Musculoskeletal examination requires FULL exposure - patient in undergarments or loose shorts/singlet
    • Cannot assess muscle wasting, deformity, swelling, or symmetry through clothing
    • Australian OSCE: Will be marked down significantly for examining through jeans/long sleeves
  • ‚ùå Not comparing both sides systematically
    • ‚úÖ ALWAYS examine both sides, even if symptoms are unilateral
    • Symmetry is critical diagnostic feature (RA = symmetrical, OA/psoriatic = often asymmetrical)
    • Need normal side as comparison for ROM, swelling, temperature
  • ‚ùå Forgetting "Look, Feel, Move" sequence
    • ‚úÖ ALWAYS follow this sequence for EVERY joint region
    • LOOK first (never touch before observing - you can't "un-see" swelling after palpating)
    • Australian OSCE: Examiners specifically mark whether you follow this structure
  • ‚ùå Moving joints roughly without warning patient
    • ‚úÖ ALWAYS warn before moving: "I'm going to move your hip now - let me know if this hurts"
    • ALWAYS watch patient's face during passive movements (facial grimace = pain even if patient doesn't verbalize)
    • Risk: Causing severe pain in inflamed/fractured joint ‚Üí patient complaint, exam fail
  • ‚ùå Not performing GALS screening when asked for "general MSK examination"
    • ‚úÖ If OSCE station says "examine this patient's musculoskeletal system" with no specific region ‚Üí perform GALS screening first
    • GALS is standard in Australian medical education and AMC exams
    • After GALS, if abnormality detected, state "I would now perform a detailed regional examination of [affected area]"
  • ‚ùå Examining only the symptomatic joint in inflammatory arthritis
    • ‚úÖ Rheumatoid arthritis, psoriatic arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis are POLYARTICULAR diseases
    • Must examine multiple joint regions to assess disease distribution
    • If patient presents with hand pain, must also examine elbows, shoulders (RA commonly affects all)
  • ‚ùå Not testing active AND passive movements
    • ‚úÖ Active movement tests muscle/tendon function
    • ‚úÖ Passive movement tests joint ROM and end-feel
    • Critical distinction: If passive > active ‚Üí muscle/tendon problem (rotator cuff tear); if both equal ‚Üí joint problem (OA, frozen shoulder)
  • ‚ùå Performing special tests incorrectly or unnecessarily
    • ‚úÖ Only perform special tests if clinically indicated by history/basic examination
    • ‚úÖ Know the correct technique (e.g., Lachman test knee at 20-30¬∞ NOT 90¬∞, McMurray requires full flexion first)
    • Incorrect technique ‚Üí false negative ‚Üí missed diagnosis
  • ‚ùå Using imprecise anatomical terms
    • ‚úÖ Say "MCP joints" not "knuckles"
    • ‚úÖ Say "DIP joints" not "finger tips"
    • ‚úÖ Say "ulnar deviation at MCPs" not "fingers pointing sideways"
    • Australian OSCE: Marks specifically allocated for correct anatomical terminology
  • ‚ùå Not explaining what you're doing during "silent" examination
    • ‚úÖ Use "Say aloud" phrases throughout: "I'm now testing internal rotation of the hip"
    • Helps examiner follow your structured approach
    • Shows patient you know what you're doing (improves rapport)
    • In real OSCE: Examiners may not give credit if they can't tell what you're assessing
  • ‚ùå Forgetting to assess function
    • ‚úÖ Joint examination is not complete without functional assessment
    • Hands: Test grip strength, precision pinch, ability to write/manipulate objects
    • Lower limb: Observe gait, ask patient to squat/stand from chair
    • Function matters more to patients than ROM measurements
  • ‚ùå Not looking for extra-articular manifestations of rheumatic disease
    • ‚úÖ Rheumatoid arthritis: Rheumatoid nodules, lung fibrosis, eye inflammation (scleritis, episcleritis)
    • ‚úÖ Psoriatic arthritis: Skin plaques, nail pitting
    • ‚úÖ Ankylosing spondylitis: Uveitis, aortic regurgitation
    • Comprehensive examination includes systemic assessment
  • ‚ùå Inadequate gait assessment
    • ‚úÖ Gait is critical part of lower limb MSK examination
    • Must observe from front, back, and side
    • Specific gaits: Antalgic (pain), Trendelenburg (hip abductor weakness), high-stepping (foot drop), ataxic (cerebellar)
    • In OSCE: If no space to walk, state "I would observe the patient's gait"
  • ‚ùå Not testing for effusions properly
    • ‚úÖ Knee effusion: Must perform BOTH patellar tap (large effusions) AND bulge test (small effusions)
    • Patellar tap alone will miss small effusions (50% of inflammatory arthritis presentations)
    • Technique matters: Must empty suprapatellar pouch first for patellar tap to work
  • ‚ùå Rushing through inspection phase
    • ‚úÖ LOOK is the most important phase - gives 70% of diagnostic information
    • Must systematically inspect: skin, nails, joints, muscle bulk, deformity, scars
    • Take 60-90 seconds for inspection - don't rush to palpation
    • In OSCE: Examiners give marks for each specific observation made

EXAMINATION MNEMONICS

GALS Screening Framework

G - Gait (observe walking)
A - Arms (upper limb screening movements)
L - Legs (lower limb screening movements)
S - Spine (cervical, thoracic, lumbar movements)

LOOK, FEEL, MOVE (Every Joint Examination)

LOOK: Skin, nails, joints (swelling, deformity), muscle wasting, scars
FEEL: Temperature, tenderness, swelling (bony vs soft), effusion
MOVE: Active (patient performs), Passive (examiner performs), Special tests

C - Cyanotic heart disease, Cirrhosis

Final OSCE Success Tips for MSK Examination:

  1. Clarify the task: "Would you like a GALS screening or detailed regional examination?"
  2. Ask about pain first: Examine painful area LAST
  3. Adequate exposure: Patient in undergarments or loose clothing - no examination through jeans!
  4. Always compare both sides: Even if symptoms unilateral
  5. Follow Look-Feel-Move religiously: LOOK before touching, never skip inspection
  6. Watch patient's face: During all palpation and movement - facial grimace = pain
  7. Active AND passive: Test both for every movement
  8. Use correct anatomical terms: MCP not knuckles, DIP not fingertips
  9. Say aloud what you're doing: Helps examiner follow your structured approach
  10. Assess function: Grip strength, gait, ability to perform ADLs
  11. Special tests only if indicated: Don't perform all tests on every patient
  12. Complete your examination: "To complete, I would examine other joints, assess for extra-articular features, request X-rays and inflammatory markers"
