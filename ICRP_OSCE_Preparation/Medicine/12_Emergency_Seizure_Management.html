Emergency Seizure Management

AMC Clinical / ICRP NSW Young Hospital Preparation

Purpose: AMC Clinical-style OSCE notes for seizure presentations and status epilepticus management Target Time: 8 minutes per station Format: Australian teaching hospital standard (ANZCOR, eTG 2024) Created: December 16, 2025
CRITICAL: Status Epilepticus = Medical Emergency
Definition: Seizure lasting >5 minutes OR recurrent seizures without full recovery of consciousness
Time is brain - immediate benzodiazepine treatment required within 5 minutes

STATUS EPILEPTICUS - EMERGENCY MANAGEMENT

Opening Statement (Emergency Scenario)

"I'm Dr [Name]. I can see this patient is having a seizure. Let me assess and manage this immediately. Can someone please help me?"

Immediate Assessment (DRABCD - Australian Resuscitation Council)

D - Danger: "Is the environment safe? Move objects away from the patient" R - Response: Check consciousness level A - Airway: Position patient on their side (recovery position), protect airway
  • DO NOT put anything in the mouth
  • DO NOT restrain the patient
B - Breathing: Check breathing, apply oxygen if available (target SpO‚ÇÇ >94%) C - Circulation: Check pulse, IV access if possible D - Disability: Check pupils, blood glucose level (BGL) - hypoglycaemia can cause seizures

Medical Management Timeline (ANZCOR Guideline 9.1.9)

TIME-CRITICAL PROTOCOL
Every minute counts in status epilepticus. Follow this structured timeline:

0-5 Minutes: First-Line Treatment

1. Benzodiazepine Administration (choose ONE route):
Route Medication & Dose Notes
IV access available Midazolam 10mg IV (or diazepam 10mg IV) FASTEST - preferred if IV access established
No IV access Midazolam 10mg IM (or buccal 10mg) Give into deltoid or lateral thigh
Pre-hospital Diazepam 10-20mg PR Last resort - less reliable absorption
2. Supportive Care:
  • Position on side (recovery position)
  • Oxygen via non-rebreather mask 15L/min (if SpO‚ÇÇ <94%)
  • Check BGL immediately - give glucose if hypoglycaemic
  • Establish IV access if not already done
  • Nil by mouth
3. Immediate Investigations:
  • Point-of-care BGL
  • Vital signs (BP, HR, RR, SpO‚ÇÇ, temperature)
  • ECG monitoring

5-10 Minutes: Second Benzodiazepine Dose

If seizure continues after 5 minutes:

  • Repeat benzodiazepine (same dose as above)
  • Call for senior help (MET call or Code Blue)
  • Consider thiamine 300mg IV (if alcohol abuse or malnutrition suspected)
  • Prepare for second-line antiepileptic drugs

10-30 Minutes: Second-Line Antiepileptic Drugs

If still seizing after 2 doses of benzodiazepines, give ONE of the following:

Drug Dose Administration Notes
Levetiracetam 60mg/kg IV (max 4.5g) Over 10-15 min PREFERRED - fewest interactions, safest in pregnancy
Phenytoin 20mg/kg IV (max 2g) Over 20-30 min Cardiac monitoring required, max rate 50mg/min
Valproate sodium 40mg/kg IV (max 3g) Over 10-15 min Avoid in women of childbearing age, liver disease
Critical Monitoring During IV Loading:
  • Continuous cardiac monitoring (phenytoin can cause arrhythmias)
  • Monitor BP (risk of hypotension)
  • Monitor respiratory rate (risk of respiratory depression)
  • Ensure resuscitation equipment ready

30+ Minutes: Refractory Status Epilepticus

If seizure continues >30 minutes = REFRACTORY STATUS EPILEPTICUS

  • URGENT ICU transfer
  • Intubation and ventilation required
  • Anaesthetic agents: Propofol or midazolam infusion or thiopentone
  • Continuous EEG monitoring
  • Neurology consultation MANDATORY

Investigations During/After Seizure

Immediate (Medicare bulk-billed):
  • Blood glucose - bedside, IMMEDIATE (hypoglycaemia causes seizures)
  • Bloods: FBC, UEC (sodium, calcium, magnesium), LFTs, glucose
  • Toxicology screen - if drug/alcohol intoxication suspected
  • Antiepileptic drug levels - if known epileptic (phenytoin, carbamazepine, valproate levels)
  • Pregnancy test - all women of childbearing age
  • ECG - check for arrhythmias
Further investigations (after stabilisation):
  • CT brain non-contrast - URGENT if:
    • First seizure
    • Focal neurological signs
    • Head trauma
    • Not returning to baseline consciousness
    • Persistent headache
  • Lumbar puncture - if meningitis/encephalitis suspected (fever, neck stiffness, altered consciousness)
  • MRI brain - if CT normal but high clinical suspicion (not urgent, can be outpatient)
  • EEG - for ongoing seizures or to characterise epilepsy type

Post-Seizure Care (Postictal Phase)

Monitoring:
  • Conscious state - may be drowsy for 30-60 minutes
  • Vital signs every 15 minutes until stable
  • Check for injuries sustained during seizure
  • Maintain lateral position until fully conscious
  • Aspiration precautions (nil by mouth until fully alert)
Documentation Required:
  • Time seizure started and stopped
  • Description of seizure (focal vs generalised, body parts involved)
  • Medications given (drug, dose, route, time)
  • Post-ictal state and recovery time
  • Any complications

FIRST SEIZURE - OUTPATIENT PRESENTATION

Opening Statement (8 minutes)

"Good morning, I'm Dr [Name]. I understand you had what sounds like a seizure for the first time. I'd like to ask you some questions to understand what happened and work out why this might have occurred. Is that okay?"

Focused History

Witness Account (CRUCIAL - seizures have amnesia):
  • "Who was with you when this happened? Can I speak with them?"
  • "Please describe exactly what you saw, from the beginning"
Before the Event (Pre-ictal):
  • "What were you doing just before it happened?"
  • "Did you have any warning signs?" (aura: strange smell, d√©j√† vu, rising epigastric sensation, visual changes)
  • "Any headache, fever, or feeling unwell beforehand?"
During the Event (Ictal):
  • "Did you lose consciousness? For how long?"
  • "What movements did you have?" (specific body parts vs whole body)
  • "Did your body go stiff first, then start jerking?" (tonic-clonic sequence)
  • "Did your eyes roll back or deviate to one side?"
  • "Did you bite your tongue? Which part?" (lateral tongue = seizure; tip = pseudoseizure)
  • "Did you lose bladder or bowel control?"
  • "How long did the shaking last?"
After the Event (Post-ictal):
  • "How did you feel when you woke up?"
  • "Were you confused? For how long?"
  • "Did you have a headache or muscle aches?"
  • "Did you sleep deeply afterwards?"
  • "Were you weak on one side of your body?" (Todd's paresis = post-seizure focal weakness, suggests focal onset)

Seizure Classification (Important for AMC OSCE)

Type Features Consciousness Example
Generalised Whole brain involved from onset Always impaired Tonic-clonic, absence, myoclonic
Focal (partial) One area of brain, may spread May be preserved or impaired Temporal lobe epilepsy, motor cortex
Focal ‚Üí bilateral tonic-clonic Starts focal, becomes generalised Eventually impaired Aura followed by generalised seizure

Generalised Tonic-Clonic Seizure (Grand Mal)

  • Tonic phase (10-30 sec): Sudden loss of consciousness, body stiffens, falls, may cry out, breathing stops
  • Clonic phase (30-60 sec): Rhythmic jerking of all limbs, jaw clenching, tongue biting, urinary incontinence
  • Post-ictal: Deep confusion, drowsiness, headache, muscle aches (lasts 15-60 minutes)

Focal Seizures

  • Focal aware: Consciousness preserved, may have motor (jerking one limb), sensory (tingling, visual), or autonomic symptoms
  • Focal impaired awareness: Altered consciousness, staring, lip-smacking, picking at clothes, wandering (automatisms)

Absence Seizures

  • Brief (5-10 sec) loss of awareness
  • Staring spell, stops mid-activity, no falling
  • Immediate return to normal (no post-ictal confusion)
  • Usually children/adolescents

Differential Diagnosis (Not All Collapses are Seizures!)

Condition Key Distinguishing Features
Epileptic Seizure Tonic-clonic movements, tongue biting (lateral), post-ictal confusion, incontinence
Syncope (Vasovagal) Preceded by lightheadedness, pallor, sweating; brief/absent jerking; rapid recovery (<1 min); situational trigger (standing, heat, pain)
Cardiac Syncope No warning, collapse during exertion, palpitations, family history sudden death, rapid recovery
Psychogenic Non-Epileptic Seizure (PNES) Eyes closed during event, pelvic thrusting, side-to-side head movements, prolonged duration (>2 min), no post-ictal confusion, tip of tongue biting, psychiatric history
Hypoglycaemia Diabetic, missed meal, sweating, confusion before event, rapid improvement with glucose
Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA) Sudden focal deficit (weakness, numbness, speech), no jerking, vascular risk factors, older age
Panic Attack Hyperventilation, anxiety, chest tightness, tingling, no loss of consciousness

Red Flags Suggesting Serious Underlying Cause

üö® RED FLAGS - Investigate Urgently:
  • Focal neurological signs (weakness, sensory loss, visual field defect)
  • Persistent headache or altered consciousness
  • Fever + neck stiffness (meningitis/encephalitis)
  • Head trauma
  • Age >55 with first seizure (higher risk of tumour/stroke)
  • Known cancer (brain metastases)
  • Anticoagulated (intracranial haemorrhage)
  • Pregnancy (eclampsia if pregnant, pre-eclampsia risk)

Aetiological History

Precipitants (VITAMINS mnemonic):
  • V - Vascular (stroke, haemorrhage, vasculitis)
  • I - Infection/Inflammation (meningitis, encephalitis, cerebral abscess)
  • T - Trauma (head injury, previous TBI)
  • A - Autoimmune (SLE, paraneoplastic)
  • M - Metabolic (hypoglycaemia, hyponatraemia, hypocalcaemia, uraemia)
  • I - Idiopathic/Inherited (genetic epilepsy syndromes)
  • N - Neoplasm (brain tumour, metastases)
  • S - Substances (alcohol withdrawal, drug toxicity/withdrawal, medications)
Specific Questions:
  • "Do you drink alcohol? Have you recently stopped or cut down?" (alcohol withdrawal)
  • "Do you use recreational drugs?" (cocaine, amphetamines)
  • "Have you had any recent head injuries?"
  • "Any recent infections or fevers?"
  • "Do you have diabetes? What was your blood sugar level at the time?"
  • "Any history of stroke, brain tumour, or meningitis?"
  • "Family history of epilepsy or seizures?"
Current Medications:
  • List all medications (some lower seizure threshold: tramadol, bupropion, clozapine, ciprofloxacin)
  • Any recent antibiotic or antidepressant changes?
Birth/Developmental History (if young patient):
  • Birth complications, neonatal seizures
  • Developmental delays
  • Febrile seizures as a child

Social Impact & Safety

Driving (CRITICAL for AMC OSCE - Australian Law):
Driving Restrictions After First Seizure (NSW/Most Australian States):
  • Private vehicle: MUST NOT drive for 6 months seizure-free (3 months if provoked seizure with removed trigger)
  • Commercial vehicle: MUST NOT drive for 12 months seizure-free, then requires specialist review
  • Doctor's legal obligation: Must inform patient of driving restrictions
  • Patient's legal obligation: Must inform insurance company and not drive during restriction period
  • Failure to comply = fines up to $3,300 and insurance void if accident occurs

Explain to patient: "I'm legally required to tell you that you cannot drive for at least 6 months after this seizure. This is for your safety and others on the road. You must inform your car insurance. When you're seizure-free for 6 months, we can reassess whether you can drive again. Do you understand this?"

Occupational Risks:
  • "What do you do for work?"
  • High-risk occupations: Working at heights, operating machinery, commercial driving, swimming instructor
  • May need workplace modifications or time off work
Safety at Home:
  • "Do you live alone? Who can stay with you for the next 24-48 hours?"
  • Avoid swimming alone, heights, operating heavy machinery
  • Supervise bathing/showering (risk of drowning if seizure occurs)

Investigations for First Seizure (eTG 2024)

Mandatory Investigations:
  • ECG - exclude cardiac syncope (long QT, Brugada syndrome, arrhythmia)
  • CT brain non-contrast - exclude structural lesion (tumour, stroke, haemorrhage)
    • URGENT if red flags present
    • Routine within 48 hours if uncomplicated first seizure
  • Bloods: FBC, UEC (sodium, calcium, magnesium), glucose, LFTs
  • Pregnancy test - all women of childbearing age (eclampsia risk)
Additional Investigations (depending on context):
  • MRI brain - more sensitive than CT, recommended if:
    • Young age (<55 years)
    • Focal seizure
    • CT normal but still suspicious for epilepsy
    • Can be done as outpatient
  • EEG - helps classify seizure type and syndrome
    • NOT diagnostic (normal EEG doesn't exclude epilepsy)
    • Useful for prognosis and treatment decisions
    • Can be outpatient
  • Lumbar puncture - only if infection suspected
  • Toxicology screen - if drug/alcohol suspected

Management of First Seizure (eTG 2024)

When to Start Antiepileptic Drugs (AEDs)?

Generally DO NOT start AEDs after single unprovoked seizure unless:

  • Structural brain lesion on imaging (tumour, old stroke, trauma)
  • Abnormal EEG with epileptiform activity
  • Second seizure occurs
  • Patient occupation/lifestyle makes recurrence very high risk (e.g., commercial pilot)
  • Patient strongly prefers treatment after discussion of risks/benefits

Recurrence risk after first unprovoked seizure: ~40-50% within 2 years

If AEDs indicated, first-line options:
Drug Starting Dose Maintenance Dose Notes
Levetiracetam 500mg BD 500-1500mg BD First choice - minimal interactions, no blood monitoring, safe in pregnancy (Category B2)
Lamotrigine 25mg daily √ó 2 weeks 100-200mg BD Slow titration to avoid rash; good for focal and generalised
Valproate sodium 200mg BD 400-1000mg BD Broad spectrum but AVOID in women of childbearing potential (Category D - teratogenic)
Carbamazepine 100mg BD 400-600mg BD Good for focal seizures; many drug interactions
PBS Requirements (Australian Context):
  • Most antiepileptic drugs are PBS-subsidised (~$31.60 per script for general patients, $7.70 for concession)
  • Requires diagnosis of epilepsy or documented seizures
  • Initial prescription often needs neurologist or specialist endorsement
Provoked Seizure:

If clear precipitant identified and removed, may NOT need long-term AEDs:

  • Alcohol withdrawal - treat withdrawal, alcohol cessation counselling
  • Severe hypoglycaemia - optimise diabetes management
  • Hyponatraemia - correct sodium carefully (risk of osmotic demyelination)
  • Drug toxicity - cease offending drug
  • Acute infection (meningitis) - treat infection

Driving restriction: 3 months (not 6) if provoked seizure with removed trigger

Referral & Follow-up

Neurology Referral (Mandatory):
  • ALL first seizures should be discussed with or referred to neurology
  • Urgent if red flags present
  • Routine referral within 2-4 weeks for first uncomplicated seizure
  • Neurology will decide on AED initiation, EEG, further imaging
GP Follow-up:
  • Review within 1 week
  • Ensure imaging completed
  • Reinforce driving restrictions
  • Safety advice
  • Provide written information

Safety-Netting

"Please call 000 or return to Emergency Department immediately if:

  • You have another seizure lasting more than 5 minutes
  • You have repeated seizures without full recovery in between
  • You develop severe headache, fever, neck stiffness, or persistent confusion
  • You develop weakness or numbness on one side of your body
  • You have any concerns at all

If you have a brief seizure that stops on its own:

  • Ensure you're safe and not injured
  • Contact your GP or neurology team the same day
  • Do NOT drive - remember the 6-month rule

Common IMG Mistakes

  1. ‚ùå Not getting witness account - Patients have amnesia for seizures, witnesses are ESSENTIAL
  2. ‚ùå Assuming all collapses with jerking are seizures - Syncope can have brief myoclonic jerks; ask about duration, post-ictal confusion
  3. ‚ùå Not asking about lateral tongue biting - Highly specific for epileptic seizure (vs tip biting in pseudoseizure)
  4. ‚ùå Forgetting to mention driving restrictions - Legal requirement to inform patient; AMC examiners will fail you
  5. ‚ùå Saying "6 months no driving" without explaining it's from LAST seizure - Clock resets with each seizure
  6. ‚ùå Not differentiating NSW vs other states - Driving laws vary slightly; know NSW rules (6 months private, 12 months commercial)
  7. ‚ùå Starting AEDs immediately after first seizure - Usually observe unless specific indications (see above)
  8. ‚ùå Using phenytoin for first-line treatment - Levetiracetam is preferred in Australia now (eTG 2024)
  9. ‚ùå Not asking about pregnancy/contraception in women - Valproate is Category D (teratogenic); many AEDs reduce OCP efficacy
  10. ‚ùå Saying "epilepsy" after one seizure - Epilepsy = ‚â•2 unprovoked seizures; one seizure ‚â† epilepsy diagnosis
  11. ‚ùå Not explaining what to do if another seizure occurs - First aid for seizures (timing, safety, when to call ambulance)
  12. ‚ùå Using US drug names - "Keppra" is acceptable but say levetiracetam; avoid "Depakote" (use valproate sodium)
  13. ‚ùå Forgetting to check BGL - Hypoglycaemia is easily reversible cause of seizures
  14. ‚ùå Not asking about alcohol - Alcohol withdrawal is common cause of seizures in ED
  15. ‚ùå Ordering MRI as first imaging - CT brain first for acute assessment; MRI is for subacute/outpatient

AMC OSCE PRACTICE SCENARIOS

Scenario 1: Status Epilepticus Management

Station Brief:
A 34-year-old man presents to ED with a witnessed tonic-clonic seizure that started 3 minutes ago and is still ongoing. Manage this patient appropriately. A nurse is available to assist.
Expected Actions (8 minutes):
  1. Introduce & assess situation (30 sec):
    • "I'm Dr [Name]. I can see this patient is having a seizure. How long has this been going on?"
    • "Let me assess him immediately"
  2. DRABCD assessment (1 min):
    • Danger: "Move objects away, protect patient"
    • Response: Check consciousness
    • Airway: "Position on side, protect airway"
    • Breathing: "Apply oxygen 15L via non-rebreather mask"
    • Circulation: "Check pulse, establish IV access"
    • Disability: "Check pupils, check BGL urgently"
  3. First-line treatment (1 min):
    • "This seizure has been going for more than 5 minutes, so this is status epilepticus - a medical emergency"
    • "Please give midazolam 10mg IV now" OR "If no IV access, give midazolam 10mg IM into deltoid"
    • "Start the clock - we'll reassess in 5 minutes"
  4. Call for help (30 sec):
    • "Please call for senior help - we may need MET call"
    • "Ensure resuscitation equipment is ready"
  5. Immediate investigations (1 min):
    • "Check BGL immediately - give IV glucose if hypoglycaemic"
    • "Send bloods: FBC, UEC, LFTs, glucose, toxicology screen, antiepileptic drug levels if known epileptic"
    • "ECG and continuous monitoring"
  6. Second-line if seizure continues (2 min):
    • "The seizure has now lasted 10 minutes despite benzodiazepines"
    • "I'll give levetiracetam 60mg/kg IV - that's approximately 4.5g for this patient"
    • "Give over 10-15 minutes with cardiac monitoring"
    • "Prepare for possible intubation if this doesn't work"
  7. Disposition & monitoring (1 min):
    • "Once seizure stops, patient needs admission for observation"
    • "Monitor conscious state, vital signs every 15 minutes"
    • "CT brain once stable"
    • "Neurology consultation"
  8. Documentation (1 min):
    • "Document seizure start and stop times, medications given, response"
Key Points for Marks:
  • Recognise status epilepticus definition (>5 minutes)
  • DRABCD assessment
  • Benzodiazepine within 5 minutes
  • Call for help
  • Check BGL
  • Second-line AED if continues (levetiracetam preferred)
  • Timeline awareness (5-10-30 minutes)

Scenario 2: First Seizure Counselling

Station Brief:
Sarah Thompson, a 28-year-old teacher, had her first witnessed tonic-clonic seizure 2 hours ago. She has now recovered and her CT brain is normal. Counsel her about the diagnosis, investigations, and management plan. Address her concerns about driving and work.
Expected Approach (8 minutes):
  1. Introduction & rapport (1 min):
    • "Hello Sarah, I'm Dr [Name]. How are you feeling now?"
    • "I understand you've had a seizure today - that must have been frightening. Can you tell me what you remember?"
  2. Explain what happened (1 min):
    • "From the description from your friend, you had what we call a generalised tonic-clonic seizure"
    • "This means there was abnormal electrical activity in your brain causing your body to shake"
    • "The good news is the seizure has stopped and your CT scan is normal"
  3. Investigations & diagnosis (1.5 min):
    • "We've done blood tests and a CT scan, which are normal"
    • "I'm going to refer you to a neurologist who may arrange an MRI and EEG - these give more detailed information"
    • "One seizure doesn't necessarily mean you have epilepsy - we need to find out why it happened"
    • "Epilepsy is diagnosed when someone has two or more unprovoked seizures"
  4. Treatment discussion (1 min):
    • "For now, we won't start medication after just one seizure"
    • "The risk of another seizure in the next 2 years is about 40-50%"
    • "The neurologist will discuss whether medication is needed after they see the MRI and EEG results"
  5. Driving restrictions (CRITICAL) (1.5 min):
    • "Sarah, I need to tell you something very important about driving"
    • "By law, you cannot drive for 6 months from today's seizure"
    • "This is for your safety - if you had another seizure while driving, it could be fatal for you or others"
    • "You must inform your car insurance company about this"
    • "If you're seizure-free for 6 months, the neurologist can reassess whether you can drive again"
    • "Do you drive to work? We may need to organise alternative transport or work from home arrangements"
  6. Work & safety (1 min):
    • "You're a teacher - are there any situations at work where a seizure could be dangerous?" (e.g., supervising swimming, climbing ladders)
    • "You should inform your employer so they can do a risk assessment"
    • "Most people can continue working with some adjustments"
    • "Avoid heights, swimming alone, and operating heavy machinery until cleared by neurology"
  7. Safety advice & follow-up (1 min):
    • "For the next 24-48 hours, make sure someone is with you"
    • "Avoid hot baths - have showers with someone nearby"
    • "If you have another seizure, time how long it lasts"
    • "If it lasts more than 5 minutes or you have repeated seizures, call 000"
    • "I'll arrange neurology follow-up within 2-4 weeks and GP appointment within 1 week"
  8. Address concerns & ICE (1 min):
    • "What concerns do you have about this?" (Ideas, Concerns, Expectations)
    • "Do you have any questions?"
    • "I'll give you some written information to take home"
Key Points for Marks:
  • Empathy and rapport
  • Explain difference between seizure and epilepsy
  • Not starting AEDs after first seizure (usual practice)
  • MUST mention 6-month driving ban (legal requirement)
  • MUST tell patient to inform insurance
  • Occupational impact and safety advice
  • Appropriate referral to neurology
  • Safety-netting for recurrent seizures

SUMMARY: AMC OSCE ESSENTIALS

Must-Know Points for AMC Clinical Exam:
  1. Status epilepticus = seizure >5 minutes = EMERGENCY
    • Benzodiazepine within 5 minutes (midazolam 10mg IV/IM preferred)
    • Second-line: levetiracetam 60mg/kg IV (preferred over phenytoin in 2024)
    • ANZCOR timeline: 0-5 min, 5-10 min, 10-30 min, 30+ min ICU
  2. First seizure workup:
    • Witness account essential (patient has amnesia)
    • Distinguish seizure from syncope (tongue biting, post-ictal confusion, incontinence)
    • CT brain + bloods + ECG mandatory
    • Neurology referral ALL first seizures
  3. Driving restrictions (NSW):
    • 6 months seizure-free for private vehicle
    • 3 months if provoked seizure with removed trigger
    • 12 months for commercial vehicle
    • MUST inform patient (legal requirement) and insurance
  4. Treatment:
    • Usually DO NOT start AEDs after single unprovoked seizure
    • If AED needed: levetiracetam first-line (minimal interactions, safe pregnancy Category B2)
    • AVOID valproate in women of childbearing age (Category D teratogenic)
  5. Safety advice:
    • No swimming alone, avoid heights, supervise bathing
    • Inform employer if occupation has risks
    • 24-48 hour supervision post-seizure
  6. Terminology:
    • Generalised vs focal (not grand mal/partial)
    • Emergency Department (not ER)
    • GP (not PCP)
    • Levetiracetam (Keppra acceptable), valproate sodium (not Depakote)
