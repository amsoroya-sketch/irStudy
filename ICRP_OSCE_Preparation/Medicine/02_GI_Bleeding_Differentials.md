# Gastrointestinal Bleeding: Differential Diagnosis and Management
## ICRP OSCE Preparation - Medicine Module (Gastroenterology)

---

## Target Audience

This guide is for **International Medical Graduates (IMGs)** preparing for the **AMC Clinical OSCE**, specifically:

- IMGs requiring systematic approach to upper and lower GI bleeding
- Candidates needing risk stratification tools (Glasgow-Blatchford, Rockall scores)
- Doctors preparing for ICRP placement in Australian Emergency Departments
- Medical professionals needing resuscitation protocols for acute GI bleeding
- Clinicians requiring Australian endoscopy and management guidelines (eTG 2024)

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1. **Differentiate** upper GI bleeding from lower GI bleeding based on presentation
2. **Generate** differential diagnoses for UGIB and LGIB from first presentation
3. **Risk stratify** patients using validated scores (Glasgow-Blatchford, Rockall)
4. **Resuscitate** patients with massive GI bleeding (massive transfusion protocol)
5. **Determine** timing of endoscopy (urgent vs routine)
6. **Manage** common causes according to Australian guidelines (eTG 2024)
7. **Identify** when to call surgery/interventional radiology
8. **Perform** confidently in OSCE stations for GI bleeding presentations

## Dr. Amir Soufi's Approach Applied to GI Bleeding

### Core Principle: LOCALIZE (Upper vs Lower) → RISK STRATIFY → RESUSCITATE → INVESTIGATE

**Traditional Approach (Less Effective)**:
- Generic history for all GI bleeding
- Consider diagnosis after full workup
- Delayed risk stratification
- Conservative resuscitation

**Dr. Soufi's Differential-Driven Approach**:

1. **LOCALIZE IMMEDIATELY** (Upper vs Lower GI bleeding):
   - **Upper GI** (proximal to ligament of Treitz): Hematemesis, coffee-ground vomiting, melena
   - **Lower GI** (distal to ligament of Treitz): Fresh red blood PR, maroon stools
   - **Note**: Massive UGIB can present with red blood PR (rapid transit)

2. **DIFFERENTIAL FROM PRESENTATION**:
   - **UGIB + hemodynamic instability** → Variceal bleed, peptic ulcer with visible vessel
   - **UGIB + epigastric pain** → Peptic ulcer
   - **UGIB + vomiting then hematemesis** → Mallory-Weiss tear
   - **LGIB + pain** → Ischemic colitis, IBD, diverticulitis
   - **LGIB painless** → Diverticular bleed, angiodysplasia, hemorrhoids

3. **RISK STRATIFY IMMEDIATELY** (Glasgow-Blatchford Score - GBS):
   - **GBS = 0**: Safe for outpatient management
   - **GBS ≥1**: Needs admission, intervention likely
   - Guides need for urgent endoscopy, ICU admission, transfusion

4. **RESUSCITATE AGGRESSIVELY** (if unstable):
   - **Two large-bore IV cannulae** (16G or larger)
   - **Massive transfusion protocol** if shock (blood products 1:1:1 ratio)
   - **Permissive hypotension** initially (SBP 90-100) until bleeding controlled
   - **Reverse anticoagulation** (if on warfarin, DOAC)

5. **TARGETED INVESTIGATION**:
   - **URGENT endoscopy** (<24 hours) if high-risk (hemodynamic instability, high GBS)
   - **Routine endoscopy** if stable, low-risk
   - **CT angiogram** if massive ongoing bleed and endoscopy non-diagnostic

6. **DEFINITIVE MANAGEMENT**:
   - Endoscopic therapy (clips, adrenaline injection, thermal coagulation)
   - Surgery if endoscopic failure
   - Interventional radiology (embolization) if surgical risk high

**Example**:
- **Presentation**: 65-year-old man, vomited fresh red blood (hematemesis), BP 85/50, HR 120, history of NSAIDs for arthritis
- **Immediate thought**: **Upper GI bleeding** (hematemesis), likely **peptic ulcer** (NSAID use), **HIGH RISK** (hemodynamic instability)
- **Immediate actions**: Resuscitate (2x large IV cannulae, crossmatch 6 units, massive transfusion if ongoing bleeding), calculate GBS (high - needs urgent intervention), **URGENT gastroscopy** (<24 hours, ideally <12 hours)
- **Differential**: Peptic ulcer (most likely), gastric erosions, esophagitis, Mallory-Weiss (but no vomiting before hematemesis)

## 1. UPPER GI BLEEDING (UGIB)

### Definition

Bleeding from source **proximal to ligament of Treitz** (duodenojejunal junction):
- Esophagus
- Stomach
- Duodenum

---

### Clinical Presentation

**Hematemesis**: Vomiting blood
- **Fresh red blood** (active bleeding, large volume)
- **Coffee-ground vomitus** (blood exposed to gastric acid, less active bleeding)

**Melena**: Black, tarry, foul-smelling stools
- Represents digested blood (usually >50-100ml blood loss)
- Takes 8-24 hours for blood to transit to rectum
- **Classic UGIB presentation**

**Hematochezia**: Fresh red blood per rectum
- **Usually LGIB**, BUT can be massive UGIB with rapid transit
- If hemodynamically unstable + fresh red blood PR → Consider massive UGIB (rule out with upper endoscopy)

**Other presentations**:
- Syncope, dizziness (volume loss)
- Dyspnea, chest pain (anemia, cardiac ischemia)
- Confusion (elderly - hypovolemia)

---

### Differential Diagnosis for UGIB

**Common Causes (Account for >90% of UGIB)**:

1. **Peptic Ulcer Disease** (35-50% of UGIB):
   - **Duodenal ulcer** (most common site)
   - **Gastric ulcer**
   - **Risk factors**: NSAIDs, aspirin, H. pylori, steroids, smoking, alcohol, stress (ICU patients)
   - **Presentation**: Hematemesis, melena, epigastric pain (may be absent in NSAID-induced), anemia
   - **High-risk features**: Age >60, shock, comorbidities, anticoagulation
   - **Endoscopy**: Ulcer with active bleeding (spurting/oozing), visible vessel, adherent clot
   - **Management**: Endoscopic therapy (adrenaline injection + clips/thermal coagulation), high-dose PPI (esomeprazole 80mg IV bolus then 8mg/hr infusion x 72 hours), H. pylori eradication if positive, stop NSAIDs

2. **Variceal Bleeding** (Esophageal or Gastric Varices) (15-20% of UGIB):
   - **Cause**: Portal hypertension from liver cirrhosis (alcohol, viral hepatitis, NAFLD)
   - **Presentation**: Large volume hematemesis, known cirrhosis, stigmata of chronic liver disease (jaundice, spider nevi, ascites, splenomegaly, palmar erythema)
   - **HIGH MORTALITY** (15-20%)
   - **Investigations**: FBC (thrombocytopenia - hypersplenism), LFTs (deranged), coagulation (elevated INR - liver dysfunction)
   - **Management**:
     - **Resuscitation**: Cautious fluids (over-resuscitation worsens portal pressure and re-bleeding)
     - **Vasoactive drugs**: **Octreotide** 50mcg bolus then 50mcg/hr infusion (reduces portal pressure) - start BEFORE endoscopy
     - **Antibiotics**: Ceftriaxone 1g IV daily (reduces infection, mortality in cirrhotic patients) - start on admission
     - **URGENT endoscopy** (<12 hours): Band ligation (esophageal varices) or tissue glue (gastric varices)
     - **Sengstaken-Blakemore tube** (balloon tamponade) if uncontrolled bleeding (temporary measure)
     - **TIPSS** (Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt) if endoscopy fails

3. **Mallory-Weiss Tear** (5-15% of UGIB):
   - **Cause**: Mucosal tear at gastroesophageal junction from forceful vomiting/retching
   - **Classic history**: Vomiting (non-bloody) → hematemesis (blood after initial vomits)
   - **Associated**: Alcohol binge, hyperemesis gravidarum, eating disorders
   - **Usually self-limiting** (90% stop bleeding spontaneously)
   - **Endoscopy**: Linear mucosal tear at GE junction
   - **Management**: Usually supportive, endoscopic therapy if active bleeding

4. **Erosive Gastritis / Esophagitis** (10-15%):
   - **Causes**: NSAIDs, alcohol, stress (ICU), H. pylori
   - **Presentation**: Usually minor bleeding (coffee-ground vomitus, melena), epigastric discomfort
   - **Management**: PPI, stop NSAIDs/alcohol, supportive

5. **Esophageal / Gastric Malignancy** (2-5%):
   - **Presentation**: Hematemesis, melena, weight loss, dysphagia, anemia
   - **Alarm symptoms**: Age >55, weight loss, dysphagia, persistent vomiting, early satiety
   - **Endoscopy**: Ulcerated mass
   - **Management**: Endoscopic hemostasis, oncology referral, palliative if advanced

6. **Dieulafoy Lesion** (1-2%):
   - **Pathology**: Aberrant submucosal artery that erodes through mucosa (usually stomach)
   - **Presentation**: Massive hematemesis, no preceding symptoms, difficult to find on endoscopy (small lesion)
   - **Management**: Endoscopic therapy (clips, thermal), surgery if fails

7. **Aortoenteric Fistula** (Rare but LETHAL):
   - **Cause**: Communication between aorta and duodenum
   - **Risk factors**: Previous aortic graft surgery, AAA
   - **Presentation**: "Herald bleed" (small UGIB) followed hours-days later by massive exsanguinating hemorrhage
   - **High index of suspicion** in patient with aortic graft + any GI bleeding
   - **Management**: **URGENT vascular surgery**

---

### Glasgow-Blatchford Score (GBS) - Risk Stratification for UGIB

**Purpose**: Predicts need for intervention (endoscopy, transfusion, surgery) and safe discharge

**Score 0 = Safe for outpatient management** (no intervention needed, can discharge from ED)

**Score ≥1 = Needs admission, likely intervention**

**Components**:

| Parameter | Value | Score |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| **Blood urea (mmol/L)** | ≥6.5 to <8.0 | 2 |
| | ≥8.0 to <10.0 | 3 |
| | ≥10.0 to <25.0 | 4 |
| | ≥25.0 | 6 |
| **Hemoglobin (g/L) - Men** | ≥120 to <130 | 1 |
| | ≥100 to <120 | 3 |
| | <100 | 6 |
| **Hemoglobin (g/L) - Women** | ≥100 to <120 | 1 |
| | <100 | 6 |
| **Systolic BP (mmHg)** | 100-109 | 1 |
| | 90-99 | 2 |
| | <90 | 3 |
| **Heart rate (bpm)** | ≥100 | 1 |
| **Melena** | Present | 1 |
| **Syncope** | Present | 2 |
| **Hepatic disease** | Present | 2 |
| **Cardiac failure** | Present | 2 |

**Interpretation**:
- **GBS 0**: Low risk - safe for outpatient endoscopy, discharge with follow-up
- **GBS 1-5**: Moderate risk - admit, likely needs endoscopy
- **GBS 6-11**: High risk - urgent intervention likely (endoscopy, transfusion)
- **GBS ≥12**: Very high risk - ICU, urgent endoscopy

**CRITICAL**: GBS performed **at presentation** guides initial management

---

### Rockall Score - Predicts Mortality and Re-bleeding (Post-Endoscopy)

**Full Rockall Score** (includes endoscopy findings):

| Variable | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|----------|---|---|---|---|
| **Age** | <60 | 60-79 | ≥80 | - |
| **Shock** | No shock (SBP ≥100, HR <100) | Tachycardia (HR ≥100) | Hypotension (SBP <100) | - |
| **Comorbidity** | None | - | Heart failure, IHD, major comorbidity | Renal/liver failure, metastatic cancer |
| **Diagnosis** | Mallory-Weiss, no lesion | All other diagnoses | GI malignancy | - |
| **Stigmata of bleeding** | None, dark spot | - | Blood, adherent clot, visible vessel | Active bleeding |

**Score**:
- **0-2**: Low risk (mortality <5%, re-bleed <5%)
- **3-4**: Moderate risk
- **5-7**: High risk (mortality >10%, re-bleed >10%)
- **≥8**: Very high risk (mortality >40%)

---

### Management of UGIB

#### Resuscitation (ABCDE Approach)

**A - Airway**:
- **Protect airway** if massive hematemesis, reduced GCS
- **Intubation** if hemodynamic instability + ongoing hematemesis (risk of aspiration)

**B - Breathing**:
- Oxygen if hypoxic (target SpO2 94-98%)

**C - Circulation**:

**IV Access**:
- **Two large-bore IV cannulae** (14G or 16G) - peripherally
- **Central line** if difficult IV access, massive transfusion, vasopressors needed

**Bloods**:
- **FBC**: Hemoglobin (may be normal acutely before hemodilution), platelets
- **Coagulation** (INR, APTT): If on anticoagulation, liver disease
- **UEC**: Urea (elevated in UGIB - protein digestion), creatinine (renal function)
- **LFTs**: Liver disease (varices?)
- **Lactate**: Tissue perfusion
- **Group and crossmatch**: **6 units** if severe bleeding

**Fluid Resuscitation**:
- **Target**: SBP 90-100 mmHg initially (**permissive hypotension**) until bleeding controlled
  - Rationale: Over-resuscitation increases bleeding (increased pressure dislodges clot)
- **Crystalloid**: 0.9% NaCl or Hartmann's - 250-500ml boluses as needed
- **Blood products**: If ongoing bleeding, shock

**Transfusion**:
- **Packed Red Blood Cells (PRBC)**:
  - Target Hb >70 g/L (restrictive strategy - evidence-based for UGIB)
  - Target Hb >80 g/L if active cardiac disease
  - **Caution in variceal bleeding**: Over-transfusion worsens portal pressure and re-bleeding
- **Platelets**: If platelets <50 x 10^9/L and active bleeding
- **Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP)**: If INR >1.5 and active bleeding
- **Cryoprecipitate**: If fibrinogen <1.5 g/L

**Massive Transfusion Protocol** (if exsanguinating hemorrhage):
- **Definition**: >4 units PRBC in 1 hour, or >10 units in 24 hours
- **Blood products in 1:1:1 ratio**: PRBC : FFP : Platelets (e.g., 6 units PRBC : 6 units FFP : 1 pool platelets)
- **Activate protocol early** (call blood bank, ICU, surgery)
- **Calcium**: Replace (diluted by citrate in transfused blood)
- **Warm blood products** (prevent hypothermia)

**Reverse Anticoagulation**:
- **Warfarin**: Vitamin K 10mg IV + Prothrombinex (prothrombin complex concentrate - PCC) 25-50 units/kg
- **DOACs** (dabigatran, rivaroxaban, apixaban):
  - **Idarucizumab** (dabigatran reversal)
  - **Andexanet alfa** (rivaroxaban, apixaban reversal) - if available
  - Otherwise: Prothrombinex, supportive
- **Aspirin/clopidogrel**: No specific reversal, platelet transfusion if severe bleeding

**D - Disability**: GCS, blood glucose

**E - Exposure**: Look for stigmata of chronic liver disease (varices?), abdominal examination

---

#### Medications

**Proton Pump Inhibitor (PPI)**:
- **High-dose IV PPI**: **Esomeprazole 80mg IV bolus** then **8mg/hr continuous infusion** x 72 hours
- Start **immediately** on presentation (before endoscopy)
- Reduces re-bleeding, need for surgery in peptic ulcer bleeding
- After 72 hours: Switch to oral PPI (esomeprazole 40mg BD x 2 weeks, then daily x 4-8 weeks)

**Vasoactive Drugs** (if suspected **variceal bleeding**):
- **Octreotide** 50mcg IV bolus then 50mcg/hr infusion
- Reduces splanchnic blood flow and portal pressure
- Start **before endoscopy** if cirrhosis suspected

**Antibiotics** (if suspected **variceal bleeding**):
- **Ceftriaxone 1g IV daily** x 7 days
- Reduces bacterial infections, mortality in cirrhotic patients
- Start on admission

**Prokinetic** (to improve endoscopic visualization):
- **Erythromycin 250mg IV** 30-60 minutes before endoscopy
- Promotes gastric emptying, clears blood clots, improves view

---

#### Endoscopy Timing

**URGENT Endoscopy** (<12-24 hours):
- **Hemodynamic instability** (shock, ongoing bleeding despite resuscitation)
- **High Glasgow-Blatchford Score** (≥12)
- **Suspected variceal bleeding** (cirrhosis)
- **High-risk features**: Age >65, comorbidities, anticoagulation

**Routine Endoscopy** (within 24 hours):
- **Stable patient**, low-risk GBS
- Most UGIB patients

**Pre-endoscopy**:
- **NBM** (nil by mouth) 6 hours before
- **Consent** (explain procedure, risks)
- **Correct coagulopathy** (INR, platelets)
- **Erythromycin** 250mg IV (30-60 min before)

---

#### Endoscopic Therapy

**Findings and Management**:

1. **Active bleeding** (spurting, oozing):
   - **Adrenaline injection** (1:10,000) into ulcer base + **clips** or **thermal coagulation**
   - **DO NOT use adrenaline alone** (must combine with second modality - clips/thermal)

2. **Visible vessel** (non-bleeding):
   - Endoscopic therapy (clips, thermal)

3. **Adherent clot**:
   - Attempt to remove clot (irrigation, forceps)
   - If visible vessel underneath → endoscopic therapy

4. **Flat pigmented spot** or **clean base**:
   - Low re-bleeding risk
   - No endoscopic therapy needed
   - Oral PPI, early discharge

5. **Esophageal varices**:
   - **Band ligation** (rubber bands placed around varices)
   - May need multiple sessions

6. **Gastric varices**:
   - **Tissue glue** (N-butyl cyanoacrylate) injection

---

#### Post-Endoscopy Management

**If successful endoscopic hemostasis**:
- **Continue high-dose PPI** (esomeprazole 8mg/hr x 72 hours)
- **NBM initially**, then clear fluids if stable, advance diet as tolerated
- **Monitor**: Hemoglobin (drop may indicate re-bleeding), vital signs, urine output
- **Repeat endoscopy** (second-look) at 24 hours if high-risk lesion (some centers)
- **H. pylori testing**: Urea breath test, stool antigen, or biopsy at endoscopy
- **H. pylori eradication** if positive: Triple therapy (PPI + amoxicillin + clarithromycin x 7 days - eTG 2024)
- **Stop NSAIDs** permanently if possible, or use COX-2 inhibitor + PPI if essential
- **Resume anticoagulation/antiplatelet**: Discuss with cardiology - timing depends on indication (usually 3-7 days if high-risk ulcer, earlier if prosthetic valve, recent stent)

**If endoscopic failure** (ongoing bleeding despite therapy):
- **Repeat endoscopy** (different operator, different technique)
- **Interventional radiology**: Angiography + embolization
- **Surgery**: Laparotomy, oversew bleeding vessel, partial gastrectomy
  - Indications: Endoscopic failure, massive transfusion (>6 units), hemodynamic instability
  - **High mortality** (10-30%) - last resort

---

### Red Flags in UGIB

- **Hemodynamic instability** (SBP <90, HR >100) → High-risk, urgent endoscopy, ICU
- **Age >65** → Higher mortality, re-bleeding risk
- **Comorbidities** (cardiac, renal, liver disease) → Higher mortality
- **Known cirrhosis** → Suspect varices, start octreotide + ceftriaxone
- **Anticoagulation** → Reverse, higher bleeding risk
- **Previous aortic surgery** + any GI bleeding → Aortoenteric fistula (URGENT vascular surgery)
- **Re-bleeding after endoscopy** → Repeat endoscopy, IR, surgery

## 2. LOWER GI BLEEDING (LGIB)

### Definition

Bleeding from source **distal to ligament of Treitz**:
- Small bowel (jejunum, ileum) - less common
- Colon
- Rectum
- Anus

---

### Clinical Presentation

**Hematochezia**: Fresh red blood per rectum
- **Most common presentation** of LGIB
- Suggests bleeding from **left colon, rectum, anus** (blood hasn't been digested)
- **Note**: Massive UGIB can also present with hematochezia (rapid transit)

**Maroon stools**: Dark red/maroon stools
- Suggests **right colon** or **small bowel** bleeding (partial digestion)

**Melena**: Black, tarry stools
- **Usually UGIB**, but can be LGIB if bleeding from right colon with slow transit

**Other presentations**:
- Occult bleeding (positive fecal occult blood test, iron-deficiency anemia)
- Hemodynamic instability (if severe)

---

### Differential Diagnosis for LGIB

**Common Causes**:

1. **Diverticular Bleeding** (30-40% of LGIB):
   - **Most common cause** of significant LGIB in elderly
   - **Pathology**: Rupture of vasa recta adjacent to diverticulum
   - **Presentation**: Sudden onset **painless** bright red blood PR, usually large volume, age >60
   - **Location**: Usually left colon (sigmoid, descending) in Western countries
   - **Usually self-limiting** (80% stop spontaneously)
   - **Investigations**: **Colonoscopy** (identify bleeding diverticulum, but often stopped by time of scope), CT angiogram if active bleeding
   - **Management**:
     - Resuscitation
     - **Colonoscopy**: If active bleeding seen → endoscopic therapy (clips, epinephrine injection)
     - **CT angiogram** + **embolization** if massive ongoing bleeding
     - **Surgery** (segmental colectomy) if recurrent, failed endoscopy/IR

2. **Angiodysplasia** (Arteriovenous Malformations) (10-15%):
   - **Pathology**: Degenerative vascular ectasias (dilated, fragile blood vessels in bowel wall)
   - **Location**: Usually right colon (cecum, ascending)
   - **Presentation**: Recurrent painless bright red or maroon blood PR, usually less severe than diverticular, elderly, associated with aortic stenosis, chronic kidney disease
   - **Endoscopy**: Small (few mm) red lesions with feeding vessel
   - **Management**:
     - Colonoscopy with **thermal coagulation** (argon plasma coagulation)
     - Recurrent bleeding common
     - Surgery if recurrent, severe

3. **Ischemic Colitis** (10-15%):
   - **Pathology**: Reduced blood flow to colon → mucosal ischemia, ulceration
   - **Location**: "Watershed areas" - splenic flexure, rectosigmoid junction (between vascular territories)
   - **Risk factors**: Age >60, vascular disease, AF, hypotension, aortic surgery, medications (NSAIDs, cocaine)
   - **Presentation**: Sudden onset **left lower abdominal pain** + bloody diarrhea (key: **PAINFUL** unlike diverticular)
   - **Investigations**:
     - **CT abdomen**: Segmental colonic wall thickening, mesenteric stranding
     - **Colonoscopy** (when stable): Segmental erythema, ulceration, thumbprinting
   - **Management**:
     - **Usually self-limiting** (mild cases)
     - Supportive: IV fluids, bowel rest, antibiotics if severe (ceftriaxone + metronidazole - concern for bacterial translocation)
     - **Surgery** if transmural infarction, perforation, peritonitis (rare)

4. **Inflammatory Bowel Disease - IBD** (Ulcerative Colitis, Crohn's) (5-10%):
   - **Ulcerative Colitis**: Bloody diarrhea (hallmark), urgency, tenesmus, LLQ pain, continuous inflammation from rectum
   - **Crohn's Disease**: Bloody diarrhea (if colonic involvement), RLQ pain, skip lesions
   - **Presentation**: Young adults, chronic/recurrent bloody diarrhea, weight loss, systemic symptoms
   - **Investigations**: Elevated CRP, anemia, low albumin, fecal calprotectin elevated, colonoscopy + biopsy
   - **Management**: Gastroenterology, 5-ASA, corticosteroids (flare), immunosuppressants

5. **Colorectal Cancer** (5-10%):
   - **Presentation**: PR bleeding, change in bowel habit, weight loss, iron-deficiency anemia, abdominal pain/mass
   - **Alarm symptoms**: Age >50, new-onset change in bowel habit, weight loss, family history
   - **Investigations**: FBC (anemia), CEA (tumor marker), **colonoscopy + biopsy** (diagnostic)
   - **Management**: Surgical resection (right/left hemicolectomy, anterior resection), oncology (chemotherapy if advanced)

6. **Hemorrhoids** (Internal) (Very Common but Usually Minor):
   - **Presentation**: Bright red blood on toilet paper, dripping into pan, **painless** (unless thrombosed external hemorrhoids)
   - **Associated**: Straining, constipation, pregnancy
   - **NOT a cause of hemodynamic instability** (if unstable, look elsewhere)
   - **Examination**: May be visible on inspection, proctoscopy
   - **Management**: Fiber, stool softeners, topical analgesia, rubber band ligation if persistent, surgical hemorrhoidectomy if severe

7. **Anal Fissure** (Very Common but Usually Minor):
   - **Presentation**: Bright red blood on toilet paper, **severe pain** with bowel motion (unlike hemorrhoids)
   - **Examination**: Linear tear in anoderm (usually posterior midline)
   - **Management**: Fiber, stool softeners, topical GTN or diltiazem, surgical lateral sphincterotomy if chronic

8. **Small Bowel Bleeding** (5-10% of obscure GI bleeding):
   - **Difficult to diagnose** (beyond reach of standard upper and lower endoscopy)
   - **Causes**: Angiodysplasia, NSAIDs, Crohn's, tumors (rare)
   - **Investigations**: **Capsule endoscopy**, CT enterography, double-balloon enteroscopy

9. **Meckel's Diverticulum** (Children and Young Adults):
   - **Rule of 2s**: 2% of population, 2 feet from ileocecal valve, 2 types of ectopic tissue (gastric, pancreatic), presents age <2 (usually)
   - **Presentation**: Painless maroon/bright red blood PR in child or young adult
   - **Investigations**: **Technetium-99m scan** (Meckel's scan - detects ectopic gastric mucosa)
   - **Management**: Surgical resection

10. **Infectious Colitis** (Campylobacter, Shigella, E. coli O157:H7):
    - **Presentation**: Bloody diarrhea, fever, cramping abdominal pain, recent travel/food poisoning
    - **Investigations**: Stool MCS (culture for Campylobacter, Shigella, Salmonella), stool PCR (E. coli O157:H7)
    - **Management**: Supportive (oral rehydration), antibiotics if severe (ciprofloxacin) - **AVOID in E. coli O157:H7** (increases HUS risk)

---

### Risk Stratification for LGIB

**No validated score like Glasgow-Blatchford for LGIB**, but risk factors for severe bleeding:

**High-risk features**:
- **Hemodynamic instability** (SBP <100, HR >100)
- **Ongoing bleeding** (repeated bloody stools in hospital)
- **Comorbidities** (cardiac, renal disease)
- **Anticoagulation**
- **Age >60**
- **Elevated blood urea** (suggests significant bleeding)

**Low-risk features**:
- **Hemodynamically stable**
- **Minor bleeding** (streaks of blood, on toilet paper only)
- **Young, otherwise healthy**
- **Likely hemorrhoidal source**

---

### Management of LGIB

#### Resuscitation (Similar to UGIB)

**ABCDE approach**:
- **Airway, Breathing**: Oxygen if needed
- **Circulation**:
  - Two large-bore IV cannulae
  - Bloods: FBC, coagulation, UEC, LFTs, lactate, group and crossmatch
  - IV fluids: Crystalloid boluses as needed (target SBP >100)
  - Transfusion: PRBC if Hb <70 (or <80 if cardiac disease), platelets if <50, reverse anticoagulation if needed

---

#### Investigation

**First: Exclude UGIB**:
- **NG tube aspiration**: If fresh red blood PR + hemodynamic instability
  - If bile-stained aspirate (no blood) → likely LGIB
  - If blood in aspirate → UGIB (but not 100% sensitive)
- **Upper endoscopy** if any doubt (massive UGIB can present as hematochezia)

**Colonoscopy**:
- **Timing**:
  - **Urgent** (<24 hours) if high-risk (ongoing bleeding, hemodynamic instability)
  - **Routine** (elective, after bowel preparation) if stable, low-risk
- **Preparation**: Polyethylene glycol (PEG) solution - rapid prep (3-4L over 2-4 hours)
- **Findings**: May identify bleeding source (diverticulum, angiodysplasia, tumor, colitis)
- **Therapy**: Endoscopic clips, thermal coagulation (if active bleeding seen)

**CT Angiogram**:
- **Indication**: Massive ongoing LGIB, hemodynamic instability, failed/non-diagnostic colonoscopy
- **Requires active bleeding** (>0.5-1 ml/min) to detect extravasation
- **Advantage**: Non-invasive, localizes bleeding site, can guide IR or surgery
- **Disadvantage**: Requires active bleeding at time of scan

**Interventional Radiology (IR) - Angiography + Embolization**:
- **Indication**: Ongoing bleeding identified on CT angiogram, failed endoscopic therapy
- **Procedure**: Selective catheterization of mesenteric vessels, embolize bleeding vessel with coils/gel foam
- **Complications**: Bowel ischemia (risk higher in LGIB than UGIB due to less collateral circulation)

**Surgery**:
- **Indications**: Failed endoscopy and IR, massive transfusion (>6 units), hemodynamic instability despite resuscitation
- **Procedure**: Segmental colectomy (if bleeding site localized), subtotal colectomy (if site unknown)
- **Morbidity/mortality**: Higher than endoscopy/IR (especially emergency surgery in elderly)

---

#### Specific Management

**Diverticular Bleeding**:
- Resuscitation
- Colonoscopy (identify, endoscopic therapy if active)
- CT angiogram + embolization if massive
- Surgery if recurrent, failed endoscopy/IR

**Angiodysplasia**:
- Colonoscopy with thermal coagulation (argon plasma coagulation)
- Recurrent bleeding common
- Surgery if recurrent, severe

**Ischemic Colitis**:
- **Supportive** (usually self-limiting)
- IV fluids, bowel rest, antibiotics if severe
- Surgery if transmural infarction, perforation

**IBD**:
- Gastroenterology
- Medical therapy (5-ASA, corticosteroids, immunosuppressants)
- Surgery if severe hemorrhage, toxic megacolon, perforation

**Colorectal Cancer**:
- Oncology referral
- Surgical resection
- Chemotherapy if advanced

**Hemorrhoids / Anal Fissure**:
- Conservative: Fiber, stool softeners
- Procedures: Rubber band ligation (hemorrhoids)
- Surgery: Hemorrhoidectomy, lateral sphincterotomy (fissure)

---

### Red Flags in LGIB

- **Hemodynamic instability** → Urgent resuscitation, investigation (consider massive UGIB presenting as hematochezia)
- **Age >60** → Higher risk of serious pathology (diverticular, angiodysplasia, cancer)
- **Alarm symptoms** (weight loss, change in bowel habit) → Colorectal cancer (colonoscopy)
- **Severe abdominal pain** + bloody diarrhea → Ischemic colitis (CT abdomen)
- **Known vascular disease, AF** → Mesenteric ischemia (CT angiogram, lactate)
- **Recurrent bleeding** → Requires investigation (colonoscopy) - not hemorrhoids until proven

---

## 3. OCCULT GI BLEEDING

### Definition

**Occult bleeding**: GI bleeding not visible to patient (no hematemesis, melena, hematochezia)

**Presentation**:
- **Iron-deficiency anemia** (Hb low, MCV low, ferritin low)
- **Positive fecal occult blood test (FOBT)**

---

### Differential

**Upper GI**:
- Peptic ulcer disease
- Gastric/esophageal cancer
- Angiodysplasia

**Lower GI**:
- Colorectal cancer (most important to exclude)
- Angiodysplasia
- IBD

**Small bowel**:
- Angiodysplasia
- Crohn's disease
- Celiac disease (malabsorption → iron deficiency, not bleeding)
- NSAIDs

---

### Investigation

**Bidirectional Endoscopy** (Upper and Lower):
- **Upper endoscopy (OGD)**: Examine esophagus, stomach, duodenum
- **Colonoscopy**: Examine colon, terminal ileum
- **If both normal** and iron-deficiency anemia persists → Investigate small bowel

**Small Bowel Investigation**:
- **Capsule endoscopy**: Swallow camera capsule, images entire small bowel
- **CT enterography**: CT with oral contrast to visualize small bowel
- **Double-balloon enteroscopy**: Deep small bowel endoscopy (if lesion found on capsule, need biopsy/therapy)

---

### Management

- **Treat underlying cause** (ulcer, cancer, angiodysplasia)
- **Iron supplementation**: Oral (ferrous sulfate 200mg daily-TDS) or IV if oral not tolerated/ineffective
- **Repeat investigations** if recurrent

---

## 4. COMMON IMG MISTAKES

1. **Not considering massive UGIB when patient presents with hematochezia**:
   - Fresh red blood PR can be massive UGIB with rapid transit
   - If hemodynamically unstable + hematochezia → NG aspirate, consider upper endoscopy first

2. **Over-resuscitating variceal bleeding**:
   - Excessive fluids/transfusion increases portal pressure and worsens bleeding
   - Target Hb >70 (not higher) in variceal bleeding
   - Permissive hypotension (SBP 90-100) until bleeding controlled

3. **Not starting octreotide + ceftriaxone in suspected variceal bleeding**:
   - Start BEFORE endoscopy if cirrhosis suspected
   - Reduces re-bleeding, infection, mortality

4. **Not using high-dose PPI protocol**:
   - Must use **80mg bolus + 8mg/hr infusion x 72 hours** for peptic ulcer bleeding
   - Standard dose PPI is inadequate

5. **Delaying endoscopy in high-risk UGIB**:
   - Glasgow-Blatchford Score ≥12, hemodynamic instability → urgent endoscopy <12-24 hours
   - Early endoscopy improves outcomes in high-risk patients

6. **Using adrenaline injection alone for endoscopic therapy**:
   - Must combine adrenaline with **clips or thermal coagulation**
   - Adrenaline alone increases re-bleeding risk

7. **Not reversing anticoagulation in severe bleeding**:
   - Warfarin → Vitamin K + Prothrombinex
   - DOACs → Specific reversal agents if available
   - Assess bleeding vs thrombosis risk (discuss with cardiology, but bleeding takes priority acutely)

8. **Missing aortoenteric fistula**:
   - Patient with previous aortic graft + any GI bleeding → high suspicion
   - "Herald bleed" followed by massive hemorrhage
   - URGENT vascular surgery consult

9. **Attributing all rectal bleeding to hemorrhoids without investigation**:
   - Age >50 + rectal bleeding → **colonoscopy** to exclude colorectal cancer
   - Hemorrhoids and cancer can coexist

10. **Not recognizing ischemic colitis**:
    - **Pain + bloody diarrhea** (unlike painless diverticular bleeding)
    - CT abdomen shows segmental colonic wall thickening
    - Usually self-limiting, but can progress to infarction/perforation

### OSCE Station 1: Upper GI Bleeding - Peptic Ulcer

**Scenario**:
You are the medical officer in the Emergency Department. A 58-year-old man, John, presents with vomiting blood. He has been taking ibuprofen for back pain for the past 2 months. Take a focused history, assess severity, and explain your management plan.

**Task (8 minutes)**:
- Take focused history
- Calculate Glasgow-Blatchford Score
- Explain investigations and management

---

**Model Performance**:

**Opening (30 sec)**:
"Hello John, I'm Dr. [Name]. I understand you've been vomiting blood. I'd like to ask some questions to understand what's happening. Is that okay?"

**Presenting Complaint (3 min)**:

**Hematemesis**:
"Can you describe what you vomited?"
- Patient: "Fresh red blood, about a cupful."
- "How many times have you vomited blood?"
- Patient: "Twice in the last 2 hours."

**Melena**:
"Have you noticed any change in your bowel motions? Dark, tarry stools?"
- Patient: "Yes, my stools have been black for the past few days."
- **✓ Melena** (confirms UGIB)

**Associated symptoms**:
"Any abdominal pain?"
- Patient: "Yes, burning pain in my upper stomach for a few weeks."
- **✓ Epigastric pain** (suggests peptic ulcer)

"Any dizziness, fainting?"
- Patient: "I felt dizzy when I stood up."
- **✓ Syncope** (volume loss, high-risk feature)

**Risk factors for peptic ulcer**:
"You mentioned ibuprofen. How much have you been taking?"
- Patient: "400mg three times a day for 2 months." (NSAIDs - major risk factor)

"Do you take aspirin or any blood thinners?"
- Patient: "No."

"How's your alcohol intake?"
- Patient: "A few beers on weekends."

"Do you smoke?"
- Patient: "Yes, half a pack a day."

**Past medical history**:
"Any medical conditions?"
- Patient: "High blood pressure, controlled with medication."
- **✓ Comorbidity** (cardiovascular disease)

**Medications**:
"What medications do you take?"
- Patient: "Amlodipine for blood pressure, and the ibuprofen."

**Examination findings** (would perform):
- BP 95/60 mmHg (hypotension)
- HR 110 bpm (tachycardia)
- Temp 37.0°C
- Pale, clammy
- Epigastric tenderness

---

**Calculate Glasgow-Blatchford Score** (1 min):

"Based on your symptoms and the blood tests we'll do, I need to calculate a score to determine how urgent your treatment is."

**Assumed bloods** (for scenario):
- Hb 95 g/L (male <100)
- Urea 12 mmol/L (elevated)

**GBS Calculation**:
- Blood urea 12 mmol/L (≥10 to <25): **4 points**
- Hemoglobin 95 g/L (male <100): **6 points**
- SBP 95 mmHg (90-99): **2 points**
- HR 110 bpm (≥100): **1 point**
- Melena present: **1 point**
- Syncope present: **2 points**
- **Total GBS = 16** (Very high risk)

---

**Differential Diagnosis** (30 sec to patient):

"Based on what you've told me, you have **upper gastrointestinal bleeding** - bleeding from your stomach or the first part of your intestine. The most likely cause is a **peptic ulcer** - an ulcer in your stomach or duodenum caused by the ibuprofen you've been taking. Other possibilities include inflammation of the stomach lining or tears from vomiting, but peptic ulcer is most likely given your ibuprofen use and symptoms."

---

**Immediate Management** (2 min):

"This is a serious situation and you need urgent treatment. Here's what we'll do:

**1. Resuscitation**:
- I'll put **two large IV drips** in your arms to give you fluids
- We'll take **blood tests** to check your blood count, kidney function, and prepare blood for transfusion
- You may need a **blood transfusion** if your blood count is low
- We'll give you **pain relief** and medication to stop nausea

**2. Medication**:
- **High-dose acid-suppressing medication** through the IV (PPI - proton pump inhibitor) to help stop the bleeding
- This will be given continuously for 3 days

**3. Investigation**:
- You'll need an **urgent gastroscopy** (camera test) - probably within the next 12-24 hours
- This is a flexible camera that goes down your throat to look at your stomach and find the source of bleeding
- If we find an ulcer, we can treat it during the same procedure with clips or injections to stop the bleeding

**4. Admission**:
- You'll be admitted to hospital for monitoring and treatment
- You'll need to **stop eating and drinking** (nil by mouth) until after the gastroscopy

**5. Stop ibuprofen**:
- **Never take ibuprofen again** - this has caused your ulcer
- We'll give you alternative pain relief for your back pain (paracetamol, or see physiotherapy)

After the gastroscopy, if we confirm it's an ulcer:
- Test for a bacteria called **H. pylori** (common cause of ulcers)
- If positive, give you **antibiotics** to eradicate it
- Continue acid-suppressing medication for 8 weeks

Do you have any questions?"

---

**Summary to Examiner**:

"58-year-old man with **upper GI bleeding** from likely **NSAID-induced peptic ulcer**:

**Presentation**:
- Hematemesis (fresh red blood)
- Melena (black stools)
- Epigastric pain (burning, few weeks)
- Syncope (orthostatic dizziness)
- **Risk factor**: NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400mg TDS x 2 months)

**Examination**: BP 95/60, HR 110 (hemodynamically compromised)

**Glasgow-Blatchford Score: 16** (Very high risk - needs urgent intervention)

**Bloods** (performed):
- FBC (Hb 95 - anemia)
- UEC (Urea 12 - elevated from protein digestion in GI tract)
- Coagulation, LFTs, group and crossmatch 6 units

**Differential**:
1. **Peptic ulcer disease** (most likely - NSAIDs, epigastric pain, hematemesis, melena)
2. Erosive gastritis (NSAIDs)
3. Mallory-Weiss tear (less likely - no preceding vomiting)

**Immediate Management**:
1. **Resuscitation**:
   - Two large-bore IV cannulae (14-16G)
   - IV fluids (0.9% NaCl 500ml boluses, target SBP >100)
   - Crossmatch 6 units PRBC
   - Transfuse if Hb <70 (restrictive strategy)
   - Correct coagulopathy if present
2. **Medications**:
   - **High-dose PPI**: Esomeprazole 80mg IV bolus, then 8mg/hr infusion x 72 hours
   - Antiemetic: Metoclopramide 10mg IV
   - Analgesia: Paracetamol 1g IV
   - **Stop NSAIDs permanently**
3. **Investigations**:
   - **URGENT upper endoscopy** (<12-24 hours, ideally <12 hours given high GBS)
   - Pre-endoscopy: Erythromycin 250mg IV 30-60 min before (prokinetic, clears stomach)
4. **Endoscopic therapy** (if active bleeding/visible vessel):
   - Adrenaline injection (1:10,000) + clips or thermal coagulation
5. **Post-endoscopy**:
   - Continue high-dose PPI x 72 hours, then switch to oral (esomeprazole 40mg BD x 2 weeks, then daily x 6-8 weeks)
   - H. pylori testing (urea breath test or biopsy)
   - If H. pylori positive: Triple therapy (PPI + amoxicillin 1g BD + clarithromycin 500mg BD x 7 days)
   - Avoid NSAIDs lifelong
   - Alternative analgesia for back pain (paracetamol, physiotherapy)
6. **Monitoring**:
   - Vital signs, urine output
   - Repeat Hb (6-12 hours) - assess for ongoing bleeding/re-bleeding
   - Watch for signs of re-bleeding (further hematemesis, melena, hemodynamic deterioration)
7. **If endoscopic failure**:
   - Repeat endoscopy (different operator/technique)
   - Interventional radiology (angiography + embolization)
   - Surgery (oversew ulcer, partial gastrectomy) - last resort

**Prognosis**: Good with early endoscopic therapy and PPI. Risk of re-bleeding ~10-15%. Need H. pylori eradication and NSAID avoidance to prevent recurrence."

### OSCE Station 2: Lower GI Bleeding - Diverticular Bleed

**Scenario**:
You are the medical officer in the Emergency Department. A 72-year-old woman, Margaret, presents with sudden onset of passing bright red blood from her rectum. She has no abdominal pain. Take a focused history and explain your management plan.

**Task (8 minutes)**:
- Take focused history
- Generate differential diagnosis
- Explain investigations and management

---

**Model Performance**:

**Opening (30 sec)**:
"Hello Margaret, I'm Dr. [Name]. I understand you've been passing blood from your rectum. I'd like to ask some questions to understand what's happening. Is that okay?"

**Presenting Complaint (3.5 min)**:

**Hematochezia**:
"Can you describe what you've noticed?"
- Patient: "I went to the toilet and passed a large amount of bright red blood. It filled the toilet bowl."
- "How many times has this happened?"
- Patient: "Three times in the past hour."
- **✓ Large volume hematochezia**

**Onset**:
"When did it start? Was it sudden?"
- Patient: "About 2 hours ago. Very sudden. No warning."
- **✓ Sudden onset** (suggests diverticular or vascular)

**Pain**:
"Do you have any abdominal pain?"
- Patient: "No pain at all."
- **✓ Painless** (diverticular bleed, angiodysplasia, vs ischemic colitis which is painful)

**Associated symptoms**:
"Any nausea, vomiting?"
- Patient: "No."

"Have you vomited any blood or had black stools?"
- Patient: "No."
- **✓ Rules out UGIB**

"Any diarrhea?"
- Patient: "No, normal bowel motion, just with blood."

"Any dizziness or fainting?"
- Patient: "I felt a bit lightheaded."

**Past history**:
"Have you had this before?"
- Patient: "No, first time."

"Do you have diverticular disease?"
- Patient: "Yes, I've had diverticulosis for years. Found on colonoscopy a few years ago."
- **✓ Major risk factor for diverticular bleeding**

"Any other medical conditions?"
- Patient: "High blood pressure, high cholesterol, atrial fibrillation."

**Medications**:
"What medications do you take?"
- Patient: "Apixaban (blood thinner for my AF), amlodipine, atorvastatin."
- **✓ Anticoagulation** (increases bleeding risk)

"Any aspirin or anti-inflammatory medications?"
- Patient: "No."

**Alarm symptoms**:
"Any weight loss recently?"
- Patient: "No."

"Any change in your bowel habits?"
- Patient: "No, regular."

**Examination findings** (would perform):
- BP 105/70 mmHg (mild hypotension)
- HR 95 bpm (mild tachycardia)
- Temp 36.8°C
- Abdomen soft, non-tender
- PR exam: Fresh blood in rectum, no masses

---

**Differential Diagnosis** (1.5 min to patient):

"Based on what you've told me, you have **lower gastrointestinal bleeding** - bleeding from your colon or rectum. Given your history of diverticulosis and the sudden onset of painless bleeding, the most likely cause is **diverticular bleeding** - bleeding from one of the diverticula (small pouches) in your colon.

Other possibilities include:
- **Angiodysplasia**: Abnormal blood vessels in the colon (also causes painless bleeding, more common on the right side of colon)
- **Ischemic colitis**: Reduced blood flow to part of the colon - but this usually causes pain, which you don't have
- Less likely but need to exclude: Polyps or cancer (though you don't have alarm symptoms like weight loss)

The blood thinner you're taking (apixaban) may have made the bleeding worse, but it's not the cause - there's an underlying source we need to find.

We'll need to do tests to confirm the cause and stop the bleeding."

---

**Investigations and Management** (2.5 min):

"Here's what we'll do:

**Immediate steps**:

**1. Resuscitation**:
- I'll put **two IV drips** in to give you fluids
- Take **blood tests**: Check your blood count (hemoglobin), kidney function, clotting
- **Prepare blood** in case you need a transfusion (though hopefully not)
- **Monitor** you closely - blood pressure, heart rate, urine output

**2. Reverse blood thinner**:
- We'll need to **temporarily hold your apixaban** (blood thinner) to help the bleeding stop
- We may give you a **reversal agent** if the bleeding is severe
- Once the bleeding stops, we'll discuss with your cardiologist when to restart it (usually a few days)

**3. Investigation**:
- **Colonoscopy**: Camera test to look inside your colon and find the bleeding source
  - If bleeding has stopped: We'll prepare your bowel (laxatives to clean it out) and do colonoscopy tomorrow or in next few days
  - If bleeding is ongoing: We may need urgent colonoscopy today
- If colonoscopy can't find the source or bleeding is very heavy:
  - **CT angiogram**: Special scan that can show where bleeding is coming from
  - **Angiography**: X-ray of blood vessels - can also **treat** the bleeding by blocking off the bleeding vessel

**4. Treatment**:
- If we find the bleeding diverticulum during colonoscopy, we can treat it with **clips** or **injections** to stop the bleeding
- If bleeding continues despite these treatments:
  - **Embolization**: Radiology procedure to block the bleeding vessel
  - **Surgery**: Remove the part of the colon that's bleeding (last resort if other treatments fail)

**Good news**: Most diverticular bleeding (about 80%) stops on its own. We'll monitor you carefully and intervene if needed.

**5. Admission**:
- You'll need to be admitted to hospital for monitoring and investigation
- **Stop eating and drinking** (nil by mouth) for now, especially if you need colonoscopy

**6. Prevention**:
- Once bleeding stops: High-fiber diet to prevent constipation and reduce diverticular complications
- Avoid NSAIDs if possible

Do you have any questions?"

---

**Summary to Examiner**:

"72-year-old woman with **acute lower GI bleeding**, likely **diverticular bleeding**:

**Presentation**:
- Sudden onset **painless** bright red blood PR, large volume
- Three episodes in past hour
- No hematemesis, no melena
- Mild orthostatic symptoms (lightheaded)

**Risk factors**:
- Known **diverticulosis** (major risk factor)
- **Anticoagulation** (apixaban for AF) - exacerbates bleeding
- Age >70

**Examination**: BP 105/70, HR 95 (mild hemodynamic compromise), abdomen soft/non-tender, fresh blood on PR exam

**Differential**:
1. **Diverticular bleeding** (most likely - known diverticulosis, sudden painless bleeding, large volume)
2. Angiodysplasia (painless, elderly, but less common)
3. Ischemic colitis (less likely - usually painful)
4. Colorectal polyp/cancer (less likely - no alarm symptoms, but need to exclude)

**Investigations**:
- **Bloods**: FBC (Hb), UEC, coagulation (INR, anti-Xa level), group and hold
- **Colonoscopy**: After bowel preparation (if bleeding stopped or slow), or urgent if ongoing
- **CT angiogram**: If massive ongoing bleeding, failed/non-diagnostic colonoscopy

**Management**:
1. **Resuscitation**:
   - Two large-bore IV cannulae
   - IV fluids (crystalloid)
   - Group and hold (crossmatch if Hb low or ongoing bleeding)
   - Transfuse PRBC if Hb <70 (or <80 if cardiac disease)
2. **Reverse anticoagulation**:
   - **Hold apixaban**
   - If severe bleeding: Consider **andexanet alfa** (reversal agent for apixaban) if available, otherwise supportive + Prothrombinex
   - Discuss with cardiology: Timing of restarting anticoagulation (balance bleeding vs stroke risk in AF - usually restart 3-7 days after hemostasis)
3. **Colonoscopy**:
   - Bowel preparation: PEG solution (3-4L over 3-4 hours)
   - Identify bleeding source (diverticulum, angiodysplasia)
   - Endoscopic therapy: Clips, epinephrine injection, thermal coagulation (if active bleeding seen)
4. **If colonoscopy non-diagnostic or massive bleeding**:
   - **CT angiogram** (requires active bleeding >0.5ml/min for detection)
   - If bleeding source identified on CT: **Angiography + embolization** by interventional radiology
5. **If failed endoscopy and IR**:
   - **Surgery**: Segmental colectomy (if bleeding site localized)
   - High morbidity in elderly - last resort
6. **Monitoring**:
   - Vital signs, urine output, repeat Hb
   - Watch for ongoing bleeding (further hematochezia, hemodynamic deterioration)
7. **Restart anticoagulation** when safe (discuss with cardiology - usually 3-7 days post-hemostasis)
8. **Secondary prevention**:
   - High-fiber diet
   - Avoid NSAIDs

**Prognosis**: 80% of diverticular bleeding stops spontaneously. 20-25% risk of re-bleeding. May need elective segmental colectomy if recurrent episodes."

## SUMMARY: KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR ICRP

### Upper GI Bleeding

**Localization**: Hematemesis, coffee-ground vomiting, melena

**Common Causes**:
- Peptic ulcer (35-50%) - NSAIDs, H. pylori
- Varices (15-20%) - cirrhosis, portal hypertension
- Mallory-Weiss tear (5-15%) - forceful vomiting

**Glasgow-Blatchford Score (GBS)**:
- **Score 0 = Safe for discharge** (outpatient endoscopy)
- **Score ≥1 = Admit, likely intervention**
- **Score ≥12 = Very high risk** (urgent endoscopy, ICU)

**Management**:
- **Resuscitate**: 2x large IV, fluids, transfuse (target Hb >70), reverse anticoagulation
- **High-dose PPI**: Esomeprazole 80mg IV bolus → 8mg/hr x 72 hours
- **If varices suspected**: Octreotide + ceftriaxone (before endoscopy)
- **URGENT endoscopy** (<12-24 hours) if high-risk
- **Endoscopic therapy**: Adrenaline + clips/thermal (not adrenaline alone)

### Lower GI Bleeding

**Localization**: Hematochezia (fresh red blood PR), maroon stools

**Common Causes**:
- Diverticular (30-40%) - sudden, painless, large volume, elderly
- Angiodysplasia (10-15%) - painless, recurrent, elderly
- Ischemic colitis (10-15%) - **painful** + bloody diarrhea, elderly

**Management**:
- **Resuscitate**: 2x large IV, fluids, transfuse (Hb >70)
- **Exclude UGIB** if hemodynamically unstable (NG aspirate, upper endoscopy)
- **Colonoscopy**: Urgent if high-risk, routine if stable
- **CT angiogram + IR embolization** if massive, failed endoscopy
- **Surgery**: Last resort (failed endoscopy + IR)

### Common IMG Mistakes to AVOID

1. **Hematochezia = always LGIB** → Can be massive UGIB (exclude in unstable patients)
2. **Over-resuscitating varices** → Target Hb >70, permissive hypotension
3. **Not starting octreotide + ceftriaxone** in suspected varices
4. **Standard dose PPI** → Need 80mg bolus + 8mg/hr infusion
5. **Adrenaline alone** for endoscopic therapy → Must add clips/thermal
6. **Not reversing anticoagulation** in severe bleeding
7. **Missing aortoenteric fistula** (previous aortic graft + GI bleed)
8. **Attributing all PR bleeding to hemorrhoids** → Age >50 needs colonoscopy
9. **Missing ischemic colitis** → Pain + bloody diarrhea, elderly
10. **Delaying endoscopy** in high-risk UGIB
