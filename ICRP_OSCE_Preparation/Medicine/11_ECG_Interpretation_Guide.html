ECG Interpretation Guide - AMC Clinical OSCE

Systematic Approach for Australian Medical Practice

Purpose: AMC Clinical-style ECG interpretation for OSCE stations
Target Time: 3-5 minutes to analyze and present findings
Format: Australian teaching hospital standard
Created: December 16, 2025

SYSTEMATIC ECG INTERPRETATION - "RRAIM" METHOD

CRITICAL OSCE SKILL: ALWAYS use a systematic approach. Never jump to diagnosis.

Say to Examiner: "I'd like to interpret this ECG systematically using the RRAIM approach: Rate, Rhythm, Axis, Intervals, and Morphology."

Step 1: RATE (Is the heart too fast, too slow, or normal?)

Method 1 - "300 Rule" (Regular Rhythms):
  • Count large squares between consecutive R waves
  • Divide 300 by number of large squares
  • Example: 4 large squares = 300/4 = 75 bpm
Method 2 - "Count and Multiply" (Irregular Rhythms):
  • Count R waves in 30 large squares (6 seconds on standard ECG)
  • Multiply by 10 to get beats per minute
Normal Values (Australian Standards):
  • Normal: 60-100 bpm
  • Bradycardia: <60 bpm
  • Tachycardia: >100 bpm

Step 2: RHYTHM (Is it regular and are P waves present?)

Questions to Ask:
  1. Is the rhythm regular or irregular?

- Use calipers or mark paper to measure R-R intervals - Regular = same distance between all R waves - Irregular = varying distances

  1. Are P waves present?

- Look in leads II, III, aVF (best for P waves) - P wave = atrial depolarization (should be <0.12s wide, <2.5mm tall)

  1. Is there a P wave before every QRS?

- Normal = 1:1 ratio (one P wave for every QRS)

  1. Is the PR interval consistent?

- PR interval = start of P wave to start of QRS - Normal: 0.12-0.20 seconds (3-5 small squares)

Common Rhythm Patterns:
  • Sinus rhythm: Regular, P before every QRS, PR 0.12-0.20s
  • Atrial fibrillation (AF): Irregularly irregular, no clear P waves, irregular R-R intervals
  • Atrial flutter: Sawtooth flutter waves (best in leads II, III, aVF)
  • Junctional rhythm: No P waves or inverted P waves after QRS

Step 3: AXIS (Is the electrical axis normal?)

Quick Method - "Lead I and aVF":
  • Normal Axis (-30° to +90°): Both Lead I and aVF positive (upright QRS)
  • Left Axis Deviation (-30° to -90°): Lead I positive, aVF negative
  • Right Axis Deviation (+90° to +180°): Lead I negative, aVF positive
  • Extreme Axis (-90° to -180°): Both Lead I and aVF negative
Causes of Axis Deviation:
  • Left Axis Deviation: LVH, left bundle branch block, inferior MI
  • Right Axis Deviation: RVH, pulmonary embolism, left posterior hemiblock
IMG Tip: Don't spend >30 seconds calculating axis in OSCE. Quick "Lead I + aVF" method is sufficient for most cases.

Step 4: INTERVALS (Are PR, QRS, and QT normal?)

Step 5: MORPHOLOGY (Are there ST changes, Q waves, or hypertrophy?)

A. ST Segment Analysis:
  • Normal: Isoelectric (at baseline)
  • ST Elevation:
- ≥1mm in limb leads (I, II, III, aVF, aVL, aVR) - ≥2mm in chest leads (V1-V6) - Causes: STEMI, pericarditis, early repolarization, LV aneurysm
  • ST Depression:
- ≥0.5mm horizontal or downsloping - Causes: NSTEMI, ischaemia, digoxin effect, LVH strain B. Q Waves:
  • Pathological Q Wave (indicates old MI):
- >0.04s (1 small square) wide - >2mm deep or >25% of R wave height - Present in ≥2 contiguous leads C. T Wave Changes:
  • T Wave Inversion: Ischaemia, old MI, LVH, PE
  • Tall Peaked T Waves: Hyperkalaemia (K+ >5.5 mmol/L)
  • Flat T Waves: Hypokalaemia, ischaemia
D. Ventricular Hypertrophy:
  • Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH):
- Sokolow-Lyon Criteria: S wave in V1 + R wave in V5 or V6 ≥35mm - Often with left axis deviation, ST depression + T wave inversion in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5-V6)
  • Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (RVH):
- Tall R wave in V1 (R:S ratio >1) - Right axis deviation - Causes: Pulmonary hypertension, chronic lung disease, congenital heart disease

LIFE-THREATENING ECG PATTERNS - DO NOT MISS

OSCE CRITICAL: These patterns require IMMEDIATE action. State urgency to examiner.

1. ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction (STEMI)

Definition (Australian Guidelines):
  • ST elevation ≥1mm in ≥2 contiguous limb leads OR ≥2mm in ≥2 contiguous chest leads
  • Must be in vascular territory (see table below)
Territory Leads with ST Elevation Coronary Artery Associated Findings
Anterior V2, V3, V4 Left Anterior Descending (LAD) Q waves in V1-V4 if old MI
Lateral I, aVL, V5, V6 Left Circumflex (LCx) Often with anterior or inferior STEMI
Inferior II, III, aVF Right Coronary Artery (RCA) Check for right ventricular involvement (V4R)
Posterior Tall R waves in V1-V2 (mirror image) RCA or LCx ST depression V1-V3 (reciprocal change)
IMMEDIATE OSCE Response:

"This ECG shows ST elevation in leads [specify], consistent with STEMI in the [territory] distribution. This is a medical emergency requiring immediate reperfusion therapy."

Management (Therapeutic Guidelines eTG 2024):
  1. Call for senior help IMMEDIATELY - Registrar/Consultant/Emergency physician
  2. MONA:
- Morphine 2.5-5mg IV (for pain) - Oxygen (only if SpO₂ <94% - avoid hyperoxia) - Nitrates - GTN 300-600mcg sublingual (avoid if inferior STEMI with RV involvement or hypotension) - Aspirin 300mg chewed (loading dose)
  1. Antiplatelet therapy: Ticagrelor 180mg or prasugrel 60mg (or clopidogrel 300-600mg)
  2. Anticoagulation: Enoxaparin 1mg/kg SC or unfractionated heparin bolus + infusion
  3. URGENT reperfusion:
- Primary PCI (percutaneous coronary intervention) - preferred if available within 90 minutes - Thrombolysis - if PCI not available within 90 min and <12 hours from symptom onset (tenecteplase or alteplase)
Common IMG Mistakes:
  • Not checking for reciprocal changes (ST depression opposite to ST elevation confirms STEMI)
  • Missing posterior STEMI (look for tall R waves in V1-V2)
  • Giving GTN in inferior STEMI with RV involvement - can cause severe hypotension (check V4R)
  • Using US terminology: "cath lab" → "coronary angiography suite" in Australia

2. Ventricular Fibrillation (VF) & Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)

Ventricular Fibrillation (VF):
  • ECG Pattern: Chaotic, irregular waveform with no identifiable QRS complexes
  • Clinical: Cardiac arrest - no pulse, no blood pressure
  • Management: IMMEDIATE CPR + defibrillation (unsynchronized shock 200J biphasic)
Ventricular Tachycardia (VT):
  • ECG Pattern:
- Wide QRS complexes (≥0.12s) - Rate >100 bpm (usually 120-250 bpm) - Regular rhythm - No discernible P waves or AV dissociation (P waves and QRS independent)
  • Clinical Types:
- Sustained VT: >30 seconds or causing haemodynamic compromise - Non-sustained VT: <30 seconds, terminates spontaneously VT vs SVT with Aberrancy (Brugada Criteria):
  1. Absence of RS complex in all chest leads → VT
  2. RS interval >100ms in any chest lead → VT
  3. AV dissociation present → VT
  4. Morphology criteria (LBBB or RBBB pattern) → If doesn't match typical BBB, likely VT
Management (ARC Guidelines 2024):
  • Pulseless VT (cardiac arrest):
- CPR immediately - Defibrillation 200J (unsynchronized shock) - Adrenaline 1mg IV every 3-5 minutes - Amiodarone 300mg IV bolus after 3rd shock
  • VT with pulse (stable):
- Continuous ECG monitoring - Amiodarone 300mg IV over 20-60 min, then 900mg over 24 hours - Prepare for cardioversion if becomes unstable
  • VT with pulse (unstable - hypotensive, chest pain, pulmonary oedema):
- Synchronized DC cardioversion 100-200J - Sedation if conscious (midazolam 2-5mg IV)

3. Complete (Third-Degree) Heart Block

ECG Pattern:
  • P waves present at regular intervals (atrial rate normal, e.g., 70 bpm)
  • QRS complexes present at slow regular intervals (ventricular rate slow, e.g., 30-40 bpm)
  • No relationship between P waves and QRS (AV dissociation)
  • QRS may be wide (if ventricular escape rhythm) or narrow (if junctional escape rhythm)
Clinical Significance:
  • Bradycardia (often <40 bpm)
  • Syncope, dizziness, fatigue
  • Risk of asystole
Management (eTG 2024):
  1. Immediate:
- Call for senior help - If unstable (hypotension, syncope, heart failure): Atropine 600mcg IV (may repeat to 3mg total) - If atropine ineffective: Transcutaneous pacing or isoprenaline infusion
  1. Definitive:
- Permanent pacemaker insertion (almost always required for complete heart block)
OSCE Statement: "This ECG shows complete heart block with AV dissociation. The patient requires urgent cardiology review for pacemaker insertion. I would assess haemodynamic stability and consider temporary pacing if unstable."

4. Long QT Syndrome

ECG Pattern:
  • QTc >0.45s (men) or >0.47s (women)
  • QTc >0.50s = high risk
Causes (Mnemonic: "LQTS Drugs"):
  • Congenital: Romano-Ward syndrome, Jervell and Lange-Nielsen syndrome
  • Medications:
- Antiarrhythmics: Amiodarone, sotalol, flecainide - Antibiotics: Macrolides (erythromycin, clarithromycin), fluoroquinolones - Antipsychotics: Haloperidol, risperidone - Antidepressants: Citalopram, escitalopram
  • Electrolytes: Hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia, hypocalcaemia
  • Other: Hypothermia, subarachnoid haemorrhage, myocardial ischaemia
Clinical Risk:
  • Torsades de Pointes (polymorphic VT) - "twisting of the points"
  • Can degenerate to VF and sudden cardiac death
Management:
  1. Stop offending drugs immediately
  2. Correct electrolytes: K+ >4.0 mmol/L, Mg2+ >1.0 mmol/L
  3. If Torsades de Pointes: Magnesium sulfate 2g IV over 10 min
  4. Cardiology referral (may need beta-blockers, ICD if high risk)

5. Hyperkalaemia (Life-Threatening)

Progressive ECG Changes as K+ Increases:
  • Mild (5.5-6.5 mmol/L): Tall, peaked T waves (narrow base, "tented")
  • Moderate (6.5-7.5 mmol/L): Flattened P waves, prolonged PR interval
  • Severe (>7.5 mmol/L):
- Loss of P waves - Widened QRS (>0.12s) - "Sine wave" pattern (pre-cardiac arrest) IMMEDIATE Management (eTG 2024):
  1. Cardiac protection (FIRST):
- Calcium gluconate 10% 10mL IV over 5-10 minutes (stabilizes cardiac membrane) - Does NOT lower K+, but prevents arrhythmias
  1. Shift K+ intracellularly:
- Insulin 10 units IV + 50mL 50% glucose (or 25g dextrose) - Salbutamol 10-20mg nebulized - Sodium bicarbonate 50-100 mmol IV (if acidotic)
  1. Remove K+ from body (slower):
- Resonium A (calcium polystyrene sulfonate) 15g PO/PR TDS - Haemodialysis if refractory or K+ >7.5 mmol/L
OSCE CRITICAL: If you see tall peaked T waves + wide QRS, state immediately: "This ECG shows signs of severe hyperkalaemia which is a medical emergency. I would give calcium gluconate immediately to stabilize the cardiac membrane, then insulin-dextrose and salbutamol to shift potassium intracellularly."

COMMON ARRHYTHMIAS (AMC OSCE Favourites)

1. Atrial Fibrillation (AF)

ECG Pattern:
  • Irregularly irregular rhythm (hallmark)
  • No clear P waves - replaced by fibrillatory waves (chaotic atrial activity)
  • Narrow QRS (unless bundle branch block)
  • Variable R-R intervals
Clinical Features:
  • Palpitations, dyspnoea, fatigue, dizziness
  • May be asymptomatic (found incidentally)
  • Major complication: Stroke risk (5x increased without anticoagulation)
Management Principles (eTG 2024):
  1. Acute Management:
- If unstable (hypotension, pulmonary oedema, angina): Synchronized DC cardioversion 100-200J - If stable: Rate or rhythm control
  1. Rate Control (most common strategy):
- Beta-blocker: Metoprolol 25-50mg BD or atenolol 25-50mg daily - Calcium channel blocker: Diltiazem 60mg TDS or verapamil 40mg TDS - Digoxin: 125-250mcg daily (if heart failure or sedentary elderly) - Target rate: <110 bpm at rest
  1. Rhythm Control (if symptomatic, young, first episode):
- Electrical cardioversion (if <48 hours duration OR adequately anticoagulated for 3 weeks) - Pharmacological: Flecainide (if no structural heart disease) or amiodarone - Long-term: Consider catheter ablation
  1. Anticoagulation (MOST IMPORTANT - prevents stroke):
- Calculate CHA₂DS₂-VASc score: - Congestive heart failure (1 point) - Hypertension (1 point) - Age ≥75 (2 points) - Diabetes (1 point) - Stroke/TIA/thromboembolism (2 points) - Vascular disease (1 point) - Age 65-74 (1 point) - Sex category (female = 1 point) - Anticoagulation if: Score ≥2 (men) or ≥3 (women) - First-line: Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) - PBS-listed - Apixaban 5mg BD (reduce to 2.5mg BD if ≥2 of: age ≥80, weight ≤60kg, Cr ≥133) - Rivaroxaban 20mg daily - Dabigatran 150mg BD - Alternative: Warfarin (target INR 2-3)
OSCE Presentation: "This ECG shows atrial fibrillation with an irregularly irregular rhythm and absent P waves. I would assess haemodynamic stability, control rate with beta-blockers, calculate CHA₂DS₂-VASc score for stroke risk, and consider anticoagulation."

2. Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT)

ECG Pattern:
  • Narrow QRS tachycardia (QRS <0.12s)
  • Regular rhythm
  • Rate usually 150-250 bpm
  • P waves may be hidden in QRS or T wave
Types:
  • AVNRT (AV nodal re-entrant tachycardia) - most common (60%)
  • AVRT (AV re-entrant tachycardia) - accessory pathway (WPW syndrome)
  • Atrial tachycardia
Management (ARC Guidelines 2024):
  1. If unstable: Synchronized DC cardioversion 100-200J
  2. If stable:
A. Vagal Manoeuvres (try first): - Valsalva manoeuvre: "Take a deep breath and bear down like you're having a bowel movement for 10-15 seconds" - Carotid sinus massage: Only if trained, never both sides simultaneously, contraindicated if carotid bruit - Diving reflex: Face in ice-cold water B. Adenosine (if vagal manoeuvres fail): - Adenosine 6mg rapid IV push (via large vein, followed by 20mL saline flush) - Warn patient: "You'll feel very unwell for a few seconds - chest tightness, feeling like you can't breathe - this is normal and will pass quickly" - If no response: 12mg, then 18mg - Continuous ECG monitoring during administration - Contraindications: Asthma (use verapamil 5-10mg IV instead), WPW with AF
  1. Prevention:
- Beta-blocker: Metoprolol 25-50mg BD - Calcium channel blocker: Verapamil 40-80mg TDS - Definitive: Catheter ablation (curative in >90%, refer to cardiology)

3. Sinus Bradycardia

ECG Pattern:
  • Rate <60 bpm
  • Regular rhythm
  • P wave before every QRS
  • Normal PR interval (0.12-0.20s)
Causes:
  • Physiological: Athletes, sleep, increased vagal tone
  • Pathological:
- Medications: Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin, amiodarone - Hypothyroidism - Hypothermia - Increased intracranial pressure (Cushing's reflex) - Sick sinus syndrome - Inferior MI (RCA supplies SA node) Management:
  • If asymptomatic and haemodynamically stable: No treatment (especially if athlete)
  • If symptomatic (dizziness, syncope, fatigue) or unstable:
- Atropine 600mcg IV (may repeat to 3mg total) - If refractory: Isoprenaline infusion or transcutaneous pacing - Definitive: Permanent pacemaker if persistent symptomatic bradycardia

4. Atrial Flutter

ECG Pattern:
  • Sawtooth flutter waves (best seen in leads II, III, aVF, V1)
  • Atrial rate typically 250-350 bpm
  • Regular ventricular response (usually 2:1, 3:1, or 4:1 AV block)
- 2:1 block = ventricular rate ~150 bpm - 3:1 block = ventricular rate ~100 bpm Management:
  • Similar to AF: Rate control, rhythm control, anticoagulation (same CHA₂DS₂-VASc criteria)
  • Rate control: Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers
  • Rhythm control: DC cardioversion or catheter ablation (more successful than AF ablation)
  • Anticoagulation: Same as AF (DOACs preferred)

ELECTROLYTE ABNORMALITIES ON ECG

Electrolyte ECG Changes Clinical Context
Hyperkalaemia (K+ >5.5) Tall peaked T waves → Flat P waves → Wide QRS → Sine wave Renal failure, ACE-I/ARB, potassium-sparing diuretics
Hypokalaemia (K+ <3.5) Flat/inverted T waves, prominent U waves, ST depression, long QT Diuretics, vomiting, diarrhoea
Hypercalcaemia (Ca2+ >2.6) Short QT interval, widened T wave Hyperparathyroidism, malignancy
Hypocalcaemia (Ca2+ <2.1) Prolonged QT interval (long ST segment) Chronic kidney disease, hypoparathyroidism
Hypomagnesaemia (Mg2+ <0.7) Similar to hypokalaemia, Torsades de Pointes Often coexists with hypokalaemia

AMC OSCE SCENARIOS - ECG STATIONS

Scenario 1: Chest Pain with ECG

Station Setup:

"Mr. Smith, 58 years old, presents with 2 hours of central crushing chest pain radiating to his left arm. Here is his ECG. Please interpret the ECG and outline your immediate management."

Systematic Approach:
  1. Identify yourself: "I'm going to interpret this ECG systematically."
  2. RRAIM method:
- Rate: ~70 bpm (state how calculated) - Rhythm: Sinus rhythm, regular - Axis: Normal (Lead I and aVF both positive) - Intervals: PR normal, QRS normal, QT normal - Morphology: "There is ST elevation of 3mm in leads V2, V3, and V4, consistent with anterior STEMI"
  1. State diagnosis: "This ECG shows an acute anterior ST-elevation myocardial infarction."
  2. Immediate management:
- "This is a medical emergency requiring immediate reperfusion therapy." - MONA protocol (morphine, oxygen if hypoxic, nitrates, aspirin) - Dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + ticagrelor/prasugrel) - Anticoagulation - URGENT cardiology referral for primary PCI

Scenario 2: Palpitations with ECG

Station Setup:

"Mrs. Jones, 72 years old, presents with palpitations and dyspnoea. Her ECG is shown. Please interpret the ECG and outline your management plan."

ECG Shows: Atrial fibrillation Systematic Approach:
  1. RRAIM:
- Rate: ~110 bpm (count and multiply method due to irregularity) - Rhythm: "Irregularly irregular rhythm with no clear P waves, consistent with atrial fibrillation" - Axis: Cannot accurately assess due to irregularity - Intervals: QRS normal width - Morphology: No ST changes
  1. Assessment of stability:
- "I would assess haemodynamic stability - BP, conscious state, chest pain, signs of heart failure" - If unstable: DC cardioversion - If stable: Rate control
  1. Management plan:
- Rate control: Beta-blocker (metoprolol) or calcium channel blocker (diltiazem) - Anticoagulation assessment: "I would calculate CHA₂DS₂-VASc score to assess stroke risk" - Age 72 = 1 point - Female = 1 point - Total ≥2 → Anticoagulation indicated - "I would start a DOAC such as apixaban 5mg BD, which is PBS-listed" - Consider rhythm control vs long-term rate control - Cardiology referral

Scenario 3: Routine Pre-Operative ECG

Station Setup:

"Mr. Brown, 65, is scheduled for elective knee replacement. This is his pre-operative ECG. Please interpret it and advise if he is fit for surgery."

ECG Shows: Left bundle branch block (LBBB) Systematic Approach:
  1. RRAIM:
- Rate: 72 bpm - Rhythm: Sinus rhythm, regular - Axis: Normal or left axis deviation - Intervals: PR normal, QRS wide (≥0.12s) - Morphology: "Wide QRS with broad notched R waves in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5-V6) and deep S waves in V1-V2, consistent with left bundle branch block"
  1. Clinical significance:
- "LBBB can indicate underlying structural heart disease" - Need to assess: History of chest pain, dyspnoea, cardiac history
  1. Further assessment:
- "I would take a cardiac history and examine the patient" - Check if new LBBB (compare to old ECGs) - new LBBB with chest pain = STEMI equivalent - Consider echocardiogram to assess LV function - Cardiology review if symptomatic or new finding
  1. Fitness for surgery:
- "If this is chronic LBBB with no new symptoms and patient is otherwise well, he is likely fit for elective surgery" - "However, I would recommend cardiology clearance and optimization before proceeding"

COMMON IMG MISTAKES - ECG INTERPRETATION

  1. Not using systematic approach
- ❌ Jumping straight to "This looks like a STEMI" - ✅ Always use RRAIM method, state each step out loud
  1. Missing reciprocal changes in STEMI
- ❌ Only looking at ST elevation - ✅ Check for ST depression in opposite leads (confirms STEMI, rules out pericarditis)
  1. Forgetting to check for posterior MI
- ❌ Missing tall R waves in V1-V2 - ✅ Posterior MI presents as ST depression V1-V3 with tall R waves (mirror image)
  1. Not correcting QT for heart rate
- ❌ Saying "QT looks long" without calculating QTc - ✅ Use Bazett's formula or quick rule (QT should be
  • Confusing VT with SVT + aberrancy
    • - ❌ Calling all wide-complex tachycardias "SVT" - ✅ Use Brugada criteria; if in doubt, treat as VT (safer)
    1. Not recognizing hyperkalaemia urgency
    - ❌ "Tall T waves, probably normal variant" - ✅ Tall peaked T waves + wide QRS = medical emergency, give calcium immediately
    1. Using US terminology
    - ❌ "Cardiac enzymes", "cath lab", "ER" - ✅ "Troponin", "coronary angiography suite", "Emergency Department"
    1. Not mentioning anticoagulation in AF
    - ❌ Only discussing rate/rhythm control - ✅ Always calculate CHA₂DS₂-VASc score and discuss stroke prevention (most important!)
    1. Forgetting to check old ECGs
    - ❌ Treating chronic changes as acute - ✅ State "I would compare this to previous ECGs" - ST elevation could be old LV aneurysm
    1. Not stating urgency for emergencies
    - ❌ "This is a STEMI, we should do something" - ✅ "This is a STEMI, a medical emergency requiring immediate reperfusion therapy. I would call for senior help now."
    1. Ignoring clinical context
    - ❌ Interpreting ECG in isolation - ✅ "This ECG shows [finding]. In the context of this patient's presentation of [symptoms], this is consistent with [diagnosis]."
    1. Not knowing Australian guidelines
    - ❌ Recommending treatments not available/PBS-listed in Australia - ✅ Know eTG 2024, mention PBS status for medications (apixaban, rivaroxaban PBS-listed for AF)

    SUMMARY - ECG INTERPRETATION CHECKLIST

    Before finishing ECG interpretation in OSCE, ensure you've covered:

    • ✅ Introduced systematic approach ("I'll use RRAIM method")
    • ✅ Stated rate and how calculated
    • ✅ Identified rhythm (regular/irregular, P waves present)
    • ✅ Assessed axis (quick Lead I + aVF method)
    • ✅ Measured intervals (PR, QRS, QT/QTc)
    • ✅ Assessed morphology (ST changes, Q waves, T waves, hypertrophy)
    • ✅ Stated diagnosis clearly
    • ✅ Mentioned clinical context (related to patient's symptoms)
    • ✅ Outlined immediate management if emergency
    • ✅ Stated urgency appropriately (STEMI, VF, complete heart block = IMMEDIATE)
    • ✅ Used Australian terminology (troponin, Emergency Department, PBS)
    • ✅ Mentioned anticoagulation if AF
    • ✅ Offered to compare with old ECGs if available
