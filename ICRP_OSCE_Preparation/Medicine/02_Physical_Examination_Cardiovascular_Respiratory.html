Physical Examination OSCE Notes - Cardiovascular & Respiratory

AMC Clinical / ICRP NSW Young Hospital Preparation

Purpose: Systematic physical examination for AMC Clinical OSCE stations Target Time: 8 minutes per examination Format: Australian teaching hospital standard (5 Ps Framework) Created: December 14, 2025

THE 5 Ps FRAMEWORK (Universal for ALL Examinations)

Every physical examination MUST follow this structure:

  1. Preparation - Wash hands, introduce, explain, gain consent
  2. Position - Position patient appropriately for examination
  3. Permission - Ask permission, ensure comfort, offer chaperone
  4. Perform - Systematic examination with appropriate exposure
  5. Present - Summarise findings to examiner

CARDIOVASCULAR EXAMINATION

1. PREPARATION (60 seconds)

Hand Hygiene: "I'm going to wash my hands first." (Use alcohol gel or wash with soap) Introduction & Consent:
  • "Good morning, my name is Dr [Name]. I'm one of the doctors here today."
  • "I understand Dr [Examiner Name] has asked me to examine your heart and circulation. Is that correct?"
  • "This will involve me examining your hands, neck, and chest. I'll need you to remove your shirt, but I'll make sure you're covered with a blanket. Is that okay?"
  • "Please let me know if you feel uncomfortable at any point."

Offer Chaperone: "Would you like a chaperone present for this examination?" Check for Pain: "Before I start, are you experiencing any pain or discomfort anywhere?"

2. POSITION

  • "I'm going to position you at 45 degrees, which is the best position to examine your heart."
  • Position patient reclined at 45 degrees with chest exposed (cover with blanket until ready)
  • Ensure good lighting
  • Stand on patient's right side

3. PERMISSION

  • "I'm going to start by examining your hands and arms, then move up to your neck and chest. Is that alright?"
  • Ensure adequate exposure: Chest exposed from neck to waist (maintain dignity with blanket)

4. PERFORM (6 minutes)

A. GENERAL INSPECTION (End of Bed)

Stand back and observe:

  • General appearance: Comfortable at rest or dyspnoeic? Cyanosed? Pale?
  • Nutritional status: Cachexic (advanced heart failure) or obese?
  • Equipment: Oxygen, GTN spray, medications on bedside table
  • Scars: Median sternotomy (CABG, valve replacement), left thoracotomy (mitral valvotomy)

Say out loud: "I'd like to start with a general inspection from the end of the bed. The patient appears comfortable at rest, not short of breath, no obvious cyanosis or pallor."

B. HANDS & ARMS

Inspect Hands:
  • Clubbing: Check for loss of angle at nailbed (causes: congenital heart disease, endocarditis)

- How to check: "I'm going to look at your fingers." (Schamroth's window test - ask patient to oppose nails of index fingers)

  • Cyanosis: Blue discolouration of fingertips (peripheral vs central)
  • Splinter haemorrhages: Dark lines under nails (endocarditis, trauma)
  • Osler's nodes: Painful red nodules on finger pulps (endocarditis)
  • Janeway lesions: Painless palmar macules (endocarditis)
  • Capillary refill: Press nail bed for 5 seconds, release - should refill in <2 seconds

Radial Pulse (Both Wrists):
  • "I'm going to check your pulse at both wrists."
  • Palpate both radial pulses simultaneously: Check for radio-radial delay (aortic dissection, coarctation)
  • Count rate for 15 seconds √ó 4 (or 30 sec √ó 2 if irregular)
  • Assess rhythm: Regular or irregular? (AF = irregularly irregular)
  • Assess character: Normal, bounding (CO‚ÇÇ retention, aortic regurgitation), low volume (aortic stenosis)

Say out loud: "Pulse is 72 beats per minute, regular rhythm, normal volume, no radio-radial delay." Brachial Pulse & Blood Pressure:
  • "I'm going to check your blood pressure now."
  • Palpate brachial pulse (medial to biceps tendon in antecubital fossa)
  • Assess character: Slow-rising (aortic stenosis), collapsing (aortic regurgitation)

- For collapsing pulse: "May I lift your arm above your head?" (Feel for collapse at radial pulse)

  • Measure BP: "I'm going to put this cuff on your arm." (State reading: "Blood pressure is 120/80")

C. FACE & NECK

Face:
  • Eyes: Pull down lower eyelid - check for pallor (anaemia) or conjunctival petechiae (endocarditis)
  • Central cyanosis: Look at tongue (blue = central cyanosis from right-to-left shunt or lung disease)
  • Malar flush: Red cheeks (mitral stenosis)

Say out loud: "I'm going to examine your face and neck now." Jugular Venous Pressure (JVP):
  • Patient at 45 degrees, head turned slightly to the left
  • "I'm going to look at the veins in your neck. Can you turn your head slightly to the left and relax?"
  • Look for internal jugular vein (between two heads of sternocleidomastoid)
  • Identify by: Double waveform, moves with respiration, non-palpable, occludes with light pressure at base of neck
  • Measure height: Vertical distance from sternal angle to top of pulsation (normal <3-4 cm)
  • Assess waveform: Prominent 'a' wave (tricuspid stenosis, pulmonary hypertension), absent 'a' wave (AF)
  • Check for hepatojugular reflux: Press on liver for 10 seconds - JVP should not rise >3cm

Say out loud: "JVP is not elevated, approximately 2cm above the sternal angle, normal waveform." Carotid Pulse:
  • "I'm going to feel the pulse in your neck now."
  • Palpate one carotid at a time (NEVER both simultaneously - can cause bradycardia/syncope)
  • Position: Medial to sternocleidomastoid, at level of thyroid cartilage
  • Character: Slow-rising (aortic stenosis), bounding (aortic regurgitation)
  • Auscultate for bruits: "I'm going to listen over your neck arteries." (Carotid stenosis)

D. PRECORDIUM (Chest)

Inspection:
  • "I'm going to examine your chest now. I'll need to expose your chest - I'll use this blanket to keep you covered."
  • Look for:

- Scars: Median sternotomy, thoracotomy, pacemaker scar (left infraclavicular) - Visible apex beat: 5th intercostal space, mid-clavicular line (may see in thin patients) - Visible pulsations: Abnormal if prominent - Chest deformity: Pectus excavatum, barrel chest (COPD)

Palpation:
  • "I'm going to feel your chest now."
  • Apex Beat Assessment (CRITICAL for OSCE):

- "I'm going to locate the apex beat." (Use palm of hand first to locate general area) - Use one finger to precisely locate - ASSESS THREE PARAMETERS:

1. POSITION (normally 5th intercostal space, mid-clavicular line):

  • Displaced laterally or inferiorly = CARDIOMEGALY/LV DILATATION - RED FLAG for HEART FAILURE
  • Count intercostal spaces down from 2nd (angle of Louis at sternal angle)
  • Measure distance from mid-clavicular line (if beyond = displaced)

2. CHARACTER:

  • Forceful/heaving: LV hypertrophy (aortic stenosis, hypertension)
  • Tapping: Palpable S1 (mitral stenosis)
  • Diffuse: LV dilatation (dilated cardiomyopathy)
  • Thrusting: Volume overload (mitral/aortic regurgitation)

3. SIZE (normally localized to one finger space):

  • Enlarged = cardiomegaly
  • Parasternal Heave: Place palm over left sternal edge - sustained systolic lift = RV hypertrophy (pulmonary hypertension, severe tricuspid regurgitation, pulmonary stenosis)
  • Thrills: Palpable murmurs (feel like a cat's purr)

- Aortic area (2nd intercostal space, right sternal edge) - Pulmonary area (2nd intercostal space, left sternal edge) - Apex (mitral area)

Say out loud: "Apex beat is located in the 5th intercostal space, mid-clavicular line, normal character. No heaves or thrills palpable." Auscultation (MOST IMPORTANT):
  • "I'm going to listen to your heart now with my stethoscope. Please breathe normally."
  • Warm stethoscope in hands first
  • Use diaphragm for high-pitched sounds (S1, S2, aortic/mitral regurgitation)
  • Use bell for low-pitched sounds (mitral stenosis, S3, S4)

4 Main Areas (+ 2 extra):
  1. Apex (Mitral) - 5th ICS, mid-clavicular line
  2. Left sternal edge (Tricuspid) - 4th ICS, left sternal edge
  3. Pulmonary area - 2nd ICS, left sternal edge
  4. Aortic area - 2nd ICS, right sternal edge
  5. Axilla - Listen for radiation of mitral regurgitation
  6. Carotids - Listen for radiation of aortic stenosis

At Each Area, Listen For:
  • Heart sounds: S1 (lub), S2 (dub)

- S1 loud in mitral stenosis, soft in mitral regurgitation - S2 split (normal in young people on inspiration)

  • Added sounds:

- S3 (ventricular gallop) - Heart failure, volume overload - S4 (atrial gallop) - LV hypertrophy, hypertension - Opening snap - Mitral stenosis (heard best at apex with bell)

  • Murmurs: If heard, describe using 7 S's:

1. Site: Where loudest? 2. Systolic or Diastolic: Time with carotid pulse 3. Shape: Crescendo, decrescendo, plateau, pan-systolic 4. Severity: Grade 1-6/6 5. Special manoeuvres: Inspiration (right-sided murmurs louder), expiration (left-sided), leaning forward (aortic regurgitation), left lateral position (mitral stenosis) 6. Spread: Does it radiate? (AS ‚Üí carotids, MR ‚Üí axilla) 7. Sound: Harsh, blowing, rumbling

Common Murmurs:
  • Aortic stenosis: Ejection systolic, harsh, radiates to carotids, slow-rising pulse
  • Aortic regurgitation: Early diastolic, high-pitched, heard best leaning forward in expiration at left sternal edge
  • Mitral regurgitation: Pan-systolic, blowing, radiates to axilla
  • Mitral stenosis: Mid-diastolic, rumbling, low-pitched (use bell), heard best in left lateral position
  • Tricuspid regurgitation: Pan-systolic at left sternal edge, louder on inspiration

Dynamic Manoeuvres:
  • "I'm going to ask you to lean forward and breathe out." (Best for aortic regurgitation)
  • "I'm going to ask you to roll onto your left side." (Best for mitral stenosis - use bell at apex)
  • "Take a deep breath in." (Right-sided murmurs louder on inspiration)

Say out loud: "Heart sounds: dual heart sounds, no added sounds, no murmurs audible."

E. LUNG BASES

  • "I'm going to listen to the back of your chest now. Can you sit forward for me?"
  • Auscultate posterior lung bases for:

- Crackles/crepitations (pulmonary oedema from heart failure) - Pleural effusion (reduced breath sounds at base)

Say out loud: "Lung bases are clear, no crepitations."

F. PERIPHERAL OEDEMA & PULSES

Ankle Oedema:
  • "I'm going to check your ankles for swelling."
  • Press firmly over medial malleolus for 5 seconds
  • Pitting oedema (heart failure, venous insufficiency, hypoalbuminaemia)
  • If present, check how far up leg it extends (sacral oedema if bedbound)

Peripheral Pulses (if time permits):
  • Femoral pulses: "I'm going to feel the pulses in your groin."

- Check for radio-femoral delay (coarctation of aorta) - Auscultate for bruits (peripheral vascular disease)

  • Posterior tibialis & dorsalis pedis: Check for peripheral vascular disease

Say out loud: "No peripheral oedema. Peripheral pulses palpable."

5. PRESENT (60-90 seconds)

"Thank you, I've completed the examination. I'll just cover you up and let you get dressed."

To Examiner: Present structured findings:

"On examination of the cardiovascular system:

  • General inspection: The patient appeared comfortable at rest, no cyanosis or distress
  • Hands: No clubbing, no splinter haemorrhages, capillary refill <2 seconds
  • Pulse: 72 beats per minute, regular, normal volume, no radio-radial delay
  • Blood pressure: 120/80 mmHg
  • Face: No anaemia, no central cyanosis
  • JVP: Not elevated at 2cm above the sternal angle
  • Apex beat: Located 5th intercostal space, mid-clavicular line, normal character
  • Heart sounds: Dual heart sounds, no added sounds, no murmurs
  • Lung bases: Clear
  • Peripheral oedema: Absent

Summary: This is a normal cardiovascular examination." If Abnormal Finding:

"I found [abnormality]. This is consistent with [diagnosis]. I would like to complete my examination by:

  • Taking a full history
  • Reviewing an ECG
  • Checking an echocardiogram
  • Reviewing medications"

Common IMG Mistakes ‚ùå

  1. ‚ùå Not washing hands - ALWAYS start with hand hygiene
  2. ‚ùå No introduction/consent - Essential in Australian OSCE
  3. ‚ùå Forgetting to position at 45 degrees - Cannot assess JVP properly otherwise
  4. ‚ùå Palpating both carotids simultaneously - DANGEROUS, can cause syncope
  5. ‚ùå Not timing murmurs with carotid pulse - Need to know if systolic or diastolic
  6. ‚ùå Forgetting dynamic manoeuvres - Left lateral position for MS, leaning forward for AR
  7. ‚ùå Not checking lung bases - Important sign of heart failure
  8. ‚ùå Poor presentation - Structure: General ‚Üí Hands ‚Üí Pulse ‚Üí BP ‚Üí JVP ‚Üí Praecordium ‚Üí Peripheries
  9. ‚ùå Not offering chaperone - Important in Australian clinical practice
  10. ‚ùå Rough examination - Always gentle, explain what you're doing

RESPIRATORY EXAMINATION

1. PREPARATION (60 seconds)

Hand Hygiene: Wash hands or use alcohol gel Introduction & Consent:
  • "Good morning, my name is Dr [Name]. I've been asked to examine your chest and lungs."
  • "This will involve examining your hands, neck, and chest. I'll need you to remove your shirt. Is that okay?"
  • "Please let me know if you feel any discomfort."

Offer Chaperone: "Would you like a chaperone present?" Check for Pain: "Are you experiencing any chest pain or discomfort?"

2. POSITION

  • "I'd like you to sit up at 45 degrees, or on the edge of the bed if you're able."
  • Patient should be sitting upright (or at 45 degrees if unable)
  • Good exposure: Chest exposed from neck to waist (maintain dignity)
  • Stand on patient's right side

3. PERMISSION

  • "I'm going to examine your hands first, then your neck and chest, front and back. Is that alright?"

4. PERFORM (6 minutes)

A. GENERAL INSPECTION

From End of Bed:
  • General appearance: Comfortable or distressed? Using accessory muscles? Pursed-lip breathing?
  • Respiratory rate: Count for 30 seconds (normal 12-20 breaths/min)
  • Cyanosis: Blue lips/tongue (central cyanosis = hypoxia)
  • Cachexia: Weight loss (malignancy, COPD)
  • Equipment: Oxygen (note flow rate/delivery device), inhalers, nebuliser, sputum pot

Say out loud: "The patient appears comfortable at rest, respiratory rate 16 breaths per minute, no use of accessory muscles, no cyanosis."

B. HANDS & ARMS

Inspect Hands:
  • "I'm going to examine your hands."
  • Clubbing: Loss of nail bed angle (lung cancer, bronchiectasis, cystic fibrosis, pulmonary fibrosis)

- Check with Schamroth's window test

  • Tar staining: Nicotine stains on fingers (smoking)
  • Cyanosis: Peripheral (blue fingertips) vs central (blue tongue)
  • Tremor: "Hold your arms out straight with hands flat." (Fine tremor = salbutamol overuse, CO‚ÇÇ retention)
  • Wasting: Small muscle wasting (Pancoast tumour affecting T1)

Radial Pulse:
  • Check rate and rhythm
  • Bounding pulse: CO‚ÇÇ retention (COPD)

Say out loud: "No clubbing, no tar staining, no tremor, pulse 78 regular."

C. FACE & NECK

Face:
  • "I'm going to examine your face and eyes."
  • Eyes: Pull down lower lid - check for anaemia (chronic disease, malignancy)
  • Horner's syndrome: Ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis (Pancoast tumour)
  • Central cyanosis: Lift tongue - blue = hypoxia
  • Pursed-lip breathing: COPD

Neck:
  • JVP: Elevated in cor pulmonale (right heart failure from chronic lung disease)
  • Lymph nodes: "I'm going to feel the glands in your neck."

- Palpate cervical and supraclavicular nodes (lung cancer metastases) - Technique: Stand behind patient, use fingertips, systematic approach

  • Trachea: "I'm going to check if your windpipe is central."

- Place index finger in sternal notch, feel for trachea - Deviation: Away from tension pneumothorax, towards collapse/fibrosis

Say out loud: "No anaemia, no central cyanosis, JVP not elevated, no lymphadenopathy, trachea central."

D. ANTERIOR CHEST

Inspection:
  • "I'm going to examine the front of your chest now."
  • Scars: Thoracotomy, chest drains
  • Shape: Barrel chest (COPD - increased AP diameter), pectus excavatum, kyphoscoliosis
  • Movement: Symmetrical chest expansion? Paradoxical movement (flail chest)?
  • Skin changes: Radiotherapy marks, bruising

Palpation:
  • "I'm going to feel your chest now."
  • Chest expansion:

- Place hands on lower chest with thumbs meeting in midline - "Take a deep breath in." - Thumbs should move apart symmetrically >5cm - Reduced expansion: COPD, pleural effusion, pneumothorax - Asymmetrical expansion: Pneumothorax, consolidation, collapse, pleural effusion (on affected side)

Say out loud: "Chest expansion is symmetrical and adequate."
  • Tactile vocal fremitus (optional, can skip if time limited):

- "I'm going to ask you to say 'ninety-nine' while I feel your chest." - Place ulnar edge of hand on chest in several positions - Increased: Consolidation (solid lung transmits vibrations better) - Decreased: Pleural effusion, pneumothorax, collapse (air/fluid blocks vibrations)

Percussion:
  • "I'm going to tap on your chest now - it won't hurt."
  • Technique:

- Place middle finger of left hand firmly on chest (perpendicular to ribs) - Tap with middle finger of right hand (wrist action, not arm) - Percuss 6 areas on each side (upper, mid, lower zones - compare left vs right)

  • Normal: Resonant (hollow sound)
  • Hyper-resonant: Pneumothorax, emphysema
  • Dull: Consolidation, pleural effusion, collapse
  • Stony dull: Large pleural effusion

Say out loud: "Percussion note is resonant throughout both lung fields." Auscultation:
  • "I'm going to listen to your chest now with the stethoscope. Please breathe in and out deeply through your mouth."
  • Warm stethoscope first
  • Use diaphragm of stethoscope
  • Listen in same 6 areas as percussion (compare left vs right)
  • Listen for:

- Breath sounds: Normal vesicular (rustling), reduced (effusion, pneumothorax), bronchial (consolidation - harsh, tubular, louder on expiration) - Added sounds: - Crackles (crepitations): Fine = pulmonary fibrosis, pulmonary oedema; Coarse = bronchiectasis, pneumonia - Wheeze: Expiratory = asthma/COPD (airway obstruction); Inspiratory = upper airway obstruction - Pleural rub: Sounds like creaking leather (pleurisy, PE) - Stridor: Harsh inspiratory sound (upper airway obstruction - not heard on auscultation, heard at bedside)

Say out loud: "Breath sounds are vesicular, equal on both sides, no added sounds." Vocal Resonance (if abnormality detected):
  • "Can you say 'ninety-nine' while I listen?"
  • Listen with stethoscope in areas of abnormality
  • Increased: Consolidation (solid lung transmits sound better)
  • Decreased: Effusion, pneumothorax, collapse

E. POSTERIOR CHEST

"I'm going to examine your back now. Can you sit forward and cross your arms in front of you?" (Opens up scapulae)

Repeat same sequence:

  1. Inspection: Scars, deformity
  2. Palpation: Chest expansion (place hands on lower back, thumbs on spine)
  3. Percussion: 6 areas each side (compare left vs right)
  4. Auscultation: Same 6 areas, listen for breath sounds and added sounds

Say out loud: "Posterior chest examination: symmetrical expansion, resonant percussion, vesicular breath sounds, no added sounds."

F. COMPLETE THE EXAMINATION

"To complete my examination, I would like to:

  • Check oxygen saturations
  • Examine sputum (colour, consistency, blood)
  • Review peak flow or spirometry results
  • Look at a chest X-ray"

5. PRESENT (60-90 seconds)

"Thank you, I've finished. You can get dressed now."

To Examiner:

"On examination of the respiratory system:

  • General: The patient was comfortable at rest, respiratory rate 16/min, no accessory muscle use
  • Hands: No clubbing, no tar staining, no tremor
  • Face: No anaemia, no central cyanosis
  • Neck: Trachea central, no lymphadenopathy, JVP not elevated
  • Chest:

- Inspection: Normal chest shape, symmetrical movement - Palpation: Chest expansion symmetrical and adequate - Percussion: Resonant throughout - Auscultation: Vesicular breath sounds bilaterally, no added sounds

Summary: This is a normal respiratory examination." If Abnormal Finding Example (Right Lower Lobe Pneumonia):

"On examination:

  • Inspection: Patient appears mildly unwell, respiratory rate 22/min
  • Palpation: Reduced chest expansion on the right lower zone
  • Percussion: Dull percussion note over right lower zone
  • Auscultation: Bronchial breath sounds over right lower zone with coarse crackles, increased vocal resonance

Summary: These findings are consistent with right lower lobe consolidation, likely pneumonia. I would like to complete my examination by checking oxygen saturations, examining sputum, and reviewing a chest X-ray."

TENSION PNEUMOTHORAX - Immediate Life-Threatening Emergency

Classic Presentation:

  • Unilateral reduced/absent breath sounds
  • Tracheal deviation AWAY from affected side (late sign - high mortality if delayed)
  • Hyperresonance to percussion on affected side
  • Hypotension (obstructed venous return to heart)
  • Tachycardia
  • Distended neck veins (JVP raised)
  • Respiratory distress
  • Reduced chest expansion on affected side

IMMEDIATE MANAGEMENT (Do NOT wait for chest X-ray):

  1. IMMEDIATE needle decompression: 14-16G cannula, 2nd intercostal space, mid-clavicular line on affected side
  2. Insert needle perpendicular to chest wall - will hear "hiss" of air escaping
  3. Leave cannula in place (remove needle)
  4. Followed by chest drain insertion (5th intercostal space, mid-axillary line - "safe triangle")
  5. Call for senior help/emergency team

KEY DIFFERENTIATION:

Finding Simple Pneumothorax TENSION Pneumothorax
Tracheal position Central or slightly deviated DEVIATED AWAY from affected side
Haemodynamics Stable BP and HR Hypotension, tachycardia
Neck veins Normal JVP Distended (raised JVP)
Urgency Urgent (hours) IMMEDIATE (minutes to death)

OSCE KEY POINT: If you find unilateral reduced breath sounds + tracheal deviation + hypotension in an examination, you MUST state: "These findings are concerning for TENSION PNEUMOTHORAX which is a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate needle decompression. I would call for senior help immediately and prepare for needle decompression of the 2nd intercostal space mid-clavicular line on the [affected] side."

Common IMG Mistakes ‚ùå

  1. ‚ùå Not counting respiratory rate - Simple but often forgotten
  2. ‚ùå Not checking for clubbing properly - Must use Schamroth's test
  3. ‚ùå Forgetting to check trachea - Important for pneumothorax/collapse
  4. ‚ùå Poor percussion technique - Need firm finger on chest, wrist flick
  5. ‚ùå Not comparing left vs right - Must always compare symmetry
  6. ‚ùå Forgetting posterior examination - Most lung pathology is in lower/posterior zones
  7. ‚ùå Not exposing patient properly - Can't see chest movement with shirt on
  8. ‚ùå Asking patient to breathe too fast - Can cause hyperventilation/dizziness
  9. ‚ùå Not presenting systematically - Always: Inspection ‚Üí Palpation ‚Üí Percussion ‚Üí Auscultation
  10. ‚ùå Forgetting to mention "complete examination" - Always offer to check sats, sputum, CXR

EXAMINATION MNEMONICS

Cardiovascular: "HIPJAP"

  • Hands (clubbing, splinters, pulses)
  • Inspection (general, scars)
  • Pulses (radial, brachial, carotid)
  • JVP
  • Apex beat & palpation
  • Praecordium auscultation (+ lung bases, oedema)

Respiratory: "IPTAP"

  • Inspection (general, hands, trachea)
  • Palpation (expansion, fremitus)
  • Tactile fremitus (optional)
  • Auscultation
  • Percussion

(Percussion and auscultation can be swapped - just be systematic)
