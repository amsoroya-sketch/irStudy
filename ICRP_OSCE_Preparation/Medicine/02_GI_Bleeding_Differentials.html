Gastrointestinal Bleeding: Differential Diagnosis and Management

ICRP OSCE Preparation - Medicine Module (Gastroenterology)

Target Audience

This guide is for International Medical Graduates (IMGs) preparing for the AMC Clinical OSCE, specifically:

  • IMGs requiring systematic approach to upper and lower GI bleeding
  • Candidates needing risk stratification tools (Glasgow-Blatchford, Rockall scores)
  • Doctors preparing for ICRP placement in Australian ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY Departments
  • Medical professionals needing resuscitation protocols for acute GI bleeding
  • Clinicians requiring Australian endoscopy and management guidelines (eTG 2024)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  1. Differentiate upper GI bleeding from lower GI bleeding based on presentation
  2. Generate differential diagnoses for UGIB and LGIB from first presentation
  3. Risk stratify patients using validated scores (Glasgow-Blatchford, Rockall)
  4. Resuscitate patients with massive GI bleeding (massive transfusion protocol)
  5. Determine timing of endoscopy (URGENT vs routine)
  6. Manage common causes according to Australian guidelines (eTG 2024)
  7. Identify when to call surgery/interventional radiology
  8. Perform confidently in OSCE stations for GI bleeding presentations

Dr. Amir Soufi's Approach Applied to GI Bleeding

Core Principle: LOCALIZE (Upper vs Lower) ‚Üí RISK STRATIFY ‚Üí RESUSCITATE ‚Üí INVESTIGATE

Traditional Approach (Less Effective):
  • Generic history for all GI bleeding
  • Consider diagnosis after full workup
  • Delayed risk stratification
  • Conservative resuscitation

Dr. Soufi's Differential-Driven Approach:
  1. LOCALIZE IMMEDIATELY (Upper vs Lower GI bleeding):

- Upper GI (proximal to ligament of Treitz): Hematemesis, coffee-ground vomiting, melena - Lower GI (distal to ligament of Treitz): Fresh red blood PR, maroon stools - Note: Massive UGIB can present with red blood PR (rapid transit)

  1. DIFFERENTIAL FROM PRESENTATION:

- UGIB + hemodynamic instability ‚Üí Variceal bleed, peptic ulcer with visible vessel - UGIB + epigastric pain ‚Üí Peptic ulcer - UGIB + vomiting then hematemesis ‚Üí Mallory-Weiss tear - LGIB + pain ‚Üí Ischemic colitis, IBD, diverticulitis - LGIB painless ‚Üí Diverticular bleed, angiodysplasia, hemorrhoids

  1. RISK STRATIFY IMMEDIATELY (Glasgow-Blatchford Score - GBS):

- GBS = 0: Safe for outpatient management - GBS ‚â•1: Needs admission, intervention likely - Guides need for URGENT endoscopy, ICU admission, transfusion

  1. RESUSCITATE AGGRESSIVELY (if unstable):

- Two large-bore IV cannulae (16G or larger) - Massive transfusion protocol if shock (blood products 1:1:1 ratio) - Permissive hypotension initially (SBP 90-100) until bleeding controlled - Reverse anticoagulation (if on warfarin, DOAC)

  1. TARGETED INVESTIGATION:

- URGENT endoscopy (<24 hours) if high-risk (hemodynamic instability, high GBS) - Routine endoscopy if stable, low-risk - CT angiogram if massive ongoing bleed and endoscopy non-diagnostic

  1. DEFINITIVE MANAGEMENT:

- Endoscopic therapy (clips, adrenaline injection, thermal coagulation) - Surgery if endoscopic failure - Interventional radiology (embolization) if surgical risk high

Example:
  • Presentation: 65-year-old man, vomited fresh red blood (hematemesis), BP 85/50, HR 120, history of NSAIDs for arthritis
  • Immediate thought: Upper GI bleeding (hematemesis), likely peptic ulcer (NSAID use), HIGH RISK (hemodynamic instability)
  • Immediate actions: Resuscitate (2x large IV cannulae, crossmatch 6 units, massive transfusion if ongoing bleeding), calculate GBS (high - needs URGENT intervention), URGENT gastroscopy (<24 hours, ideally <12 hours)
  • Differential: Peptic ulcer (most likely), gastric erosions, esophagitis, Mallory-Weiss (but no vomiting before hematemesis)

1. UPPER GI BLEEDING (UGIB)

Definition

Bleeding from source proximal to ligament of Treitz (duodenojejunal junction):

  • Esophagus
  • Stomach
  • Duodenum

Clinical Presentation

Hematemesis: Vomiting blood
  • Fresh red blood (active bleeding, large volume)
  • Coffee-ground vomitus (blood exposed to gastric acid, less active bleeding)

Melena: Black, tarry, foul-smelling stools
  • Represents digested blood (usually >50-100ml blood loss)
  • Takes 8-24 hours for blood to transit to rectum
  • Classic UGIB presentation

Hematochezia: Fresh red blood per rectum
  • Usually LGIB, BUT can be massive UGIB with rapid transit
  • If hemodynamically unstable + fresh red blood PR ‚Üí Consider massive UGIB (rule out with upper endoscopy)

Other presentations:
  • Syncope, dizziness (volume loss)
  • Dyspnea, chest pain (anemia, cardiac ischemia)
  • Confusion (elderly - hypovolemia)

Differential Diagnosis for UGIB

Common Causes (Account for >90% of UGIB):
  1. Peptic Ulcer Disease (35-50% of UGIB):

- Duodenal ulcer (most common site) - Gastric ulcer - Risk factors: NSAIDs, aspirin, H. pylori, steroids, smoking, alcohol, stress (ICU patients) - Presentation: Hematemesis, melena, epigastric pain (may be absent in NSAID-induced), anemia - High-risk features: Age >60, shock, comorbidities, anticoagulation - Endoscopy: Ulcer with active bleeding (spurting/oozing), visible vessel, adherent clot - Management: Endoscopic therapy (adrenaline injection + clips/thermal coagulation), high-dose PPI (esomeprazole 80mg IV bolus then 8mg/hr infusion x 72 hours), H. pylori eradication if positive, stop NSAIDs

  1. Variceal Bleeding (Esophageal or Gastric Varices) (15-20% of UGIB):

- Cause: Portal hypertension from liver cirrhosis (alcohol, viral hepatitis, NAFLD) - Presentation: Large volume hematemesis, known cirrhosis, stigmata of chronic liver disease (jaundice, spider nevi, ascites, splenomegaly, palmar erythema) - HIGH MORTALITY (15-20%) - Investigations: FBC (thrombocytopenia - hypersplenism), LFTs (deranged), coagulation (elevated INR - liver dysfunction) - Management: - Resuscitation: Cautious fluids (over-resuscitation worsens portal pressure and re-bleeding) - Vasoactive drugs: Octreotide 50mcg bolus then 50mcg/hr infusion (reduces portal pressure) - start BEFORE endoscopy - Antibiotics: Ceftriaxone 1g IV daily (reduces infection, mortality in cirrhotic patients) - start on admission - URGENT endoscopy (<12 hours): Band ligation (esophageal varices) or tissue glue (gastric varices) - Sengstaken-Blakemore tube (balloon tamponade) if uncontrolled bleeding (temporary measure) - TIPSS (Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt) if endoscopy fails

  1. Mallory-Weiss Tear (5-15% of UGIB):

- Cause: Mucosal tear at gastroesophageal junction from forceful vomiting/retching - Classic history: Vomiting (non-bloody) ‚Üí hematemesis (blood after initial vomits) - Associated: Alcohol binge, hyperemesis gravidarum, eating disorders - Usually self-limiting (90% stop bleeding spontaneously) - Endoscopy: Linear mucosal tear at GE junction - Management: Usually supportive, endoscopic therapy if active bleeding

  1. Erosive Gastritis / Esophagitis (10-15%):

- Causes: NSAIDs, alcohol, stress (ICU), H. pylori - Presentation: Usually minor bleeding (coffee-ground vomitus, melena), epigastric discomfort - Management: PPI, stop NSAIDs/alcohol, supportive

  1. Esophageal / Gastric Malignancy (2-5%):

- Presentation: Hematemesis, melena, weight loss, dysphagia, anemia - Alarm symptoms: Age >55, weight loss, dysphagia, persistent vomiting, early satiety - Endoscopy: Ulcerated mass - Management: Endoscopic hemostasis, oncology referral, palliative if advanced

  1. Dieulafoy Lesion (1-2%):

- Pathology: Aberrant submucosal artery that erodes through mucosa (usually stomach) - Presentation: Massive hematemesis, no preceding symptoms, difficult to find on endoscopy (small lesion) - Management: Endoscopic therapy (clips, thermal), surgery if fails

  1. Aortoenteric Fistula (Rare but LETHAL):

- Cause: Communication between aorta and duodenum - Risk factors: Previous aortic graft surgery, AAA - Presentation: "Herald bleed" (small UGIB) followed hours-days later by massive exsanguinating hemorrhage - High index of suspicion in patient with aortic graft + any GI bleeding - Management: URGENT vascular surgery

  • GBS 1-5: Moderate risk - admit, likely needs endoscopy
  • GBS 6-11: High risk - URGENT intervention likely (endoscopy, transfusion)
  • GBS ‚â•12: Very high risk - ICU, URGENT endoscopy

CRITICAL: GBS performed at presentation guides initial management

Full Rockall Score (includes endoscopy findings):

Score:
  • 0-2: Low risk (mortality <5%, re-bleed <5%)
  • 3-4: Moderate risk
  • 5-7: High risk (mortality >10%, re-bleed >10%)
  • ‚â•8: Very high risk (mortality >40%)

Management of UGIB

Resuscitation (ABCDE Approach)

A - Airway:
  • Protect airway if massive hematemesis, reduced GCS
  • Intubation if hemodynamic instability + ongoing hematemesis (risk of aspiration)

B - Breathing:
  • Oxygen if hypoxic (target SpO2 94-98%)

C - Circulation: IV Access:
  • Two large-bore IV cannulae (14G or 16G) - peripherally
  • Central line if difficult IV access, massive transfusion, vasopressors needed

Bloods:
  • FBC: Hemoglobin (may be normal acutely before hemodilution), platelets
  • Coagulation (INR, APTT): If on anticoagulation, liver disease
  • UEC: Urea (elevated in UGIB - protein digestion), creatinine (renal function)
  • LFTs: Liver disease (varices?)
  • Lactate: Tissue perfusion
  • Group and crossmatch: 6 units if severe bleeding

Fluid Resuscitation:
  • Target: SBP 90-100 mmHg initially (permissive hypotension) until bleeding controlled

- Rationale: Over-resuscitation increases bleeding (increased pressure dislodges clot)

  • Crystalloid: 0.9% NaCl or Hartmann's - 250-500ml boluses as needed
  • Blood products: If ongoing bleeding, shock

Transfusion:
  • Packed Red Blood Cells (PRBC):

- Target Hb >70 g/L (restrictive strategy - evidence-based for UGIB) - Target Hb >80 g/L if active cardiac disease - Caution in variceal bleeding: Over-transfusion worsens portal pressure and re-bleeding

  • Platelets: If platelets <50 x 10^9/L and active bleeding
  • Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP): If INR >1.5 and active bleeding
  • Cryoprecipitate: If fibrinogen <1.5 g/L

Massive Transfusion Protocol (if exsanguinating hemorrhage):
  • Definition: >4 units PRBC in 1 hour, or >10 units in 24 hours
  • Blood products in 1:1:1 ratio: PRBC : FFP : Platelets (e.g., 6 units PRBC : 6 units FFP : 1 pool platelets)
  • Activate protocol early (call blood bank, ICU, surgery)
  • Calcium: Replace (diluted by citrate in transfused blood)
  • Warm blood products (prevent hypothermia)

Reverse Anticoagulation:
  • Warfarin: Vitamin K 10mg IV + Prothrombinex (prothrombin complex concentrate - PCC) 25-50 units/kg
  • DOACs (dabigatran, rivaroxaban, apixaban):

- Idarucizumab (dabigatran reversal) - Andexanet alfa (rivaroxaban, apixaban reversal) - if available - Otherwise: Prothrombinex, supportive

  • Aspirin/clopidogrel: No specific reversal, platelet transfusion if severe bleeding

D - Disability: GCS, blood glucose E - Exposure: Look for stigmata of chronic liver disease (varices?), abdominal examination

Medications

Proton Pump Inhibitor (PPI):
  • High-dose IV PPI: Esomeprazole 80mg IV bolus then 8mg/hr continuous infusion x 72 hours
  • Start immediately on presentation (before endoscopy)
  • Reduces re-bleeding, need for surgery in peptic ulcer bleeding
  • After 72 hours: Switch to oral PPI (esomeprazole 40mg BD x 2 weeks, then daily x 4-8 weeks)

Vasoactive Drugs (if suspected variceal bleeding):
  • Octreotide 50mcg IV bolus then 50mcg/hr infusion
  • Reduces splanchnic blood flow and portal pressure
  • Start before endoscopy if cirrhosis suspected

Antibiotics (if suspected variceal bleeding):
  • Ceftriaxone 1g IV daily x 7 days
  • Reduces bacterial infections, mortality in cirrhotic patients
  • Start on admission

Prokinetic (to improve endoscopic visualization):
  • Erythromycin 250mg IV 30-60 minutes before endoscopy
  • Promotes gastric emptying, clears blood clots, improves view

Endoscopy Timing

URGENT Endoscopy (<12-24 hours):
  • Hemodynamic instability (shock, ongoing bleeding despite resuscitation)
  • High Glasgow-Blatchford Score (‚â•12)
  • Suspected variceal bleeding (cirrhosis)
  • High-risk features: Age >65, comorbidities, anticoagulation

Routine Endoscopy (within 24 hours):
  • Stable patient, low-risk GBS
  • Most UGIB patients

Pre-endoscopy:
  • NBM (nil by mouth) 6 hours before
  • Consent (explain procedure, risks)
  • Correct coagulopathy (INR, platelets)
  • Erythromycin 250mg IV (30-60 min before)

Endoscopic Therapy

Findings and Management:
  1. Active bleeding (spurting, oozing):

- Adrenaline injection (1:10,000) into ulcer base + clips or thermal coagulation - DO NOT use adrenaline alone (must combine with second modality - clips/thermal)

  1. Visible vessel (non-bleeding):

- Endoscopic therapy (clips, thermal)

  1. Adherent clot:

- Attempt to remove clot (irrigation, forceps) - If visible vessel underneath ‚Üí endoscopic therapy

  1. Flat pigmented spot or clean base:

- Low re-bleeding risk - No endoscopic therapy needed - Oral PPI, early discharge

  1. Esophageal varices:

- Band ligation (rubber bands placed around varices) - May need multiple sessions

  1. Gastric varices:

- Tissue glue (N-butyl cyanoacrylate) injection

Post-Endoscopy Management

If successful endoscopic hemostasis:
  • Continue high-dose PPI (esomeprazole 8mg/hr x 72 hours)
  • NBM initially, then clear fluids if stable, advance diet as tolerated
  • Monitor: Hemoglobin (drop may indicate re-bleeding), vital signs, urine output
  • Repeat endoscopy (second-look) at 24 hours if high-risk lesion (some centers)
  • H. pylori testing: Urea breath test, stool antigen, or biopsy at endoscopy
  • H. pylori eradication if positive: Triple therapy (PPI + amoxicillin + clarithromycin x 7 days - eTG 2024)
  • Stop NSAIDs permanently if possible, or use COX-2 inhibitor + PPI if essential
  • Resume anticoagulation/antiplatelet: Discuss with cardiology - timing depends on indication (usually 3-7 days if high-risk ulcer, earlier if prosthetic valve, recent stent)

If endoscopic failure (ongoing bleeding despite therapy):
  • Repeat endoscopy (different operator, different technique)
  • Interventional radiology: Angiography + embolization
  • Surgery: Laparotomy, oversew bleeding vessel, partial gastrectomy

- Indications: Endoscopic failure, massive transfusion (>6 units), hemodynamic instability - High mortality (10-30%) - last resort

üö® RED FLAGs in UGIB

  • Hemodynamic instability (SBP <90, HR >100) ‚Üí High-risk, URGENT endoscopy, ICU
  • Age >65 ‚Üí Higher mortality, re-bleeding risk
  • Comorbidities (cardiac, renal, liver disease) ‚Üí Higher mortality
  • Known cirrhosis ‚Üí Suspect varices, start octreotide + ceftriaxone
  • Anticoagulation ‚Üí Reverse, higher bleeding risk
  • Previous aortic surgery + any GI bleeding ‚Üí Aortoenteric fistula (URGENT vascular surgery)
  • Re-bleeding after endoscopy ‚Üí Repeat endoscopy, IR, surgery

2. LOWER GI BLEEDING (LGIB)

Definition

Bleeding from source distal to ligament of Treitz:

  • Small bowel (jejunum, ileum) - less common
  • Colon
  • Rectum
  • Anus

Clinical Presentation

Hematochezia: Fresh red blood per rectum
  • Most common presentation of LGIB
  • Suggests bleeding from left colon, rectum, anus (blood hasn't been digested)
  • Note: Massive UGIB can also present with hematochezia (rapid transit)

Maroon stools: Dark red/maroon stools
  • Suggests right colon or small bowel bleeding (partial digestion)

Melena: Black, tarry stools
  • Usually UGIB, but can be LGIB if bleeding from right colon with slow transit

Other presentations:
  • Occult bleeding (positive fecal occult blood test, iron-deficiency anemia)
  • Hemodynamic instability (if severe)

Differential Diagnosis for LGIB

Common Causes:
  1. Diverticular Bleeding (30-40% of LGIB):

- Most common cause of significant LGIB in elderly - Pathology: Rupture of vasa recta adjacent to diverticulum - Presentation: Sudden onset painless bright red blood PR, usually large volume, age >60 - Location: Usually left colon (sigmoid, descending) in Western countries - Usually self-limiting (80% stop spontaneously) - Investigations: Colonoscopy (identify bleeding diverticulum, but often stopped by time of scope), CT angiogram if active bleeding - Management: - Resuscitation - Colonoscopy: If active bleeding seen ‚Üí endoscopic therapy (clips, epinephrine injection) - CT angiogram + embolization if massive ongoing bleeding - Surgery (segmental colectomy) if recurrent, failed endoscopy/IR

  1. Angiodysplasia (Arteriovenous Malformations) (10-15%):

- Pathology: Degenerative vascular ectasias (dilated, fragile blood vessels in bowel wall) - Location: Usually right colon (cecum, ascending) - Presentation: Recurrent painless bright red or maroon blood PR, usually less severe than diverticular, elderly, associated with aortic stenosis, chronic kidney disease - Endoscopy: Small (few mm) red lesions with feeding vessel - Management: - Colonoscopy with thermal coagulation (argon plasma coagulation) - Recurrent bleeding common - Surgery if recurrent, severe

  1. Ischemic Colitis (10-15%):

- Pathology: Reduced blood flow to colon ‚Üí mucosal ischemia, ulceration - Location: "Watershed areas" - splenic flexure, rectosigmoid junction (between vascular territories) - Risk factors: Age >60, vascular disease, AF, hypotension, aortic surgery, medications (NSAIDs, cocaine) - Presentation: Sudden onset left lower abdominal pain + bloody diarrhea (key: PAINFUL unlike diverticular) - Investigations: - CT abdomen: Segmental colonic wall thickening, mesenteric stranding - Colonoscopy (when stable): Segmental erythema, ulceration, thumbprinting - Management: - Usually self-limiting (mild cases) - Supportive: IV fluids, bowel rest, antibiotics if severe (ceftriaxone + metronidazole - concern for bacterial translocation) - Surgery if transmural infarction, perforation, peritonitis (rare)

  1. Inflammatory Bowel Disease - IBD (Ulcerative Colitis, Crohn's) (5-10%):

- Ulcerative Colitis: Bloody diarrhea (hallmark), urgency, tenesmus, LLQ pain, continuous inflammation from rectum - Crohn's Disease: Bloody diarrhea (if colonic involvement), RLQ pain, skip lesions - Presentation: Young adults, chronic/recurrent bloody diarrhea, weight loss, systemic symptoms - Investigations: Elevated CRP, anemia, low albumin, fecal calprotectin elevated, colonoscopy + biopsy - Management: Gastroenterology, 5-ASA, corticosteroids (flare), immunosuppressants

  1. Colorectal Cancer (5-10%):

- Presentation: PR bleeding, change in bowel habit, weight loss, iron-deficiency anemia, abdominal pain/mass - Alarm symptoms: Age >50, new-onset change in bowel habit, weight loss, family history - Investigations: FBC (anemia), CEA (tumor marker), colonoscopy + biopsy (diagnostic) - Management: Surgical resection (right/left hemicolectomy, anterior resection), oncology (chemotherapy if advanced)

  1. Hemorrhoids (Internal) (Very Common but Usually Minor):

- Presentation: Bright red blood on toilet paper, dripping into pan, painless (unless thrombosed external hemorrhoids) - Associated: Straining, constipation, pregnancy - NOT a cause of hemodynamic instability (if unstable, look elsewhere) - Examination: May be visible on inspection, proctoscopy - Management: Fiber, stool softeners, topical analgesia, rubber band ligation if persistent, surgical hemorrhoidectomy if severe

  1. Anal Fissure (Very Common but Usually Minor):

- Presentation: Bright red blood on toilet paper, severe pain with bowel motion (unlike hemorrhoids) - Examination: Linear tear in anoderm (usually posterior midline) - Management: Fiber, stool softeners, topical GTN or diltiazem, surgical lateral sphincterotomy if chronic

  1. Small Bowel Bleeding (5-10% of obscure GI bleeding):

- Difficult to diagnose (beyond reach of standard upper and lower endoscopy) - Causes: Angiodysplasia, NSAIDs, Crohn's, tumors (rare) - Investigations: Capsule endoscopy, CT enterography, double-balloon enteroscopy

  1. Meckel's Diverticulum (Children and Young Adults):

- Rule of 2s: 2% of population, 2 feet from ileocecal valve, 2 types of ectopic tissue (gastric, pancreatic), presents age <2 (usually) - Presentation: Painless maroon/bright red blood PR in child or young adult - Investigations: Technetium-99m scan (Meckel's scan - detects ectopic gastric mucosa) - Management: Surgical resection

  1. Infectious Colitis (Campylobacter, Shigella, E. coli O157:H7):

- Presentation: Bloody diarrhea, fever, cramping abdominal pain, recent travel/food poisoning - Investigations: Stool MCS (culture for Campylobacter, Shigella, Salmonella), stool PCR (E. coli O157:H7) - Management: Supportive (oral rehydration), antibiotics if severe (ciprofloxacin) - AVOID in E. coli O157:H7 (increases HUS risk)

Risk Stratification for LGIB

No validated score like Glasgow-Blatchford for LGIB, but risk factors for severe bleeding: High-risk features:
  • Hemodynamic instability (SBP <100, HR >100)
  • Ongoing bleeding (repeated bloody stools in hospital)
  • Comorbidities (cardiac, renal disease)
  • Anticoagulation
  • Age >60
  • Elevated blood urea (suggests significant bleeding)

Low-risk features:
  • Hemodynamically stable
  • Minor bleeding (streaks of blood, on toilet paper only)
  • Young, otherwise healthy
  • Likely hemorrhoidal source

Management of LGIB

Resuscitation (Similar to UGIB)

ABCDE approach:
  • Airway, Breathing: Oxygen if needed
  • Circulation:

- Two large-bore IV cannulae - Bloods: FBC, coagulation, UEC, LFTs, lactate, group and crossmatch - IV fluids: Crystalloid boluses as needed (target SBP >100) - Transfusion: PRBC if Hb <70 (or <80 if cardiac disease), platelets if <50, reverse anticoagulation if needed

Investigation

First: Exclude UGIB:
  • NG tube aspiration: If fresh red blood PR + hemodynamic instability

- If bile-stained aspirate (no blood) ‚Üí likely LGIB - If blood in aspirate ‚Üí UGIB (but not 100% sensitive)

  • Upper endoscopy if any doubt (massive UGIB can present as hematochezia)

Colonoscopy:
  • Timing:

- URGENT (<24 hours) if high-risk (ongoing bleeding, hemodynamic instability) - Routine (elective, after bowel preparation) if stable, low-risk

  • Preparation: Polyethylene glycol (PEG) solution - rapid prep (3-4L over 2-4 hours)
  • Findings: May identify bleeding source (diverticulum, angiodysplasia, tumor, colitis)
  • Therapy: Endoscopic clips, thermal coagulation (if active bleeding seen)

CT Angiogram:
  • Indication: Massive ongoing LGIB, hemodynamic instability, failed/non-diagnostic colonoscopy
  • Requires active bleeding (>0.5-1 ml/min) to detect extravasation
  • Advantage: Non-invasive, localizes bleeding site, can guide IR or surgery
  • Disadvantage: Requires active bleeding at time of scan

Interventional Radiology (IR) - Angiography + Embolization:
  • Indication: Ongoing bleeding identified on CT angiogram, failed endoscopic therapy
  • Procedure: Selective catheterization of mesenteric vessels, embolize bleeding vessel with coils/gel foam
  • Complications: Bowel ischemia (risk higher in LGIB than UGIB due to less collateral circulation)

Surgery:
  • Indications: Failed endoscopy and IR, massive transfusion (>6 units), hemodynamic instability despite resuscitation
  • Procedure: Segmental colectomy (if bleeding site localized), subtotal colectomy (if site unknown)
  • Morbidity/mortality: Higher than endoscopy/IR (especially ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY surgery in elderly)

Specific Management

Diverticular Bleeding:
  • Resuscitation
  • Colonoscopy (identify, endoscopic therapy if active)
  • CT angiogram + embolization if massive
  • Surgery if recurrent, failed endoscopy/IR

Angiodysplasia:
  • Colonoscopy with thermal coagulation (argon plasma coagulation)
  • Recurrent bleeding common
  • Surgery if recurrent, severe

Ischemic Colitis:
  • Supportive (usually self-limiting)
  • IV fluids, bowel rest, antibiotics if severe
  • Surgery if transmural infarction, perforation

IBD:
  • Gastroenterology
  • Medical therapy (5-ASA, corticosteroids, immunosuppressants)
  • Surgery if severe hemorrhage, toxic megacolon, perforation

Colorectal Cancer:
  • Oncology referral
  • Surgical resection
  • Chemotherapy if advanced

Hemorrhoids / Anal Fissure:
  • Conservative: Fiber, stool softeners
  • Procedures: Rubber band ligation (hemorrhoids)
  • Surgery: Hemorrhoidectomy, lateral sphincterotomy (fissure)

üö® RED FLAGs in LGIB

  • Hemodynamic instability ‚Üí URGENT resuscitation, investigation (consider massive UGIB presenting as hematochezia)
  • Age >60 ‚Üí Higher risk of serious pathology (diverticular, angiodysplasia, cancer)
  • Alarm symptoms (weight loss, change in bowel habit) ‚Üí Colorectal cancer (colonoscopy)
  • Severe abdominal pain + bloody diarrhea ‚Üí Ischemic colitis (CT abdomen)
  • Known vascular disease, AF ‚Üí Mesenteric ischemia (CT angiogram, lactate)
  • Recurrent bleeding ‚Üí Requires investigation (colonoscopy) - not hemorrhoids until proven

3. OCCULT GI BLEEDING

Definition

Occult bleeding: GI bleeding not visible to patient (no hematemesis, melena, hematochezia) Presentation:
  • Iron-deficiency anemia (Hb low, MCV low, ferritin low)
  • Positive fecal occult blood test (FOBT)

Differential

Upper GI:
  • Peptic ulcer disease
  • Gastric/esophageal cancer
  • Angiodysplasia

Lower GI:
  • Colorectal cancer (most important to exclude)
  • Angiodysplasia
  • IBD

Small bowel:
  • Angiodysplasia
  • Crohn's disease
  • Celiac disease (malabsorption ‚Üí iron deficiency, not bleeding)
  • NSAIDs

Investigation

Bidirectional Endoscopy (Upper and Lower):
  • Upper endoscopy (OGD): Examine esophagus, stomach, duodenum
  • Colonoscopy: Examine colon, terminal ileum
  • If both normal and iron-deficiency anemia persists ‚Üí Investigate small bowel

Small Bowel Investigation:
  • Capsule endoscopy: Swallow camera capsule, images entire small bowel
  • CT enterography: CT with oral contrast to visualize small bowel
  • Double-balloon enteroscopy: Deep small bowel endoscopy (if lesion found on capsule, need biopsy/therapy)

Management

  • Treat underlying cause (ulcer, cancer, angiodysplasia)
  • Iron supplementation: Oral (ferrous sulfate 200mg daily-TDS) or IV if oral not tolerated/ineffective
  • Repeat investigations if recurrent

4. COMMON IMG MISTAKES

  1. Not considering massive UGIB when patient presents with hematochezia:

- Fresh red blood PR can be massive UGIB with rapid transit - If hemodynamically unstable + hematochezia ‚Üí NG aspirate, consider upper endoscopy first

  1. Over-resuscitating variceal bleeding:

- Excessive fluids/transfusion increases portal pressure and worsens bleeding - Target Hb >70 (not higher) in variceal bleeding - Permissive hypotension (SBP 90-100) until bleeding controlled

  1. Not starting octreotide + ceftriaxone in suspected variceal bleeding:

- Start BEFORE endoscopy if cirrhosis suspected - Reduces re-bleeding, infection, mortality

  1. Not using high-dose PPI protocol:

- Must use 80mg bolus + 8mg/hr infusion x 72 hours for peptic ulcer bleeding - Standard dose PPI is inadequate

  1. Delaying endoscopy in high-risk UGIB:

- Glasgow-Blatchford Score ‚â•12, hemodynamic instability ‚Üí URGENT endoscopy <12-24 hours - Early endoscopy improves outcomes in high-risk patients

  1. Using adrenaline injection alone for endoscopic therapy:

- Must combine adrenaline with clips or thermal coagulation - Adrenaline alone increases re-bleeding risk

  1. Not reversing anticoagulation in severe bleeding:

- Warfarin ‚Üí Vitamin K + Prothrombinex - DOACs ‚Üí Specific reversal agents if available - Assess bleeding vs thrombosis risk (discuss with cardiology, but bleeding takes priority acutely)

  1. Missing aortoenteric fistula:

- Patient with previous aortic graft + any GI bleeding ‚Üí high suspicion - "Herald bleed" followed by massive hemorrhage - URGENT vascular surgery consult

  1. Attributing all rectal bleeding to hemorrhoids without investigation:

- Age >50 + rectal bleeding ‚Üí colonoscopy to exclude colorectal cancer - Hemorrhoids and cancer can coexist

  1. Not recognizing ischemic colitis:

- Pain + bloody diarrhea (unlike painless diverticular bleeding) - CT abdomen shows segmental colonic wall thickening - Usually self-limiting, but can progress to infarction/perforation
