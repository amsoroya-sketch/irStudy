Definition & Diagnosis

Anaphylaxis Criteria (Australian Resuscitation Council - ANZCOR)

Anaphylaxis is HIGHLY LIKELY when ANY ONE of the following occurs:

  1. Acute onset (minutes to hours) with skin/mucosal involvement PLUS one of:
    • Respiratory compromise (wheeze, stridor, hypoxia)
    • Hypotension or end-organ dysfunction
  2. TWO or more of the following after exposure to likely allergen:
    • Skin/mucosal involvement (urticaria, angioedema)
    • Respiratory compromise
    • Hypotension or end-organ dysfunction
    • Persistent gastrointestinal symptoms (vomiting, cramping abdominal pain)
  3. Hypotension after exposure to KNOWN allergen:
    • Systolic BP <90 mmHg (adults)
    • OR >30% decrease from patient's baseline

Clinical Features

System Clinical Features
Skin (90%) Urticaria (hives), angioedema, flushing, pruritis (itching)
Respiratory (70%) Upper airway: Stridor, angioedema of tongue/throat
Lower airway: Wheeze, dyspnoea, chest tightness, hypoxia
Cardiovascular (45%) Hypotension, tachycardia, dizziness, syncope, cardiac arrest
Gastrointestinal (45%) Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, cramping abdominal pain
Neurological Sense of impending doom, confusion, altered consciousness

Immediate Management (AMC OSCE Station)

üö® IMMEDIATE ACTIONS (First 60 Seconds)

  1. CALL FOR HELP - Medical emergency team, resuscitation equipment
  2. STOP TRIGGER - Remove allergen if possible (stop IV medication, remove bee sting)
  3. POSITION PATIENT:
    • Supine with legs elevated (if hypotensive - most common)
    • Sitting upright (if severe respiratory distress with wheeze)
    • Recovery position (if vomiting/unconscious)
    • NEVER stand patient up (can cause sudden cardiac arrest)
  4. ADRENALINE IM 0.5mg (0.5mL of 1:1000) - IMMEDIATELY

Adrenaline Administration (CRITICAL)

ADRENALINE IS THE CORNERSTONE OF ANAPHYLAXIS TREATMENT

There are NO absolute contraindications to adrenaline in anaphylaxis

Supportive Management

1. OXYGEN (High-Flow):

  • 15L/min via non-rebreather mask
  • Target SpO2 >94%

2. IV FLUID RESUSCITATION (if hypotensive):

  • 0.9% NaCl 1-2L rapid bolus (20mL/kg in children)
  • Capillary leak + vasodilation ‚Üí massive fluid requirements (some need 4-7L in first hour)

3. ADJUNCT MEDICATIONS (NOT first-line, only AFTER adrenaline):

  • Antihistamine (H1 blocker): Promethazine 25-50mg IM OR Cetirizine 10mg PO
    • Reduces urticaria/itch (does NOT treat life-threatening features)
    • NO role in acute resuscitation (adrenaline is priority)
  • Corticosteroids: Hydrocortisone 200mg IV OR Prednisolone 50mg PO
    • May prevent biphasic reaction (4-8 hours later)
    • Onset 4-6 hours (NO immediate benefit)
  • Bronchodilators (if wheeze): Salbutamol nebuliser 5mg
    • Adjunct only (adrenaline is bronchodilator of choice)

Refractory Anaphylaxis

If NO response to 2-3 doses IM adrenaline ‚Üí escalate:

  1. IV adrenaline infusion: 0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/min (requires ICU/HDU monitoring)
  2. Call ICU/anaesthetics - intubation may be required
  3. Consider glucagon (if patient on beta-blockers - adrenaline may not work)

Observation & Discharge

Observation Period

Risk Category Observation Time
Mild reaction (skin only, resolved) 4 hours minimum
Moderate reaction (respiratory/cardiovascular involvement, resolved) 6-12 hours
Severe reaction (hypotension, needed >1 dose adrenaline) 12-24 hours (admit)
Biphasic reaction risk Occurs in 5-20% of cases (second phase 4-72 hours later)

Common Triggers (Australian Context)

Trigger Examples & Notes
Foods (most common in children) Peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, fish, eggs, cow's milk, soy, wheat, sesame
Insect stings (common in adults) Bees, wasps, jack jumper ants (Tasmania), fire ants (Queensland)
Medications Penicillin, NSAIDs, aspirin, contrast media, muscle relaxants (anaesthesia)
Tick bites Tick-induced mammalian meat allergy (alpha-gal syndrome - Australian east coast)

AMC OSCE Stations - Common Scenarios

Station 1: Acute Anaphylaxis Management

  • Scenario: Patient with urticaria, wheeze, hypotension after peanut exposure
  • Key actions: Immediate IM adrenaline 0.5mg, IV fluids, oxygen, repeat adrenaline if needed
  • Common errors: Giving antihistamine before adrenaline, using wrong adrenaline dose/route

Station 2: EpiPen¬Æ Counselling

  • Scenario: Discharge counselling after first anaphylactic episode
  • Key actions: Demonstrate EpiPen¬Æ use, provide ASCIA plan, allergy specialist referral, emphasize calling 000
  • Common errors: Not prescribing TWO EpiPens, not demonstrating technique
