Weakness and Neurological Limb Examination

ICRP OSCE Preparation - Medicine Module (Neurology)

Target Audience

This guide is for International Medical Graduates (IMGs) preparing for the AMC Clinical OSCE, specifically:

  • IMGs requiring systematic approach to weakness assessment and neurological examination
  • Candidates needing pattern recognition (UMN vs LMN, distribution patterns)
  • Doctors preparing for ICRP placement in Australian hospitals
  • Medical professionals needing stroke protocols and ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY neurology management
  • Clinicians requiring competency in neurological limb examination for OSCE

Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  1. Differentiate upper motor neuron (UMN) from lower motor neuron (LMN) weakness
  2. Recognize patterns of weakness (hemiplegia, paraplegia, quadriplegia, proximal, distal, monoplegia)
  3. Identify üö® RED FLAGs requiring URGENT intervention (stroke, spinal cord compression, cauda equina, GBS)
  4. Perform systematic neurological examination of upper and lower limbs
  5. Apply MRC power grading scale accurately
  6. Manage acute stroke according to Australian protocols (thrombolysis, thrombectomy)
  7. Generate differential diagnoses based on examination findings
  8. Perform confidently in OSCE stations for neurological examination

Dr. Amir Soufi's Approach Applied to Weakness

Core Principle: PATTERN RECOGNITION ‚Üí UMN vs LMN ‚Üí ANATOMICAL LOCALIZATION

Traditional Approach (Less Effective):
  • Generic neurological examination
  • Consider all causes equally
  • Delayed pattern recognition

Dr. Amir Soufi's Differential-Driven Approach:
  1. üö® RED FLAGS FIRST (Life-Threatening Causes):

- Sudden onset unilateral weakness ‚Üí STROKE until proven otherwise (thrombolysis window 4.5 hours) - Progressive ascending weakness + areflexia ‚Üí Guillain-Barr√© Syndrome (respiratory monitoring, may need ICU) - Bilateral leg weakness + saddle anesthesia + urinary retention ‚Üí Cauda equina syndrome (URGENT MRI, surgery within 48 hours) - Bilateral leg weakness + sensory level + bowel/bladder ‚Üí Spinal cord compression (URGENT MRI, high-dose steroids, surgery/radiotherapy)

  1. PATTERN OF WEAKNESS (Immediate Differential):

- Hemiplegia (one side of body) ‚Üí Stroke, brain lesion - Paraplegia (both legs) ‚Üí Spinal cord lesion (thoracic/lumbar) - Quadriplegia (all four limbs) ‚Üí Spinal cord (cervical), brainstem - Monoplegia (one limb) ‚Üí Peripheral nerve, plexus, cortical lesion - Proximal weakness (shoulders, hips) ‚Üí Muscle disease (myopathy, myositis) - Distal weakness (hands, feet) ‚Üí Peripheral neuropathy

  1. UMN vs LMN SIGNS (Localizes Level):

UMN (brain, spinal cord):

- Increased tone (spasticity, velocity-dependent) - Brisk reflexes (hyperreflexia), clonus - Extensor plantar (Babinski positive) - NO muscle wasting (early), NO fasciculations - Weakness in pyramidal pattern: Extensors > flexors (upper limb), Flexors > extensors (lower limb)

LMN (anterior horn cell, nerve root, peripheral nerve, NMJ, muscle):

- Decreased tone (flaccid, hypotonia) - Reduced/absent reflexes (hyporeflexia, areflexia) - Flexor plantar (normal) - Muscle wasting (atrophy), fasciculations (visible muscle twitches)

  1. ANATOMICAL LOCALIZATION:

- Cortex/internal capsule ‚Üí Hemiplegia (face + arm + leg, contralateral), UMN signs - Brainstem ‚Üí Cranial nerve signs + contralateral weakness - Spinal cord ‚Üí Bilateral weakness below level, sensory level, bowel/bladder, UMN signs - Anterior horn cell (motor neuron disease) ‚Üí Fasciculations, wasting, mixed UMN + LMN - Nerve root (radiculopathy) ‚Üí Dermatomal sensory loss, myotomal weakness, reduced reflex - Peripheral nerve ‚Üí Specific nerve distribution (e.g., radial nerve ‚Üí wrist drop) - Neuromuscular junction (myasthenia gravis) ‚Üí Fatigable weakness, ptosis, diplopia - Muscle (myopathy) ‚Üí Proximal weakness, normal sensation, normal reflexes (until late)

  1. TIMING (Onset and Progression):

- Sudden onset (seconds-minutes) ‚Üí Stroke, vascular - Rapid progression (hours-days) ‚Üí GBS, transverse myelitis, spinal cord compression - Subacute (weeks) ‚Üí Myasthenia gravis, inflammatory myopathy - Chronic progressive (months-years) ‚Üí Motor neuron disease, peripheral neuropathy, myopathy

Example:
  • Presentation: 72-year-old man, sudden onset right arm and leg weakness 2 hours ago, facial droop, slurred speech
  • Immediate thought: STROKE (hemiplegia, sudden onset, vascular risk factors age >70)
  • Pattern: Right hemiplegia ‚Üí Left hemisphere stroke (motor cortex/internal capsule)
  • Immediate actions: ROSIER score (stroke vs mimic), CT brain URGENT (rule out hemorrhage), check time of onset (thrombolysis window <4.5 hours), FAST stroke team activation
  • Examination findings expected: Right UMN facial palsy (forehead spared), right arm and leg weakness (UMN - increased tone, brisk reflexes, extensor plantar), dysarthria

1. üö® RED FLAGS IN WEAKNESS

Acute Stroke

"TIME IS BRAIN" - Thrombolysis Window 4.5 Hours Presentation:
  • Sudden onset (
  • Hemiplegia: Weakness of face, arm, leg (one side)
  • Dysarthria (slurred speech) or dysphasia (language impairment)
  • Facial droop (UMN facial palsy - forehead spared)
  • Hemianopia (visual field loss)
  • Ataxia, vertigo, diplopia (posterior circulation stroke)
  • Headache (hemorrhagic stroke more common)

FAST Assessment (Public recognition tool):
  • F - Face drooping
  • A - Arm weakness
  • S - Speech difficulty
  • T - Time to call ambulance

ROSIER Score (Recognition of Stroke in ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY Room - Differentiates Stroke from Mimics):

| Feature | Score | |---------|-------| | Loss of consciousness or syncope | -1 | | Seizure activity | -1 | | Acute onset of: | | | - Asymmetric facial weakness | +1 | | - Asymmetric arm weakness | +1 | | - Asymmetric leg weakness | +1 | | - Speech disturbance | +1 | | - Visual field defect | +1 |

Score >0 = Stroke likely (sens 92%, spec 86%) Score ‚â§0 = Stroke unlikely (consider mimic - seizure, hypoglycemia, syncope, migraine)
Investigations:
  • URGENT non-contrast CT brain (rule out hemorrhage - contraindication to thrombolysis)

- Ischemic stroke: CT often normal acutely (<6 hours), later shows hypodensity - Hemorrhagic stroke: Hyperdense (white) area

  • Bloods: Glucose (hypoglycemia mimic), FBC, UEC, coagulation (INR - if elevated, contraindication to thrombolysis), troponin (cardiac source)
  • ECG: Atrial fibrillation (cardioembolic stroke)
  • CT angiography (if considering thrombectomy - large vessel occlusion)

Management (Australian Stroke Guidelines): Hyperacute (<4.5 hours from onset):
  1. Thrombolysis (if eligible):

- Alteplase (tPA - tissue plasminogen activator) 0.9mg/kg IV (10% bolus, 90% infusion over 1 hour)

Inclusion criteria:

- Ischemic stroke with measurable deficit - Onset <4.5 hours (last seen well time) - CT brain shows NO hemorrhage

Exclusion criteria (major):

- Hemorrhagic stroke on CT - Time >4.5 hours from onset (or unknown onset time) - Recent surgery (<14 days major, <7 days minor) - Recent stroke or head trauma (<3 months) - Previous ICH - Bleeding disorder, anticoagulation (INR >1.7, on DOAC within 48 hours, platelets <100) - Uncontrolled hypertension (SBP >185 or DBP >110 despite treatment) - Glucose <2.7 or >22.2 mmol/L - Pregnancy - Seizure at onset with residual deficits (Todd's paralysis)

Benefits: 13% absolute increase in good outcome (mRS 0-1) if given <3 hours, 10% if 3-4.5 hours Complications: Symptomatic ICH 6%, major bleeding
  1. Thrombectomy (Endovascular Clot Retrieval):

- Indications: Large vessel occlusion (ICA, M1 MCA, basilar), large penumbra on imaging, <24 hours (selected patients) - CT/MR angiography + perfusion to select patients - Can combine with thrombolysis - Transfer to thrombectomy-capable center if not available locally

Acute Stroke Unit Care (All stroke patients):
  • Admit to stroke unit (specialized nursing, monitoring - reduces mortality 18%)
  • Nil by mouth until swallow screen passed (aspiration risk)
  • IV fluids if NBM, dehydrated
  • BP management: Permissive hypertension initially (allow BP 185/110 if NOT thrombolysing - brain autoregulation impaired, needs higher BP to perfuse penumbra). Lower if >220/120 or if thrombolysing
  • Glucose control: Avoid hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia (target 6-10 mmol/L)
  • Temperature: Treat fever (paracetamol)
  • DVT prophylaxis: Compression stockings, early mobilization (avoid heparin first 24-48 hours)
  • Swallow assessment (speech pathology) before oral intake
  • Early mobilization (within 24-48 hours if stable)

Secondary Prevention (after acute phase):
  • Antiplatelet: Aspirin 100-300mg daily (start 24 hours after thrombolysis, or immediately if no thrombolysis) + clopidogrel 75mg daily x 21 days (dual antiplatelet), then aspirin alone long-term
  • Anticoagulation: If AF - warfarin or DOAC (start 2-14 days after stroke depending on size)
  • Statin: Atorvastatin 80mg daily (all ischemic strokes)
  • BP control: Target <130/80 (start few days post-stroke)
  • Diabetes management: HbA1c <7%
  • Lifestyle: Smoking cessation, exercise, Mediterranean diet, alcohol moderation

Rehabilitation: Physiotherapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy (early intensive therapy improves outcomes)

Spinal Cord Compression

URGENT MRI + HIGH-DOSE STEROIDS + SURGERY/RADIOTHERAPY WITHIN 24-48 HOURS Presentation:
  • Bilateral leg weakness (progressive over hours-weeks)
  • Sensory level (band of altered sensation at level of compression)
  • Bowel/bladder dysfunction (retention ‚Üí late incontinence)
  • Back pain (localized, worse lying flat, nocturnal)
  • UMN signs below level (increased tone, brisk reflexes, extensor plantars)

Causes:
  1. Metastatic cancer (most common - lung, breast, prostate, kidney, myeloma):

- History of malignancy, weight loss, back pain

  1. Epidural abscess:

- Fever, back pain, sepsis, IVDU, immunosuppressed

  1. Trauma:

- Vertebral fracture, hematoma

  1. Primary spinal tumor:

- Meningioma, neurofibroma

  1. Disc prolapse (central):

- Acute onset, younger patients

üö® RED FLAG: Urinary retention = URGENT (irreversible bladder dysfunction if >48 hours) Investigations:
  • URGENT MRI whole spine (T2 shows cord compression, edema; T1 + gadolinium shows lesion)
  • Bloods: FBC, CRP (infection), PSA (prostate cancer in men)
  • CXR: Lung cancer

Management:
  1. High-dose dexamethasone: 16mg IV/PO immediately (reduce cord edema)
  2. URGENT neurosurgery / oncology consult
  3. Decompressive surgery (laminectomy) within 24-48 hours if:

- Single level compression - Neurologically intact or incomplete deficit - Good performance status

  1. Radiotherapy: If not surgical candidate, multiple levels, radiosensitive tumor
  2. Analgesia: Opioids for severe back pain
  3. Bladder catheter: If retention

Prognosis: Ambulatory at presentation = 80% remain ambulatory if treated within 24-48 hours. Paraplegic at presentation = <10% regain ambulation.

Cauda Equina Syndrome

SURGICAL ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY - DECOMPRESSION WITHIN 48 HOURS Anatomy: Cauda equina = nerve roots L2-S5 (below conus medullaris at L1/L2) - LMN, not spinal cord Presentation:
  • Bilateral leg weakness (asymmetric, flaccid)
  • Saddle anesthesia (perineum, buttocks - S2-S5 dermatomes)
  • Bladder/bowel dysfunction:

- Urinary retention (painless, overflow incontinence) - Fecal incontinence, loss of anal tone

  • Sexual dysfunction (erectile dysfunction, loss of sensation)
  • Severe low back pain, sciatica (bilateral)
  • LMN signs: Reduced tone, reduced/absent reflexes (ankle jerks), weakness

Cause: Central lumbar disc prolapse (L4/L5 or L5/S1 most common), tumor, trauma, abscess Examination:
  • Reduced anal tone (PR exam - critical finding)
  • Loss of perianal sensation
  • Bilateral leg weakness (LMN)
  • Absent ankle reflexes

Investigations:
  • URGENT MRI lumbosacral spine (shows disc prolapse compressing cauda equina)
  • Post-void residual bladder scan (retention >200ml abnormal)

Management:
  • URGENT neurosurgery consult
  • Decompressive laminectomy + discectomy within 48 hours (ideally <24 hours)
  • Bladder catheter (retention)
  • Analgesia

Prognosis: Early surgery (<48 hours) gives best chance of bladder/bowel/sexual function recovery. Delay >48 hours = permanent dysfunction common.

Guillain-Barr√© Syndrome (GBS)

RAPIDLY PROGRESSIVE ASCENDING PARALYSIS - ICU MONITORING (RESPIRATORY FAILURE) Presentation:
  • Ascending weakness (starts in legs, ascends to arms, can involve face, respiratory muscles)
  • Progressive over days-weeks (nadir at 2-4 weeks)
  • Areflexia (absent reflexes - hallmark)
  • Distal paresthesias (hands, feet - "glove and stocking")
  • Minimal sensory signs (predominantly motor)
  • Autonomic dysfunction (tachycardia, labile BP, arrhythmias, ileus)
  • Respiratory compromise (25% need ventilation)
  • Preceding illness (2/3 of patients - GI infection Campylobacter, respiratory infection, Zika, COVID)

Subtypes:
  • AIDP (Acute Inflammatory Demyelinating Polyneuropathy - most common 85%)
  • AMAN (Acute Motor Axonal Neuropathy)
  • Miller-Fisher syndrome: Triad = Ataxia, Areflexia, Ophthalmoplegia (cranial nerve variant)

Investigations:
  • LP: Albuminocytologic dissociation (elevated protein >0.55 g/L, normal WCC) - classic, but may be normal in first week
  • Nerve conduction studies: Demyelination (slowed conduction velocities, prolonged latencies, conduction block)
  • Spirometry (FVC - forced vital capacity): Monitor respiratory function (if FVC <15-20 ml/kg ‚Üí intubation)
  • Bloods: Rule out differential (HIV, Lyme, porphyria)

Management:
  1. ICU admission (monitoring for respiratory failure, autonomic instability):

- Serial FVC (every 4-6 hours initially) - Intubate if: FVC <15-20 ml/kg, or <1L, or rapid decline, or bulbar weakness - Cardiac monitoring (arrhythmias from autonomic dysfunction)

  1. Immunotherapy (start within 2 weeks of symptom onset):

- IV Immunoglobulin (IVIG) 0.4g/kg/day x 5 days (first-line - easier than plasma exchange) - Plasma exchange (alternative - 5 exchanges over 2 weeks) - Equivalent efficacy, IVIG preferred (easier, fewer complications) - NOT corticosteroids (ineffective in GBS)

  1. Supportive care:

- DVT prophylaxis: Heparin (high risk - paralyzed, immobile) - Physiotherapy: Prevent contractures, early mobilization when recovering - Analgesia: Neuropathic pain common (gabapentin, pregabalin) - Bladder catheter if retention - NGT feeding if bulbar weakness (aspiration risk)

  1. Monitor for complications: Aspiration pneumonia, DVT/PE, pressure ulcers, ileus

Prognosis: 80% full recovery, but takes months. 5% mortality (respiratory failure, autonomic complications). 20% residual deficit.

2. UPPER MOTOR NEURON vs LOWER MOTOR NEURON SIGNS

Critical for anatomical localization

| Feature | UMN (Brain, Spinal Cord) | LMN (Anterior Horn, Nerve Root, Peripheral Nerve, NMJ, Muscle) | |---------|--------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------| | Tone | Increased (spasticity - velocity-dependent, clasp-knife) | Decreased (flaccid, hypotonia) | | Power | Decreased (pyramidal pattern) | Decreased (specific nerve/myotome distribution) | | Reflexes | Brisk (hyperreflexia), clonus | Reduced or absent (hyporeflexia, areflexia) | | Plantar response | Extensor (Babinski positive - upgoing toe) | Flexor (normal - downgoing toe) | | Muscle wasting | Absent early, mild late (disuse atrophy) | Present (prominent, early) | | Fasciculations | Absent | Present (visible muscle twitches) | | Distribution | Pyramidal pattern, hemiplegic, paraplegic | Specific nerve/root distribution | | Sensation | May be affected (if sensory cortex/cord involved) | May be affected (nerve root/peripheral nerve) or normal (NMJ, muscle) |

Pyramidal Pattern of Weakness (UMN):
  • Upper limb: Extensors weaker than flexors (arm drifts down and pronates)
  • Lower limb: Flexors weaker than extensors (leg circumducts when walking)

3. PATTERNS OF WEAKNESS (Anatomical Localization)

Hemiplegia (One Side of Body)

Lesion: Contralateral brain (cortex, internal capsule, brainstem) Presentation:
  • Face + Arm + Leg weakness (one side)
  • UMN signs (increased tone, brisk reflexes, extensor plantar)
  • Cortical signs (if cortical lesion): Dysphasia (dominant hemisphere - left), neglect (non-dominant - right)
  • Homonymous hemianopia (visual field loss)

Causes:
  1. Stroke (ischemic or hemorrhagic) - most common
  2. Brain tumor
  3. Head trauma (subdural, extradural hematoma)
  4. Demyelination (MS)
  5. Brain abscess

Localization:
  • Cortex (MCA territory): Face + Arm > Leg
  • Internal capsule (lacunar stroke): Face + Arm + Leg equally
  • ACA territory: Leg > Arm
  • Brainstem: Cranial nerve signs ipsilateral + hemiplegia contralateral (crossed signs)

Paraplegia (Both Legs)

Lesion: Spinal cord (thoracic or lumbar) Presentation:
  • Bilateral leg weakness
  • Sensory level (at level of lesion)
  • Bowel/bladder dysfunction
  • UMN signs (if cord) or LMN signs (if cauda equina)

Causes:
  1. Spinal cord compression (metastases, epidural abscess, disc)
  2. Transverse myelitis (inflammation of cord - post-infectious, MS, idiopathic)
  3. Spinal cord infarction (anterior spinal artery)
  4. Trauma (vertebral fracture)
  5. Cauda equina syndrome (LMN paraplegia)

Lesion level:
  • Thoracic cord: Paraplegia, sensory level at thoracic dermatome
  • Lumbar cord/conus medullaris (L1-L2): Paraplegia, saddle anesthesia
  • Cauda equina (below L2): LMN paraplegia (flaccid, areflexia)

Quadriplegia (All Four Limbs)

Lesion: Cervical spinal cord or brainstem Presentation:
  • Weakness all four limbs + trunk
  • Respiratory muscles may be affected (C3-C5 - phrenic nerve)
  • Sensory level (if cord)
  • Bowel/bladder dysfunction

Causes:
  1. Cervical spinal cord compression
  2. Cervical spine trauma (fracture, dislocation)
  3. Transverse myelitis (cervical)
  4. Brainstem stroke
  5. High cervical cord lesion (C1-C4)

‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY: High cervical lesions (above C4) ‚Üí respiratory failure (phrenic nerve C3-C5)

Proximal Weakness (Shoulders, Hips)

Lesion: Muscle (myopathy) or Neuromuscular junction Presentation:
  • Difficulty rising from chair, climbing stairs, lifting arms above head, combing hair
  • Shoulder/hip girdle weakness
  • Normal reflexes (until late), normal sensation
  • No UMN/LMN signs

Causes: Myopathy:
  1. Inflammatory myopathy:

- Polymyositis: Proximal weakness, elevated CK, muscle pain - Dermatomyositis: Polymyositis + rash (heliotrope rash, Gottron's papules, shawl sign) - Inclusion body myositis: Proximal + distal weakness (finger flexors), older adults

  1. Endocrine myopathy:

- Hypothyroid myopathy: Proximal weakness, elevated CK, hypothyroid symptoms - Steroid myopathy: Chronic steroid use, proximal weakness, normal CK - Cushing's syndrome

  1. Drug-induced myopathy:

- Statins (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors): Myalgia, elevated CK, rarely rhabdomyolysis - Corticosteroids - Alcohol

  1. Muscular dystrophies:

- Duchenne (boys, early onset, calf pseudohypertrophy, elevated CK) - Becker (milder, later onset) - Limb-girdle (proximal weakness, adolescent/adult onset)

Investigations: CK (elevated in inflammatory myopathy, muscular dystrophy), TSH, EMG, muscle biopsy Neuromuscular Junction:
  • Myasthenia gravis: Fatigable weakness (worse with repetition), ptosis, diplopia, normal CK

Distal Weakness (Hands, Feet)

Lesion: Peripheral neuropathy Presentation:
  • Weakness hands and feet ("foot drop", difficulty with buttons/writing)
  • Glove-and-stocking sensory loss
  • Reduced/absent reflexes (LMN)
  • Distal wasting

Causes: Peripheral Neuropathy:
  1. Diabetes (most common - diabetic neuropathy)
  2. Alcohol
  3. Vitamin B12 deficiency
  4. Chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (CIDP): GBS-like but chronic (>8 weeks), responds to IVIG
  5. Hereditary: Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease (pes cavus, distal wasting, sensory loss, family history)
  6. Toxins: Heavy metals (lead, arsenic), medications (vincristine, isoniazid, metronidazole)
  7. Vasculitis: Mononeuritis multiplex (multiple individual nerves affected)

Motor Neuron Disease (if no sensory loss):
  • Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS): Mixed UMN + LMN signs, no sensory loss, fasciculations, progressive

4. NEUROLOGICAL EXAMINATION OF LIMBS

Systematic approach: "I-P-T-P-R-S-C"
  1. I - Inspection
  2. P - Tone (Passive movement)
  3. P - Power (MRC grading)
  4. R - Reflexes
  5. S - Sensation
  6. C - Coordination

UPPER LIMB NEUROLOGICAL EXAMINATION

Opening (OSCE):

"Hello, I'm Dr. [Name]. I've been asked to examine the movement and sensation in your arms. Is that okay? Could you remove your shirt/roll up your sleeves please?"

Position patient: Sitting on edge of bed or chair, arms exposed

1. INSPECTION

Look for:
  • Muscle wasting (LMN sign):

- Small muscles of hand (thenar, hypothenar, interossei) - Forearm muscles - Deltoid, biceps, triceps

  • Fasciculations (LMN sign): Watch for 30 seconds - visible muscle twitches
  • Deformity: Claw hand, wrist drop, contractures
  • Scars: Surgery
  • Tremor: At rest (Parkinson's), intention (cerebellar), postural (essential tremor)
  • Posture: Flexed posture (UMN - spastic hemiplegia)

While inspecting, assess for pronator drift:
  • "Close your eyes, hold your arms out in front, palms up like holding a tray"
  • UMN weakness: Arm drifts down and pronates (palm turns down) - sensitive sign
  • Observe for 10-20 seconds

2. TONE

Test passive movement of joints:

"I'm going to move your arm. Try to stay relaxed and let me do all the work."

Elbow:
  • Support patient's elbow, grasp wrist
  • Flex and extend elbow through full range
  • Roll elbow (rapid pronation/supination of forearm)

Wrist:
  • Grasp patient's hand, flex/extend wrist, ulnar/radial deviation

Findings:
  • Normal: Smooth, no resistance
  • Increased tone (UMN):

- Spasticity: Velocity-dependent resistance (clasp-knife - initial resistance then sudden give) - Rigidity: Constant resistance throughout range (lead-pipe), cogwheel (if tremor superimposed - Parkinson's)

  • Decreased tone (LMN): Arm flops, increased range of movement

3. POWER (MRC Grading Scale)

MRC Scale (0-5):

| Grade | Description | Functional | |-------|-------------|------------| | 0 | No contraction | Paralyzed | | 1 | Flicker of contraction | No movement | | 2 | Movement with gravity eliminated | Cannot lift against gravity | | 3 | Movement against gravity | Can lift, but not against resistance | | 4 | Movement against resistance | Weak | | 5 | Normal power | Normal |

4 can be subdivided: 4-, 4, 4+ (mild, moderate, nearly normal weakness)
Test major muscle groups (myotomes C5-T1): Shoulder abduction (C5 - axillary nerve):
  • "Lift your arms out to the sides like a bird. Don't let me push them down."
  • Deltoid muscle

Elbow flexion (C5-C6 - musculocutaneous nerve):
  • "Bend your elbow and pull towards you. Don't let me straighten it."
  • Biceps muscle

Elbow extension (C7 - radial nerve):
  • "Straighten your elbow. Push against my hand. Don't let me bend it."
  • Triceps muscle

Wrist extension (C6 - radial nerve):
  • "Cock your wrist back like this. Don't let me push it down."
  • Wrist extensors
  • Radial nerve palsy ‚Üí wrist drop (cannot extend wrist)

Finger extension (C7 - radial nerve):
  • "Straighten your fingers. Don't let me bend them."

Finger flexion (C8 - median/ulnar nerves):
  • "Squeeze my fingers as hard as you can."
  • Compare both sides

Finger abduction (T1 - ulnar nerve):
  • "Spread your fingers apart. Don't let me squeeze them together."
  • Interossei muscles
  • Ulnar nerve palsy ‚Üí weakness finger abduction

Thumb abduction (C8-T1 - median nerve):
  • "Point your thumb to the ceiling. Don't let me push it down."
  • Abductor pollicis brevis (thenar eminence)
  • Median nerve palsy (carpal tunnel) ‚Üí weakness, wasting thenar eminence

Document findings: "Power 5/5 throughout right upper limb. Power 4/5 elbow flexion/extension, 3/5 wrist extension left upper limb."

4. REFLEXES

Test deep tendon reflexes with tendon hammer: Positioning: Patient relaxed, muscle slightly stretched Technique:
  • Hold hammer loosely at end (let weight do work)
  • Strike tendon briskly with wrist flick
  • Compare left and right

Upper limb reflexes:
  1. Biceps reflex (C5-C6):

- Place thumb on biceps tendon in antecubital fossa - Strike your thumb with hammer - Observe biceps contraction, elbow flexion

  1. Triceps reflex (C7):

- Support patient's arm (elbow flexed 90¬∞, arm across body) - Strike triceps tendon just above olecranon - Observe triceps contraction, elbow extension

  1. Supinator reflex (C6) - often omitted:

- Strike brachioradialis tendon (lateral forearm, 10cm above wrist) - Observe forearm supination, elbow flexion

If reflexes absent: Reinforcement (Jendrassik maneuver)
  • "Clench your teeth" or "Hook your fingers together and pull"
  • Increases tone throughout body, may elicit absent reflexes

Grading:
  • 0: Absent
  • +: Hyporeflexia (reduced)
  • ++: Normal
  • +++: Brisk (hyperreflexia)
  • ++++: Very brisk, with clonus

Clonus (sustained rhythmic contractions - UMN sign):
  • Not usually tested in upper limb (more sensitive in lower limb)

Document: "Reflexes ++ throughout. Brisk (+++) left biceps/triceps."

5. SENSATION

Test sensory modalities: Light touch:
  • Cotton wool
  • "Close your eyes. Say 'yes' when you feel me touch you."
  • Compare both arms, dermatomes

Dermatomes - Upper Limb:
  • C5: Lateral upper arm (over deltoid)
  • C6: Thumb, lateral forearm
  • C7: Middle finger
  • C8: Little finger, medial forearm
  • T1: Medial upper arm

Pinprick (if indicated - peripheral neuropathy, spinal cord lesion):
  • Neurotip (disposable)
  • "Is this sharp or dull? Does it feel the same on both sides?"

Proprioception (joint position sense):
  • "Close your eyes. I'm going to move your finger up or down. Tell me which way."
  • Hold sides of distal phalanx of index finger, move up/down small movements
  • Test both sides
  • Impaired: Posterior column lesion (B12 deficiency, spinal cord), peripheral neuropathy

Vibration sense:
  • 128 Hz tuning fork
  • Strike fork, place on bony prominence (wrist, elbow)
  • "Tell me when the buzzing stops"
  • Stop fork with your hand - patient should notice immediately
  • Impaired: Peripheral neuropathy, posterior column lesion (same as proprioception)

6. COORDINATION

Test cerebellar function: Finger-nose test:
  • "Touch your nose, then touch my finger. Keep going back and forth as fast as you can."
  • Move your finger to different positions
  • Observe for:

- Intention tremor (tremor worse as approaching target - cerebellar) - Past-pointing (overshoots target) - Dysmetria (inaccurate movements)

Dysdiadochokinesia (rapid alternating movements):
  • "Tap the back of your hand with your other hand, then turn over and tap with palm. Keep going as fast as you can like this."
  • Impaired: Slow, clumsy, irregular (cerebellar)

Rebound test (optional):
  • "Flex your elbow against my resistance. I'm going to let go suddenly."
  • Normally: Arm stops before hitting face (cerebellar brake)
  • Abnormal: Arm flies back uncontrolled (cerebellar lesion)

Closing:

"Thank you. You can put your shirt back on. I'd like to examine the rest of your nervous system, including your legs and walking."

LOWER LIMB NEUROLOGICAL EXAMINATION

Opening (OSCE):

"Hello, I'm Dr. [Name]. I've been asked to examine the movement and sensation in your legs. Is that okay? Could you lie on the bed and expose your legs please?"

Position patient: Lying flat on bed, legs exposed

1. INSPECTION

Look for:
  • Muscle wasting (LMN):

- Quadriceps, hamstrings - Calf muscles (gastrocnemius) - Foot muscles (intrinsic)

  • Fasciculations: Watch for 30 seconds
  • Deformity: Pes cavus (high arch - Charcot-Marie-Tooth), foot drop, contractures
  • Scars: Surgery
  • Posture: Leg position (UMN - leg extended/internally rotated)

2. TONE

"I'm going to move your leg. Stay relaxed and let me do all the work."

Hip:
  • Place hand under knee, lift leg and let drop
  • Increased tone: Knee doesn't drop, leg stiff

Knee:
  • Support knee (slightly flexed), grasp ankle, flex/extend knee
  • Can also do roll test: Roll thigh side to side (observe foot - normally lags behind, with spasticity moves with thigh)

Ankle:
  • Support calf, grasp forefoot, dorsiflex/plantarflex ankle quickly

Ankle clonus (test if increased tone suspected):
  • Support knee (slightly flexed), grasp forefoot
  • Briskly dorsiflex ankle and maintain pressure
  • Clonus: Rhythmic oscillations (plantarflexion/dorsiflexion) - UMN sign
  • Sustained clonus: >5 beats = abnormal (UMN)

3. POWER (MRC Grading 0-5)

Hip flexion (L1-L2 - iliopsoas):
  • "Lift your leg up off the bed. Don't let me push it down."
  • Place hand on thigh, push down

Hip extension (L4-L5, S1 - gluteus maximus):
  • "Push your leg down into the bed."
  • Place hand under thigh, patient pushes down

Knee extension (L3-L4 - quadriceps):
  • "Straighten your knee. Don't let me bend it."
  • Quadriceps muscle
  • L3/L4 root lesion ‚Üí weakness, reduced knee jerk

Knee flexion (L5, S1 - hamstrings):
  • "Bend your knee. Don't let me straighten it."

Ankle dorsiflexion (L4-L5 - tibialis anterior):
  • "Pull your foot up towards you. Don't let me push it down."
  • L5 root lesion or common peroneal nerve palsy ‚Üí foot drop (cannot dorsiflex)

Ankle plantarflexion (S1-S2 - gastrocnemius):
  • "Push your foot down like pressing a pedal. Don't let me pull it up."
  • Very strong muscle - test by asking patient to stand on tiptoes (if able)

Great toe extension (L5 - extensor hallucis longus):
  • "Point your big toe to the ceiling. Don't let me push it down."
  • L5 radiculopathy ‚Üí weakness great toe extension (sensitive sign)

4. REFLEXES

Lower limb reflexes:
  1. Knee jerk (L3-L4 - patellar reflex):

- Patient lying or sitting (knee flexed, relaxed) - Strike patellar tendon just below patella - Observe quadriceps contraction, leg extension - Reinforcement (if absent): Patient interlocks fingers and pulls

  1. Ankle jerk (S1-S2 - Achilles reflex):

- Method 1 (patient lying): Flex hip and knee, externally rotate leg (leg in figure-4 position), dorsiflex ankle slightly, strike Achilles tendon - Method 2 (patient kneeling): Patient kneels on chair, feet hanging off edge, strike Achilles tendon - Observe plantarflexion - Absent: S1 radiculopathy, peripheral neuropathy (diabetes, alcohol)

  1. Plantar reflex (Babinski test):

- Stroke lateral sole of foot with sharp object (orange stick end of tendon hammer) - Start at heel, go along lateral border, curve medially at ball of foot - Observe great toe: - Flexor (downgoing) = Normal - Extensor (upgoing) with fanning of other toes = Babinski positive = UMN lesion - Note: Newborns have extensor plantar (normal until 12-18 months) - Withdraw response (patient pulls foot away) ‚â† Babinski

Document: "Knee jerks ++, ankle jerks +, plantar reflexes flexor bilaterally." "Brisk knee jerks (+++), ankle clonus, extensor plantar left leg (Babinski positive)."

5. SENSATION

Light touch (dermatomes):
  • L1: Inguinal region
  • L2: Anterior thigh
  • L3: Medial knee
  • L4: Medial malleolus
  • L5: Dorsum of foot, between 1st and 2nd toe
  • S1: Lateral foot, sole
  • S2-S4: Perineum (saddle area - cauda equina)

Proprioception:
  • Hold sides of great toe, move up/down

Vibration:
  • Tuning fork on medial malleolus, then tibial tuberosity if impaired

Pinprick (if indicated)

6. COORDINATION

Heel-shin test:
  • "Lift your leg up, touch my finger with your heel, then run your heel down your shin from knee to ankle. Repeat."
  • Observe for: Intention tremor, past-pointing, irregular jerky movements (cerebellar)

Alternatively (less commonly tested in lower limb):
  • Rapid foot tapping (equivalent to dysdiadochokinesia)

Closing:

"Thank you. To complete my examination, I would like to assess your gait, examine the upper limbs and cranial nerves, and perform fundoscopy."

5. GAIT EXAMINATION

Essential part of neurological examination (often high-yield in OSCE) Opening: "I'd like to watch you walk. Walk to the end of the room, turn around, and walk back." Observe:

Normal Gait

  • Heel strike ‚Üí midstance ‚Üí toe-off ‚Üí swing
  • Arm swing (symmetrical)
  • Stride length equal
  • Base (width between feet) normal (10-15cm)

Abnormal Gaits

1. Hemiplegic Gait (UMN - stroke):
  • Affected leg: Extended, circumducts (swings out in arc)
  • Affected arm: Flexed, adducted, no arm swing
  • Drags toes

2. Parkinsonian Gait:
  • Shuffling (short steps)
  • Reduced arm swing
  • Stooped posture
  • Festinating (accelerating, difficulty stopping)
  • Freezing (difficulty initiating steps)
  • Reduced turning (en bloc - body turns as unit)

3. Ataxic Gait (Cerebellar):
  • Broad-based (wide)
  • Unsteady, lurching
  • Veers to side of lesion
  • Difficulty tandem walking (heel-to-toe)

4. High-stepping Gait (Foot drop - L5 radiculopathy, common peroneal nerve palsy):
  • Lifts knee high to prevent toes dragging
  • Foot slaps down
  • Bilateral: Peripheral neuropathy

5. Waddling Gait (Proximal muscle weakness - myopathy):
  • Trendelenburg (pelvis drops on opposite side with each step)
  • Waddles side-to-side
  • Difficulty rising from chair

6. Scissoring Gait (Spastic paraplegia):
  • Legs stiff, adducted (cross over)
  • Toes drag

7. Stamping Gait (Sensory ataxia - posterior column loss):
  • Watches feet when walking (visual compensation for lost proprioception)
  • High steps, stamps feet
  • Romberg positive (see below)

Special tests: Tandem gait (heel-to-toe):
  • "Walk in a straight line, placing your heel directly in front of your toes each step."
  • Impaired: Cerebellar ataxia, posterior column loss, alcohol intoxication

Romberg test (posterior column function):
  • "Stand with your feet together and arms by your side. Now close your eyes."
  • Stand close to catch patient if falls
  • Positive (sways/falls with eyes closed): Sensory ataxia (posterior column loss - B12 deficiency, tabes dorsalis, peripheral neuropathy)

- Relies on vision to compensate for lost proprioception

  • Negative (stable with eyes closed): Vestibular ataxia (sways with eyes open and closed)
  • Cerebellar ataxia: Sways with eyes open, worse with eyes closed (but not diagnostic)

Tip-toe walking: Tests S1 (plantarflexion) Heel walking: Tests L4-L5 (dorsiflexion)

6. COMMON CAUSES OF WEAKNESS (Differential by Pattern)

Acute Hemiplegia (Sudden Onset)

1. Stroke (ischemic or hemorrhagic):
  • Most common
  • Vascular risk factors
  • Management: See Acute Stroke section above

2. Todd's paralysis (post-ictal):
  • Transient hemiparesis after focal seizure
  • Resolves within 24-48 hours
  • Diagnosis of exclusion (image to rule out stroke)

3. Hemiplegic migraine:
  • Rare migraine variant
  • Hemiparesis during aura (reversible)
  • Family history often present
  • Diagnosis of exclusion (image to rule out stroke)

Acute Ascending Weakness

Guillain-Barr√© Syndrome: See üö® RED FLAGs section above

Fluctuating Weakness (Fatigable)

Myasthenia Gravis: Presentation:
  • Fatigable weakness (worse with repetition, improves with rest)
  • Ptosis (drooping eyelid, worse as day progresses)
  • Diplopia (double vision)
  • Bulbar symptoms: Dysarthria, dysphagia
  • Proximal limb weakness (difficulty rising from chair)
  • Respiratory muscles (can cause crisis - ICU, intubation)
  • No sensory symptoms

Investigations:
  • Acetylcholine receptor antibodies (80% positive)
  • Anti-MuSK antibodies (if AChR negative)
  • CT chest: Thymoma (15% - thymus hyperplasia common)
  • Tensilon test (edrophonium): Improvement in ptosis/weakness after IV edrophonium (rarely done now)
  • Electromyography: Decremental response to repetitive nerve stimulation

Management:
  • Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors: Pyridostigmine 60mg TDS-QID (symptomatic relief)
  • Immunosuppression: Prednisolone, azathioprine
  • Thymectomy if thymoma present (improves outcomes even without thymoma)
  • Myasthenic crisis (respiratory failure): ICU, intubation, plasmapheresis or IVIG

Progressive Weakness (Chronic)

Motor Neuron Disease (MND) / Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS): Presentation:
  • Mixed UMN + LMN signs (pathognomonic)
  • Progressive weakness (months-years)
  • Fasciculations (widespread)
  • Muscle wasting
  • NO sensory loss (pure motor)
  • Bulbar involvement: Dysarthria, dysphagia (50%)
  • Respiratory involvement (eventually - leading cause of death)

Investigations:
  • EMG: Widespread denervation, fasciculations
  • Exclude mimics: MRI spine (cord compression), B12, thyroid, HIV

Management:
  • Riluzole: Slows progression (3-6 months survival benefit)
  • Multidisciplinary: Neurology, respiratory, dietitian, speech therapy, palliative care
  • NIV (non-invasive ventilation): Improves quality of life, survival
  • PEG feeding: If dysphagia, weight loss
  • Prognosis: Median survival 3-5 years from diagnosis

7. COMMON IMG MISTAKES

  1. Not recognizing acute stroke time window:

- Thrombolysis window 4.5 hours - "TIME IS BRAIN" - Check last seen well time, not when patient noticed symptoms

  1. Not using ROSIER score:

- Differentiates stroke from mimics (seizure, hypoglycemia) - Prevents unnecessary thrombolysis

  1. Delaying imaging in suspected spinal cord compression:

- URGENT MRI + high-dose steroids (dexamethasone 16mg) - Prognosis depends on speed of treatment (<24-48 hours)

  1. Missing cauda equina syndrome:

- Bilateral leg weakness + saddle anesthesia + urinary retention = cauda equina - Always do PR exam (reduced anal tone - critical finding) - URGENT surgical decompression (<48 hours)

  1. Not monitoring respiratory function in GBS:

- Serial FVC (forced vital capacity) every 4-6 hours - Intubate if FVC <15-20 ml/kg or rapid decline - 25% need ventilation

  1. Confusing UMN and LMN signs:

- UMN: Increased tone, brisk reflexes, extensor plantar, NO wasting - LMN: Decreased tone, reduced reflexes, wasting, fasciculations

  1. Not testing for pronator drift:

- Sensitive sign of UMN weakness (more sensitive than power testing) - Arm drifts down and pronates

  1. Incorrect MRC grading:

- Grade 4: Movement against resistance (NOT just against gravity) - Grade 3: Movement against gravity only

  1. Not checking plantar reflex correctly:

- Stroke lateral sole (not medial - causes withdraw response) - Observe great toe (upgoing = Babinski positive = UMN)

  1. Missing myasthenia gravis:

- Fatigable weakness is key (ask about variation during day) - Ptosis worse as day progresses - Acetylcholine receptor antibodies, thymoma
