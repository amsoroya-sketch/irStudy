Abdominal Pain: Differential Diagnosis and Management

ICRP OSCE Preparation - Medicine Module (Gastroenterology)

Target Audience

This guide is for International Medical Graduates (IMGs) preparing for the AMC Clinical OSCE, specifically:

  • IMGs requiring differential-driven approach to abdominal pain presentations
  • Candidates needing systematic frameworks for acute and chronic abdominal pain
  • Doctors preparing for ICRP placement in Australian ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY Departments and general medicine wards
  • Medical professionals needing pattern recognition for common and life-threatening causes of abdominal pain
  • Clinicians requiring Australian investigation and management protocols

Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  1. Generate differential diagnoses for abdominal pain based on location, character, and associated features
  2. Apply systematic history-taking frameworks (SOCRATES, associated symptoms, üö® RED FLAGs)
  3. Recognize life-threatening causes requiring immediate intervention (AAA, perforated viscus, ectopic pregnancy, mesenteric ischemia)
  4. Investigate abdominal pain appropriately (bloods, imaging, risk stratification)
  5. Manage common causes of abdominal pain according to Australian guidelines (eTG 2024)
  6. Identify surgical vs medical causes (when to call surgery)
  7. Avoid common IMG mistakes in abdominal pain assessment
  8. Perform confidently in 8-minute OSCE stations for abdominal pain

Dr. Amir Soufi's Approach Applied to Abdominal Pain

Core Principle: DIFFERENTIAL from LOCATION + CHARACTER

Traditional Approach (Less Effective):
  • Take complete generic abdominal history
  • Consider diagnosis at end
  • All abdominal pain treated identically

Dr. Soufi's Differential-Driven Approach:
  1. LOCATION FIRST: Differential diagnosis generated IMMEDIATELY from pain location

- RUQ pain ‚Üí Biliary, hepatic, pneumonia - Epigastric ‚Üí Peptic ulcer, pancreatitis, cardiac - RLQ ‚Üí Appendicitis, gynae (women), renal - Etc.

  1. CHARACTER REFINES: Nature of pain narrows differential

- Colicky ‚Üí Obstruction (bowel, renal, biliary) - Constant severe ‚Üí Inflammation, perforation, ischemia - Burning ‚Üí Peptic, reflux

  1. ASSOCIATED FEATURES REFINE FURTHER:

- Fever ‚Üí Infection, inflammation (appendicitis, cholecystitis, diverticulitis) - Jaundice ‚Üí Biliary obstruction, hepatitis - Vomiting ‚Üí Obstruction, inflammation - Diarrhea/PR bleeding ‚Üí Colitis, gastroenteritis

  1. üö® RED FLAGS IMMEDIATELY RECOGNIZED:

- AAA (ruptured/rupturing abdominal aortic aneurysm) ‚Üí Abdominal/back pain, age >60, vascular risk factors, hypotension ‚Üí IMMEDIATE vascular surgery - Perforated viscus ‚Üí Sudden severe abdominal pain, peritonism, air under diaphragm ‚Üí IMMEDIATE surgery - Ectopic pregnancy ‚Üí Abdominal pain + amenorrhea + PV bleeding in woman of childbearing age ‚Üí IMMEDIATE gynae - Mesenteric ischemia ‚Üí Severe pain out of proportion to examination, elderly, AF/vascular disease ‚Üí IMMEDIATE surgery

  1. RULE OUT LIFE-THREATENING FIRST:

- Always consider: AAA, perforation, ectopic, mesenteric ischemia, bowel obstruction - Even if diagnosis seems obvious (e.g., "looks like gastroenteritis"), exclude üö® RED FLAGs

  1. TARGETED INVESTIGATIONS:

- Let differential guide investigations - Example: RUQ pain + fever ‚Üí LFTs, lipase, ultrasound (biliary) - Example: Elderly + AF + severe abdominal pain ‚Üí Lactate, CT abdomen (mesenteric ischemia)

Example:
  • Presenting complaint: 65-year-old man, sudden severe central abdominal pain radiating to back
  • Immediate differential (from pattern): AAA rupture, pancreatitis, perforated peptic ulcer, mesenteric ischemia
  • Key question IMMEDIATELY: "Any history of AAA?" "Vascular disease?" "Blood pressure stable?" (Rule out AAA - life-threatening)
  • Targeted examination: Pulsatile abdominal mass? Peritonism? Hemodynamically stable?
  • Targeted investigations: URGENT CT angiogram if AAA suspected, lipase if pancreatitis suspected

1. SYSTEMATIC HISTORY-TAKING FOR ABDOMINAL PAIN

SOCRATES Framework (Pain Characterization)

S - Site: Where is the pain? (Point with one finger - maximum tenderness) O - Onset: Sudden or gradual?
  • Sudden (seconds to minutes) ‚Üí Perforation, AAA rupture, ectopic rupture, renal colic
  • Rapid (minutes to hours) ‚Üí Appendicitis, cholecystitis, pancreatitis, bowel obstruction
  • Gradual (hours to days) ‚Üí Gastroenteritis, constipation, IBS

C - Character: What does it feel like?
  • Colicky (comes and goes in waves) ‚Üí Obstruction (bowel, renal, biliary)
  • Constant, severe ‚Üí Inflammation (appendicitis, diverticulitis), perforation, ischemia
  • Burning ‚Üí Peptic ulcer, reflux
  • Cramping ‚Üí Gastroenteritis, IBS

R - Radiation: Does it spread anywhere?
  • To back ‚Üí Pancreatitis, AAA, renal
  • To shoulder ‚Üí Diaphragmatic irritation (perforated ulcer, ruptured spleen, cholecystitis)
  • To groin ‚Üí Renal colic, AAA

A - Associated symptoms: (See detailed section below) T - Timing/Temporal pattern:
  • Constant ‚Üí Inflammation, ischemia, obstruction (late)
  • Intermittent ‚Üí Biliary colic, renal colic, IBS
  • Worse after eating ‚Üí Peptic ulcer (if soon after), biliary (if fatty meal), mesenteric angina (if 30 min after)
  • Worse before bowel motion, better after ‚Üí IBS, constipation
  • Wakes from sleep ‚Üí Peptic ulcer (alarm symptom), pancreatitis

E - Exacerbating/Relieving factors:
  • Movement worsens ‚Üí Peritonitis (lie still)
  • Worse with coughing ‚Üí Peritonitis
  • Food worsens ‚Üí Peptic ulcer (if duodenal ulcer: food may relieve, pain returns 2-3 hours later)
  • Antacids relieve ‚Üí Peptic ulcer, reflux
  • Lying flat worsens ‚Üí Reflux, pancreatitis
  • Leaning forward relieves ‚Üí Pancreatitis

S - Severity (0-10 scale):
  • Severe (8-10/10), sudden ‚Üí AAA, perforation, ectopic rupture, mesenteric ischemia
  • Severe pain out of proportion to examination ‚Üí Mesenteric ischemia (CRITICAL üö® RED FLAG)

Associated Symptoms (Critical for Differential)

Gastrointestinal:
  • Nausea/vomiting:

- Early vomiting ‚Üí Gastroenteritis, gastritis, peptic ulcer - Late vomiting (after pain) ‚Üí Appendicitis, obstruction - Bilious vomiting ‚Üí Bowel obstruction (distal to ampulla of Vater) - Feculent vomiting ‚Üí Distal bowel obstruction, bowel ischemia (late)

  • Diarrhea:

- Watery ‚Üí Gastroenteritis - Bloody ‚Üí IBD (ulcerative colitis, Crohn's), ischemic colitis, infectious colitis (Campylobacter, Shigella)

  • Constipation:

- Absolute constipation (no flatus or feces) ‚Üí Complete bowel obstruction

  • PR bleeding:

- Fresh red blood ‚Üí Lower GI (diverticulitis, IBD, ischemic colitis, cancer) - Melaena (black, tarry) ‚Üí Upper GI bleeding

  • Change in bowel habit: IBD, colorectal cancer (alarm symptom if >50 years)

Systemic:
  • Fever: Infection, inflammation (appendicitis, cholecystitis, diverticulitis, pyelonephritis, pneumonia)
  • Weight loss: Malignancy, IBD, chronic pancreatitis (alarm symptom)

Other:
  • Jaundice: Biliary obstruction (gallstones, pancreatic head cancer), hepatitis
  • Dysuria/frequency: UTI, pyelonephritis, renal colic
  • Gynaecological (in women of childbearing age - ALWAYS ASK):

- Last menstrual period (LMP): Ectopic pregnancy if missed period - Vaginal bleeding: Ectopic, miscarriage, PID - Vaginal discharge: PID

üö® RED FLAGs (Life-Threatening Causes - Recognize IMMEDIATELY)

"The Deadly Six" of Abdominal Pain:
  1. AAA (Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm - Ruptured/Rupturing):

- Pattern: Age >60, sudden severe abdominal/back pain, pulsatile abdominal mass, hypotension, syncope, vascular risk factors (smoking, hypertension, atherosclerosis) - Action: IMMEDIATE vascular surgery consult, URGENT CT angiogram (if stable), theatre if unstable - Mortality: 50-80% if ruptured

  1. Perforated Viscus (Peptic Ulcer, Diverticulitis, Bowel Perforation):

- Pattern: Sudden severe abdominal pain, peritonism (guarding, rigidity, rebound), history of NSAID use / steroids / diverticular disease - Action: IMMEDIATE surgery consult, erect CXR (air under diaphragm), CT abdomen - Mortality: High if delayed

  1. Ectopic Pregnancy (Ruptured):

- Pattern: Woman of childbearing age (15-50 years) with abdominal pain + amenorrhea (missed period) + PV bleeding, shoulder tip pain, syncope, hypotension - Action: IMMEDIATE gynae consult, pregnancy test (Œ≤hCG), pelvic ultrasound - ALWAYS exclude in women of childbearing age with abdominal pain

  1. Mesenteric Ischemia (Acute Arterial Occlusion of Bowel):

- Pattern: Severe pain out of proportion to examination (CRITICAL sign), elderly, AF/vascular disease, bloody diarrhea (late) - Action: IMMEDIATE surgery consult, URGENT CT angiogram, lactate (elevated) - Mortality: >50% if delayed (bowel infarction)

  1. Bowel Obstruction (Especially Closed Loop, Strangulated):

- Pattern: Colicky pain ‚Üí constant, vomiting, absolute constipation (no flatus/feces), distension, previous surgery (adhesions) - Action: URGENT surgery consult, CT abdomen, resuscitation (IV fluids, NGT)

  1. Acute Pancreatitis (Severe):

- Pattern: Severe epigastric pain radiating to back, vomiting, alcohol/gallstones, tachycardia, hypotension (if severe) - Action: Resuscitation, lipase, CT abdomen (if severe), ICU if severe (Ranson's criteria, APACHE II score)

KEY PRINCIPLE: ALWAYS consider these six in ANY patient with abdominal pain (especially elderly, hemodynamically unstable, severe pain)

2. DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS BY LOCATION

Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ) Pain

Anatomy: Liver, gallbladder, bile ducts, right kidney, hepatic flexure of colon, lung base Immediate Differential:
  1. Biliary Colic (Gallstone in cystic duct - transient obstruction):

- Pattern: Intermittent severe RUQ/epigastric pain, often after fatty meal, radiates to back/right shoulder - No fever (if fever ‚Üí cholecystitis) - Investigations: Normal WCC, normal LFTs (or mildly elevated ALT/GGT), ultrasound shows gallstones - Management: Analgesia, elective cholecystectomy

  1. Acute Cholecystitis (Infected gallbladder):

- Pattern: Constant severe RUQ pain, fever, nausea/vomiting, Murphy's sign positive - Investigations: Elevated WCC, CRP, mildly elevated LFTs, ultrasound (gallstones, thickened gallbladder wall, pericholecystic fluid) - Management: NBM, IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone + metronidazole - eTG 2024), IV fluids, analgesia, surgery consult (cholecystectomy within 72 hours preferred - early vs delayed)

  1. Ascending Cholangitis (Infection of bile ducts - medical ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY):

- Pattern: Charcot's triad = RUQ pain + fever + jaundice - Reynolds' pentad = Charcot's triad + confusion + hypotension (severe) - Cause: Biliary obstruction (gallstone in common bile duct, stricture, malignancy) ‚Üí infection - Investigations: Elevated WCC, CRP, elevated bilirubin (conjugated), elevated ALP/GGT, elevated ALT, blood cultures (E. coli, Klebsiella), ultrasound (dilated bile ducts) - Management: Resuscitation (sepsis protocol), IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone + metronidazole), URGENT ERCP within 24-48 hours (decompress bile duct)

  1. Acute Hepatitis (Viral, Drug-Induced, Alcoholic):

- Pattern: RUQ discomfort/pain, jaundice, nausea, fatigue, history of alcohol / drugs / viral exposure - Investigations: Markedly elevated ALT/AST (>10x normal in acute viral hepatitis), elevated bilirubin, hepatitis serology (HAV, HBV, HCV), paracetamol level if overdose suspected - Management: Supportive (most), stop hepatotoxic drugs, N-acetylcysteine if paracetamol overdose, refer hepatology if severe (INR elevated, encephalopathy)

  1. Peptic Ulcer Disease (Duodenal ulcer - can present as RUQ):

- Pattern: Epigastric/RUQ burning pain, worse 2-3 hours after eating (duodenal) or soon after eating (gastric), relieved by antacids - Risk factors: H. pylori, NSAIDs, smoking, steroids - Management: See epigastric pain section

  1. Pyelonephritis (Right):

- Pattern: RUQ/flank pain, fever, dysuria, frequency, renal angle tenderness - Investigations: Elevated WCC, CRP, urine MCS (pyuria, bacteriuria, nitrites), blood cultures - Management: IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone or gentamicin - eTG 2024), IV fluids, analgesia

  1. Pneumonia (Right lower lobe):

- Pattern: RUQ pain (referred from diaphragmatic irritation), fever, cough, dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain - Investigations: Elevated WCC, CRP, CXR (consolidation right lower lobe) - Management: Antibiotics as per CAP guidelines

  1. Hepatocellular Carcinoma / Liver Metastases:

- Pattern: RUQ dull ache, weight loss, jaundice (late), hepatomegaly, risk factors (chronic HBV/HCV, cirrhosis)

Epigastric Pain

Anatomy: Stomach, duodenum, pancreas, aorta (AAA) Immediate Differential:
  1. Peptic Ulcer Disease (Gastric or Duodenal Ulcer):

- Pattern: - Duodenal ulcer: Pain 2-3 hours after eating, relieved by food (temporarily), wakes at night - Gastric ulcer: Pain soon after eating, not relieved by food - Character: Burning, gnawing - Risk factors: H. pylori, NSAIDs, smoking, steroids, alcohol - Investigations: Urea breath test (H. pylori), or endoscopy if alarm symptoms (age >55, weight loss, dysphagia, persistent vomiting, GI bleeding) - Management: - If H. pylori positive: Triple therapy (amoxicillin + clarithromycin + PPI x 7 days - eTG 2024) - If NSAID-induced: Stop NSAID, PPI (esomeprazole 40mg daily x 8 weeks) - Alarm symptoms ‚Üí endoscopy (rule out malignancy)

  1. GORD (Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease):

- Pattern: Burning epigastric/retrosternal pain, worse lying flat, after large meals, relieved by antacids - Associated: Regurgitation, waterbrash (excessive salivation) - Management: Lifestyle (weight loss, avoid late meals, raise bed head), PPI (esomeprazole 20mg daily)

  1. Acute Pancreatitis:

- Pattern: Severe epigastric pain radiating to back, constant, nausea/vomiting, patient leans forward for relief - Causes: Gallstones (40%), alcohol (30%), drugs (azathioprine, valproate), hypertriglyceridemia, hypercalcemia, trauma, ERCP - Investigations: - Lipase >3x upper limit normal (diagnostic) - Amylase elevated (less sensitive/specific than lipase) - LFTs (if gallstone cause: elevated bilirubin, ALP) - Calcium, triglycerides - CT abdomen if severe or diagnosis uncertain (pancreatic necrosis, collections) - Severity assessment: Ranson's criteria, APACHE II, CRP >150 at 48 hours - Management: - Resuscitation: IV fluids (aggressive - 250-500ml/hr initially), analgesia (opioids) - NBM initially, early enteral feeding if tolerated (within 48 hours - reduces complications) - Treat cause: ERCP if biliary obstruction, stop alcohol - ICU if severe (organ failure, extensive necrosis) - Surgery consult if infected necrosis (antibiotics + debridement)

  1. Perforated Peptic Ulcer:

- Pattern: Sudden severe epigastric pain, peritonism (board-like rigidity), history of PUD or NSAID use - Investigations: Erect CXR (air under diaphragm in 70%), CT abdomen - Management: IMMEDIATE surgery consult (laparotomy, repair), resuscitation, IV antibiotics

  1. AAA (Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm - Ruptured/Rupturing):

- Pattern: Age >60, sudden severe epigastric/central abdominal pain radiating to back, pulsatile abdominal mass, hypotension - Management: IMMEDIATE vascular surgery, URGENT CT angiogram if stable, theatre if unstable

  1. Acute Coronary Syndrome (MI - Inferior):

- Pattern: Epigastric pain (may mimic indigestion), especially inferior MI, nausea/vomiting, dyspnea, diaphoresis - Risk factors: Age >50, cardiac risk factors (smoking, hypertension, diabetes, family history) - Investigations: ECG IMMEDIATELY (ST elevation in II, III, aVF = inferior STEMI), troponin - Management: Dual antiplatelet (aspirin + ticagrelor), anticoagulation, cardiology (PCI if STEMI)

  1. Gastritis / Gastroenteritis:

- Gastritis: Epigastric pain, nausea, alcohol, NSAIDs - Gastroenteritis: Epigastric/central abdominal cramping, diarrhea, vomiting, often viral or food poisoning

Left Upper Quadrant (LUQ) Pain

Anatomy: Spleen, stomach, pancreas (tail), left kidney, splenic flexure of colon, lung base Immediate Differential:
  1. Splenic Pathology:

- Splenic infarction: LUQ pain, fever, history of AF / endocarditis (emboli) - Splenic rupture: LUQ pain, left shoulder pain (Kehr's sign), trauma, hypotension - Splenomegaly (various causes): LUQ dull ache, early satiety

  1. Pyelonephritis (Left):

- LUQ/flank pain, fever, dysuria, renal angle tenderness

  1. Gastritis / Gastric Ulcer: (Can present as LUQ)

  1. Pancreatitis: (Tail of pancreas - may present as LUQ)

  1. Pneumonia (Left lower lobe):

- LUQ referred pain, fever, cough, dyspnea

Right Lower Quadrant (RLQ) Pain

Anatomy: Appendix, cecum, terminal ileum, right ovary/fallopian tube (women), right ureter Immediate Differential:
  1. Acute Appendicitis (Most common surgical ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY):

- Classic pattern (only 50% of patients): - Periumbilical pain (initially - visceral pain from midgut) - Migrates to RLQ after 12-24 hours (parietal peritoneum involvement) - Anorexia (loss of appetite - very common) - Nausea/vomiting (after pain onset) - Fever (low-grade initially, higher if perforated) - Examination: RLQ tenderness (McBurney's point), guarding, rebound (if peritonitis), Rovsing's sign (RLQ pain when palpating LLQ), psoas sign (pain with right hip extension) - Investigations: - WCC elevated (left shift - neutrophilia) - CRP elevated - CT abdomen (if diagnosis uncertain - sensitivity 95%, shows inflamed appendix, appendicolith, periappendiceal fat stranding) - Ultrasound (less sensitive, useful in children, pregnancy) - Management: IMMEDIATE surgery consult, appendicectomy (laparoscopic preferred), IV antibiotics if perforated (ceftriaxone + metronidazole)

  1. Ectopic Pregnancy (Right) - ALWAYS consider in women of childbearing age:

- Pattern: RLQ pain + amenorrhea (missed period) + PV bleeding - Investigations: Œ≤hCG (pregnancy test), pelvic ultrasound (no intrauterine pregnancy, adnexal mass, free fluid) - Management: IMMEDIATE gynae consult, resuscitation if ruptured, laparoscopy

  1. Ovarian Torsion (Right):

- Pattern: Sudden severe RLQ pain, nausea/vomiting, history of ovarian cyst or mass - Examination: Tender adnexal mass - Investigations: Pelvic ultrasound (enlarged ovary, reduced blood flow on Doppler) - Management: URGENT gynae consult (laparoscopy within hours - untwist ovary to preserve)

  1. Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID):

- Pattern: RLQ or bilateral lower abdominal pain, vaginal discharge, fever, sexually active, history of STI - Examination: Cervical motion tenderness, adnexal tenderness - Investigations: Elevated WCC, CRP, vaginal swabs (chlamydia, gonorrhea) - Management: Antibiotics (ceftriaxone 500mg IM stat + doxycycline 100mg BD + metronidazole 400mg BD x 14 days - eTG 2024), contact tracing

  1. Renal Colic (Right):

- Pattern: Severe colicky RLQ/flank pain radiating to groin, patient cannot lie still (unlike peritonitis), hematuria - Investigations: Urine MCS (hematuria), non-contrast CT KUB (gold standard - identifies stone, hydronephrosis) - Management: Analgesia (NSAIDs - ketorolac, diclofenac; opioids), IV fluids, urology referral if large stone (>5mm), obstructed + infected (‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY), bilateral obstruction

  1. Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) - Crohn's (Terminal Ileum):

- Pattern: Chronic/recurrent RLQ pain, diarrhea (may be bloody), weight loss, mouth ulcers, perianal disease - Investigations: Elevated CRP, anemia, low albumin, fecal calprotectin elevated, colonoscopy + biopsy (skip lesions, transmural inflammation) - Management: Gastroenterology referral, corticosteroids (acute flare), immunosuppressants (azathioprine, biologics)

  1. Cecal Pathology:

- Cecal volvulus: Sudden RLQ pain, distension, absolute constipation, "coffee bean sign" on AXR - Cecal cancer: Chronic RLQ dull ache, change in bowel habit, weight loss, iron-deficiency anemia, colonoscopy

Left Lower Quadrant (LLQ) Pain

Anatomy: Sigmoid colon, left ovary/fallopian tube (women), left ureter Immediate Differential:
  1. Acute Diverticulitis ("Left-sided appendicitis"):

- Pattern: LLQ pain, fever, change in bowel habit (diarrhea or constipation), age >50 - Examination: LLQ tenderness, guarding (if complicated) - Investigations: Elevated WCC, CRP, CT abdomen (diagnostic - diverticula, fat stranding, abscess, perforation) - Management: - Uncomplicated: Oral antibiotics (amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125mg BD x 7 days - eTG 2024), clear fluids initially, analgesia - Complicated (abscess, perforation, obstruction): Surgery consult, IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone + metronidazole), NBM, IV fluids, CT-guided drainage if large abscess, surgery if perforated - Colonoscopy 6-8 weeks after resolution (rule out colorectal cancer)

  1. Ectopic Pregnancy (Left) / Ovarian Torsion (Left) / PID: (As per RLQ, but left-sided)

  1. Renal Colic (Left):

  1. Ulcerative Colitis:

- Pattern: LLQ pain, bloody diarrhea (hallmark), urgency, tenesmus - Investigations: Elevated CRP, anemia, low albumin, fecal calprotectin, colonoscopy (continuous inflammation from rectum proximally, pseudopolyps) - Management: Gastroenterology, 5-ASA (mesalazine), corticosteroids (flare), immunosuppressants

  1. Colorectal Cancer (Sigmoid):

- Pattern: LLQ pain, change in bowel habit (alternating diarrhea/constipation), PR bleeding, weight loss, age >50 - Alarm symptoms: Age >50 + change in bowel habit, PR bleeding, weight loss, iron-deficiency anemia - Investigations: FBC (anemia), fecal occult blood test (FOBT), colonoscopy + biopsy - Management: Surgical resection, oncology

Central Abdominal Pain

Anatomy: Small bowel (jejunum, ileum), aorta, stomach Immediate Differential:
  1. Small Bowel Obstruction:

- Pattern: Central colicky pain, vomiting (early, bilious), absolute constipation (no flatus/feces), distension - Causes: Adhesions (previous surgery), hernias, malignancy, Crohn's, intussusception - Investigations: AXR (dilated small bowel loops >3cm, air-fluid levels, no gas in rectum), CT abdomen (identifies level and cause) - Management: Surgery consult, NBM, NGT (decompression), IV fluids (resuscitation), surgery if strangulated / closed loop / complete obstruction

  1. Gastroenteritis:

- Pattern: Central cramping abdominal pain, diarrhea, vomiting, fever, recent food / unwell contacts - Management: Supportive (oral rehydration, antiemetics), antibiotics if severe / dysentery (ciprofloxacin)

  1. Early Appendicitis: (Periumbilical pain before migration to RLQ)

  1. Mesenteric Ischemia (Acute):

- Pattern: Severe central abdominal pain out of proportion to examination (CRITICAL sign), elderly, AF / vascular disease, bloody diarrhea (late) - Investigations: Elevated lactate (tissue ischemia), CT angiogram (arterial occlusion, bowel wall thickening, pneumatosis) - Management: IMMEDIATE surgery consult (laparotomy, bowel resection), resuscitation, anticoagulation if embolic, high mortality if delayed

  1. AAA (Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm):

- Pattern: Central abdominal pain radiating to back, pulsatile abdominal mass, age >60, vascular risk factors

Suprapubic Pain

Anatomy: Bladder, uterus, prostate Immediate Differential:
  1. UTI (Cystitis):

- Pattern: Suprapubic pain, dysuria, frequency, urgency, cloudy/smelly urine - Investigations: Urine MCS (pyuria, bacteriuria, nitrites positive) - Management: Antibiotics (trimethoprim 300mg daily x 3 days or cephalexin 500mg BD x 5 days - eTG 2024)

  1. Urinary Retention (Acute):

- Pattern: Suprapubic pain, inability to void, distended bladder (dull to percussion, palpable mass) - Causes: BPH (men), medications (anticholinergics, opioids), neurological (spinal cord injury) - Management: Urinary catheter (immediate relief), treat underlying cause, urology referral

  1. Pelvic Inflammatory Disease:

  1. Ectopic Pregnancy / Miscarriage:

  1. Endometriosis:

- Pattern: Cyclical pelvic pain (worse during menstruation), dysmenorrhea, dyspareunia, infertility - Management: Gynecology referral, NSAIDs, hormonal therapy (COCP, GnRH agonists), laparoscopy (diagnostic and therapeutic)

Diffuse Abdominal Pain

Immediate Differential:
  1. Gastroenteritis: Most common

  1. Peritonitis (Generalized):

- Causes: Perforated viscus, ruptured appendix, perforated diverticulitis - Pattern: Diffuse severe pain, peritonism (guarding, rigidity, rebound), lying still - Management: IMMEDIATE surgery, resuscitation, IV antibiotics

  1. Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA):

- Pattern: Diffuse abdominal pain, polyuria, polydipsia, vomiting, Kussmaul breathing, fruity breath - Investigations: Elevated glucose, ketones (blood or urine), metabolic acidosis (low pH, low HCO3) - Management: Resuscitation (IV fluids, insulin infusion, potassium replacement), treat precipitant (infection)

  1. Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS):

- Pattern: Chronic/recurrent diffuse cramping abdominal pain, relieved by defecation, change in stool form/frequency, bloating - Diagnosis of exclusion (rule out üö® RED FLAGs first) - Management: Reassurance, dietary advice (low FODMAP), antispasmodics (mebeverine), psychological therapy

3. INVESTIGATIONS FOR ABDOMINAL PAIN

Bedside

  • Vital signs: Temperature (fever?), BP (hypotension = shock), HR (tachycardia), RR
  • Urine pregnancy test (Œ≤hCG) - MANDATORY in all women of childbearing age
  • Urine MCS (dipstick + formal MCS)
  • Blood glucose (if DKA suspected)
  • ECG (if age >50, cardiac risk factors, epigastric pain)

Bloods

First-line:
  • FBC: WCC (elevated in infection/inflammation), hemoglobin (anemia if GI bleeding or chronic disease)
  • CRP: Elevated in inflammation/infection
  • Lipase: If pancreatitis suspected (>3x upper limit normal = diagnostic)
  • LFTs: If RUQ pain, jaundice (AST/ALT/ALP/GGT/bilirubin)
  • UEC: Renal function, electrolytes (vomiting/diarrhea)
  • Lactate: If mesenteric ischemia suspected (elevated)
  • Œ≤hCG (blood): If ectopic pregnancy suspected, confirm pregnancy

Second-line (if indicated):
  • Blood cultures: If sepsis (cholangitis, perforated viscus, severe infection)
  • Amylase: Less specific than lipase for pancreatitis
  • Calcium: Hypercalcemia ‚Üí pancreatitis
  • Troponin: If cardiac cause suspected (ACS)
  • Coagulation (INR, APTT): If bleeding, liver disease
  • Group and hold / crossmatch: If surgery likely, GI bleeding

Imaging

Ultrasound:
  • RUQ pain: Gallstones, cholecystitis, dilated bile ducts
  • Pelvic ultrasound (transvaginal): Ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, ovarian cyst, PID
  • Renal tract: Hydronephrosis (obstruction)
  • AAA screening: If suspected (but CT angiogram preferred if unstable)

CT Abdomen/Pelvis with IV Contrast:
  • GOLD STANDARD for most acute abdominal pain (if diagnosis uncertain)
  • Indications: Appendicitis (if uncertain), diverticulitis, bowel obstruction, pancreatitis (severe), abscess, perforation, malignancy
  • CT angiogram: AAA, mesenteric ischemia

Erect Chest X-Ray (CXR):
  • Perforation: Air under diaphragm (free gas)
  • Pneumonia (lower lobe)

Abdominal X-Ray (AXR) (limited use):
  • Bowel obstruction: Dilated bowel loops, air-fluid levels
  • Perforation: Free gas
  • Renal calculi: Radiopaque stones (but CT better)

MRI:
  • Pregnancy (if CT contraindicated)
  • Liver / pancreatic lesions (better characterization)

Endoscopy

Upper GI Endoscopy (OGD):
  • Indications: Peptic ulcer (if alarm symptoms), upper GI bleeding, dysphagia, persistent vomiting

Colonoscopy:
  • Indications: PR bleeding, change in bowel habit (age >50), IBD diagnosis/monitoring, 6-8 weeks after diverticulitis (rule out cancer)

ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography):
  • Indications: Ascending cholangitis (URGENT decompression), biliary obstruction (stone removal), pancreatic duct pathology

4. MANAGEMENT PRINCIPLES

Resuscitation (ABCDE Approach for Acute Abdomen)

A - Airway: Maintain airway (if vomiting, obtunded) B - Breathing: Oxygen if hypoxic, monitor RR C - Circulation:
  • Two large-bore IV cannulae (if unstable, suspected bleeding, sepsis)
  • IV fluids: Crystalloid (0.9% NaCl or Hartmann's) - rapid bolus if hypotensive (500ml-1L)
  • Bloods: FBC, UEC, lipase, LFTs, lactate, group and hold, cultures

D - Disability: GCS, glucose (DKA? Hypoglycemia?) E - Exposure: Full abdominal examination, look for peritonism, masses, hernias

Analgesia (Pain Relief)

OLD MYTH: "Don't give analgesia before surgical review - it will mask signs" MODERN PRACTICE: Give adequate analgesia - improves patient comfort, does NOT adversely affect diagnostic accuracy, improves cooperation with examination Options:
  • Paracetamol 1g IV/PO (mild pain)
  • NSAIDs: Ketorolac 10-30mg IV (good for renal colic, biliary colic) - avoid if suspected perforation, renal impairment
  • Opioids: Morphine 2.5-5mg IV (titrate), oxycodone 5-10mg PO, fentanyl 25-50mcg IV
  • Antispasmodics: Hyoscine butylbromide (Buscopan) 20mg IV (for colicky pain - bowel, biliary, renal)

When to Call Surgery

IMMEDIATE Surgery Consult:
  • Peritonitis (guarding, rigidity, rebound)
  • Perforation (air under diaphragm, severe peritonitis)
  • AAA (ruptured/rupturing)
  • Bowel obstruction (especially if complete, strangulated, closed loop)
  • Appendicitis
  • Mesenteric ischemia
  • Acute cholecystitis (if complicated - perforation, empyema)

URGENT Surgery Consult:
  • Diverticulitis (if complicated - abscess, perforation)
  • Ascending cholangitis (may need ERCP, but discuss with surgery)

5. COMMON IMG MISTAKES

  1. Not considering ectopic pregnancy in women of childbearing age:

- ALWAYS do pregnancy test in women 15-50 years with abdominal pain - Ectopic can present with vague abdominal pain, not always classical presentation

  1. Missing AAA in elderly with abdominal pain:

- ALWAYS consider AAA in patients >60 with abdominal/back pain - Palpate for pulsatile abdominal mass - URGENT CT angiogram if suspected

  1. Not recognizing mesenteric ischemia:

- "Pain out of proportion to examination" = CRITICAL üö® RED FLAG - Elderly + AF + severe abdominal pain = mesenteric ischemia until proven otherwise - Check lactate, URGENT CT angiogram, IMMEDIATE surgery consult

  1. Withholding analgesia before surgical review:

- Give adequate analgesia - does NOT mask signs, improves patient comfort

  1. Not doing CT for uncertain appendicitis:

- If diagnosis uncertain, CT abdomen (sensitivity 95%) - Reduces negative laparotomy rate

  1. Discharging "gastroenteritis" without considering üö® RED FLAGs:

- Always consider AAA, mesenteric ischemia, early appendicitis, ectopic - If elderly, immunosuppressed, severe pain, hemodynamic instability ‚Üí investigate further

  1. Not checking for peritonism systematically:

- Examine properly: guarding, rigidity, rebound tenderness, percussion tenderness, lying still - Peritonitis = surgical ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY

  1. Missing ascending cholangitis:

- Charcot's triad (RUQ pain + fever + jaundice) = ascending cholangitis - URGENT resuscitation, antibiotics, ERCP within 24-48 hours - Can progress rapidly to septic shock (Reynolds' pentad)

  1. Inadequate resuscitation in pancreatitis:

- Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation critical (250-500ml/hr initially) - Early enteral feeding (within 48 hours) reduces complications - Assess severity (Ranson's, APACHE II)

  1. Not documenting examination findings clearly:

- Document: Location, peritonism (present/absent), bowel sounds, masses, hernias, rectal exam (if indicated) - Medicolegal protection

Differential-Driven Approach by Location

  • RUQ: Biliary (colic, cholecystitis, cholangitis), hepatitis, peptic ulcer, pyelonephritis, pneumonia
  • Epigastric: Peptic ulcer, GORD, pancreatitis, perforation, AAA, ACS
  • LUQ: Splenic pathology, gastritis, pancreatitis (tail), pyelonephritis, pneumonia
  • RLQ: Appendicitis, ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, PID, renal colic, Crohn's
  • LLQ: Diverticulitis, ectopic pregnancy, ulcerative colitis, renal colic
  • Central: Bowel obstruction, gastroenteritis, mesenteric ischemia, AAA
  • Suprapubic: UTI, urinary retention, PID, ectopic pregnancy
  • Diffuse: Gastroenteritis, peritonitis, DKA, IBS
