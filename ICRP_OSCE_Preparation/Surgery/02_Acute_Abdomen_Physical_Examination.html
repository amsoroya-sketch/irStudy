INTRODUCTION

Examination Driven by Differential (Dr. Amir's Approach)

Traditional approach: Do complete examination ‚Üí Think about findings Dr. Amir's approach: Know your differential from history ‚Üí Examination CONFIRMS or EXCLUDES each differential ‚Üí Findings narrow diagnosis Example:
  • History suggests appendicitis
  • Examination purpose: Confirm localized RLQ tenderness, rule out peritonitis, rule out other causes
  • You're looking for: McBurney's point tenderness, Rovsing's sign, rebound (local vs generalized), psoas sign, obturator sign

The 5 Ps Framework

THE 5 Ps FRAMEWORK

P1: PREPARATION (15 seconds)

What to Say:

"I'm going to examine your abdomen. This will involve me looking at and feeling your tummy. Is that okay?"

Actions:
  • Wash hands / hand sanitizer (ALWAYS - examiner watching)
  • Ensure privacy (draw curtain, close door)
  • Chaperone offered (especially male doctor, female patient)
  • Adequate lighting
  • Equipment ready: Stethoscope

P2: POSITION (15 seconds)

What to Say:

"Can you lie flat on the bed for me, with your arms by your sides? I'll need to examine from here [indicate chest] to here [indicate pelvis]."

Patient Position:
  • Lying flat (supine)
  • One pillow under head (comfortable, but not too propped up)
  • Arms by sides (NOT folded across chest - tenses abdomen)
  • Exposure: Nipples to knees (need to see full abdomen, groins, and potentially examine hernial orifices)

Examiner Position:
  • Stand on patient's RIGHT side
  • At level of patient's abdomen (not at head or feet)

Examiner Note: In OSCE, patient usually already positioned. Just check positioning is correct.

P3: PERMISSION (10 seconds)

What to Say:

"Before I start, are you in any pain right now?"

[Patient points to area]

"Okay, I'll examine that area last so I don't cause you unnecessary discomfort."

Why This Matters:
  • Shows compassion
  • Tactical: If you palpate painful area first, patient will guard throughout rest of examination (muscle tension)
  • Correct approach: Palpate painful area LAST

P4: PERFORM - INSPECTION (30-45 seconds)

What to Say:

"I'm going to start by looking at your abdomen."

Stand at foot of bed (better view of symmetry, contours)

4A: GENERAL INSPECTION

Look at Patient as Whole:
  • Does patient look unwell? (Septic, shocked, in pain)
  • Position: Lying still (peritonitis) vs writhing (renal colic)
  • Colour: Pale (anaemia, shock), jaundiced (biliary pathology)

4B: INSPECTION OF ABDOMEN

Systematic Approach - Walk Around Bed if Needed: 1. SHAPE & CONTOUR:
  • Flat (normal)
  • Scaphoid (concave - malnutrition, elderly)
  • Distended (generalized or localized)

- Generalized distension: 5 Fs - Flatus (bowel obstruction) - Fluid (ascites) - Fetus (pregnancy) - Fat (obesity) - Feces (severe constipation) - Localized distension: Mass, organomegaly, hernia

2. SCARS:
  • Indicates previous surgery ‚Üí Adhesions (cause of bowel obstruction)
  • Common scars:

- Midline (laparotomy) - Right subcostal (cholecystectomy - open) - Paramedian - Pfannenstiel (C-section, gynae surgery) - Laparoscopic port sites (small, often in umbilicus + RUQ/LUQ) - Appendicectomy (RLQ - McBurney's point or laparoscopic)

What to Say: "I can see you've had previous surgery [indicate scar]. What operation was that?" 3. SKIN CHANGES:
  • Striae (stretch marks - pregnancy, Cushing's, rapid weight change)
  • Dilated veins (caput medusae - portal hypertension)
  • Erythema (cellulitis, herpes zoster)
  • Bruising:

- Grey Turner's sign (flank bruising) - Haemorrhagic pancreatitis, retroperitoneal bleed - Cullen's sign (periumbilical bruising) - Haemorrhagic pancreatitis, ruptured ectopic

4. HERNIAS (Look before you palpate):
  • Umbilical hernia: Protrusion at umbilicus
  • Incisional hernia: At scar site
  • Inguinal hernia: Groin swelling
  • Femoral hernia: Below inguinal ligament

What to Say: "Can you cough for me please?" [Watch for hernia appearing/enlarging on cough impulse] 5. VISIBLE MASSES:
  • Large masses may be visible (AAA pulsation, large tumor)

6. MOVEMENT WITH RESPIRATION:
  • Abdomen should rise and fall with breathing
  • Absent movement ‚Üí Peritonitis (patient breathing with chest only, abdomen rigid)

P4: PERFORM - PALPATION (3-4 minutes)

This is the MOST important part of abdominal examination

4C: LIGHT PALPATION

Purpose: Assess for tenderness, guarding, masses Technique:
  • Kneel or bend so your hand is horizontal to patient's abdomen (not pressing down from height)
  • Watch patient's face (while palpating - see pain reaction)
  • Use flat of hand (not fingertips - less uncomfortable)
  • Gentle pressure (2-3 cm depth)

What to Say:

"I'm going to feel your tummy gently. Let me know if any area is tender."

Systematic Approach - 9 Regions (or 4 quadrants):

Start in region FURTHEST from pain, work towards painful area

9 Regions:
  1. Right hypochondrium
  2. Epigastrium
  3. Left hypochondrium
  4. Right lumbar
  5. Umbilical
  6. Left lumbar
  7. Right iliac fossa
  8. Suprapubic
  9. Left iliac fossa

What You're Feeling For:
  • Tenderness: Patient winces, says "ow"
  • Guarding: Involuntary muscle contraction (abdomen tenses when you palpate)

- Voluntary guarding: Patient tenses (anticipating pain) - throughout abdomen - Involuntary guarding: Reflex muscle contraction - localized to area of inflammation

  • Rigidity: Board-like hardness - indicates peritonitis

4D: DEEP PALPATION

Purpose: Feel for masses, organomegaly Technique:
  • Use one hand on top of other (for more pressure)
  • Deeper pressure (5-6 cm)
  • Same 9-region systematic approach

What to Say:

"Now I'm going to feel a bit deeper. Again, let me know if anything hurts."

What You're Feeling For:
  • Masses: Describe by 7 Ss (see below)
  • Organomegaly: Liver, spleen, kidneys (see below)

4E: PALPATION OF ORGANS

LIVER (Right Hypochondrium): Technique:
  • Start in RLF (right iliac fossa)
  • Hand parallel to right costal margin
  • Ask patient to "take a deep breath in"
  • Feel for liver edge descending onto your fingers
  • Move hand up 2cm, repeat, until reach costal margin

What to Say: "I'm going to feel for your liver. Take a deep breath in for me." Normal: Liver edge not palpable (OR just palpable at costal margin in thin patients / on deep inspiration) Abnormal - Hepatomegaly:
  • Palpable >2 cm below costal margin
  • Causes in acute abdomen: Hepatitis, liver abscess, hepatic congestion (CCF), metastases

If liver enlarged - describe:
  • Size (cm below costal margin)
  • Edge (smooth or nodular)
  • Consistency (soft, firm, hard)
  • Tenderness (tender in hepatitis, abscess)
  • Pulsatile (TR - tricuspid regurgitation)

SPLEEN (Left Hypochondrium): Technique:
  • Start in RIF (right iliac fossa) - yes, right (spleen enlarges diagonally towards RIF)
  • Hand parallel to left costal margin
  • Ask patient to deep breath in
  • Feel for spleen edge
  • If not felt: Ask patient to roll onto right side (brings spleen forward) - repeat

What to Say: "Now I'm checking for your spleen. Deep breath in." Normal: Spleen not palpable Abnormal - Splenomegaly:
  • Palpable below left costal margin
  • Causes in acute abdomen: Splenic rupture (trauma), splenic infarct, infectious mononucleosis (rare)

KIDNEYS (Lumbar regions): Technique - Bimanual Ballottement:
  • Place one hand in renal angle (posteriorly in flank)
  • Place other hand anteriorly in lumbar region
  • Ballot kidney between two hands (push from back, catch anteriorly)
  • Do for both kidneys

What to Say: "I'm checking your kidneys now." Normal: Kidneys usually not palpable (except thin patients, right kidney sometimes) Abnormal:
  • Palpable enlarged kidney - Hydronephrosis (obstructed), polycystic kidney disease, tumor
  • Tender - Pyelonephritis, renal stone

BLADDER (Suprapubic): Palpate suprapubic region Normal: Not palpable (unless full) Abnormal - Palpable bladder:
  • Urinary retention (palpable, dull to percussion)

ABDOMINAL AORTA (Midline, epigastric): Technique:
  • Palpate deeply in epigastric region, just left of midline
  • Feel for pulsation
  • Assess width (place fingers on either side of aorta, measure width)

What to Say: "I'm checking the main blood vessel in your abdomen." Normal: Pulsation felt, width <3 cm Abnormal - AAA:
  • Expansile pulsation (pushes fingers apart, not just up and down)
  • Width >3 cm (AAA definition)
  • CRITICAL: If palpable AAA + abdominal pain ‚Üí Think ruptured AAA

IMPORTANT: In thin patients, normal aortic pulsation is easily felt - don't panic. AAA is EXPANSILE (sideways) and WIDE.

4F: PALPATION OF REBOUND TENDERNESS

This tests for peritonitis Technique:
  • Press down gently (2-3 cm) over area of tenderness
  • Hold for 2-3 seconds
  • Quickly release
  • Positive = Pain is WORSE on release than on pressing down (rebound tenderness)

What to Say: "I'm going to press down here and then let go quickly. Tell me which hurts more - when I press down, or when I let go." Interpretation:
  • Positive rebound = Peritoneal inflammation (peritonitis)
  • Localized rebound (RLQ only) = Localized peritonitis (appendicitis, diverticulitis)
  • Generalized rebound (all quadrants) = Generalized peritonitis (perforated viscus)

GENTLER ALTERNATIVE (if patient clearly in pain):
  • Percussion tenderness (see below) - less painful way to assess peritoneal inflammation
  • Cough test: "Does it hurt when you cough?" (peritoneal inflammation)
  • Shaking bed: Gently shake bed - if patient winces, indicates peritonitis

NOTE: Some examiners dislike rebound testing (too painful). Know it, but use gentler methods in OSCE unless specifically instructed.

P4: PERFORM - PERCUSSION (1-2 minutes)

Purpose:
  • Assess for fluid (ascites)
  • Assess for air (obstruction, perforation)
  • Assess organomegaly
  • Gentler way to assess peritoneal inflammation

Technique:
  • Place middle finger of left hand flat on abdomen (pleximeter)
  • Tap with middle finger of right hand (plexor)
  • Listen to sound

- Resonant (normal - air-filled bowel) - Dull (solid organ, fluid, mass) - Tympanic (gas-filled - obstruction)

What to Say: "I'm going to tap on your abdomen to check for fluid."

4G: PERCUSSION - 9 REGIONS

Percuss same 9 regions:
  • Liver: Dull in right hypochondrium (normal)
  • Spleen: Dull in left hypochondrium if enlarged
  • Hollow viscera: Resonant (stomach, bowel)
  • Bladder: Dull suprapubically if full

SPECIFIC FINDING:
  • Loss of liver dullness (resonant where should be dull)

- Indicates: Pneumoperitoneum (perforated viscus - free air displaces liver)

4H: PERCUSSION FOR ASCITES

Shifting Dullness (Most reliable clinical test for ascites): Technique:
  1. Percuss from midline (umbilicus) towards flank
  2. Sounds changes from resonant (midline) ‚Üí dull (flank)
  3. Mark transition point
  4. Ask patient to roll onto side (towards you)
  5. Wait 30 seconds (for fluid to shift)
  6. Percuss again at same point
  7. Positive test: Area that was dull is now resonant (fluid has shifted away)

Interpretation:
  • Shifting dullness present = Ascites (at least 1-2 litres)
  • Causes in acute abdomen: Perforation (free fluid), pancreatitis (fluid), liver disease, malignancy

P4: PERFORM - AUSCULTATION (30 seconds)

Purpose: Assess bowel sounds Technique:
  • Place stethoscope in RLQ (ileocaecal area - most active)
  • Listen for 5-10 seconds minimum (don't rush)

What to Say: "I'm going to listen to your bowel sounds." Normal:
  • Bowel sounds present (gurgling every 5-10 seconds)

Abnormal: Absent / Very Reduced:
  • Ileus (bowel not moving)
  • Peritonitis (bowel shuts down)
  • Advanced obstruction
  • Need to listen for 2 minutes to declare truly absent (but in OSCE, 30 seconds is acceptable)

High-Pitched / Tinkling:
  • Bowel obstruction (bowel trying to push against obstruction)

Increased / Hyperactive:
  • Gastroenteritis (increased motility)
  • Early obstruction (increased peristalsis trying to overcome)

Vascular Bruits:
  • Listen over aorta (midline, epigastric)
  • Listen over renal arteries (left and right of umbilicus)
  • Bruit = Stenosis or aneurysm
  • In acute abdomen: AAA may have bruit

SPECIAL TESTS FOR SPECIFIC DIFFERENTIALS

For APPENDICITIS

1. McBurney's Point Tenderness:
  • Point: 2/3 from umbilicus to ASIS (anterior superior iliac spine)
  • Maximum tenderness at this point = Appendicitis

2. Rovsing's Sign:
  • Palpate deeply in LLQ
  • Positive: Pain in RLQ (referred pain due to peritoneal inflammation)

3. Psoas Sign:
  • Patient lies on left side
  • Extend right hip (pull right leg back)
  • Positive: Pain in RLQ
  • Indicates: Retrocaecal appendix (inflamed appendix lying on psoas muscle)

4. Obturator Sign:
  • Patient supine
  • Flex right hip and knee to 90 degrees
  • Internally rotate hip
  • Positive: Hypogastric/RLQ pain
  • Indicates: Pelvic appendix (inflamed appendix near obturator internus)

For CHOLECYSTITIS

Murphy's Sign:
  • Place hand below right costal margin
  • Ask patient to take deep breath in
  • Liver descends, pushing inflamed gallbladder onto your hand
  • Positive: Patient stops breathing in due to pain (arrest of inspiration)
  • Indicates: Cholecystitis

For PERITONITIS

1. Percussion Tenderness:
  • Gently percuss over area
  • Positive: Pain on percussion (peritoneal inflammation)
  • Gentler than rebound test

2. Guarding:
  • Involuntary muscle contraction on palpation
  • Localized (appendicitis) vs generalized (perforated viscus)

3. Rigidity:
  • Board-like hardness of abdomen
  • Involuntary (can't be relaxed even if patient tries)
  • Indicates: Severe peritonitis (perforated viscus, severe pancreatitis)

4. Absent Bowel Sounds:
  • Ileus from peritonitis

For BOWEL OBSTRUCTION

Visible Peristalsis:
  • Watch abdomen
  • May see waves of peristalsis moving across abdomen (bowel trying to overcome obstruction)
  • More common in thin patients

Tinkling Bowel Sounds:
  • High-pitched, frequent

Distension:
  • Generalized abdominal distension

Hernial Orifices:
  • MUST EXAMINE for incarcerated hernia (cause of obstruction)
  • Palpate inguinal and femoral regions
  • Ask patient to cough (cough impulse)

For AAA

Palpable Pulsatile Mass:
  • Epigastric, midline
  • Expansile pulsation (pushes fingers apart laterally, not just up/down)
  • Width >3 cm

Bruit:
  • Auscultate over AAA

COMPLETE EXAMINATION

At end of abdomen examination, say to examiner:

"To complete my examination, I would like to:

  • Examine hernial orifices (inguinal, femoral, umbilical - if not already done)
  • Perform digital rectal examination (if indicated - e.g., suspected GI bleeding, pelvic mass)
  • Examine external genitalia (if indicated)
  • Assess peripheral pulses (if concerned about vascular pathology)
  • Check observations (HR, BP, temperature, RR, O2 sats)
  • Perform urinalysis (blood, protein, nitrites, leukocytes)"

ACUTE ABDOMEN - CRITICAL FINDINGS

Recognize These Immediately

PERITONITIS:
  • Board-like rigidity
  • Generalized guarding and rebound
  • Absent bowel sounds
  • Patient lies still
  • ‚Üí Perforated viscus ‚Üí URGENT SURGERY

SHOCK (Ruptured AAA, Ruptured Ectopic, Severe Pancreatitis):
  • Hypotension
  • Tachycardia
  • Cold peripheries
  • Delayed capillary refill
  • ‚Üí RESUSCITATE + URGENT INTERVENTION

BOWEL OBSTRUCTION:
  • Distension
  • Tinkling bowel sounds
  • Tender mass in hernial orifice (incarcerated hernia)
  • If peritonitis ‚Üí Strangulated bowel ‚Üí URGENT SURGERY

AAA:
  • Palpable expansile pulsatile mass
  • If + hypotension ‚Üí Ruptured AAA ‚Üí ACTIVATE MASSIVE TRANSFUSION + OR

INTEGRATION: HOW EXAMINATION FINDINGS NARROW YOUR DIFFERENTIAL

Case Example

History: 35F, RLQ pain for 12 hours, started centrally then migrated, anorexia, vomiting after pain started, low-grade fever Differential from History:
  1. Appendicitis
  2. Ectopic pregnancy (must exclude)
  3. Ovarian pathology
  4. IBD
  5. Renal stone

Examination Findings:
  • Lying still
  • RLQ tenderness maximal at McBurney's point
  • Localized guarding RLQ
  • Rovsing's sign POSITIVE
  • Rebound tenderness localized to RLQ
  • Normal bowel sounds
  • No masses palpable
  • Temperature 38.1¬∞C

How Examination Narrows Differential:

‚úÖ Appendicitis - Most likely (localized RLQ tenderness, McBurney's point, Rovsing's +ve, fever, appropriate history)

‚ùå Ectopic pregnancy - Less likely (would need Œ≤HCG to exclude, but exam doesn't suggest ruptured ectopic - no shock, no generalized peritonitis from haemoperitoneum)

‚ùå Ovarian pathology - Less likely (tenderness at McBurney's point is too medial for ovary, positive Rovsing's more consistent with appendicitis)

‚ùå IBD - Less likely (acute presentation, no chronic history, exam findings localized to appendix)

‚ùå Renal stone - Excluded (patient lying still [stone patients writhe], no loin tenderness, tenderness at McBurney's not loin-to-groin)

Your Final Differential After Examination:
  1. Acute appendicitis (most likely)
  2. Ectopic pregnancy (must still exclude with Œ≤HCG)

Your Management:
  • Bloods: FBC, CRP, Œ≤HCG (to exclude ectopic)
  • Imaging: CT abdomen if diagnosis unclear (but this is classic appendicitis clinically)
  • Surgical consult for appendicectomy
