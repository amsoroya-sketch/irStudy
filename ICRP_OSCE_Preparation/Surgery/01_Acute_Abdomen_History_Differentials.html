INTRODUCTION

The Differential-Driven Approach (Dr. Amir Soufi Method)

Traditional approach: Take complete history ‚Üí Think about diagnosis at end Dr. Amir's approach: Start thinking differentials FROM THE FIRST QUESTION ‚Üí Let differential guide your questions ‚Üí Narrow differential with each answer Example:
  • Patient says "right lower quadrant pain"
  • Immediately think: Appendicitis, ectopic pregnancy (if female), ovarian pathology, inflammatory bowel disease, right ureteric colic
  • Your next questions are driven by differentials: "Are you pregnant?", "Any fever?", "Any urinary symptoms?"

Why This Matters for OSCE

AMC Clinical examiners assess:
  • Can you generate a sensible differential diagnosis?
  • Do your questions logically narrow the differential?
  • Do you identify üö® RED FLAGs?
  • Can you formulate a management plan based on differential?

THE 9-STEP STRUCTURED HISTORY

Overview - 8 Minutes Total

Time Allocation:
  1. Introduction & PC: 30 sec
  2. HPC (including SOCRATES): 3.5 min
  3. Systemic Review: 1.5 min
  4. PMHx/PSHx: 1 min
  5. Medications: 30 sec
  6. Allergies: 15 sec
  7. Family Hx: 15 sec
  8. Social Hx: 30 sec
  9. Summarize & Differential: 1 min

STEP 1: INTRODUCTION & PRESENTING COMPLAINT (30 seconds)

Opening:

"Hello, I'm Dr [Name]. I'm one of the doctors here in the ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY Department. I understand you've come in with abdominal pain. Is that right?"

Permission:

"I'd like to ask you some questions about your pain and your health to work out what might be causing it. Is that okay?"

Open Question - Let Patient Tell Story:

"Can you tell me about this abdominal pain - when did it start and what's it been like?"

[Listen to patient's narrative for 30-60 seconds - don't interrupt]

STEP 2: HISTORY OF PRESENTING COMPLAINT (3.5 minutes)

This is where differential thinking happens

PAIN CHARACTERISTICS - SOCRATES WITH DIFFERENTIAL THINKING

S - SITE

Question: "Where exactly is the pain? Can you point to it with one finger?" Differential Thinking by Location:

Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ) Pain

Think immediately:
  1. Cholecystitis / biliary colic
  2. Hepatitis
  3. Right lower lobe pneumonia
  4. Peptic ulcer (duodenal)
  5. Retrocaecal appendicitis (can present high)

Epigastric Pain

Think immediately:
  1. Peptic ulcer (gastric or duodenal)
  2. Gastritis / GORD
  3. Pancreatitis
  4. Abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA)
  5. Myocardial infarction (referred)

Left Upper Quadrant (LUQ) Pain

Think immediately:
  1. Splenic pathology (rupture, infarct)
  2. Gastric ulcer
  3. Pancreatitis (can be LUQ or epigastric)
  4. Left lower lobe pneumonia
  5. Splenic flexure pathology

Right Lower Quadrant (RLQ) Pain

Think immediately:
  1. Appendicitis (most common)
  2. Ectopic pregnancy (if female of childbearing age) - MUST EXCLUDE
  3. Ovarian pathology (cyst torsion, rupture)
  4. Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's - terminal ileum)
  5. Right ureteric colic
  6. Meckel's diverticulitis (rare)

Left Lower Quadrant (LLQ) Pain

Think immediately:
  1. Diverticulitis
  2. Inflammatory bowel disease (ulcerative colitis)
  3. Ovarian pathology (if female)
  4. Left ureteric colic
  5. Sigmoid volvulus (elderly)

Central/Periumbilical Pain

Think immediately:
  1. Early appendicitis (starts central, migrates to RLQ)
  2. Small bowel obstruction
  3. Early mesenteric ischaemia
  4. Abdominal aortic aneurysm
  5. Gastroenteritis

Suprapubic Pain

Think immediately:
  1. Urinary tract infection / cystitis
  2. Urinary retention
  3. Pelvic inflammatory disease (if female)
  4. Ectopic pregnancy (if female)

Diffuse/Generalized Pain

Think immediately:
  1. Peritonitis (perforated viscus) - ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY
  2. Ruptured AAA - ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY
  3. Bowel obstruction
  4. Mesenteric ischaemia
  5. Gastroenteritis (usually less severe)

O - ONSET

Question: "How did the pain start - suddenly or gradually?" Differential Thinking: SUDDEN onset (seconds to minutes):
  • Perforated viscus (peptic ulcer, diverticular)
  • Ruptured AAA
  • Ruptured ectopic pregnancy
  • Renal colic (ureteric stone)
  • Ovarian cyst rupture/torsion
  • Mesenteric ischaemia (if vascular occlusion)

Gradual onset (hours):
  • Appendicitis
  • Cholecystitis
  • Diverticulitis
  • Pancreatitis
  • Bowel obstruction

Question: "What were you doing when it started?" Differential Clues:
  • Started after eating fatty meal ‚Üí Cholecystitis
  • Started during/after heavy lifting ‚Üí Incarcerated hernia
  • Started after trauma ‚Üí Splenic rupture, solid organ injury

C - CHARACTER

Question: "Can you describe what the pain feels like?" Differential Thinking: Colicky (comes in waves, cramping):
  • Ureteric colic (renal stone) - "worst pain of my life", comes in waves
  • Bowel obstruction - crampy, intermittent
  • Biliary colic (early, before cholecystitis)

Constant, Sharp:
  • Peritonitis - sharp, constant, worse with movement
  • Appendicitis (once established)
  • Diverticulitis

Constant, Dull/Aching:
  • Cholecystitis
  • Pancreatitis (boring, deep)
  • AAA (deep, constant)

Burning:
  • Peptic ulcer
  • GORD

R - RADIATION

Question: "Does the pain spread anywhere else?" Differential Thinking: RUQ ‚Üí Right shoulder/scapula:
  • Cholecystitis (referred via phrenic nerve)

Epigastric ‚Üí Back:
  • Pancreatitis (classic - "boring through to back")
  • Peptic ulcer (posterior wall perforation)
  • AAA

Loin ‚Üí Groin:
  • Ureteric colic (renal stone)

No radiation:
  • More likely localized pathology (appendicitis, diverticulitis)

A - ASSOCIATED SYMPTOMS

This is CRITICAL for differential diagnosis Ask systematically: Gastrointestinal:
  • "Any nausea or vomiting?"

- Vomiting BEFORE pain ‚Üí Gastroenteritis - Pain BEFORE vomiting ‚Üí Surgical abdomen (appendicitis, cholecystitis, obstruction)

  • "Any change in bowel habit - diarrhea or constipation?"

- Diarrhea ‚Üí Gastroenteritis, IBD - Constipation + no flatus ‚Üí Bowel obstruction

  • "Any blood in vomit or stool?"

- Haematemesis ‚Üí Peptic ulcer, gastritis - PR bleeding ‚Üí IBD, diverticulitis, ischaemic colitis

Urinary:
  • "Any urinary symptoms - burning, frequency, blood in urine?"

- Dysuria + frequency ‚Üí UTI - Haematuria ‚Üí Renal stone, UTI

Gynecological (if female):
  • "When was your last menstrual period?"

- Late period + abdominal pain ‚Üí ECTOPIC PREGNANCY until proven otherwise

  • "Any vaginal bleeding or discharge?"

- Bleeding + pain ‚Üí Ectopic pregnancy, miscarriage, PID

  • "Is there any chance you could be pregnant?"

- MUST ASK every female of childbearing age (12-55)

Constitutional:
  • "Any fever or chills?"

- Fever ‚Üí Infection (appendicitis, cholecystitis, diverticulitis, PID)

  • "Any weight loss?"

- Unintentional weight loss ‚Üí Malignancy, IBD

Cardiovascular:
  • "Any chest pain or shortness of breath?"

- May indicate MI (referred to epigastrium), pneumonia (referred to upper abdomen)

T - TIME COURSE / TIMING

Questions:
  • "How long have you had this pain?"
  • "Has it been constant or does it come and go?"
  • "Is it getting better, worse, or staying the same?"

Differential Thinking: Hours (hyperacute):
  • Appendicitis (typically <24 hours at presentation)
  • Cholecystitis
  • Renal colic
  • Perforated viscus

Days:
  • Diverticulitis
  • Pancreatitis

Weeks-Months (chronic, now acute):
  • IBD flare
  • Malignancy (with acute complication)

Pattern:
  • Constant and worsening ‚Üí Appendicitis, peritonitis, ischaemia
  • Comes and goes ‚Üí Biliary colic, renal colic, bowel obstruction

E - EXACERBATING/RELIEVING FACTORS

Questions:
  • "Is there anything that makes the pain worse?"
  • "Is there anything that makes it better?"

Differential Thinking: Worse with movement/coughing ‚Üí Peritonitis (patient lies still) Worse with eating ‚Üí Peptic ulcer, cholecystitis, pancreatitis Better with eating ‚Üí Duodenal ulcer (classic - pain 2-3 hours after eating, relieved by food) Better with leaning forward ‚Üí Pancreatitis Better after vomiting ‚Üí Gastritis, GORD Worse with urination ‚Üí UTI Can't find comfortable position ‚Üí Renal colic (patient writhes in pain, contrasts with peritonitis where patient lies still)

S - SEVERITY

Question: "On a scale of 0 to 10, with 0 being no pain and 10 being the worst pain imaginable, how would you rate your pain?" Also ask: "Is this the worst pain you've ever had?" Differential Thinking: 10/10 "worst pain of my life":
  • Ruptured AAA
  • Perforated viscus
  • Renal colic
  • Ruptured ectopic

7-9/10 severe:
  • Appendicitis
  • Cholecystitis
  • Pancreatitis

4-6/10 moderate:
  • Gastroenteritis
  • UTI
  • Constipation

üö® RED FLAGS - DON'T MISS DIAGNOSES

The SURGICAL EMERGENCIES (Recognize Immediately)

1. RUPTURED AAA
  • Triad: Abdominal/back pain + Pulsatile mass + Hypotension
  • ‚Üí Activate massive transfusion ‚Üí OR

2. RUPTURED ECTOPIC PREGNANCY
  • Triad: Abdominal pain + Amenorrhoea + Shock
  • ‚Üí Œ≤HCG + USS ‚Üí URGENT gynae consult

3. PERFORATED VISCUS
  • Sudden severe pain ‚Üí Board-like rigidity ‚Üí Peritonitis
  • ‚Üí Erect CXR ‚Üí URGENT surgical consult

4. MESENTERIC ISCHAEMIA
  • Pain out of proportion to examination + AF + Bloody diarrhoea
  • ‚Üí ‚ÜëLactate + CT angiogram ‚Üí URGENT vascular surgery

5. ACUTE APPENDICITIS WITH PERFORATION
  • Peritonitis, sepsis
  • ‚Üí URGENT surgical consult

üö® RED FLAG Features

Vital Signs:
  • Hypotension (SBP <90)
  • Tachycardia (HR >120)
  • Fever >38.5¬∞C (sepsis)
  • Tachypnea (peritonitis, sepsis)

Pain:
  • Sudden onset severe pain
  • Pain migrating to generalized
  • "Worst pain of my life"

Examination (you won't do this in history station, but know it):
  • Rigidity, rebound tenderness (peritonitis)
  • Pulsatile mass (AAA)
  • Absent bowel sounds (ileus, peritonitis)

History:
  • Age >65 + vascular disease (AAA risk)
  • Female + amenorrhoea + pain (ectopic)
  • Recent surgery (bowel obstruction from adhesions)
  • AF + abdominal pain (mesenteric ischaemia)

SYSTEMATIC REVIEW FOR ACUTE ABDOMEN

After SOCRATES and associated symptoms, quickly screen systems: Cardiovascular: "Any chest pain, palpitations, or leg swelling?"
  • MI, DVT/PE

Respiratory: "Any cough, shortness of breath?"
  • Pneumonia (referred pain to upper abdomen)

Remaining GI: "Any difficulty swallowing, heartburn, change in appetite?" Genitourinary: Already covered (urinary symptoms, LMP) Constitutional: "Any night sweats, unintentional weight loss, fatigue?"
  • Malignancy

CLINICAL REASONING - NARROWING THE DIFFERENTIAL

The Mental Process (Dr. Amir's Approach)

STEP 1: Location gives you starting differential (5-7 possibilities) STEP 2: Onset narrows it
  • Sudden ‚Üí Perforation, rupture, torsion, stone
  • Gradual ‚Üí Inflammatory (appendicitis, cholecystitis, diverticulitis)

STEP 3: Character helps
  • Colicky ‚Üí Stone, obstruction
  • Constant sharp ‚Üí Peritonitis, appendicitis
  • Burning ‚Üí Ulcer

STEP 4: Associated symptoms NARROW SIGNIFICANTLY
  • Fever ‚Üí Infection (appendicitis, cholecystitis, diverticulitis, PID)
  • Vomiting before pain ‚Üí Gastroenteritis (medical)
  • Pain before vomiting ‚Üí Surgical
  • Haematuria ‚Üí Stone
  • Missed period ‚Üí Ectopic
  • Bloody stool ‚Üí IBD, diverticulitis, ischaemia

STEP 5: üö® RED FLAGs identify EMERGENCIES

Example Clinical Reasoning

Case: 25F, RLQ pain, missed period by 1 week, vaginal spotting, shoulder tip pain Step 1 (Location - RLQ): Appendicitis, ectopic, ovarian, renal stone, IBD Step 2 (Onset - sudden): Favors ectopic rupture, ovarian torsion, stone over appendicitis Step 3 (Character - sharp): Consistent with rupture or torsion Step 4 (Associated - missed period, vaginal bleeding): ECTOPIC PREGNANCY moves to top Step 5 (üö® RED FLAG - shoulder tip pain): Haemoperitoneum ‚Üí RUPTURED ECTOPIC ‚Üí ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY Your differential in presentation:
  1. Ruptured ectopic pregnancy (most likely, ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY)
  2. Ovarian cyst rupture/torsion
  3. Appendicitis (less likely given sudden onset and gynae symptoms)

Your next steps: Œ≤HCG, URGENT USS, gynae consult
