S01: Right Iliac Fossa Pain - Appendicitis Assessment

üéØ Key Learning Objectives:

  • Systematic pain assessment using SOCRATES framework
  • Recognize CLASSICAL APPENDICITIS features (migration, anorexia, peritonism)
  • MANDATORY gynaecological history in ALL females with abdominal pain
  • Differentiate surgical vs gynaecological causes

üìã SOCRATES Framework for Pain:

  • S - Site: Where is the pain? Did it start elsewhere and move?
  • O - Onset: When did it start? Sudden or gradual?
  • C - Character: Sharp, dull, cramping, constant, colicky?
  • R - Radiation: Does it spread anywhere?
  • A - Associated symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, anorexia, fever
  • T - Timing: Constant vs comes-and-goes? Severity (0-10)?
  • E - Exacerbating: What makes it worse? (movement = peritonism!)
  • S - Severity: 0-10 scale, progression (better/worse?)

üö® CLASSICAL APPENDICITIS TRIAD:

  1. PAIN MIGRATION: Periumbilical ‚Üí RIF (within 12-24 hours) = PATHOGNOMONIC
  2. ANOREXIA: Loss of appetite (95% sensitivity - KEY diagnostic feature)
  3. PERITONISM: Pain worse with movement/coughing/car bumps

If all 3 present ‚Üí Appendicitis HIGHLY likely

‚úÖ MUST ASK Questions:

  1. "Where did the pain START?" (looking for periumbilical origin)
  2. "Has the pain MOVED anywhere?" (migration to RIF = classic)
  3. "Have you felt like eating? Are you hungry?" (anorexia screening)
  4. "What makes the pain worse?" (movement/coughing = peritonism)
  5. "Any nausea or vomiting? Which came FIRST - pain or vomiting?" (pain before vomiting = appendicitis)
  6. "Any fever or feeling hot/sweaty?"
  7. "Any diarrhoea?" (absence of diarrhoea favors appendicitis over gastroenteritis)

üö® MANDATORY IN ALL FEMALES WITH ABDOMINAL PAIN:

  1. "When was your last period?" (LMP) - NON-NEGOTIABLE
  2. "Is there any chance you could be pregnant?" - MUST ASK
  3. "Are your periods regular?"
  4. "Any vaginal bleeding or discharge?"
  5. "Any pain with sex?" (dyspareunia)
  6. "What contraception do you use?"

WHY: Ectopic pregnancy, ovarian cyst, PID can all mimic appendicitis. Missing pregnancy = CRITICAL FAILURE.

  • ‚ùå Not asking LMP in female patient (AUTOMATIC FAIL)
  • ‚ùå Not asking about pain migration (periumbilical ‚Üí RIF)
  • ‚ùå Not screening for anorexia ("How's your appetite?")
  • ‚ùå Not assessing peritonism ("Does movement or coughing make it worse?")
  • ‚ùå Not checking sequence (pain BEFORE vomiting in appendicitis)

S02: Groin Lump - Hernia Examination

üéØ Key Learning Objectives:

  • Systematic groin examination (MUST examine standing AND lying)
  • Differentiate inguinal vs femoral hernia ("above and medial" rule)
  • Differentiate direct vs indirect inguinal hernia (occlusion test)
  • Assess for complications (irreducibility, strangulation)

üö® CRITICAL: MUST EXAMINE PATIENT STANDING!

Reducible hernias disappear when lying flat. If you only examine lying down, YOU WILL MISS THE HERNIA.

Examination sequence: STANDING (lump visible) ‚Üí LYING (lump disappears) ‚Üí STANDING (test cough impulse)

üìã Systematic Groin Examination Steps:

  1. Introduction, consent, chaperone offer, hand washing
  2. Adequate exposure: Patient in underwear, groin visible
  3. Inspection - STANDING: Look for visible lump
  4. Inspection - LYING: Does lump disappear? (reducible)
  5. Palpation (standing): Temperature, tenderness, consistency, size
  6. Cough impulse test: "Can you give me a big cough?" (GOLD STANDARD for hernia)
  7. Reducibility: Apply gentle pressure - does it reduce?
  8. Identify pubic tubercle: KEY LANDMARK
  9. "Above and medial" test: Inguinal vs femoral differentiation
  10. Deep ring occlusion test: Direct vs indirect differentiation
  11. Examine contralateral side
  12. Thank patient, allow re-dressing

üö® "ABOVE AND MEDIAL" RULE - MEMORIZE THIS:

Hernia Type Relation to Pubic Tubercle Remember
INGUINAL Hernia ABOVE and MEDIAL to pubic tubercle "Inguinal = Above"
FEMORAL Hernia BELOW and LATERAL to pubic tubercle "Femoral = Below (femur is below!)"

MUST identify pubic tubercle first, then assess lump position relative to it.

üìã Deep Ring Occlusion Test (Direct vs Indirect):

  1. Patient lies down, hernia reduces
  2. Locate deep inguinal ring (1-2cm above mid-inguinal point)
  3. Place fingers firmly over deep ring
  4. Ask patient to stand or cough while maintaining pressure
  5. IF hernia does NOT appear: INDIRECT (emerges through deep ring which you're occluding)
  6. IF hernia STILL appears: DIRECT (emerges medially through Hesselbach's triangle)

‚úÖ MUST DO During Examination:

  1. Offer chaperone (groin examination = intimate, mandatory to offer)
  2. Examine STANDING first (lump visible)
  3. Test cough impulse (hand over lump, ask patient to cough)
  4. Test reducibility (apply gentle pressure)
  5. Identify pubic tubercle (palpate along superior pubic ramus from medial to lateral)
  6. Apply "above and medial" rule (inguinal vs femoral)
  7. Examine both sides (10-15% have bilateral hernias)
  8. Check for strangulation signs (tenderness, erythema, warmth, irreducibility)

‚ö†Ô∏è Common IMG Mistakes:

  • ‚ùå Not examining patient standing (CRITICAL FAILURE - you'll miss the hernia!)
  • ‚ùå Not offering chaperone (professional conduct failure)
  • ‚ùå Not testing cough impulse (gold standard for confirming hernia)
  • ‚ùå Cannot identify pubic tubercle (essential landmark)
  • ‚ùå Not using "above and medial" rule (can't differentiate inguinal vs femoral)
  • ‚ùå Not examining contralateral side (miss bilateral hernias)
  • ‚ùå Not checking for strangulation signs (patient safety issue)
