What to tell Jennifer today:

‚ö†Ô∏è IMG Common Mistakes - Critical Errors to Avoid

Heavy menstrual bleeding is the most common reason for gynaecological referral in Australia. Missing endometrial cancer in a woman >45 is a CRITICAL FAILURE. These 10 mistakes will lead to automatic failure in AMC OSCE.

‚ùå MISTAKE #1: Not Recognizing Age >45 + Abnormal Bleeding = MANDATORY Endometrial Sampling
Why this fails: RANZCOG 2024 guideline states ALL women aged ‚â•45 years with abnormal uterine bleeding MUST have endometrial sampling to exclude endometrial cancer/hyperplasia. This is NON-NEGOTIABLE in Australian practice. Endometrial cancer peaks age 60-70, but 15-20% occur in women <50. Not recognizing this = patient safety failure.

What happens: IMG candidates diagnose "likely fibroids" based on symptoms and suggest pelvic ultrasound only. They forget that ultrasound does NOT exclude endometrial cancer - you MUST sample the endometrium in women >45.
‚úÖ CORRECT: In ANY woman aged ‚â•45 with abnormal uterine bleeding, your investigation plan MUST include: (1) FBC, (2) ENDOMETRIAL SAMPLING (pipelle biopsy or hysteroscopy), (3) Pelvic ultrasound. You must state "Endometrial sampling is mandatory to exclude endometrial cancer given her age."
‚ùå MISTAKE #2: Not Quantifying Bleeding Severity
Why this fails: "Heavy periods" is subjective - one woman's "heavy" is another woman's "normal." You MUST quantify bleeding using objective measures: pad/tampon count per day, clot size, flooding episodes, duration of bleeding. Without quantification, you cannot assess severity or monitor treatment response.

What happens: IMG candidates ask "How heavy is the bleeding?" and patient says "Very heavy." Candidate accepts this without follow-up. Australian OSCE expects systematic quantification.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask: (1) "How many pads or tampons do you use per day?" (>8 = severe), (2) "Do you pass clots? How big?" (>2cm = abnormal), (3) "Do you ever flood through onto your clothes?" (flooding = severe), (4) "How long does bleeding last?" (>7 days = prolonged), (5) "How often do periods come?" (<21 days = frequent).
‚ùå MISTAKE #3: Not Asking About Intermenstrual and Postcoital Bleeding
Why this fails: Intermenstrual bleeding (IMB - bleeding between periods) and postcoital bleeding (PCB - bleeding after sex) are RED FLAGS for malignancy (endometrial cancer, cervical cancer, endometrial polyps). Not asking = missing critical diagnostic clues.

What happens: IMG candidates focus on heavy menstrual bleeding and forget to ask about bleeding at OTHER times. IMB/PCB significantly changes the differential and urgency of investigation.
‚úÖ CORRECT: ALWAYS ask: (1) "Do you have any bleeding between your periods?" (IMB), (2) "Do you have any bleeding after sex?" (PCB). If YES to either ‚Üí higher suspicion for malignancy/polyps ‚Üí more urgent investigation.
‚ùå MISTAKE #4: Not Assessing Quality of Life Impact
Why this fails: Heavy menstrual bleeding profoundly affects women's lives - work absence, social restriction, anxiety, depression, relationship strain. QOL impact is a MAJOR factor in deciding treatment (e.g., surgical vs medical management). Not assessing QOL = incomplete holistic assessment.

What happens: IMG candidates gather clinical facts but don't ask about the HUMAN impact: "How is this affecting your daily life? Work? Social activities? Relationships?" Australian medicine emphasizes patient-centered care.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask: (1) "How is this affecting your work?" (time off, concentration), (2) "Impact on social life?" (cancelled activities, restrictions), (3) "How do you feel emotionally?" (anxiety, low mood), (4) "Impact on relationships/sex life?" Women with severe QOL impact often prefer surgical management (hysterectomy/ablation) over medical.
‚ùå MISTAKE #5: Not Screening for Anaemia Symptoms
Why this fails: Chronic heavy bleeding ‚Üí iron-deficiency anaemia in 50-60% of women with menorrhagia. Severe anaemia can cause heart failure (high-output cardiac failure). Not recognizing anaemia symptoms means you don't treat it urgently.

What happens: IMG candidates focus on the bleeding but don't ask: "Do you feel tired? Short of breath? Dizzy? Do you look pale?" Missing severe anaemia (Hb <70 g/L) could mean patient needs blood transfusion.
‚úÖ CORRECT: ALWAYS screen for anaemia symptoms in heavy menstrual bleeding: (1) "How's your energy level? Feeling tired?" (fatigue), (2) "Do you get short of breath easily?" (exertional dyspnoea), (3) "Any dizziness?" (postural hypotension), (4) "Has anyone said you look pale?" (pallor), (5) "Any palpitations or chest pain?" (cardiac compensation). Then check FBC urgently and start iron supplementation.
‚ùå MISTAKE #6: Not Asking About Future Fertility Wishes
Why this fails: Treatment choices differ dramatically based on fertility wishes. Endometrial ablation and hysterectomy are CONTRAINDICATED if patient wants future pregnancy. Not asking = inappropriate treatment recommendation.

What happens: IMG candidates assume woman aged 48 doesn't want more children and recommend ablation/hysterectomy without asking. Some women want to preserve fertility even at 48 (e.g., new relationship, previous pregnancy loss).
‚úÖ CORRECT: ALWAYS ask: (1) "Do you have children?" (obstetric history), (2) "Are you planning any future pregnancies?" or "Is your family complete?" If patient wants to preserve fertility ‚Üí options limited to medical management (Mirena, tranexamic acid) or myomectomy. If family complete ‚Üí endometrial ablation and hysterectomy are options.
‚ùå MISTAKE #7: Not Asking About Last Cervical Screening Test
Why this fails: Cervical cancer can present with heavy bleeding (though more commonly IMB/PCB). If cervical screening is overdue, patient needs screening + speculum examination to exclude cervical pathology. Not asking = incomplete gynae assessment.

What happens: IMG candidates assume heavy menstrual bleeding = uterine problem and don't consider cervical causes. Australian OSCE expects comprehensive gynae history including screening status.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask "When was your last cervical screening test (CST)?" Australia uses 5-yearly HPV-based screening. If overdue (>5 years) ‚Üí arrange CST. If up-to-date and normal ‚Üí cervical cancer unlikely. Document screening status in all gynae consultations.
‚ùå MISTAKE #8: Not Asking About Pressure Symptoms (Missing Fibroids)
Why this fails: Fibroids cause heavy bleeding AND pressure/bulk symptoms: pelvic fullness, urinary frequency, constipation, abdominal distension. Not asking about pressure symptoms means you miss the clinical clue that suggests fibroids rather than other causes (adenomyosis, polyps, dysfunctional bleeding).

What happens: IMG candidates ask about bleeding but don't ask "Do you feel any pressure or fullness in your pelvis?" Large fibroids can be palpable abdominally - if candidate doesn't ask about pressure/bloating, they miss the diagnosis.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask about PRESSURE SYMPTOMS: (1) "Do you feel any pressure or heaviness in your lower tummy?" (pelvic mass), (2) "Have you noticed your abdomen getting bigger or clothes feeling tighter?" (enlarged uterus), (3) "How often do you pass urine?" (urinary frequency from bladder compression), (4) "Any constipation?" (rectal pressure). These symptoms point to STRUCTURAL cause (fibroids/adenomyosis) rather than functional bleeding.
‚ùå MISTAKE #9: Using "Wait and See" Approach in Woman >45
Why this fails: In a woman <45 with heavy bleeding and no red flags, expectant management ("let's see if it settles") may be acceptable SHORT-TERM. But in woman ‚â•45, you CANNOT use "wait and see" - you MUST investigate to exclude malignancy. Suggesting "wait and see" in a 48-year-old = unsafe practice.

What happens: IMG candidates think "She's perimenopausal, periods will stop soon, let's wait" - THIS IS WRONG. Perimenopause is a HIGH-RISK time for endometrial cancer (unopposed oestrogen from anovulatory cycles).
‚úÖ CORRECT: In woman ‚â•45 with abnormal bleeding, your plan MUST include: (1) FBC now, (2) Endometrial sampling within 2-4 weeks, (3) Pelvic USS within 2-4 weeks. Say "We need to investigate this to make sure there's nothing serious like cancer." Do NOT suggest "wait and see" - this is unsafe in this age group.
‚ùå MISTAKE #10: Not Showing Empathy for Significant QOL Impact
Why this fails: This patient is flooding through clothes at work, missing work, too exhausted to exercise, anxious about bleeding, relationship strain. If candidate treats this as "routine heavy periods" without acknowledging the profound impact, they fail the communication/empathy domain.

What happens: IMG candidates focus intensely on clinical facts and forget the patient is suffering. Patient becomes tearful when describing work embarrassment and candidate doesn't respond empathetically.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Show empathy throughout: (1) "That sounds really distressing - flooding at work must be so embarrassing and stressful," (2) "I can see this is really affecting your quality of life - we're going to find the cause and get this sorted for you," (3) When patient becomes tearful: pause, offer tissue, acknowledge distress. Use phrases like "I can see this has been really difficult for you" and "You're right to seek help - this isn't something you should just put up with."
