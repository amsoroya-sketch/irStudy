üö® FALLS ASSESSMENT - Critical Risk Factors

üìã Systematic Falls Assessment Framework - "The Big 8"

You MUST cover all 8 categories systematically:

  1. Previous Falls History - "Have you fallen before?" (‚â•2 falls/year = HIGH RISK)
  2. Medications - Polypharmacy (‚â•5 meds) + fall-risk drugs (benzodiazepines, antihypertensives)
  3. Vision - Cataracts, poor vision, glasses not updated
  4. Gait & Balance - Unsteady, uses walking aid, reduced mobility
  5. Cardiovascular - Postural hypotension, syncope, arrhythmia
  6. Neurological - Stroke, Parkinson's, peripheral neuropathy, cognitive impairment
  7. Home Environment - Loose rugs, poor lighting, no rails, hazards
  8. Fear of Falling - Reduced confidence ‚Üí reduced activity ‚Üí deconditioning ‚Üí increased fall risk

üö® RECURRENT FALLER = HIGH RISK

Why critical: Single fall may be accident; recurrent falls indicate underlying problem requiring comprehensive multifactorial assessment

Must ask: "Have you fallen before?" and "How many times in the past year?"

‚úÖ MUST-ASK QUESTIONS - Falls Assessment

Circumstances of THIS Fall:

Question Why Critical / What You're Looking For
"What happened - can you describe it?" Mechanical fall (trip/slip) vs medical cause (syncope/seizure/stroke)
"Did you lose consciousness or black out?" YES = syncope (cardiac/neurological) - requires urgent workup (ECG, Holter)
"Do you remember the fall?" NO = seizure or amnesia for event - neurological assessment needed
"Any warning - dizziness, chest pain, palpitations?" Prodrome suggests cardiac cause (arrhythmia, MI, PE)
"Were you able to get up yourself?" Long lie (>1 hour on floor) ‚Üí rhabdomyolysis, pressure sores, hypothermia risk

Intrinsic Risk Factors - Detailed Questions:

üö® MEDICATIONS (CRITICAL - Most Modifiable):

  • "What medications do you take?" - Count them (‚â•5 = polypharmacy)
  • "Have any been changed recently?" - Temporal association with falls
  • "Do you take sleeping tablets?" - Benzodiazepines = HIGH RISK
  • "Do you feel dizzy when you stand up?" - Postural hypotension from antihypertensives

üö® VISION (Major Modifiable Risk Factor):

  • "How is your eyesight?"
  • "Do you have cataracts?" - Cataract surgery reduces fall risk by 30-40%
  • "When did you last see an optometrist?" - Should be yearly in elderly
  • "Do you wear glasses? Can you see clearly when walking?"

GAIT & BALANCE:

  • "Do you have problems with walking or balance?"
  • "Do you use a walking stick or frame?"
  • "Do you feel unsteady?"
  • "Any weakness in your legs?" - Arthritis, muscle weakness

CARDIOVASCULAR:

  • "Do you feel dizzy when you stand up?" - Postural hypotension
  • "Have you ever fainted?" - Syncope history
  • "Any heart problems?" - Arrhythmia, heart failure

URINARY:

  • "Do you get up at night to pass urine?" - Nocturia (rushing to toilet in dark = fall risk)
  • "How many times per night?" - ‚â•2 times = significant

Extrinsic Risk Factors - Home Environment:

Question What You're Assessing
"Do you have any loose rugs or mats?" Trip hazard - REMOVE or secure with non-slip backing
"How is the lighting in your home?" Poor lighting (especially hallway at night) - INSTALL brighter bulbs + night lights
"Do you have rails in the bathroom?" Bathroom is HIGH RISK area (wet, slippery) - INSTALL grab rails
"Do you have a night light?" For nocturia - illuminate path to bathroom
"What shoes do you wear at home?" Loose slippers = HIGH RISK - recommend supportive, non-slip footwear
"Do you live alone?" Isolated = higher risk if falls and can't get help
"Do you have a personal alarm?" Medical alert pendant - can call for help if falls alone

üö® FEAR OF FALLING Assessment (CRITICAL):

  • "Are you worried about falling again?" - Fear of falling is common and impacts behavior
  • "Has this affected what you do?" - Reduced activity due to fear
  • "Do you feel confident walking around?" - Loss of confidence

‚ö†Ô∏è Vicious Cycle: Fear of falling ‚Üí Reduced activity ‚Üí Deconditioning/muscle weakness ‚Üí INCREASED fall risk

üíä MEDICATION RECONCILIATION - Key Principles

üîÑ Medication Reconciliation Steps:

  1. Step 1: REVIEW Both Lists
    • HOME medications (before hospital)
    • DISCHARGE medications (from hospital)
  2. Step 2: IDENTIFY Discrepancies
    • Which medications CONTINUE?
    • Which medications are NEW?
    • Which medications should STOP?
    • Any dose changes?
  3. Step 3: EDUCATE Patient
    • Explain PURPOSE of each medication (in simple language)
    • Explain WHEN to take each (morning/night, with food)
    • Explain DURATION (long-term vs short course)
    • Warn about common side effects
  4. Step 4: CHECK Understanding (TEACH-BACK METHOD)
    • "Can you tell me which tablets you'll take in the morning?"
    • "Which ones will you stop after 5 days?"
  5. Step 5: PROVIDE Written Instructions
    • Typed medication list with doses, times, purposes
    • Calendar marked with finish dates for short courses
  6. Step 6: ARRANGE Compliance Aids
    • Webster pack (weekly blister pack from pharmacy)
    • Dosette box (7-day pill organizer)

üö® CRITICAL Safety Message

You MUST explicitly state which medications to STOP vs CONTINUE.

Bad: "Here are your new medications" (patient assumes they replace old ones)

Good: "Your 4 regular medications - CONTINUE those. The hospital has ADDED 2 new medications for the infection - these are temporary for 5 days, then STOP."

üí¨ TEACH-BACK METHOD

What is Teach-Back?

After explaining medications, ASK THE PATIENT to explain back to YOU:

  • "Can you tell me which tablets you'll take in the morning?"
  • "Can you show me which tablets you'll stop after 3 days?"
  • "What will you do if you get a rash from the antibiotic?"

Why Use Teach-Back?

  • Explaining ‚â† Understanding
  • 50% of information is forgotten within minutes
  • Teach-back confirms patient has understood AND remembered
  • Australian Safety & Quality Standards recommend teach-back for medication education

How to Phrase Teach-Back (Don't Blame Patient):

‚úÖ GOOD: "I want to make sure I explained clearly - can you tell me which tablets you'll take in the morning?"

‚ùå BAD: "Do you understand?" (Patient says "yes" even if they don't)

‚ùå BAD: "Can you tell me if you understood?" (Implies patient is at fault if they didn't)

‚ùå TOP 10 IMG MISTAKES

Falls Assessment Mistakes:

1. FALLS: Not Asking About Previous Falls
Why fails: Recurrent falls (‚â•2/year) = HIGH RISK requiring comprehensive assessment. Single fall may be accident; recurrent falls indicate underlying problem.
‚úÖ CORRECT: ALWAYS ask "Have you fallen before?" and "How many times in the past year?"
2. FALLS: Not Asking About Medications
Why fails: Polypharmacy (‚â•5 meds) and fall-risk drugs (benzodiazepines, antihypertensives) are MAJOR modifiable risk factors.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask "What medications do you take?" and "Have any been changed recently?" Review for fall-risk drugs.
3. FALLS: Not Asking About Loss of Consciousness
Why fails: Must differentiate mechanical fall (trip) from syncope (cardiac/neurological). Syncope requires urgent cardiac workup.
‚úÖ CORRECT: ALWAYS ask "Did you lose consciousness or black out?" If YES ‚Üí ECG, Holter monitor, consider inpatient telemetry.
4. FALLS: Not Asking About Vision
Why fails: Vision impairment (cataracts) is major modifiable risk factor. Cataract surgery reduces fall risk by 30-40%.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask "How is your eyesight?" "Do you have cataracts?" "When did you last see an optometrist?"
5. FALLS: Not Assessing Home Environment
Why fails: Extrinsic factors (loose rugs, poor lighting) are HIGHLY MODIFIABLE - removing hazards prevents falls.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask about loose rugs, lighting, bathroom rails, footwear, stairs. Recommend OT home visit.

Medication Reconciliation Mistakes:

6. MEDS: Not Clarifying STOP vs CONTINUE
Why fails: Patients assume new medications REPLACE old ones. If you don't explicitly say "Continue your regular meds," patient may stop them.
‚úÖ CORRECT: "Your 4 regular tablets - keep taking those. The hospital has ADDED 2 new medications - these are temporary."
7. MEDS: Using Medical Jargon
Why fails: "Take your beta-blocker and PPI" means nothing to patients. Use simple language.
‚úÖ CORRECT: "Take your blood pressure tablet (metoprolol) and your stomach tablet (omeprazole)."
8. MEDS: Not Using Teach-Back Method
Why fails: Explaining is not enough - you must CHECK the patient understands.
‚úÖ CORRECT: After explaining, ask "Can you tell me which tablets you'll take in the morning?"
9. MEDS: Not Providing Written Instructions
Why fails: Verbal instructions alone are forgotten. 50% of information forgotten within minutes.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Always provide WRITTEN medication list with names, doses, times, purposes.
10. MEDS: Not Arranging Compliance Aid
Why fails: Elderly patients with polypharmacy struggle to manage multiple medications. Webster packs dramatically improve adherence.
‚úÖ CORRECT: "Let's organize a Webster pack from pharmacy - all your tablets pre-packed for each day."

Falls Prevention - Multifactorial Intervention:

Domain Interventions
1. Medications üö® ‚Ä¢ STOP benzodiazepines (temazepam) - use melatonin 2mg instead for insomnia
‚Ä¢ REDUCE antihypertensives if postural hypotension (back to previous dose)
‚Ä¢ Aim for <5 medications if possible

‚Ä¢ Update glasses prescription
‚Ä¢ Annual optometry review
3. Home Safety ‚Ä¢ OT home visit
‚Ä¢ REMOVE loose rugs OR secure with non-slip backing
‚Ä¢ INSTALL brighter bulbs (60-100W) + night lights
‚Ä¢ INSTALL grab rails (bathroom - shower, toilet)

‚Ä¢ Consider personal alarm (pendant)

üéØ Quick Exam Tips

Falls Assessment Station:

  • Use systematic framework: "The Big 8" (Previous falls, Meds, Vision, Gait, CV, Neuro, Home, Fear)
  • ALWAYS ask: (1) Previous falls? (2) Medications? (3) Loss of consciousness? (4) Vision? (5) Home hazards?
  • Identify fall-risk medications: Benzodiazepines, antihypertensives, opioids, anticholinergics, antipsychotics
  • Don't forget extrinsic factors: Ask about rugs, lighting, rails, footwear
  • Assess fear of falling: "Are you worried about falling again?" "Has this affected what you do?"
  • Management is MULTIFACTORIAL: Address ALL identified risk factors

Medication Reconciliation Station:

  • Review BOTH home medications AND discharge medications
  • Explicitly state which to CONTINUE vs which to STOP
  • Explain PURPOSE of each medication in simple language (no jargon)
  • Use TEACH-BACK: "Can you tell me which tablets you'll take in the morning?"
  • Provide WRITTEN medication list (typed, clear)
  • Arrange compliance aid (Webster pack) for elderly patients with polypharmacy
  • Address patient's specific concerns ("I'm worried about mixing them up")
  • Safety net: "If you get a rash, stop and call us"
