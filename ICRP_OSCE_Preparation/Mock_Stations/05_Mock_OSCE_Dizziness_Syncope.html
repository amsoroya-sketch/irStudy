  • Mention the driving concern

This station assesses the candidate's ability to:

  1. Take a focused syncope history
  2. Differentiate cardiac vs neurological vs vasovagal syncope

  5. Assess driving safety (Australian legal requirements)

MODEL ANSWER

üìù Model Summary to Examiner

"This is a 65-year-old man with a witnessed syncopal episode yesterday at Woolworths, plus recurrent presyncope over the past 2 weeks.

Key positive findings:

  • Complete loss of consciousness for 10-15 seconds
  • Prodrome of palpitations and lightheadedness (cardiac warning)
  • Syncope while standing still (not orthostatic)
  • Brief arm twitching (anoxic seizure, not epilepsy)
  • Immediate recovery, no post-ictal confusion (against seizure)
  • Known atrial fibrillation
  • Recent increase in metoprolol from 25mg to 50mg BD (3 weeks ago) - KEY!

Key negative findings:

  • No tongue biting, no incontinence (against seizure)
  • No vertigo or spinning (not vestibular)
  • No focal neurological symptoms (against stroke/TIA)
  • No chest pain (against MI)

My differential diagnoses are:

  1. Cardiac syncope due to bradycardia from beta-blocker overdose (most likely) - Recent dose increase of metoprolol, known AF, palpitations, minimal prodrome, rapid recovery
  2. Cardiac arrhythmia (AF with rapid ventricular response or complete heart block) - Known AF, palpitations suggest either RVR or bradycardia
  3. Vasovagal syncope - Less likely (no clear trigger, has palpitations), but possible
  4. Seizure (anoxic vs epileptic) - Brief twitching was present, but absence of tongue bite, incontinence, and post-ictal confusion argue against epilepsy. More likely anoxic seizure from cardiac syncope
  5. Stroke/TIA - Less likely without focal neurology, but patient on anticoagulation for AF stroke prevention

    6. Immediate investigations:

    • URGENT 12-lead ECG (to check for bradycardia, heart block, AF with RVR)
    • 24-48 hour Holter monitor (to correlate symptoms with arrhythmias)
    • Echocardiogram (to assess structural heart disease)
    • Bloods: FBC, UEC, glucose, troponin, thyroid function
    • Lying & standing BP (to exclude orthostatic hypotension)
    • Consider CT head if atypical features (but unlikely needed here)

    Immediate management:

    • Admit for telemetry monitoring
    • Review beta-blocker dose - likely needs reduction back to 25mg BD or alternative rate control (diltiazem, digoxin)
    • Ensure adequate anticoagulation (apixaban for AF)
    • Cardiology consult if recurrent syncope or significant arrhythmia
    • üö® DRIVING: Patient must NOT drive - Austroads guidelines: Syncope = driving ban until cleared by cardiologist (minimum 4 weeks if cardiac cause identified and treated)

    This patient should NOT be discharged without telemetry monitoring and beta-blocker review."

üö® Critical Errors That Lead to Failure

1. NOT ASKING ABOUT RECENT MEDICATION CHANGES

Mistake: Reviewing current medications but not asking "Have any doses been changed recently?"

Why this fails: The beta-blocker dose increase is THE key diagnostic clue. Missing this = automatic fail

Correct approach:

  • "What medications are you taking?" (lists them)
  • "Have any of these changed recently? New medications or dose changes?"
  • Patient reveals: "My GP increased metoprolol from 25mg to 50mg BD 3 weeks ago"
  • Candidate must recognize: Timing correlates with dizzy spells (2 weeks onset)

2. MISSING THE "5 Ps" OF SYNCOPE

Mistake: Asking random questions without systematic approach

Australian OSCE expectation: Use the "5 Ps" framework (taught in all Australian medical schools)

  • P1: Precipitant/Prodrome - What brought it on? Warning symptoms?
  • P2: Posture - Standing, sitting, lying? (orthostatic?)
  • P3: Phenomena - What happened during? (LOC, jerking, tongue bite, incontinence)
  • P4: Post-ictal - How did you feel after? (confusion?)
  • P5: Previous episodes - First time or recurrent?

3. CONFUSING "DIZZY" TERMINOLOGY

Mistake: Patient says "dizzy" ‚Üí Candidate accepts this without clarification

Why this fails: "Dizzy" has multiple meanings - must differentiate

Correct approach - Ask:

  • "When you say dizzy, do you mean:
  • (a) The room is spinning around you (vertigo)?
  • (b) You feel lightheaded like you're going to faint (presyncope)?
  • (c) You feel unsteady or off-balance (disequilibrium)?
  • (d) Your head feels woolly or unclear (non-specific dizziness)?"

This patient: Lightheaded/presyncope (cardiac), NOT vertigo (vestibular)

4. MISSING PALPITATIONS

Mistake: Not specifically asking about palpitations before syncope

Why this fails: Palpitations + syncope = arrhythmia until proven otherwise

Must ask:

  • "Did you feel your heart racing or pounding before you collapsed?"
  • "Was your heartbeat regular or irregular?"
  • "How fast was it?"

This patient: "Yes, heart racing and skipping beats" = AF with RVR OR bradycardia

5. NOT DISTINGUISHING ANOXIC SEIZURE FROM EPILEPSY

Mistake: Witness says "arms twitched" ‚Üí Candidate thinks "seizure = epilepsy"

Why this fails: Brief twitching from syncope (anoxic seizure) is common and does NOT mean epilepsy

Anoxic Seizure (from syncope):

  • Brief twitching only (not tonic-clonic)
  • NO tongue biting
  • NO incontinence
  • NO post-ictal confusion (immediate recovery)
  • Caused by cerebral hypoperfusion

True Epileptic Seizure:

  • Tonic-clonic movements (whole body)
  • Tongue biting common (lateral tongue)
  • Incontinence common
  • Prolonged post-ictal confusion (30 min - 2 hours)

Australian teaching: "If confusion after = seizure. If no confusion = syncope with anoxic jerks"

6. FORGETTING TO ADDRESS DRIVING SAFETY

Mistake: Completing history without mentioning driving

Why this fails: Australian OSCEs test legal/safety awareness. Syncope = mandatory driving restriction

Austroads Guidelines 2024 (Australian standard):

  • Private vehicle: No driving until cause identified and treated (minimum 4 weeks if cardiac cause)
  • Commercial vehicle: No driving for 6-12 months depending on cause
  • Doctor's duty: Advise patient NOT to drive (document in notes)
  • If patient refuses: May need to notify Road Traffic Authority (RTA/RMS in NSW)

Must tell patient: "I understand you've stopped driving, which was the right decision. You'll need to avoid driving until we've identified the cause and you've been cleared by a cardiologist. This is for your safety and the safety of others."

7. WRONG DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS

Mistake #1: "Top differential = vasovagal syncope"

Why this fails: Patient has AF, palpitations, and recent beta-blocker increase ‚Üí CARDIAC until proven otherwise

Mistake #2: "Top differential = seizure"

Why this fails: No tongue bite, no incontinence, no post-ictal confusion ‚Üí Not epilepsy

Mistake #3: "Top differential = stroke"

Why this fails: No focal neurology, rapid recovery ‚Üí Not stroke

Correct answer: "Cardiac syncope due to beta-blocker-induced bradycardia" (metoprolol dose increase)

8. NOT ASKING ABOUT WITNESS ACCOUNT

Mistake: Patient was unconscious ‚Üí Can't describe what happened ‚Üí Candidate moves on

Why this fails: Witness information is GOLD STANDARD for syncope/seizure diagnosis

Must ask:

  • "Your wife was with you - what did she see?"
  • "Did she notice any jerking or shaking?"
  • "How long does she think you were out for?"
  • "What were you like when you woke up?"

Australian OSCE tip: Simulated patient will have been briefed with witness details - ASK FOR THEM

9. POOR TIME MANAGEMENT

Mistake: Spending 5 minutes on introduction/social history ‚Üí Running out of time for critical questions

Correct time allocation (8 minutes):

  • 30 sec: Introduction
  • 30 sec: Open question
  • 4 min: "5 Ps" syncope history
  • 1.5 min: PMH, medications (especially RECENT CHANGES), red flags
  • 30 sec: Social/family history
  • 30 sec: Patient concerns
  • 1 min: Summary to examiner

10. MISSING BETA-BLOCKER SIDE EFFECTS

Mistake: Not recognizing beta-blockers cause bradycardia ‚Üí syncope

Key Australian pharmacology (eTG 2024):

  • Metoprolol (Betaloc/Lopressor): Cardioselective beta-blocker
  • Common in AF: For rate control (target HR 60-80 bpm)
  • Side effects: Bradycardia, hypotension, fatigue, dizziness, SYNCOPE
  • Overdose signs: HR <50 bpm, syncope, hypotension

This patient: Metoprolol increased from 25mg to 50mg BD (doubled dose) ‚Üí Likely bradycardia <50 bpm ‚Üí Syncope

Management: Reduce dose or switch to alternative rate control (diltiazem, digoxin)
