Lifestyle (risk factors):

  • Occupation: "IT Project Manager - I sit at a desk all day"

  • Smoking: "Never smoked"
  • Alcohol: "Social drinker - maybe 5-6 standard drinks per week, usually beer"

BMI clue (if asked about weight):

If asked about other systems:

  • No chest pain
  • No shortness of breath at rest (gets puffed easily with stairs)
  • No abdominal pain
  • No headaches
  • No cough

EMOTIONS & CONCERNS

Emotional State:

  • You're worried but not surprised
  • Concerned about starting medication
  • Worried about complications (father had amputation)

"I think I might have diabetes like my parents. My father ended up having his toes amputated from diabetes complications. I don't want that to happen to me. But I also don't want to be on medication for life."

Questions you should ask:

  • "Do you think it's diabetes?"
  • "Will I need insulin?"
  • "Can I reverse it with diet and exercise?"

Classical presentation:

  • Polyuria + Polydipsia + Weight loss
  • Blurred vision (osmotic lens swelling)
  • Fatigue
  • Recurrent infections
  • Poor wound healing

Risk factors present:

  • Strong family history (both parents + 2 siblings)
  • South Asian ethnicity (4√ó risk)

  • Hypertension + hypercholesterolaemia (metabolic syndrome)

MODEL ANSWER

üìù Model Summary to Examiner

"This is a 45-year-old Indian Australian man presenting with a 2-3 month history of polyuria, polydipsia, and unintentional weight loss - the classical triad of diabetes mellitus.

Key positive findings:

  • Classical symptoms: Polyuria (hourly, 3-4√ó nocturia), polydipsia (4-5 L/day), weight loss (8kg unintentional), increased appetite
  • Hyperglycaemia symptoms: Blurred vision, fatigue, recurrent skin infections, poor wound healing, peripheral neuropathy (tingling feet)

Multiple risk factors for Type 2 Diabetes:

  • Very strong family history: Both parents + 2 siblings with T2DM
  • South Asian ethnicity: 4√ó increased risk compared to Caucasians
  • Obesity: BMI 31 (was 34 before weight loss)
  • Sedentary lifestyle: Desk job, no exercise
  • Poor diet: High carbohydrate (white rice), takeaway, sweets
  • Metabolic syndrome: Hypertension + hypercholesterolaemia already diagnosed
  • Age: 45 years

My differential diagnoses:

  1. Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (most likely - 95% certain)
    • Classical triad present
    • Multiple risk factors
    • Age 45 (typical for T2DM)
    • Gradual onset (2-3 months)
    • Obese (typical for T2DM)
  2. Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus (less likely)
    • Could present at any age (LADA - Latent Autoimmune Diabetes in Adults)
    • But: Age 45, obese, strong T2DM family history argue against
    • Would need GAD antibodies/C-peptide to confirm
  3. Diabetes Insipidus (very unlikely)
    • Would cause polyuria + polydipsia
    • But: DI does NOT cause weight loss (causes weight gain from water)
    • DI does NOT cause blurred vision or infections
    • No family history of DI
  4. Other causes (unlikely):
    • Hypercalcaemia (would need Ca¬≤‚Å∫ level)
    • Chronic kidney disease (would need UEC)
    • Hyperthyroidism (would have other features)
    • Psychogenic polydipsia (no psychiatric history)

Investigations I would order TODAY:

  • Diagnostic tests:
    • Fasting Blood Glucose (FBG) - expect >7.0 mmol/L
    • HbA1c - expect >6.5% (48 mmol/mol) = diagnostic
    • Random BSL (if symptomatic) - >11.1 mmol/L = diagnostic
    • Urine dipstick (glycosuria, proteinuria, ketones)
  • Baseline tests:
    • FBC (infection)
    • UEC (renal function - for metformin safety)
    • LFTs
    • Lipid profile (likely abnormal)
    • Urine ACR (albumin:creatinine ratio for nephropathy)
  • Complications screening:
    • Ophthalmology referral for retinal photography
    • Comprehensive foot exam (neuropathy, vascular assessment)
    • ECG (cardiac risk)

Initial management plan (Australian eTG 2024):

  • Lifestyle modification (FIRST-LINE):
    • Dietitian referral (low GI diet, portion control)
    • Exercise physiologist (aim 150 min/week moderate activity)
    • Weight loss target: 5-10% body weight
  • Pharmacological (if HbA1c >7% or symptomatic):
    • Metformin 500mg daily, titrate to 1000mg BD (eTG first-line)
    • Continue perindopril (ACE-I for renoprotection)
    • Continue statin (aim LDL <1.8 mmol/L)
  • Education:
    • Diabetes education program (NDSS registration)
    • SMBG (self-monitoring blood glucose) training
    • Hypoglycaemia awareness
    • Sick day management
    • Foot care education
  • Follow-up:
    • Review in 1 week with results
    • HbA1c every 3 months until target <7%
    • Annual comprehensive diabetes review (eyes, feet, kidneys)

Counselling points for patient:

  • "The good news is, if this is diabetes - which seems very likely - it's very treatable"
  • "Many people with Type 2 diabetes can achieve remission with weight loss and lifestyle changes"
  • "You may not need insulin - we usually start with tablets and lifestyle"
  • "Your father's complications happened because diabetes control was different 20-30 years ago. With modern treatment and good glucose control, we can prevent most complications"
  • "The most important thing is catching it early - which we have - and treating it aggressively from the start"

üö® Critical Errors That Lead to Failure

1. MISSING THE CLASSICAL TRIAD

Mistake: Not asking about ALL three: polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss

Why this fails: This is THE diagnostic triad for new diabetes

MUST ASK ALL THREE:

  • Polyuria: "How often are you going to the toilet? How much each time? Waking at night?"
  • Polydipsia: "How much are you drinking per day? What type of drinks?"
  • Weight loss: "Any weight changes? How much? Over what time?" ‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL

2. NOT ASKING ABOUT ETHNICITY

Mistake: Assuming ethnicity doesn't matter

Why this fails: In Australian diabetes guidelines, ethnicity is a MAJOR risk factor

3. CONFUSING TYPE 1 VS TYPE 2

Mistake: Young adult (45) with weight loss ‚Üí Assumes Type 1

Why this fails: Type 2 causes weight loss too (from glycosuria)

Key Differences:

4. MISSING COMPLICATIONS SCREENING

Mistake: Making diagnosis but not mentioning complications screening

Why this fails: Australian OSCEs expect comprehensive diabetes management from day 1

IMMEDIATE screening needed (eTG 2024):

  • Eyes: Retinal photography within 3 months (can have retinopathy at diagnosis)
  • Feet: Comprehensive foot exam (monofilament, pulses, inspection)
  • Kidneys: Urine ACR (albumin:creatinine ratio)
  • Cardiovascular: Lipids, BP, ECG (diabetes = CVD risk equivalent)
  • Neuropathy: Monofilament, vibration sense, ankle reflexes

5. WRONG FIRST-LINE TREATMENT

Mistake #1: "I would start insulin immediately"

Why this fails: Type 2 diabetes first-line is lifestyle + metformin (NOT insulin)

Mistake #2: "Just lifestyle changes, no medication"

Why this fails: Patient is symptomatic with classical features ‚Üí Needs metformin immediately (eTG 2024)

Correct approach (Australian eTG):

  1. LIFESTYLE FIRST (always): Diet + exercise + weight loss
  2. METFORMIN (if symptomatic or HbA1c >7%): Start 500mg daily, titrate to 1000mg BD
  3. Second-line (if HbA1c remains >7% after 3 months): Add SGLT2-i or GLP-1 RA
  4. Insulin (only if): HbA1c >10%, ketones present, or failure of oral agents

6. NOT QUANTIFYING FLUID INTAKE

Mistake: "Do you drink a lot?" ‚Üí Patient says "Yes" ‚Üí Candidate moves on

Why this fails: Need to quantify to distinguish diabetes from psychogenic polydipsia

Must ask: "How many litres per day?" or "How many bottles of water?"

Interpretation:

  • >3 L/day + weight loss = Diabetes ‚úÖ
  • >3 L/day + weight gain = Diabetes insipidus or psychogenic
  • <3 L/day = Unlikely to be pathological polyuria

7. FORGETTING NOCTURIA

Mistake: Asking about daytime urination only

Why this fails: Nocturia is a KEY feature of diabetes (vs BPH which causes frequency without nocturia)

Must ask: "Do you wake up at night to urinate? How many times?"

This patient: 3-4√ó per night = Significant nocturia (normal = 0-1√ó)

8. NOT ASSESSING WEIGHT PROPERLY

Mistake: "Have you lost weight?" ‚Üí "Yes" ‚Üí Moves on

Why this fails: Need to quantify weight loss and timeframe

Must ask:

  • "How much weight have you lost?" (8 kg)
  • "Over what period?" (3 months)
  • "Was this intentional or unintentional?" (Unintentional = red flag)
  • "What's your current weight and height?" (Calculate BMI)

Significance: 8kg in 3 months unintentional = Massive weight loss ‚Üí Diabetes or malignancy

9. MISSING BLURRED VISION

Mistake: Not asking about vision changes

Why this fails: Blurred vision is a CLASSICAL diabetes symptom (often first symptom patients notice)

Mechanism: Hyperglycaemia ‚Üí Osmotic swelling of lens ‚Üí Refractive error ‚Üí Blurred vision (reversible with glucose control)

Must ask: "Any changes in your vision? Blurry? Difficulty reading?"

10. POOR COUNSELLING FOR FAMILY HISTORY

Mistake: Not addressing patient's fear of complications (father's amputation)

Why this fails: Australian OSCEs expect empathetic, realistic counselling

Must address:

  • "I understand your father had complications. That must be frightening"
  • "The good news is diabetes management has improved enormously in the past 20 years"
  • "With good glucose control from the start, most complications are preventable"
  • "We've caught this early, which gives us the best chance of preventing complications"
  • "Your father's generation didn't have the medications we have now - we can do much better"

  • Diagnostic criteria: FBG ‚â•7.0 OR HbA1c ‚â•6.5% OR RBG ‚â•11.1 (with symptoms)
  • Target HbA1c: <7% for most, <6.5% if young, <8% if elderly/frail
  • First-line medication: Metformin (unless contraindicated)
  • NDSS registration: All patients registered with National Diabetes Services Scheme (free supplies)
  • Medicare items: 5 GP visits + 5 allied health visits per year (Team Care Arrangement)

Cultural Points:

  • Involve family in education (collectivist culture)
  • Address stigma around diabetes in some communities
  • Consider financial barriers to healthy food
