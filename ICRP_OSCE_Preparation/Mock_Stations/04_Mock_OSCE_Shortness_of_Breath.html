  • No night sweats or weight loss (actually gained weight)
  • No haemoptysis (coughing blood)
  • No recent travel or long flights
  • No recent surgery

EMOTIONS & BEHAVIOR

Emotional State:

  • You're quite worried and frustrated
  • You're afraid of becoming dependent on your daughter
  • If doctor shows empathy, you open up more about your fears

If asked "What are you most worried about?":

"I'm worried this is my heart again. After my heart attack, I was told I needed to look after myself. I don't want to be a burden on my daughter."

If doctor shows good communication:

  • Be more forthcoming with information
  • Express relief that they're taking you seriously

If doctor is rushed or dismissive:

  • Become more anxious
  • May downplay symptoms to avoid "wasting their time"

QUESTIONS YOU SHOULD ASK (at appropriate time)

  • "Is this my heart acting up again?"
  • "Do I need to be admitted to hospital?"
  • "Will I be able to stay in my own home?"

Key positive findings:

  • Orthopnoea (3 pillows)
  • Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea (wakes 2-3 times/night)
  • Bilateral ankle oedema
  • Weight gain 4kg in 1 month
  • Productive cough with white frothy sputum

Key negative findings:

  • No fever, wheeze, chest pain, haemoptysis
  • No recent travel or surgery

Significant past history:

  • Previous MI 5 years ago (stent)
  • Hypertension, type 2 diabetes, hypercholesterolaemia
  • Ex-smoker (40 pack-years)
  • Strong family history of IHD

My differential diagnoses are:

  1. Acute decompensated heart failure (most likely) - supported by orthopnoea, PND, peripheral oedema, weight gain, frothy sputum, previous MI, and cardiac risk factors
  2. COPD exacerbation - possible given 40 pack-year smoking history, though absence of wheeze and lack of previous COPD diagnosis make this less likely
  3. Coronary artery disease/ischaemia - could be contributing to heart failure, though no chest pain
  4. Pulmonary embolism - less likely with gradual onset and no risk factors, but should exclude
  5. Pneumonia - less likely without fever or chest pain

Immediate investigations I would request:

  • ECG (within 10 minutes)
  • Chest X-ray (looking for pulmonary oedema, cardiomegaly)
  • NT-proBNP or BNP (to confirm heart failure)
  • FBC (anaemia can worsen heart failure)
  • UEC (renal function - important for diuretic therapy)
  • Troponin (to exclude acute MI)
  • ABG/VBG (if respiratory distress)
  • Echocardiogram (to assess LV function, though may not be immediately available)

  • Admit for monitoring
  • Oxygen if SpO2 <94%
  • IV frusemide for diuresis
  • Consider GTN if pulmonary oedema confirmed
  • Senior/cardiology review

üö® Critical Errors That Lead to Failure

1. MISSING THE "MAGIC THREE" FOR HEART FAILURE

Mistake: Failing to ask about orthopnoea, PND, or peripheral oedema

Why this fails: These are pathognomonic for left heart failure. Missing even ONE is a critical error.

Australian expectation: ALL three must be explicitly asked and documented. Examiners expect: "How many pillows?" not "Do you get short of breath lying flat?"

2. NOT CALCULATING PACK-YEARS

Mistake: Asking "Do you smoke?" ‚Üí Patient says "I quit 5 years ago" ‚Üí Candidate moves on

Why this fails: 40 pack-years is massive COPD risk. Australian OSCEs expect calculation: "20 cigarettes/day for 40 years = 1 pack √ó 40 years = 40 pack-years"

Correct approach:

  • "How many cigarettes per day?"
  • "For how many years?"
  • "When did you quit?"
  • Calculate aloud: "So that's about 40 pack-years"

3. POOR FUNCTIONAL ASSESSMENT

Mistake: Asking "Do you get short of breath?" ‚Üí Yes ‚Üí Moving on

Why this fails: Doesn't quantify severity. Australian OSCEs expect NYHA classification evidence:

  • NYHA I: No limitation (>500m)
  • NYHA II: Slight limitation (100-500m)
  • NYHA III: Marked limitation (<100m) ‚Üí This patient
  • NYHA IV: Symptoms at rest

Correct approach: "How far can you walk on flat ground before you need to stop? Can you manage stairs?"

4. CONFUSING "FROTHY SPUTUM" WITH "PURULENT SPUTUM"

Mistake: Patient says "white frothy sputum" ‚Üí Candidate thinks "pneumonia" ‚Üí Wrong differential

Why this fails:

  • Frothy white/pink sputum = Pulmonary oedema (heart failure) ‚úÖ THIS PATIENT
  • Purulent yellow/green sputum = Infection (pneumonia, bronchitis)

Australian teaching: "Frothy = Flash pulmonary oedema"

5. MISSING WEIGHT GAIN IN HEART FAILURE

Mistake: Not asking about weight change

Why this fails: 4kg in 1 month = fluid retention = decompensated heart failure

Australian context: NSW Health HF guidelines use daily weights for monitoring. AMC expects you to ask: "Have you noticed any weight changes?"

6. CALLING IT "SOB" INSTEAD OF "DYSPNOEA"

Mistake: Using "SOB" in summary

Why this fails: While "SOB" is common in notes, Australian OSCE examiners prefer formal terminology in presentations

Use instead:

  • "Dyspnoea" or "Shortness of breath" ‚úÖ
  • NOT "SOB" ‚ùå

7. FORGETTING TO ASK ABOUT MEDICATION COMPLIANCE

Mistake: Patient lists medications ‚Üí Candidate moves on

Why this fails: Non-compliance is the #1 cause of heart failure exacerbations in Australia

Must ask: "Do you take these every day? Do you ever miss doses? Any problems affording your medications?" (PBS coverage important in Australian context)

8. NOT ASKING ABOUT ICE

Mistake: Rushing through history without asking "What are you most worried about?"

Why this fails: AMC specifically tests ICE (Ideas, Concerns, Expectations). This patient is worried about "being a burden on her daughter" - key psychosocial factor

Australian OSCE marker: "Did candidate explore patient's concerns?" - worth 2-5 marks

9. WRONG MANAGEMENT PLAN

Mistake #1: "I would discharge with GP follow-up"

Why this fails: NYHA III heart failure with acute decompensation = ADMISSION. Discharging is unsafe.

Mistake #2: "I would start antibiotics"

Why this fails: No fever, no purulent sputum, no consolidation on exam ‚Üí Antibiotics inappropriate

Mistake #3: Not mentioning NT-proBNP

Why this fails: Australian guidelines (NHFA 2018) require BNP/NT-proBNP for heart failure diagnosis. Must mention this test.

10. POOR SUMMARIZATION STRUCTURE

Mistake: Rambling 3-minute summary listing every detail

Why this fails: Australian OSCEs give 1 minute for summary. Must be concise.

Correct structure (60 seconds):

  1. Signpost (5 sec): "This is a 72-year-old woman with progressive dyspnoea"
  2. Key positives (15 sec): "Orthopnoea, PND, peripheral oedema, weight gain, frothy sputum"
  3. Key negatives (10 sec): "No fever, chest pain, or haemoptysis"
  4. Risk factors (10 sec): "Previous MI, HTN, DM, 40 pack-years smoking"
  5. Top 3 differentials (15 sec): "1. Acute decompensated heart failure (most likely), 2. COPD, 3. PE"
  6. Plan (5 sec): "ECG, CXR, BNP, bloods, admit for diuresis"
