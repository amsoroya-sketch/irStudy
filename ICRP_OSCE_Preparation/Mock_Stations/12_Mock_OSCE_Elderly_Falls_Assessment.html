Scenario

You are a GP registrar working at Young Medical Centre. Your next patient is Mrs. Margaret Robertson, an 82-year-old woman who has presented following a fall at home 2 days ago. She was not injured but is worried about falling again.

Your Task (8 minutes)

Take a focused history to identify risk factors for falls and assess the multifactorial causes. You do NOT need to examine the patient.

You Should Cover:

  • Circumstances of the fall (what happened, when, where, how)
  • üö® Red flags for serious causes (syncope, seizure, stroke)
  • Intrinsic risk factors (vision, gait/balance, medications, cardiovascular, neurological, cognitive)
  • Extrinsic risk factors (environmental hazards, footwear, lighting)
  • Previous falls history
  • Functional impact and fear of falling
‚è∞ Time Warning: The examiner will give you a 1-minute warning at the 7-minute mark to allow you to summarize your findings and outline your management plan.
üö® CRITICAL: Falls in elderly people are MULTIFACTORIAL - average 4-5 risk factors per person. You must systematically assess ALL major risk factor categories. Missing medications (polypharmacy) or vision impairment = incomplete assessment.
üí° Australian Context: Falls are the leading cause of injury-related hospitalization in Australians aged >65 (NSW Health 2024). 30% of people >65 fall each year, rising to 50% in those >80. Examiners expect systematic multifactorial assessment covering medical, functional, and environmental factors.

üë§ Simulated Patient Script

Patient Background

Name: Margaret Robertson

Age: 82 years old

Occupation: Retired teacher

Presentation: Fall at home 2 days ago, uninjured but worried about falling again

Clinical Scenario

Diagnosis: Mechanical fall (tripped on rug) with multiple intrinsic and extrinsic risk factors

Risk Factors Identified:

  • Intrinsic: Age 82, poor vision (cataracts), reduced mobility (arthritis), polypharmacy (5 medications including sedative), postural hypotension (recent BP medication increase)
  • Extrinsic: Loose rugs at home, poor lighting in hallway, wearing slippers
  • Functional: Fear of falling, reduced confidence, declining independence

Opening Statement

(Patient walks slowly into room using a walking stick, appears steady but cautious)

"Hello doctor. I'm here because I had a fall at home two days ago. Luckily I wasn't hurt, but it really shook me up. I'm worried it might happen again. My daughter insists I come to see you."

Fall History - Detailed

Circumstances of THIS Fall:

  • When did the fall happen? "Two days ago, in the evening - about 7pm"
  • Where were you? "At home, in my hallway. I was walking from the lounge to the kitchen"
  • What happened - can you describe exactly what happened? "I was walking to the kitchen and I tripped on the edge of my hallway rug. My foot caught on it and I went down forwards onto my hands and knees" (mechanical fall - tripped)
  • Did you lose consciousness or blackout? "No, I was awake the whole time. I remember everything" (rules out syncope)
  • Do you remember falling? "Yes, I remember it all" (rules out seizure/amnesia)
  • Any warning before the fall - dizziness, lightheadedness? "No warning, I just tripped" (no prodrome - mechanical fall)
  • Any chest pain, palpitations, or shortness of breath before? "No, nothing like that"
  • Did you feel dizzy or faint? "No, I felt fine - I just caught my foot on the rug"
  • Were you able to get up by yourself? "Yes, I got up straight away. It took me a minute because I'm a bit stiff, but I managed"
  • Any injuries? "Just some bruising on my knees and hands - nothing serious. No cuts. I didn't hit my head"

Previous Falls History - CRITICAL

  • Have you fallen before? "Yes, actually I had another fall about 4 months ago. I fell in the bathroom" üö® (recurrent faller - high risk)
  • How many falls in the past year? "This is my second fall this year" (‚â•2 falls/year = high fall risk)
  • What happened with the bathroom fall? "I was getting out of the shower and slipped on the wet floor. I fell onto my hip - that time I did hurt myself. I had quite a bad bruise"
  • Did you see a doctor after that fall? "No, I just rested at home. My daughter wanted me to come in but I said I was fine"

Intrinsic Risk Factors - Medical

Vision - KEY RISK FACTOR:

  • How is your eyesight? "Not great actually. I have cataracts in both eyes. Things are quite blurry, especially in dim light" üö®
  • When did you last see an optometrist? "About 2 years ago"
  • Do you wear glasses? "Yes, I have reading glasses and distance glasses. But sometimes I forget which ones I'm wearing"
  • Can you see clearly when you're walking? "Not really - everything is a bit fuzzy. I've been meaning to get my eyes checked but I keep putting it off"

Gait & Balance:

  • Do you have any problems with walking or balance? "I'm not as steady as I used to be. I use a walking stick now - I've been using it for about a year"
  • Do you feel unsteady when you walk? "Yes, sometimes. Especially on uneven ground or when I turn around"
  • Any weakness in your legs? "My legs feel weak sometimes. I have arthritis in both knees and hips, so it's quite painful to walk"

Cardiovascular - Postural Hypotension:

  • Do you ever feel dizzy when you stand up? "Actually yes - especially in the mornings when I get out of bed. I feel quite lightheaded and have to hold onto something for a moment" üö® (postural hypotension)
  • How long does the dizziness last? "Just a minute or so, then it passes"
  • Have you ever fainted or blacked out? "No, never actually fainted. Just felt dizzy"
  • Any heart problems? "I have high blood pressure - I take tablets for that"

Neurological:

  • Any history of stroke? "No"
  • Any tremor or Parkinson's disease? "No"
  • Any numbness or tingling in your feet? "A little bit of numbness in my toes - the doctor said it might be circulation"

Cognitive Function:

  • How is your memory? "Pretty good for my age. I remember my grandchildren's birthdays and what I did yesterday. Sometimes I forget names but nothing too bad"
  • Any confusion or getting lost? "No, nothing like that"

Urinary Symptoms (Nocturia - Fall Risk):

  • Do you get up at night to pass urine? "Yes, usually 2-3 times a night" üö® (nocturia - increased fall risk, rushing to toilet in dark)
  • Do you rush to the toilet? "Sometimes, yes. When I need to go, I need to go quite urgently"
  • Any incontinence? "Occasionally I leak a little bit if I don't get there in time"

Medications - CRITICAL ASSESSMENT

Patient has brought medication list from pharmacy

  • What medications do you take? "I have a list here from the pharmacy. I take quite a few tablets"

Current Medications (5 medications = POLYPHARMACY):

  1. Amlodipine 10mg daily (for hypertension - can cause postural hypotension)
  2. Ramipril 10mg daily (for hypertension - RECENT INCREASE from 5mg to 10mg TWO WEEKS AGO üö® - likely causing postural hypotension)
  3. Atorvastatin 40mg nocte (for cholesterol)
  4. Temazepam 10mg nocte (sedative - FALL RISK üö®)
  5. Paracetamol 500mg as needed (for arthritis pain - up to 8 tablets/day)

    6. If Asked About When Medications Started/Changed:

    • Ramipril increase: "The doctor increased my blood pressure tablet about 2 weeks ago because my blood pressure was still a bit high" üö® KEY - temporal association with recent fall
    • Temazepam: "I've been taking the sleeping tablet for about 3 months. I was having trouble sleeping"

    If Asked About Side Effects:

    • Dizziness on standing: "Yes, as I mentioned - especially in the mornings. It's got worse in the past couple of weeks" (since ramipril increase)
    • Drowsiness from temazepam: "The sleeping tablet works well - maybe too well. I sometimes feel a bit groggy in the morning and I get up once or twice in the night still feeling a bit drowsy"

    Extrinsic Risk Factors - Home Environment

    Home Hazards:

    • Do you live in a house or unit? "A house - I've lived there for 40 years"
    • Any stairs? "No, it's all single level, thank goodness"
    • Do you have any loose rugs or mats? "Yes, I have rugs in the hallway and lounge room. That's what I tripped on" üö®
    • How is the lighting in your home? "Not great - the hallway is quite dark, especially at night. I've been meaning to put a brighter bulb in" üö®
    • Do you have rails in the bathroom? "No, I don't have any rails. I've been thinking about getting some installed"
    • Do you have a night light? "No - maybe I should get one for the hallway for when I get up at night"
    • What shoes do you usually wear at home? "Slippers - they're quite old and a bit loose" üö® (poor footwear)

    Living Situation:

    • Do you live alone? "Yes, I've lived alone since my husband passed away 5 years ago"
    • Does anyone check on you regularly? "My daughter visits twice a week and calls every day"
    • Do you have a personal alarm or medical alert? "No, I don't have one. My daughter has been suggesting I get one but I keep saying I don't need it"

    Functional Impact & Fear of Falling

    Activities of Daily Living (ADLs):

    • Can you manage your own shopping, cooking, cleaning? "I can cook, but my daughter does the shopping for me now. Cleaning is hard - I can't bend down very well"
    • Can you manage showering and dressing yourself? "Yes, but it takes me a long time. I have to be very careful in the shower"

    Fear of Falling - CRITICAL:

    • Are you worried about falling again? "Yes, very worried. That's why I'm here. I keep thinking about what would have happened if I'd hit my head or broken my hip" üö®
    • Has this affected what you do? "Definitely. I've stopped going to my walking group because I'm scared I might fall outside. I don't go out as much as I used to" üö® (fear of falling ‚Üí reduced activity ‚Üí deconditioning ‚Üí increased fall risk = vicious cycle)
    • Do you feel confident walking around? "Not really - I'm always on edge, worried I might trip over something"

    Past Medical History

    • Hypertension: On treatment (amlodipine, ramipril)
    • Hyperlipidaemia: On atorvastatin
    • Osteoarthritis: Bilateral knees and hips
    • Cataracts: Bilateral, not yet operated
    • Insomnia: On temazepam

    Social History

    • Alcohol? "A small glass of sherry before bed sometimes"
    • Smoking? "Never smoked"
    • Living arrangements? "Live alone in own house"
    • Family support? "Daughter visits twice weekly"

    Patient Concerns (ICE)

    If Asked "What are you most worried about?"

    "I'm worried I'll fall and break my hip like my friend did. She fell and broke her hip and never really recovered - she ended up in a nursing home. I don't want to end up like that. I want to stay in my own home."

    If Asked "What were you hoping we could do?"

    "I want to know why I keep falling and what I can do to stop it. Maybe there's something wrong with my balance? Or my medications? My daughter thinks I should move into an aged care facility but I want to stay at home as long as possible."

    Emotional State

    Patient appears anxious but cooperative. She's motivated to prevent further falls because she wants to maintain independence. Slightly tearful when discussing fear of nursing home placement.

üìù Model Answer - Summary

Patient Summary

"This is Mrs. Margaret Robertson, an 82-year-old woman presenting after her second fall in 4 months. Both falls were mechanical (trip), but she has MULTIPLE MODIFIABLE RISK FACTORS including recent medication change (ramipril increase causing postural hypotension), polypharmacy (5 medications including temazepam - fall-risk medication), vision impairment (cataracts), home hazards (loose rugs, poor lighting), and fear of falling leading to reduced activity. This requires comprehensive multifactorial falls prevention intervention."

Risk Factors Identified

INTRINSIC (Medical) Risk Factors:

  1. üö® AGE 82 years - advanced age
  2. üö® RECURRENT FALLER - 2 falls in 4 months (HIGH RISK)
  3. üö® POLYPHARMACY - 5 medications
  4. üö® FALL-RISK MEDICATIONS:
    • Temazepam (benzodiazepine - sedation, impaired balance)
    • Amlodipine + Ramipril (antihypertensives - postural hypotension)
    • Ramipril RECENTLY INCREASED (temporal association with falls)
  5. üö® POSTURAL HYPOTENSION - dizzy on standing (worse since ramipril increase)
  6. üö® VISION IMPAIRMENT - bilateral cataracts, blurry vision
  7. Reduced mobility - uses walking stick, osteoarthritis knees/hips
  8. Muscle weakness - legs feel weak, arthritis limits activity
  9. Nocturia - gets up 2-3 times/night (rushing in dark)
  10. Peripheral neuropathy - numbness in toes

EXTRINSIC (Environmental) Risk Factors:

  1. üö® HOME HAZARDS:
    • Loose rugs in hallway/lounge (tripped on rug)
    • Poor lighting in hallway (especially at night)
    • No bathroom rails
    • No night light
  2. üö® POOR FOOTWEAR - loose slippers
  3. Lives alone - no immediate help if falls
  4. No personal alarm

FUNCTIONAL Impact:

  • üö® FEAR OF FALLING - very anxious about recurrence
  • üö® REDUCED ACTIVITY - stopped walking group, going out less (deconditioning)
  • Declining independence - daughter does shopping
  • Loss of confidence

Immediate Management Plan - NSW Health Falls Prevention Guidelines 2024

1. URGENT Medication Review (TODAY):

  • üö® STOP Temazepam immediately - benzodiazepines are HIGH RISK for falls in elderly (eTG 2024 - avoid in >65 years)
  • üö® REDUCE Ramipril back to 5mg (previous dose) - recent increase likely causing postural hypotension
  • Monitor lying/standing blood pressure - confirm postural drop
  • Alternative for insomnia: Sleep hygiene advice, consider melatonin 2mg nocte (safer alternative)
  • Continue: Amlodipine (but monitor BP), atorvastatin, paracetamol

2. Vision Assessment (URGENT - within 2 weeks):

  • Refer to ophthalmology - cataract surgery assessment
  • Interim: Update glasses prescription
  • Cataract surgery reduces fall risk by 30-40%

3. Home Safety Assessment:

  • Occupational therapy HOME VISIT - comprehensive environmental assessment
  • Immediate recommendations:
    • REMOVE or secure loose rugs with non-slip backing
    • INSTALL brighter light bulbs in hallway (60-100W)
    • INSTALL night lights in bedroom/hallway/bathroom
    • INSTALL grab rails in bathroom (shower, toilet)
    • REPLACE loose slippers with supportive, non-slip footwear
  • Consider: Personal alarm system (pendant alarm) for if falls when alone

4. Falls Prevention Exercise Program:

  • Physiotherapy referral - strength and balance retraining
  • Group-based exercise program: e.g., Stepping On program (evidence-based - reduces falls by 30%)
  • Home exercise program: Daily balance exercises, strengthening
  • Walking aid review: Check stick is correct height, consider 4-wheel walker for outdoor stability

5. Bone Health Assessment (Secondary Prevention):

  • Bone density scan (DEXA) - assess for osteoporosis
  • Calcium and vitamin D: Caltrate D 600mg/400IU daily OR Ostelin Vitamin D 1000IU daily
  • If osteoporosis: Consider bisphosphonate (alendronate) - reduces fracture risk

6. Manage Nocturia:

  • Fluid restriction after 6pm
  • Toileting before bed
  • Commode beside bed (reduce need to walk to bathroom at night in poor light)
  • Night lights (bedroom ‚Üí bathroom route)

7. Address Fear of Falling:

  • Acknowledge concerns: "It's very understandable you're worried. We're going to work together to make you safer"
  • Goal setting: "What activities would you like to get back to?" (e.g., walking group)
  • Graduated return to activity: Start with supervised walking, build confidence
  • Falls prevention education: Written information, support groups

8. Follow-up Plan:

  • Review in 1 week: Check BP lying/standing after medication changes
  • Review in 4-6 weeks: Reassess falls risk, check exercise program adherence
  • Multidisciplinary team: GP + Physiotherapist + Occupational Therapist + Ophthalmologist

Expected Outcome with Intervention:

  • Medication optimization ‚Üí reduce postural hypotension, remove sedation
  • Cataract surgery ‚Üí improved vision
  • Home modifications ‚Üí remove environmental hazards
  • Exercise program ‚Üí improve strength, balance, confidence
  • Estimated fall risk reduction: 50-70% with comprehensive multifactorial intervention
