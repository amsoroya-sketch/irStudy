FOR THE CANDIDATE

INSTRUCTIONS (Read Before Entering Station - 1 minute)

You are a junior doctor working on the ‚ö†Ô∏è MEDICAL ward at Young District Hospital, NSW.

Clinical Scenario:

Mrs Dorothy Williams, an 82-year-old woman, was admitted 2 days ago with a urinary tract infection. She has been receiving IV antibiotics. The nursing staff have reported that she has become increasingly confused over the past 24 hours.

Task: Mrs Williams' daughter has come to visit. Take a collateral history from the daughter to help assess the patient's confusion.

You have 8 minutes for this station.

At the 7-minute mark, the examiner will ask you to summarize your findings and provide your differential diagnoses.

Note: The patient herself is not available for this station. You are speaking with her daughter only.

You may now enter the station.

FOR THE RELATIVE/INFORMANT (Simulated Daughter)

BACKGROUND INFORMATION (Candidate CANNOT see this)

Your Name: Susan Williams (daughter)

Patient's Name: Dorothy Williams (your mother)

Patient's Age: 82 years old

Your Relationship: Daughter, visit mum twice a week usually

PRESENTING CONCERN

Opening statement: "I'm really worried about mum. She's not herself at all. She didn't even recognize me when I visited this morning. This isn't like her."

PATIENT'S BASELINE (Before admission)

If asked about usual mental state/cognition:

  • "Mum has always been sharp as a tack. She lives independently, does her own shopping, manages her own medications, pays her bills. She does the crossword every day."
  • "She has never had memory problems before"
  • "No dementia, no Alzheimer's - the GP said her mind was fine at her last checkup 3 months ago"

Activities of Daily Living (before admission):

  • Fully independent with all ADLs
  • Drives to the shops (has a current licence)
  • Cooks, cleans, gardens
  • Active in church community

TIMELINE OF CONFUSION

If asked when confusion started:

  • "When I visited her 3 days ago at home, before she came to hospital, she was completely normal"
  • "She was admitted 2 days ago with a urine infection"
  • "Yesterday (day 1 in hospital) she seemed okay when I visited in the afternoon - a bit sleepy but knew who I was"
  • "But this morning (day 2) when I came in at 9am, she didn't recognize me at all. She called me 'nurse' and asked me to take her home"
  • "The nurses said she was up all night wandering around, trying to leave"

CURRENT BEHAVIOUR (What nurses/you have observed)

If asked to describe current confusion:

  • "She doesn't know where she is - thinks she's at home or sometimes at a hotel"
  • "She doesn't know what day it is"
  • "She keeps asking for her husband - but he died 10 years ago"
  • "She's seeing things that aren't there - this morning she was swatting at 'flies' but there were no flies"
  • "At night she's worse - the nurses said she was very agitated, shouting, trying to pull her IV out"
  • "During the day she's sometimes drowsy, sometimes agitated"

PAST MEDICAL HISTORY

If asked about medical conditions:

  • High blood pressure - for 20 years, well controlled
  • Osteoarthritis in her knees
  • No diabetes
  • No previous strokes or heart attacks
  • No previous psychiatric illness
  • No dementia (confirmed by GP 3 months ago)

MEDICATIONS (Before admission)

If asked about usual medications:

  • Perindopril 4mg daily (for blood pressure)
  • Paracetamol as needed for arthritis pain (maybe 2-3 times a week)
  • "That's all - just those two"

If asked about NEW medications in hospital:

  • "I don't know exactly what they're giving her here"
  • "I know she's on antibiotics through the drip"
  • "The nurse mentioned they gave her something to help her sleep last night because she was so agitated"

REASON FOR ADMISSION

If asked why she came to hospital:

  • "She had a urine infection - she was having pain when she weed, and her urine was dark and smelly"
  • "She also had a fever - about 38.5 degrees"
  • "But she was mentally fine when she was admitted - she was talking normally to me"

SOCIAL HISTORY

Living situation:

  • Lives alone in her own house (single-storey)
  • Widowed 10 years ago
  • You (daughter) visit twice a week, son visits once a week
  • Neighbours check on her

Alcohol:

  • "Mum doesn't drink - maybe a sherry at Christmas, that's all"
  • Definitely not a heavy drinker

Smoking:

  • Never smoked

RECENT CHANGES (Past week before admission)

If asked about recent changes:

  • "She mentioned she was having trouble sleeping the week before - said she was only getting 3-4 hours a night"
  • "She seemed a bit tired, but her mind was still sharp"
  • "No falls recently"
  • "No new medications started"

YOUR CONCERNS & QUESTIONS

Emotional state:

  • You're quite distressed and tearful
  • Worried this is "the beginning of the end"
  • Concerned she might need a nursing home

If asked "What are you most worried about?":

"I'm terrified this is dementia or Alzheimer's. My grandmother had Alzheimer's and I watched her deteriorate. Is that what's happening to mum? Will she ever go back to normal?"

Questions you should ask:

  • "Is this dementia?"
  • "Will she get better?"
  • "Can she come home or will she need to go into a nursing home?"

FOR THE EXAMINER

EXAMINER INSTRUCTIONS

Your Role:

  • Observe silently
  • Mark using checklist below
  • At 7 minutes: "You have 1 minute remaining. Please summarize and provide your differential diagnoses."
  • At 8 minutes: "Thank you, time is up."

STATION OBJECTIVES

This station assesses:

  1. Ability to take collateral history for acute confusion
  2. Distinguish delirium from dementia from depression
  3. Identify üö® RED FLAGs for delirium
  4. Assess baseline cognitive function
  5. Identify precipitating factors (infection, medications)
  6. Communication with distressed relative

üö® DIAGNOSIS (For Examiner)

This is DELIRIUM (hyperactive type), NOT dementia

Key features present:

  • Acute onset (24-48 hours)
  • Fluctuating course (worse at night = sundowning)
  • Inattention + disorientation
  • Perceptual disturbances (visual hallucinations - "flies")
  • Normal baseline cognition (no dementia)

Precipitating factors:

  • Infection (UTI)
  • Unfamiliar environment (hospital)
  • Sleep deprivation
  • Possible medication (benzodiazepine for agitation)

Against dementia: Acute onset, previously normal cognition, fluctuating

MODEL ANSWER

üìù Model Summary to Examiner

"This is an 82-year-old woman who has developed acute confusion following admission for urinary tract infection.

Key findings suggesting DELIRIUM:

  • Acute onset: Confusion started 24 hours ago (not gradual)
  • Normal baseline cognition: Previously independent, sharp, no dementia
  • Fluctuating course: Worse at night (sundowning), drowsy during day
  • Inattention: Disorientated to place and time
  • Perceptual disturbances: Visual hallucinations (seeing flies)
  • Altered consciousness: Hyperactive type (agitated, wandering)

Precipitating factors identified:

  • Infection (UTI) - most common cause in elderly
  • Unfamiliar environment (hospital admission)
  • Sleep deprivation (poor sleep week before + disrupted in hospital)
  • Possible sedative medication (given for agitation)

Against dementia:

  • Acute onset (dementia is insidious over months-years)
  • Previously normal cognition 3 days ago
  • Fluctuating course (dementia is progressive, not fluctuating)

My diagnosis: Hyperactive delirium secondary to UTI

Investigations needed:

  • Urine M/C/S (likely already sent)
  • FBC (infection markers)
  • UEC (renal function, dehydration)
  • LFTs
  • Glucose (hypoglycaemia can cause confusion)
  • Calcium (hypercalcaemia)
  • TSH (thyroid)
  • Medication review (check for deliriogenic drugs)
  • Consider CT head if focal neurology or fall

Management plan:

  • Treat underlying cause: Continue antibiotics for UTI
  • Review medications: STOP benzodiazepines/sedatives if given
  • Non-pharmacological management (first-line):
    • Reorientation strategies (clock, calendar visible)
    • Familiar objects from home (photos)
    • Encourage family visits for reassurance
    • Ensure glasses/hearing aids in place
    • Avoid restraints
    • Regular toileting, mobilisation
    • Sleep hygiene (reduce noise at night)
  • Pharmacological (last resort only if severe agitation/harm risk):
    • Low-dose haloperidol 0.5-1mg (avoid benzodiazepines)
    • Or risperidone 0.25-0.5mg

Prognosis & counselling for daughter:

  • "This is delirium, not dementia or Alzheimer's"
  • "Delirium is usually reversible once we treat the infection"
  • "It may take days to weeks to fully resolve in elderly patients"
  • "She's likely to return to her baseline cognitive function"
  • "However, delirium does increase risk of future dementia, so we'll arrange follow-up cognitive assessment in 3 months"
  • "For now, we need your help with reorientation - bring in family photos, talk to her about familiar topics"

üö® Critical Errors That Lead to Failure

1. NOT ESTABLISHING BASELINE COGNITION

Mistake: Jumping straight to "What's happening now?" without asking about usual mental state

Why this fails: Cannot distinguish delirium from dementia without knowing baseline

MUST ASK:

  • "Before this hospital admission, what was your mother's memory like?"
  • "Could she manage her own medications? Pay her bills? Do her shopping?"
  • "Did she have any diagnosis of dementia or memory problems?"

This patient: Normal baseline = delirium (not dementia)

2. CONFUSING DELIRIUM WITH DEMENTIA

Mistake: Elderly + confused = dementia

Why this fails: Delirium is REVERSIBLE, dementia is PROGRESSIVE

Key Differences (Australian eTG teaching):

Feature DELIRIUM ‚úÖ This Patient DEMENTIA ‚ùå
Onset Acute (hours-days) Insidious (months-years)
Course Fluctuating (worse at night) Progressive (slowly worsening)
Duration Days-weeks (reversible) Permanent
Consciousness Altered (drowsy or agitated) Normal until late stages
Hallucinations Common (visual) Rare (until late)
Reversible? YES (treat cause) NO

3. MISSING THE "CAM" CRITERIA FOR DELIRIUM

Mistake: Not using structured approach

Australian OSCEs expect CAM (Confusion Assessment Method):

  1. Acute onset + fluctuating course ‚úÖ This patient (24 hours, worse at night)
  2. Inattention ‚úÖ This patient (disorientated, can't focus)
  3. Disorganized thinking OR
  4. Altered level of consciousness ‚úÖ This patient (hyper active, agitated)

Delirium diagnosis: Need #1 + #2 + (#3 OR #4)

4. NOT ASKING ABOUT "SUNDOWNING"

Mistake: Not asking "Is she worse at a particular time of day?"

Why this fails: Sundowning (worsening at night) is PATHOGNOMONIC for delirium

Must ask: "Is the confusion worse during the day or at night?"

This patient: Agitated at night, drowsy during day = CLASSIC sundowning

5. FORGETTING TO ASK ABOUT MEDICATIONS

Mistake: Not asking "Have any new medications been started in hospital?"

Why this fails: Medications are the #2 most common cause of delirium (after infection)

Deliriogenic drugs (Australian eTG):

  • Benzodiazepines - #1 culprit (temazepam, diazepam) ‚ö†Ô∏è This patient got "something to sleep"
  • Anticholinergics (oxybutynin, hyoscine)
  • Opioids (morphine, oxycodone)
  • Antihistamines (promethazine)
  • Corticosteroids

Australian nursing practice: Often give "PRN temazepam" for agitation ‚Üí WORSENS delirium!

6. WRONG INITIAL MANAGEMENT

Mistake #1: "I would start her on haloperidol"

Why this fails: Non-pharmacological first! Drugs are LAST resort

Correct approach (Australian Delirium Guidelines 2024):

  1. FIRST: Treat underlying cause (UTI antibiotics)
  2. SECOND: Non-pharmacological (reorientation, familiar objects, family presence)
  3. THIRD: Review medications (STOP benzodiazepines)
  4. LAST RESORT: Antipsychotics (only if severe agitation risking harm)

Mistake #2: "Continue the sleeping tablet"

Why this fails: Benzodiazepines CAUSE delirium - must STOP them

7. NOT REASSURING THE DAUGHTER

Mistake: Giving medical information without addressing emotional concerns

Why this fails: Daughter is terrified this is "the beginning of the end"

Must explicitly say:

  • "This is NOT dementia or Alzheimer's"
  • "This is delirium, which is usually reversible"
  • "Your mum is likely to return to her normal self once we treat the infection"
  • "It may take a few days or weeks, but we expect good recovery"

Australian cultural expectation: Families are VERY involved in care - reassure and include them

8. MISSING THE "PINCH" MNEMONIC

Mistake: Not systematically ruling out other causes

Australian teaching - PINCH ME causes of delirium:

  • P - Pain ‚úÖ Ask about
  • I - Infection ‚úÖ This patient (UTI)
  • N - Nutrition/Hydration ‚úÖ Check
  • C - Constipation ‚úÖ Ask about
  • H - Hypoxia ‚úÖ Check sats
  • M - Medications ‚úÖ This patient (benzodiazepine)
  • E - Environment change ‚úÖ This patient (hospital admission)

9. POOR COLLATERAL HISTORY TECHNIQUE

Mistake: Talking to relative like they're the patient

Correct approach:

  • "Thank you for coming in. I'd like to ask you some questions about your mother to help me understand what's been happening"
  • Use third person: "How is she normally?" not "How are you?"
  • Get daughter's perspective: "What have you noticed?" "What are you most worried about?"

10. NOT DISCUSSING PROGNOSIS

Mistake: Making diagnosis but not explaining what happens next

Australian OSCE expects prognostic counselling:

  • "Most cases of delirium resolve within 1-2 weeks"
  • "However, in elderly patients, full recovery can take up to 3 months"
  • "About 10-20% may have persistent cognitive changes"
  • "Delirium does slightly increase future dementia risk, so we'll arrange follow-up cognitive testing in 3 months"
  • "For now, your mum will likely be able to return home once the infection clears and confusion improves"

üí° Australian-Specific Tips for IMGs

Terminology:

‚ùå Avoid ‚úÖ Use (Australian)
Acute confusional state / Acute brain syndrome Delirium (preferred term in Australia)
Senile dementia Dementia (never use "senile" - considered ageist)
Nursing home Residential aged care facility (RACF) or "aged care"
ADL Activities of Daily Living (spell out in OSCE)

NSW Health Delirium Pathway:

  • All patients >65 screened for delirium risk on admission
  • CAM used for diagnosis
  • Non-pharmacological interventions FIRST
  • Benzodiazepines avoided (contraindicated in delirium)
  • Family encouraged to stay bedside for reorientation

Cultural Points:

  • Australian families expect to be involved in care decisions
  • Explain medical terms in plain English
  • Address both clinical facts AND emotional concerns
  • Discharge planning starts from day 1 (discuss with family early)
