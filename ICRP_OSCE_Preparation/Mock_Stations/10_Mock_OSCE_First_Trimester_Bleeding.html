Definitive Management Options (RANZCOG 2024)

Option 1: EMERGENCY SURGERY üö® (If Ruptured or Imminent Rupture)

Indications:

  • Haemodynamic instability (hypotension, tachycardia, shock)
  • Severe or increasing abdominal pain
  • Significant free fluid on ultrasound (haemoperitoneum)
  • Clinical suspicion of ruptured ectopic

Surgical Options:

  • Laparoscopy (preferred if haemodynamically stable):
    • Diagnostic + therapeutic
    • Salpingectomy (remove affected tube) - GOLD STANDARD
    • OR Salpingotomy (linear incision in tube, remove ectopic, preserve tube) - if fertility preservation critical and contralateral tube damaged
    • Evacuate haemoperitoneum
    • Shorter recovery, less pain, better cosmesis
  • Laparotomy (if haemodynamically UNSTABLE or massive haemoperitoneum):
    • Faster access
    • Better haemostasis control in massive bleeding
    • Salpingectomy ¬± blood transfusion

Option 2: MEDICAL MANAGEMENT (If Unruptured, Stable, Early)

Criteria for Medical Management (ALL must be met):

  • Haemodynamically STABLE
  • No signs of rupture (no free fluid, minimal pain)
  • Ectopic mass <35mm diameter
  • No fetal heartbeat visible
  • Œ≤hCG <3000 IU/L (some centres use <5000)
  • Normal liver and renal function
  • Patient able to attend for close follow-up
  • Patient understands risks and consents

Initial Discussion (in ED):

  • Guidelines: RANZCOG (Royal Australian and New Zealand College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists) Ectopic Pregnancy Guidelines 2024
  • eTG 2024: First-line antibiotic if septic miscarriage suspected: Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g IV + gentamicin 5-7mg/kg IV
  • Anti-D: 250 IU (50 micrograms) for <12 weeks (Australian dosing - differs from UK 250 IU for all trimesters)
  • Referral pathway: ED ‚Üí O&G registrar ‚Üí O&G consultant ‚Üí Theatre if surgical
  • Follow-up: O&G outpatient clinic 4-6 weeks post-surgery OR weekly if medical management

‚ö†Ô∏è IMG Common Mistakes - Critical Errors to Avoid

Ectopic pregnancy is the LEADING cause of maternal death in the first trimester. Missing this diagnosis = patient death. These 10 mistakes are commonly made by IMG candidates and will lead to automatic failure.

‚ùå MISTAKE #1: Not Asking About Shoulder Tip Pain
Why this fails: Shoulder tip pain is PATHOGNOMONIC (diagnostic) for haemoperitoneum (blood in the peritoneal cavity irritating the diaphragm, causing referred pain to C3-C5 dermatome = shoulder). Missing this means you miss a ruptured or near-ruptured ectopic pregnancy. This is a CRITICAL FAILURE.

What happens: IMG candidates focus on abdominal/pelvic symptoms and forget to specifically ask "Do you have any shoulder pain?" This is a MUST-ASK question in ALL first trimester bleeding cases.

Why patients don't volunteer this: Patients don't connect shoulder pain with pregnancy bleeding - they think they "slept funny." YOU must ask specifically.
‚úÖ CORRECT: In EVERY first trimester bleeding case, you MUST ask: "Have you had any pain in your shoulder or shoulder tip?" This is NON-NEGOTIABLE. If answer is YES ‚Üí ruptured/rupturing ectopic until proven otherwise ‚Üí EMERGENCY.
‚ùå MISTAKE #2: Not Asking About Feeling Faint/Dizzy (Orthostatic Symptoms)
Why this fails: Orthostatic symptoms (feeling faint when standing up, lightheaded, near-syncope) indicate hypovolemia from internal bleeding. This is a RED FLAG for significant haemoperitoneum. Not asking about this means you don't recognize the patient is bleeding internally and may collapse.

What happens: IMG candidates ask about pain and bleeding but don't systematically assess for signs of blood loss (dizziness, feeling faint, syncope). Australian OSCE expects this as standard practice.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask "Have you felt dizzy or lightheaded?" "Have you felt faint or like you might pass out?" "Have you actually fainted or collapsed?" If YES to any ‚Üí suggests blood loss ‚Üí ruptured ectopic possible ‚Üí EMERGENCY.
‚ùå MISTAKE #3: Not Differentiating Unilateral vs Bilateral Abdominal Pain
Why this fails: UNILATERAL (one-sided) pain strongly suggests ectopic pregnancy (pregnancy in left or right tube). BILATERAL or central pain is more consistent with miscarriage (intrauterine pregnancy loss). Not asking "Is the pain on one side or both?" means you miss a critical diagnostic clue.

What happens: IMG candidates ask "Where is the pain?" but don't specifically clarify ONE side vs BOTH sides. The patient says "my stomach" or "down low" and the candidate doesn't follow up with "Which side specifically?"
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask "Is the pain mainly on one side, or is it in the middle/both sides?" If patient says ONE SIDE (right or left iliac fossa) ‚Üí ectopic pregnancy is MOST LIKELY diagnosis. Central/bilateral = more consistent with miscarriage.
‚ùå MISTAKE #4: Not Asking About Previous Pelvic Infection (PID/Chlamydia)
Why this fails: Previous pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - especially from chlamydia or gonorrhoea - is the BIGGEST modifiable risk factor for ectopic pregnancy. PID causes tubal scarring and damage, preventing the embryo from traveling to the uterus. PID increases ectopic risk 6-10 fold. Not asking = missing major risk factor.

What happens: IMG candidates are often embarrassed or uncomfortable asking about sexual history/STIs. In Australian medical culture, asking about STI history is ROUTINE and expected. Not asking is considered incomplete history.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask matter-of-factly "Have you ever had any pelvic infections in the past?" "Have you ever been diagnosed with chlamydia, gonorrhoea, or pelvic inflammatory disease?" This is standard sexual health history in Australia. Don't be embarrassed - it's essential information.
‚ùå MISTAKE #5: Not Confirming Pregnancy Status (Pregnancy Test Done? Result?)
Why this fails: You cannot diagnose ectopic pregnancy or miscarriage if you don't confirm the patient is actually pregnant. Surprisingly, some IMG candidates assume the patient is pregnant based on "missed period" without explicitly asking "Have you done a pregnancy test? What was the result?"

What happens: Patient mentions "late period" and candidate jumps to pregnancy-related diagnoses without confirming pregnancy. Could be other causes of amenorrhoea + bleeding (anovulatory cycle, PCOS, etc.).
‚úÖ CORRECT: ALWAYS ask: "Have you done a pregnancy test?" "When did you do it?" "What was the result?" Confirm POSITIVE pregnancy test before pursuing ectopic/miscarriage differentials. If no test done ‚Üí offer bedside urine Œ≤hCG in ED.
‚ùå MISTAKE #6: Not Asking if Ultrasound Scan Done (IUP Confirmed?)
Why this fails: If an intrauterine pregnancy (IUP) has been CONFIRMED on ultrasound (gestational sac in uterus with yolk sac/fetal pole), then ectopic pregnancy is almost impossible (heterotopic pregnancy = IUP + ectopic = 1 in 30,000, except 1 in 3,000 with IVF). Not asking about previous scans means you don't know if IUP is confirmed.

What happens: IMG candidates assume if pregnancy test is positive, they need to differentiate ectopic vs miscarriage. But if IUP already confirmed on scan, ectopic is essentially ruled out.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask "Have you had any ultrasound scans yet?" "Did they see the pregnancy in your womb?" If YES (IUP confirmed) ‚Üí ectopic extremely unlikely, focus on miscarriage differentials. If NO (no scan yet) ‚Üí ectopic vs miscarriage both possible.
‚ùå MISTAKE #7: Not Asking About Blood Group (Rh Status)
Why this fails: If patient is Rh NEGATIVE, she MUST receive Anti-D immunoglobulin within 72 hours of any bleeding in pregnancy, miscarriage, or ectopic pregnancy. Failure to give Anti-D can cause alloimmunisation, leading to haemolytic disease of the newborn in future pregnancies (potentially fatal for future babies). Not asking about blood group = missing critical preventive treatment.

What happens: IMG candidates from countries where Anti-D protocols differ may not ask about Rh status. In Australia, asking blood group is STANDARD in any pregnancy-related consultation.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask "Do you know your blood group?" If patient is Rh NEGATIVE (O neg, A neg, B neg, AB neg) OR unknown ‚Üí document need for Anti-D. If Rh POSITIVE ‚Üí Anti-D not needed. If unknown ‚Üí check with lab urgently + give Anti-D if time-critical.
‚ùå MISTAKE #8: Not Recognizing URGENCY - Treating as Routine First Trimester Bleeding
Why this fails: This patient has MULTIPLE red flags (shoulder tip pain, orthostatic symptoms, unilateral pain, previous PID). This is a SURGICAL EMERGENCY, not routine first trimester bleeding. If candidate suggests "arrange outpatient ultrasound" or "follow up with GP" ‚Üí patient could rupture and die. This is a patient safety CRITICAL FAILURE.

What happens: IMG candidates recognize "first trimester bleeding" but don't triage urgency. They think "ectopic is possible" but don't communicate that this requires IMMEDIATE O&G review. Australian OSCE expects you to demonstrate urgency when it's warranted.
‚úÖ CORRECT: When presenting your summary, use language that conveys URGENCY: "This is a 28-year-old woman with suspected ruptured ectopic pregnancy - this is a SURGICAL EMERGENCY requiring URGENT obstetric review within 30 minutes. I would call the O&G registrar immediately while starting resuscitation with two large-bore IV cannulae and fluids." Show you understand this is life-threatening.
‚ùå MISTAKE #9: Not Assessing Severity of Bleeding (Quantifying Pad Use)
Why this fails: "Vaginal bleeding" can mean anything from spotting (few drops) to torrential haemorrhage (soaking multiple pads per hour). Ectopic pregnancy typically has LIGHT bleeding (spotting), whereas inevitable/incomplete miscarriage has HEAVY bleeding. Not quantifying bleeding means you can't differentiate.

What happens: IMG candidates ask "How much bleeding?" and patient says "some bleeding" - candidate doesn't follow up with "How many pads have you used?" or "Are you soaking through pads?"
‚úÖ CORRECT: Quantify bleeding: "How many pads or panty liners have you used in the last 24 hours?" "Are you soaking through pads, or just spotting?" LIGHT bleeding (spotting, 1-2 pads/day) is MORE consistent with ectopic. HEAVY bleeding (>1 pad/hour) is MORE consistent with miscarriage.
‚ùå MISTAKE #10: Poor Empathy - Not Acknowledging Patient's Distress
Why this fails: This patient is losing her first pregnancy, is in pain, frightened, and potentially facing emergency surgery. If candidate launches into clinical questioning without acknowledging her distress ("I can see you're worried and in pain"), they fail the communication/empathy domain. Australian OSCE heavily weights empathy, especially in emotionally charged scenarios like pregnancy loss.

What happens: IMG candidates focus intensely on "getting the history right" and forget to connect with the patient emotionally. They ask rapid-fire questions without pausing to acknowledge the patient is crying or distressed.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Start with empathy: "I can see you're in pain and this is very worrying for you. I'm going to ask you some questions to work out what's happening, and we'll make sure you're safe and get the right treatment." Pause when patient is tearful. Offer tissue. Use phrases like "I'm sorry this is happening" and "We're going to take good care of you."

  2. ‚úÖ Ask about feeling faint/dizzy - orthostatic symptoms from blood loss
  3. ‚úÖ Ask if pain is ONE SIDE or BOTH SIDES - unilateral = ectopic
  4. ‚úÖ Ask about previous PID/chlamydia - MAJOR risk factor (6√ó risk)
  5. ‚úÖ Confirm pregnancy test done and result - don't assume
  6. ‚úÖ Ask if ultrasound scan done - IUP confirmed rules out ectopic
  7. ‚úÖ Ask about blood group (Rh status) - Anti-D needed if Rh negative
  8. ‚úÖ Quantify bleeding severity - light bleeding = ectopic, heavy = miscarriage

üéØ IMG Success Tips for First Trimester Bleeding Stations:

  • Memorize the ECTOPIC RED FLAGS: Shoulder tip pain, orthostatic symptoms, unilateral pain, previous PID, amenorrhoea 6-10 weeks. If ANY present ‚Üí ectopic until proven otherwise.
  • Practice saying "Have you had any shoulder pain?": This question alone can save a life. Make it automatic in your first trimester bleeding history.
  • Learn the typical presentations:
    • Ectopic: LIGHT bleeding + ONE-SIDED pain + shoulder pain/dizziness
    • Threatened miscarriage: LIGHT bleeding + central cramping + IUP on scan
    • Inevitable miscarriage: HEAVY bleeding + clots/tissue + open cervix
  • Know when to panic: Shoulder tip pain + orthostatic symptoms = ruptured ectopic = call O&G NOW, get IV access, prepare for emergency theatre. Show urgency in your body language and tone.
  • Don't forget Anti-D: If patient is Rh negative ‚Üí Anti-D within 72 hours. Mention this in your management plan.
  • Be systematic: Even in an emergency, use a structure: Menstrual history ‚Üí Bleeding history ‚Üí Pain history ‚Üí Red flags ‚Üí Risk factors ‚Üí Past history. This prevents you missing key questions.
  • Communicate urgency: Use words like "URGENT," "EMERGENCY," "IMMEDIATELY" when appropriate. Don't be wishy-washy - "This might need urgent review" is weak. Say "This requires URGENT O&G review within 30 minutes."
