  • üö® Anorexia: Complete loss of appetite (present in 95% of appendicitis cases)
  • üö® Peritonism: Pain worse with movement, coughing, car bumps

  • ‚ùå No diarrhoea (makes gastroenteritis less likely)
  • ‚ùå No urinary symptoms (makes UTI/pyelonephritis less likely)

  • ‚ùå No vaginal bleeding or discharge (makes PID less likely)

  • ‚ùå No previous episodes
  • ‚ùå No previous abdominal surgery

  1. Acute appendicitis (MOST LIKELY) üö®
    • Classical migration of pain (periumbilical ‚Üí RIF)

‚ö†Ô∏è IMG Common Mistakes - Critical Errors to Avoid

These 10 mistakes are commonly made by international medical graduates and will lead to failure in the AMC OSCE. Learn from these to avoid critical errors.

‚ùå MISTAKE #1: Not Asking About Last Menstrual Period (LMP) in Female Patient
Why this fails: In Australia, asking about LMP is MANDATORY for ALL females of reproductive age (roughly 12-55 years) presenting with abdominal pain. This is a CRITICAL FAILURE if omitted because ectopic pregnancy can be life-threatening and presents with abdominal pain.

What happens: IMG candidates often jump straight to surgical assessment without considering gynaecological causes. Australian examiners expect systematic gynae history in all females with abdominal pain.
‚úÖ CORRECT: "When was your last menstrual period?" and "Is there any chance you could be pregnant?" are NON-NEGOTIABLE questions. Ask these early in the history.
‚ùå MISTAKE #2: Not Asking About Anorexia (Loss of Appetite)
Why this fails: Anorexia is present in 95% of appendicitis cases and is one of the most important diagnostic features. Not asking about appetite/food intake is a critical omission.

What happens: IMG candidates often focus on pain and vomiting but forget to ask "Have you been able to eat?" or "How is your appetite?" This is a KEY discriminator between appendicitis and other causes.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask explicitly "How has your appetite been?" or "Have you felt like eating?" The answer "I haven't felt hungry at all" or "The thought of food makes me sick" is HIGHLY suggestive of appendicitis.
‚ùå MISTAKE #3: Not Recognizing Peritonism (Pain Worse with Movement)
Why this fails: Peritonism (peritoneal inflammation) is a red flag for surgical emergency. If the patient says "pain worse with coughing" or "lying very still," this indicates peritoneal irritation and requires urgent surgical review.

What happens: IMG candidates often don't ask the specific question "What makes the pain worse?" and miss the critical clue that movement/coughing exacerbates the pain.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask "What makes the pain worse?" and "What makes it better?" Listen for: worse with movement, coughing, car bumps, walking. This patient said "Even when the car went over speed bumps it was agony" - CLASSIC peritonism.
‚ùå MISTAKE #4: Not Asking About Pain Migration (Periumbilical ‚Üí RIF)
Why this fails: Classical appendicitis presents with pain starting AROUND THE BELLY BUTTON (periumbilical) which then MIGRATES to the right iliac fossa (RIF) over several hours. This migration is pathognomonic for appendicitis.

What happens: IMG candidates ask "Where is the pain?" but don't ask "Where did it start?" or "Has the pain moved?" Missing this migration pattern means missing the diagnosis.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask "Where exactly did the pain start?" and "Has it moved anywhere?" The answer "It started around my belly button and moved to the right lower side" is GOLD STANDARD for appendicitis.
‚ùå MISTAKE #5: Using American/British Terminology Instead of Australian
Why this fails: Australian medical culture uses specific terminology. Using American terms like "RLQ" (right lower quadrant) instead of "RIF" (right iliac fossa), or "NPO" instead of "NBM" (nil by mouth) marks you as unfamiliar with Australian practice.

What happens: While this alone won't fail you, accumulated terminology errors signal to examiners that you haven't adapted to Australian medical culture.
‚úÖ CORRECT Australian terms: "RIF" not "RLQ", "NBM" not "NPO", "theatre" not "OR", "paracetamol" not "acetaminophen", "ED" not "ER", "bloods" not "labs", "FBC" not "CBC".
‚ùå MISTAKE #6: Not Asking About Sequence of Symptoms (Pain Before Vomiting)
Why this fails: In appendicitis, PAIN comes BEFORE vomiting (95% of cases). If vomiting comes before pain, this suggests gastroenteritis, not appendicitis. The sequence matters diagnostically.

What happens: IMG candidates ask about vomiting but don't establish the timeline. "When did you start vomiting in relation to the pain?" is crucial.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask "Did the pain start before or after the vomiting?" In appendicitis: pain first (periumbilical), then anorexia, then nausea, then vomiting (several hours later).
‚ùå MISTAKE #7: Not Excluding Ectopic Pregnancy (Even if Patient Says "No Chance")
Why this fails: In Australian practice, ectopic pregnancy MUST be formally excluded with Œ≤hCG in ALL females of reproductive age with abdominal pain, regardless of contraception use, LMP, or patient's stated pregnancy risk.

What happens: IMG candidates take the patient's word ("I'm on the pill, I can't be pregnant") and don't mention Œ≤hCG testing. This is unsafe practice - contraception can fail, and ectopic pregnancy can be life-threatening.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Even though this patient is on OCP with normal LMP 2 weeks ago, your summary MUST include "We'll need to do a pregnancy test to be safe" or "Œ≤hCG to exclude pregnancy." This is NON-NEGOTIABLE in Australian EM/Surgical practice.
‚ùå MISTAKE #8: Not Asking About Diarrhoea (Key Negative Finding)
Why this fails: ABSENCE of diarrhoea is a key discriminator. Gastroenteritis (common differential) typically presents with diarrhoea + vomiting. Appendicitis does NOT cause diarrhoea. Not asking means you can't differentiate.

What happens: IMG candidates focus on positive findings (pain, vomiting) but forget that NEGATIVE findings (no diarrhoea, no urinary symptoms) are equally important for building a differential.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Ask "Have you had any diarrhoea?" The answer "No" helps you exclude gastroenteritis and supports appendicitis diagnosis. Always ask about bowel habits in abdominal pain.
‚ùå MISTAKE #9: Poor Time Management - Running Out of Time Before Completing Gynae History
Why this fails: IMG candidates often spend too long on pain history (asking repetitive questions about the same pain) and run out of time before asking about LMP, pregnancy, sexual history. This results in CRITICAL FAILURES.

What happens: You have 8 minutes. If you spend 5 minutes on pain alone, you won't have time for gynae history (which is MANDATORY). Structure is everything.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Time allocation for RIF pain in female: Introduction (30 sec) ‚Üí Pain SOCRATES (2-3 min) ‚Üí Associated GI symptoms (1 min) ‚Üí Gynae history INCLUDING LMP/pregnancy (1.5 min) ‚Üí PMHx/Meds/Allergies (1 min) ‚Üí ICE + Summary (1.5 min). Practice this structure.
‚ùå MISTAKE #10: Not Demonstrating Empathy for Patient in Acute Pain
Why this fails: This patient is in 7-8/10 pain and lying very still. If you launch into rapid-fire questions without acknowledging their discomfort ("I can see you're in a lot of pain, I'll be as quick as I can"), you'll fail the communication domain.

What happens: IMG candidates sometimes focus so hard on "getting the history right" that they forget the patient is suffering. Australian OSCE heavily weights empathy and communication.
‚úÖ CORRECT: Start with "I can see you're uncomfortable" or "I know this is painful, I'll try to be as quick as possible" or "Please let me know if you need a break." Show awareness of their distress throughout the consultation.
