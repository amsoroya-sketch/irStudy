Common Paediatric Presentations with Differential Diagnosis

Target Audience: International Medical Graduates (IMGs) preparing for AMC Clinical OSCE and ICRP NSW Junior Hospital Program paediatric rotations Learning Objectives: By the end of this document, you will be able to:
  1. Apply Dr. Amir Soufi's differential-driven approach to common paediatric presentations
  2. Recognize age-specific patterns for fever, respiratory distress, rashes, seizures, and failure to thrive
  3. Systematically assess severity and formulate management plans
  4. Identify üö® RED FLAGs requiring URGENT intervention or admission
  5. Understand Australian guidelines (APLS, RCH, eTG 2024) for common paediatric conditions
  6. Perform confident OSCE stations on paediatric emergencies and common presentations

Dr. Amir Soufi's Principle Applied to Paediatric Presentations

"In paediatrics, the SAME presentation has DIFFERENT causes at DIFFERENT ages. A febrile neonate is septic until proven otherwise. A febrile toddler with wheeze is bronchiolitis. A febrile school-age child with wheeze is asthma exacerbation. AGE is the most powerful discriminator in your differential. Think: What can cause THIS presentation at THIS age?"

The Presentation-Driven Mindset

Traditional Approach:
  • Child presents with fever
  • Take history, examine, investigate
  • At end, consider differential
  • Problem: Delayed recognition of life-threatening conditions, non-systematic approach

Dr. Amir's Differential-Driven Approach:
  1. Age + Presentation = Immediate Pattern Recognition

- Example: "6-month-old + wheeze + winter" ‚Üí IMMEDIATE thought: Bronchiolitis (RSV) - Example: "3-year-old + barking cough + stridor" ‚Üí IMMEDIATE thought: Croup - Example: "Neonate + jaundice day 2" ‚Üí IMMEDIATE thought: Physiological vs pathological?

  1. Severity Assessment FIRST (ABC)

- Before detailed history: Is this child critically unwell? - Airway: Patent? Stridor? Drooling? - Breathing: Respiratory rate? Recession? Saturations? - Circulation: Capillary refill? Heart rate? Blood pressure? - Disability: Alert? Responding? GCS? - Critically unwell ‚Üí Resuscitation + senior help NOW

  1. Let Age + Severity Guide Management

- Age <3 months + fever ‚Üí Admission for IV antibiotics (low threshold) - Age 6 months + bronchiolitis + saturations 94% ‚Üí Observe, likely discharge - Age 6 months + bronchiolitis + saturations 88% ‚Üí Admission for oxygen

FEVER WITHOUT FOCUS (No Obvious Source)

Definition: Fever ‚â•38¬∞C with no localizing signs on history or examination Age-Specific Management:

Neonate (<28 days) with Fever ‚â•38¬∞C

Dr. Amir's Principle: "EVERY febrile neonate is septic until proven otherwise. Do not wait. Admit immediately." Why?:
  • Immature immune system
  • Non-specific presentation (can deteriorate rapidly)
  • High risk of bacterial sepsis (GBS, E. coli, Listeria)

Management (ALL neonates with fever):
  1. Admit immediately to hospital
  2. Full septic screen:

- Blood culture (before antibiotics) - FBC, CRP - Urine MCS (catheter or suprapubic aspiration - NOT bag specimen) - Lumbar puncture (if stable) - CSF MCS, glucose, protein, cell count - CXR if respiratory signs

  1. IV antibiotics immediately (within 1 hour):

- Benzylpenicillin 60 mg/kg IV 12-hourly + Gentamicin 5 mg/kg IV daily - If suspected HSV (maternal HSV, vesicles, seizures): Add acyclovir 20 mg/kg IV 8-hourly

  1. Supportive care: IV fluids, oxygen, NICU/PICU admission
  2. Review cultures at 48-72 hours: If negative and clinically well, consider stopping antibiotics

Lumbar Puncture in Neonates:
  • Contraindications: Haemodynamically unstable, coagulopathy, thrombocytopenia, cardiorespiratory compromise
  • If contraindicated: Start antibiotics, defer LP until stable

Young Infant (1-3 months) with Fever ‚â•38¬∞C

Management (based on NICE Feverish Child Guidelines): Low-risk criteria (Rochester criteria):
  • Well-appearing, previously healthy, full-term
  • No focal infection on examination
  • WCC 5-15 √ó 10‚Åπ/L, normal urinalysis

If low-risk:
  • Urine MCS (catheter specimen)
  • Blood tests (FBC, CRP, blood culture)
  • Consider discharge with 24-hour review if:

- Parent reliable - Access to healthcare - No concerns

If high-risk OR any concerns:
  • Admit for IV antibiotics (same as neonate)
  • Full septic screen including LP

Common IMG Mistake: Discharging febrile infant <3 months without senior review. In Australia, low threshold for admission in this age group.

Older Infant/Child (>3 months) with Fever ‚â•38¬∞C

NICE Traffic Light System for Feverish Children:

| Risk | Features | Management | |------|----------|------------| | GREEN (Low Risk) | Alert, responsive, smiling, normal feeding, normal hydration, normal colour | Manage at home with safety-net advice, paracetamol/ibuprofen, return if worsens | | AMBER (Intermediate) | Age 3-6 months with fever ‚â•39¬∞C, reduced activity, dry mucous membranes, fever ‚â•5 days, nasal flaring | Clinical review within 2-4 hours, consider investigations (urine MCS, bloods), safety-net | | RED (High Risk) | Age <3 months with fever ‚â•38¬∞C, non-blanching rash, cyanosis, severe respiratory distress, reduced consciousness, bile-stained vomiting, bulging fontanelle, neck stiffness, seizure | URGENT hospital admission, investigations, IV antibiotics if sepsis suspected |

Investigations for Fever Without Focus (age >3 months):
  • Urine MCS (catheter or clean catch): UTI is common occult source in infants/toddlers
  • Blood tests (if unwell or high-risk): FBC, CRP, blood culture
  • CXR (if respiratory signs): Pneumonia
  • Stool MCS (if diarrhea): Gastroenteritis

Empirical Antibiotics (if sepsis suspected):
  • Ceftriaxone 50 mg/kg IV (max 2 g) daily OR
  • Cefotaxime 50 mg/kg IV 6-8 hourly
  • If meningitis suspected: Add vancomycin (MRSA cover)

KAWASAKI DISEASE

Dr. Amir's Principle: "Fever >5 days in a child? ALWAYS think Kawasaki disease. It's a clinical diagnosis - don't wait for investigations. Early IVIG prevents coronary artery aneurysms." Diagnostic Criteria (requires ALL):
  • Fever ‚â•5 days (essential)
  • PLUS 4 of 5 following:

1. Bilateral non-purulent conjunctivitis (limbic sparing) 2. Mucositis: Strawberry tongue, cracked lips, pharyngeal injection 3. Cervical lymphadenopathy (‚â•1.5 cm, usually unilateral) 4. Polymorphous rash (maculopapular, erythematous, NO vesicles) 5. Extremity changes: Erythema/oedema of hands/feet (acute), desquamation of fingertips (convalescent, 2-3 weeks)

Investigations:
  • Blood: FBC (‚Üë platelets after day 7, thrombocytosis 500-1000), ‚Üë CRP, ‚Üë ESR, ‚Üì albumin, ‚Üë WCC
  • Echocardiogram: Baseline (coronary artery assessment), repeat at 2 weeks and 6-8 weeks
  • Urine: Sterile pyuria (WBCs without bacteria)

Management:
  1. IVIG (intravenous immunoglobulin) 2 g/kg over 12 hours (within 10 days of fever onset to prevent coronary artery aneurysms)
  2. High-dose aspirin 30-50 mg/kg/day (divided QID) until fever settles for 48 hours, THEN low-dose aspirin 3-5 mg/kg/day for 6-8 weeks (antiplatelet)
  3. Cardiology follow-up: Echo at 2 weeks, 6-8 weeks
  4. Admit to paediatric ward

Complications (if untreated):
  • Coronary artery aneurysms (25% if untreated, <5% if treated with IVIG)
  • Myocardial infarction, arrhythmias, myocarditis

Common IMG Mistake: Not considering Kawasaki disease in prolonged fever. Australian guideline: ANY child with fever >5 days should be screened for Kawasaki disease.

BRONCHIOLITIS

Dr. Amir's Principle: "Infant <12 months + wheeze + winter = Bronchiolitis until proven otherwise. It's a clinical diagnosis. No CXR needed unless atypical. Management is SUPPORTIVE - no antibiotics, no salbutamol, no steroids." Epidemiology:
  • Peak age: 3-6 months
  • Peak season: Winter (May-September in Australia)
  • Causative organism: RSV (Respiratory Syncytial Virus) in 80%

Clinical Features:
  • Prodrome: Coryzal symptoms (runny nose, cough) for 1-2 days
  • Progressive: Wheeze, crackles, feeding difficulty, respiratory distress
  • Examination: Wheeze, fine crackles (bilateral), subcostal/intercostal recession, tachypnoea
  • Apnoea: Risk in very young infants (<6 weeks), ex-prems

Differential Diagnosis:
  • Viral-induced wheeze / first episode wheeze
  • Pneumonia (focal signs, high fever, unilateral crackles)
  • Asthma (rare <12 months, consider if atopic history, response to salbutamol)
  • Heart failure (structural heart disease, hepatomegaly, poor feeding from birth)
  • Foreign body aspiration (sudden onset, unilateral wheeze, choking episode)

Investigations:
  • NONE needed if typical presentation
  • Consider CXR if: Atypical (focal signs, persistent high fever), severe distress, O‚ÇÇ sats not improving
  • Nasopharyngeal aspirate (NPA): RSV PCR (if needed for cohorting in hospital, NOT for diagnosis)
  • Blood tests: NOT routinely needed

Management: Mild (Majority - 90%):
  • Supportive care at home:

- Ensure adequate hydration (smaller, more frequent feeds) - Upright positioning - Paracetamol for fever/discomfort - Nasal saline drops (clear secretions)

  • Safety-net advice:

- Return if: Respiratory distress worsens, unable to feed, fewer wet nappies, drowsy, apnoea

Moderate-Severe (Admission Criteria):
  • Age <3 months (high risk of apnoea)
  • O‚ÇÇ saturations <92% in air
  • Severe respiratory distress (grunting, severe recession, RR >60-70)
  • Feeding <50% of normal intake or clinical dehydration
  • Apnoea (witnessed or reported)
  • Pre-existing condition (chronic lung disease, congenital heart disease, immunodeficiency)
  • Social concerns (parent unable to cope, distance from hospital)

Hospital Management:
  1. Oxygen: If sats <92%, aim for ‚â•92% (humidified, via nasal prongs or head box)
  2. Fluids: NG feeds or IV fluids if unable to feed
  3. Monitoring: Continuous pulse oximetry, apnoea monitoring if <6 weeks
  4. Supportive: Nasal suction (if needed), upright positioning

What NOT to do (No evidence of benefit):
  • ‚ùå Antibiotics (unless secondary bacterial pneumonia)
  • ‚ùå Salbutamol (ineffective in bronchiolitis)
  • ‚ùå Steroids (ineffective)
  • ‚ùå Adrenaline nebulizers (no benefit)
  • ‚ùå Hypertonic saline (controversial, not routine in Australia)

Natural History:
  • Peak severity: Day 3-5
  • Total duration: 7-10 days (cough may persist 3-4 weeks)

Common IMG Mistake: Prescribing salbutamol or antibiotics for bronchiolitis. Australian guidelines: Supportive care ONLY. If child improves with salbutamol, diagnosis is viral-induced wheeze, NOT bronchiolitis.

CROUP (Laryngotracheobronchitis)

Dr. Amir's Principle: "Barking cough + inspiratory stridor + age 6 months to 3 years = Croup. Give dexamethasone (single dose) to ALL children - it reduces admission rates. If severe stridor at rest, give nebulized adrenaline and observe." Epidemiology:
  • Age: 6 months - 3 years (peak 2 years)
  • Cause: Parainfluenza virus (most common), RSV, adenovirus
  • Season: Autumn/winter

Clinical Features:
  • Prodrome: URTI symptoms for 1-2 days
  • Classic triad:

1. Barking cough ("seal-like") 2. Inspiratory stridor 3. Hoarse voice

  • Worse at night: Symptoms often peak at night
  • Examination: Stridor (inspiratory), barking cough, NO drooling, child not toxic-appearing

Differential Diagnosis:
  • Epiglottitis (RARE post-Hib vaccine): Drooling, sitting forward, high fever, toxic appearance, DO NOT examine throat
  • Bacterial tracheitis (RARE): High fever, toxic, purulent secretions, may need intubation
  • Foreign body: Sudden onset, unilateral wheeze, witnessed choking
  • Anaphylaxis: Sudden onset, urticaria, angioedema, exposure history

Severity Assessment (Westley Croup Score - simplified):

| Severity | Stridor | Recession | Air Entry | Consciousness | |----------|---------|-----------|-----------|---------------| | Mild | None at rest (only when agitated) | None/mild | Normal | Normal | | Moderate | At rest | Moderate | Reduced | Normal | | Severe | At rest, loud | Severe | Markedly reduced | Agitated/drowsy |

Investigations:
  • NONE needed (clinical diagnosis)
  • Do NOT order: CXR (not helpful, "steeple sign" insensitive), lateral neck X-ray (delays treatment)

Management: ALL children with croup:
  • Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg PO (max 8 mg) SINGLE DOSE

- Reduces admission rates, symptom duration - Given even for mild croup - Alternatives: Prednisolone 1 mg/kg PO OR budesonide 2 mg nebulized (if vomiting)

Mild croup (no stridor at rest):
  • Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg PO
  • Discharge home with safety-net advice
  • Return if: Stridor at rest, unable to drink, drowsy, increased work of breathing

Moderate-Severe croup (stridor at rest):
  • Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg PO
  • Nebulized adrenaline (1:1000) 5 mL nebulized with oxygen

- Rapid onset (within 10 minutes), lasts 2 hours - Observe for 2-4 hours (rebound possible)

  • If improves and stable after 2-4 hours ‚Üí Discharge
  • If worsens or no improvement ‚Üí Admit

Severe/Life-Threatening (exhaustion, cyanosis, drowsy):
  • Senior help + anaesthetist
  • High-flow oxygen
  • Nebulized adrenaline
  • IV dexamethasone (if unable to take PO)
  • Prepare for intubation (if imminent respiratory failure)
  • PICU admission

Common IMG Mistake: Not giving dexamethasone to mild croup. Australian guideline: ALL children with croup should receive dexamethasone (reduces symptom duration and admission rates).

ASTHMA EXACERBATION

Dr. Amir's Principle: "School-age child + wheeze + triggers (viral URTI, exercise, allergen) = Asthma. Assess severity FIRST (mild, moderate, severe, life-threatening). Give salbutamol, ipratropium, steroids. If life-threatening, call for help and prepare for ICU." Asthma Triggers:
  • Viral URTI (most common)
  • Allergens (pollen, dust mite, pets)
  • Exercise
  • Cold air
  • Smoke exposure

Severity Assessment (APLS - Advanced Paediatric Life Support):

| Feature | Mild-Moderate | Severe | Life-Threatening | |---------|---------------|--------|------------------| | Ability to speak | Sentences | Words only | Unable to speak | | Respiratory rate | <30/min (>5 years) | >30/min (>5 years) | Fatigue, exhaustion | | Heart rate | <120 bpm (>5 years) | >120 bpm (>5 years) | Bradycardia | | O‚ÇÇ saturations | ‚â•92% | <92% | <90% | | Wheeze | Moderate | Loud, throughout chest | Silent chest | | Peak flow | >50% predicted | 33-50% predicted | <33% predicted | | Consciousness | Alert | Agitated | Drowsy, confused, unconscious |

Silent chest: Severely reduced air entry due to poor air movement ‚Üí LIFE-THREATENING Investigations:
  • Peak expiratory flow rate (PEFR): If child >5 years and cooperative (% of predicted)
  • O‚ÇÇ saturations: Continuous monitoring
  • CXR: NOT routine (only if pneumothorax suspected, severe/atypical)
  • Blood gas: If life-threatening (normal/high CO‚ÇÇ = respiratory failure)

Management (Australian Asthma Handbook 2024): Mild-Moderate Exacerbation:
  1. Salbutamol (Œ≤‚ÇÇ-agonist):

- MDI + spacer (preferred in ED): 6 puffs (600 mcg), repeat every 20 minutes PRN - Nebulized (if severe): 2.5-5 mg nebulized with oxygen, repeat every 20 minutes

  1. Ipratropium bromide (anticholinergic):

- Add if moderate-severe: 250 mcg nebulized with salbutamol (or 8 puffs via MDI + spacer)

  1. Oral steroids:

- Prednisolone 1 mg/kg PO (max 50 mg) OR - Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg PO (max 16 mg) - single dose, alternative if vomiting

  1. Oxygen: If sats <92%, aim for ‚â•94%

If improves after 1 hour:
  • Discharge home with:

- Salbutamol MDI + spacer (continue 4-6 hourly for 24-48 hours) - Oral steroids (3-5 day course) - Asthma action plan - GP follow-up in 2-7 days

Severe Exacerbation:
  1. Call senior help
  2. High-flow oxygen (via face mask, aim sats ‚â•94%)
  3. Continuous nebulized salbutamol (2.5-5 mg continuously or back-to-back)
  4. Ipratropium bromide 250 mcg nebulized (with salbutamol)
  5. IV hydrocortisone 4 mg/kg (max 100 mg) if unable to take PO, OR oral prednisolone 1 mg/kg
  6. Magnesium sulfate 40 mg/kg IV over 20 minutes (max 2 g) - bronchodilator
  7. Admit to hospital (high-dependency unit or PICU if life-threatening)

Life-Threatening Exacerbation:
  1. IMMEDIATE senior help + anaesthetist
  2. High-flow oxygen 100%
  3. Continuous nebulized salbutamol + ipratropium
  4. IV hydrocortisone 4 mg/kg
  5. IV magnesium sulfate 40 mg/kg over 20 minutes
  6. IV salbutamol infusion (if not responding)
  7. Consider: IV aminophylline (loading dose 5 mg/kg over 20 min, then infusion)
  8. Prepare for intubation (if deteriorating, rising CO‚ÇÇ, exhaustion)
  9. PICU admission

Common IMG Mistake: Not giving steroids early in moderate-severe asthma exacerbation. Australian guideline: Oral steroids should be given within 1 hour for moderate-severe exacerbations (reduces admission, speeds recovery).

3. GASTROENTERITIS AND DEHYDRATION

Dr. Amir's Principle: "Gastroenteritis kills through dehydration, not infection. Assess hydration status FIRST. Oral rehydration is first-line. Only use IV if severe dehydration or unable to tolerate oral. Ondansetron helps oral rehydration." Definition: Vomiting and/or diarrhea, usually viral (rotavirus, norovirus, adenovirus) Causes by Age:
  • Infant: Rotavirus (less common post-vaccination), norovirus, bacterial (Salmonella, Campylobacter if bloody)
  • Older child: Norovirus, bacterial (if foreign travel, bloody diarrhea)

DEHYDRATION ASSESSMENT (CRITICAL)

Clinical Assessment of Dehydration (WHO):

| Clinical Sign | No Dehydration | Some Dehydration (3-5%) | Severe Dehydration (‚â•5%) | |---------------|----------------|------------------------|-------------------------| | Appearance | Alert, well | Restless, irritable, sunken eyes | Lethargic, floppy, drowsy, very sunken eyes | | Thirst | Drinks normally | Eager to drink, thirsty | Unable to drink | | Skin turgor | Normal | Reduced (skin slowly returns) | Skin tenting (skin very slowly returns) | | Mucous membranes | Moist | Dry | Very dry | | Tears | Present | Reduced | Absent | | Urine output | Normal | Reduced | Minimal/none | | Capillary refill | <2 sec | 2-3 sec | >3 sec | | Heart rate | Normal | Tachycardia | Marked tachycardia | | Fontanelle (if infant) | Normal | Sunken | Very sunken |

CRITICAL ASSESSMENT:
  • Sunken eyes = Moderate dehydration (3-5%)
  • Skin tenting = Severe dehydration (‚â•5%)
  • Reduced consciousness = Severe dehydration, shock

Common IMG Mistake: Assessing dehydration by urine output alone. Australian guideline: Use multiple clinical signs (eyes, skin turgor, mucous membranes, behaviour) to assess dehydration.

MANAGEMENT OF GASTROENTERITIS

Principles:
  1. Assess dehydration (no, some, severe)
  2. Oral rehydration first-line (unless severe dehydration or shocked)
  3. Ondansetron facilitates oral rehydration
  4. No antibiotics (unless specific indication)
  5. No anti-motility agents (loperamide contraindicated in children)

NO DEHYDRATION

Management:
  • Continue normal diet (including milk - do NOT withhold)
  • Increase fluids: Encourage oral fluids (water, breastmilk, formula)
  • Oral rehydration solution (ORS): Gastrolyte, Hydralyte (optional, not essential if tolerating normal fluids)
  • Antiemetics: Consider ondansetron if vomiting prevents oral intake (see below)
  • Safety-net advice:

- Return if: Increased vomiting, bloody diarrhea, reduced urine output, drowsy, sunken eyes, unable to keep fluids down

Discharge home

SOME DEHYDRATION (3-5%)

Management:
  • Oral rehydration therapy (ORT):

- Gastrolyte or Hydralyte (oral rehydration solution) - Give 50 mL/kg over 4 hours (in addition to usual fluids) - Example: 10 kg child ‚Üí 500 mL over 4 hours (125 mL/hour) - Frequent small sips (5 mL every 5 minutes, gradually increase)

  • Ondansetron (if vomiting):

- Dose: Weight-based - 8-15 kg: 2 mg PO (wafer or oral solution) - 15-30 kg: 4 mg PO - >30 kg: 8 mg PO - Single dose, repeat once after 8 hours if needed - Evidence: Reduces vomiting, increases success of oral rehydration, reduces IV fluid use and admission

  • Reassess after 4 hours:

- If improved (alert, drinking, passing urine) ‚Üí Continue ORT at home, normal diet - If not improving or worsening ‚Üí Consider IV fluids, admission

Common IMG Mistake: Not using ondansetron in vomiting child. Australian guideline (RCH): Ondansetron is recommended for vomiting children to facilitate oral rehydration (reduces admission rates).

SEVERE DEHYDRATION (‚â•5%) OR SHOCKED

Management:
  • URGENT: This is a medical ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY
  • IV fluid resuscitation:

- 0.9% saline 20 mL/kg bolus over 10-20 minutes - Reassess, repeat if needed (up to 60 mL/kg total) - Once stable, maintenance IV fluids + deficit replacement over 24-48 hours

  • Senior review
  • Investigations:

- Blood: U&E (hypo/hypernatraemia?), glucose, VBG (if shocked) - Urine: MCS (if fever, rule out UTI) - Stool: MCS (if bloody diarrhea, if needed for public health notification)

  • Admit to hospital

Maintenance IV Fluids (4-2-1 rule):
  • 4 mL/kg/hr for first 10 kg
  • 2 mL/kg/hr for next 10 kg
  • 1 mL/kg/hr for each kg over 20 kg
  • Example: 15 kg child ‚Üí (4√ó10) + (2√ó5) = 40 + 10 = 50 mL/hr maintenance

üö® RED FLAGS in Gastroenteritis

Admit if:
  • Severe dehydration or shock
  • Unable to tolerate oral fluids despite ondansetron
  • üö® RED FLAG symptoms:

- Bile-stained (green) vomiting ‚Üí Bowel obstruction/malrotation - Bloody diarrhea + severe abdominal pain ‚Üí Intussusception, HUS - Abdominal distension + not passing stool ‚Üí Obstruction - Drowsy, reduced consciousness ‚Üí Severe dehydration, shock - Age <3 months ‚Üí Low threshold for admission - Immunocompromised ‚Üí Risk of severe infection

4. RASHES IN CHILDREN

Dr. Amir's Principle: "The FIRST question for ANY rash: Does it blanch? Non-blanching = meningococcal septicaemia until proven otherwise. Give IM/IV benzylpenicillin and blue-light to hospital IMMEDIATELY."

GLASS TEST (Blanching vs Non-Blanching)

How to perform:
  1. Press a clear glass firmly against the rash
  2. Observe:

- Blanches (disappears with pressure) = Capillaries compressed, blood flow stopped temporarily ‚Üí SAFE - Non-blanching (stays visible with pressure) = Blood leaked into skin (purpura/petechiae) ‚Üí DANGEROUS

NON-BLANCHING RASH = ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY

Meningococcal Septicaemia:
  • Causative organism: Neisseria meningitidis
  • Presentation: Fever, non-blanching petechial/purpuric rash, headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, vomiting, drowsiness, rapid deterioration
  • Rash: Starts as blanching (erythematous, maculopapular), progresses to non-blanching petechiae, then purpura (larger >2 mm)
  • Distribution: Starts on peripheries (hands, feet), spreads centrally

IMMEDIATE MANAGEMENT (pre-hospital):
  1. IM/IV benzylpenicillin IMMEDIATELY (do NOT wait for hospital):

- <1 year: 300 mg - 1-9 years: 600 mg - ‚â•10 years: 1200 mg

  1. Call ambulance (blue light to hospital)
  2. Do NOT delay for LP or investigations

Hospital Management:
  1. Paediatric Sepsis 6 (within 1 hour):

- High-flow oxygen - IV/IO access, bloods (FBC, CRP, lactate, blood cultures, coagulation) - IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone 50 mg/kg) - IV fluid resuscitation (20 mL/kg bolus, repeat PRN) - Senior clinician involvement - Consider inotropes (if shock despite fluids)

  1. PICU admission
  2. LP (if stable, often deferred if shocked/coagulopathy)
  3. Public health notification (contact tracing, prophylaxis for close contacts)

Complications:
  • Death (10%)
  • Limb loss (ischaemic necrosis from DIC)
  • Multi-organ failure
  • Hearing loss, neurological sequelae (if meningitis)

Prophylaxis for Close Contacts:
  • Ciprofloxacin (single dose) OR rifampicin (2 days)
  • Household contacts, kissing contacts

BLANCHING RASHES (Viral Exanthems)

MANAGEMENT OF VIRAL EXANTHEMS

General Principles:
  • Most are self-limiting (supportive care only)
  • Paracetamol/ibuprofen for fever/discomfort
  • Hydration
  • Isolate (prevent spread to others)
  • Safety-net: Return if non-blanching rash, respiratory distress, drowsy, unable to drink

Measles:
  • Notifiable disease (public health notification)
  • Supportive care
  • Vitamin A supplementation (if malnourished, immunocompromised)
  • Isolate (highly contagious)
  • Contact tracing

Scarlet fever:
  • Antibiotic: Penicillin V 250-500 mg PO TDS for 10 days (or azithromycin if allergic)
  • Notifiable (in some states)
  • Prevents rheumatic fever

Chickenpox:
  • Supportive: Paracetamol (NOT ibuprofen - may increase necrotizing fasciitis risk), calamine lotion, cool baths
  • Antihistamines: Chlorphenamine (if very itchy)
  • Acyclovir: If immunocompromised, neonatal, severe
  • Isolate: Until all lesions crusted (5-7 days)

5. SEIZURES IN CHILDREN

Dr. Amir's Principle: "First seizure in a febrile child aged 6 months to 5 years = Febrile convulsion (most common). But ALWAYS rule out meningitis first. If afebrile seizure, think epilepsy, head injury, hypoglycaemia, or metabolic cause."

FEBRILE CONVULSION

Definition: Seizure occurring in child aged 6 months to 5 years (peak 12-18 months) associated with fever (‚â•38¬∞C), with NO CNS infection or other cause Epidemiology:
  • 3-5% of children have at least one febrile convulsion
  • Peak age: 12-18 months
  • Family history common (genetic predisposition)

Types:

| Type | Features | Recurrence Risk | Risk of Epilepsy | |------|----------|----------------|------------------| | Simple febrile convulsion (80%) | Generalized tonic-clonic, <15 minutes, single episode in 24 hours, no focal features, rapid recovery | 30-40% | 1-2% (same as general population) | | Complex febrile convulsion (20%) | Focal features, >15 minutes, multiple episodes in 24 hours, or prolonged post-ictal phase | Higher | 4-12% |

Differential Diagnosis (ALWAYS consider):
  1. Meningitis/Encephalitis: Fever + seizure + neck stiffness/drowsiness/petechial rash
  2. Epilepsy: Afebrile seizure, recurrent seizures
  3. Hypoglycaemia: BGL <3 mmol/L (check immediately)
  4. Electrolyte imbalance: Hypo/hypernatraemia (recent diarrhea/vomiting?)
  5. Head injury: Recent trauma
  6. Poisoning: Accidental ingestion

MANAGEMENT OF FEBRILE CONVULSION

During Seizure (if witnessed):
  1. Protect child: Move from danger, cushion head, do NOT restrain
  2. Airway: Place in recovery position (if possible), do NOT put anything in mouth
  3. Time seizure: Note start time
  4. Oxygen: If available
  5. If seizure >5 minutes:

- Give buccal midazolam 0.5 mg/kg (or rectal diazepam 0.5 mg/kg) - Call ambulance

Post-Seizure (in ED/GP): History:
  • Seizure description (generalized vs focal, duration, single vs multiple, post-ictal recovery)
  • Fever onset and source
  • Recent illness, contacts
  • Developmental history
  • Family history of febrile convulsions or epilepsy

Examination:
  • Rule out meningitis: Neck stiffness, Kernig's/Brudzinski's signs, bulging fontanelle, petechial rash, drowsiness
  • Identify source of fever: ENT examination, chest, abdomen
  • Neurological examination: Tone, reflexes, GCS (should be normal after recovery)

Investigations (NOT routine for simple febrile convulsion):
  • NONE needed if:

- Simple febrile convulsion (generalized, <15 minutes, single) - Identifies source of fever - Child fully recovered - No meningism signs

  • Consider investigations if:

- Age <18 months: LP (to rule out meningitis - clinical signs unreliable at this age) - Meningism signs: LP, blood culture, FBC, CRP - Complex features: EEG, neuroimaging (CT/MRI brain) - Recurrent or prolonged: U&E, glucose, calcium, magnesium

Lumbar Puncture Indications:
  • Clinical signs of meningitis (neck stiffness, drowsy, petechiae)
  • Age <18 months (meningitis signs unreliable)
  • Prolonged post-ictal drowsiness
  • Complex febrile convulsion

Management:
  • Treat fever source (e.g., antibiotics for otitis media)
  • Antipyretics: Paracetamol 15 mg/kg, ibuprofen 10 mg/kg (for comfort, does NOT prevent recurrence)
  • Parental education:

- Febrile convulsions do NOT cause brain damage - Recurrence risk 30-40% (higher if <18 months, family history, low-grade fever) - What to do if seizure recurs (recovery position, time, call ambulance if >5 min) - No evidence that antipyretics prevent recurrence

  • Discharge (if simple febrile convulsion, well, no meningism)

Common IMG Mistake: Performing LP on every child with febrile convulsion. Australian guideline: LP NOT routine for simple febrile convulsion (only if meningism or age <18 months).

STATUS EPILEPTICUS

Definition: Continuous seizure >5 minutes OR recurrent seizures without recovery between episodes Management (APLS Protocol): 0-5 minutes:
  • Airway, breathing, circulation
  • High-flow oxygen
  • Protect from injury
  • Time seizure

5-10 minutes (if seizure continues):
  • Buccal midazolam 0.5 mg/kg (max 10 mg) OR
  • IV lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg (if IV access)

10-20 minutes (if seizure continues after first dose):
  • Repeat buccal midazolam 0.5 mg/kg OR IV lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg
  • Call senior help
  • Check BGL (give IV dextrose if hypoglycaemic)

20-40 minutes (if seizure continues after second dose):
  • IV phenytoin 20 mg/kg over 20 minutes (cardiac monitoring) OR
  • IV levetiracetam 40 mg/kg over 5 minutes
  • Prepare for PICU transfer

>40 minutes (refractory status):
  • Intubation and ventilation
  • Thiopentone or midazolam infusion
  • PICU admission

6. FAILURE TO THRIVE (FTT)

Dr. Amir's Principle: "Failure to thrive is NOT a diagnosis - it's a symptom. The differential is HUGE: inadequate intake (poverty, neglect, feeding difficulty), inadequate absorption (coeliac, CF), or increased losses (chronic diarrhea, diabetes). Plot on growth chart. If crossing centiles downward = organic cause more likely." Definition: Inadequate weight gain or weight loss in a child, resulting in weight crossing 2 or more centile lines downward on growth chart Classification:
  1. Organic (30%): Medical cause (GI, cardiac, renal, metabolic, chromosomal)
  2. Non-organic (50%): Psychosocial (inadequate feeding, poverty, neglect, maternal depression)
  3. Mixed (20%): Both organic and non-organic

DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS OF FAILURE TO THRIVE

| Category | Examples | |----------|----------| | Inadequate Intake | Poverty/food insecurity, neglect, incorrect formula preparation, breastfeeding difficulty, oral-motor dysfunction (cerebral palsy), cleft palate | | Inadequate Absorption | Coeliac disease, cystic fibrosis, cow's milk protein allergy, chronic diarrhea, short gut syndrome | | Increased Losses | Chronic diarrhea, vomiting (GORD, pyloric stenosis), diabetes (polyuria) | | Increased Metabolic Demand | Congenital heart disease (high energy expenditure), chronic lung disease, hyperthyroidism, malignancy | | Chromosomal/Genetic | Down syndrome, Turner syndrome, Noonan syndrome, Prader-Willi syndrome | | Chronic Infection | HIV, TB, recurrent UTIs |

ASSESSMENT OF FAILURE TO THRIVE

History:
  • Feeding history (DETAILED):

- Breastfed or formula? If formula: type, preparation (dilution?), volume, frequency - Solids introduced? Types, frequency, acceptance - 24-hour dietary recall: "Tell me everything [child] ate and drank yesterday from wake-up to bedtime" - Feeding difficulties: Choking, gagging, vomiting, refusing food

  • GI symptoms: Vomiting, diarrhea, constipation, abdominal pain
  • Respiratory: Chronic cough, wheeze, recurrent infections (CF?)
  • Urinary: Polyuria, polydipsia (diabetes?)
  • Development: Milestones (global delay suggests chromosomal/genetic)
  • Birth history: Prematurity, birth weight, NICU stay
  • Family history: Coeliac, CF, short stature (familial?)
  • Social history: Who feeds child? Financial difficulties? Maternal mental health? Safeguarding concerns?

Examination:
  • Growth parameters:

- Plot on growth chart: Weight, height, head circumference (all 3!) - Calculate weight-for-length: Low in acute malnutrition - Mid-parental height: Expected adult height based on parents' heights

  • General: Wasted appearance, muscle bulk, subcutaneous fat, skin (dry, eczema)
  • Systemic: Cardiac (murmur?), respiratory (crackles?), abdominal (organomegaly, masses), dysmorphic features

Investigations (guided by history/examination):
  • Initial screen:

- FBC (anaemia?), iron studies - U&E, LFTs - Coeliac serology (anti-tTG IgA + total IgA) - Urine MCS (UTI?) - Stool MCS (chronic diarrhea)

  • Second-line (if indicated):

- Sweat test (CF if recurrent chest infections, steatorrhoea) - Thyroid function (hyperthyroidism, hypothyroidism) - Chromosome analysis (if dysmorphic) - HIV serology (if risk factors)

MANAGEMENT OF FAILURE TO THRIVE

Non-Organic (Psychosocial):
  • Multidisciplinary approach: Paediatrician, dietitian, social worker, community nurse
  • Increase caloric intake:

- Frequent small meals, high-calorie foods - Fortify feeds (add extra formula powder, butter, cream to foods)

  • Address social factors: Food insecurity, parental mental health, parenting support
  • Safeguarding: If neglect suspected, involve child protection
  • Regular follow-up: Weekly weight checks, growth monitoring

Organic:
  • Treat underlying cause:

- Coeliac disease ‚Üí Gluten-free diet - CF ‚Üí Pancreatic enzyme replacement, high-calorie diet - Heart failure ‚Üí Treat cardiac condition, fortified feeds

  • Nutritional support: High-calorie feeds, NG tube feeding (if severe)
  • Specialist referral: Gastroenterology, cardiology, endocrinology (as needed)

Admission Criteria:
  • Severe malnutrition (weight <3rd centile, wasted)
  • Safeguarding concerns
  • Failed outpatient management
  • Need for NG tube feeding initiation

7. NEONATAL JAUNDICE

Dr. Amir's Principle: "Jaundice in the first 24 hours is ALWAYS pathological (haemolysis, sepsis). Jaundice days 2-14 is usually physiological, but check bilirubin. Prolonged jaundice >14 days (term) or >21 days (preterm) needs investigation for biliary atresia (pale stools + dark urine = biliary obstruction)." Physiology: Neonatal jaundice due to:
  • Increased RBC breakdown (high haematocrit)
  • Immature liver (reduced conjugation)
  • Increased enterohepatic circulation

CLASSIFICATION BY TIMING

| Timing | Classification | Causes | Management | |--------|---------------|--------|------------| | <24 hours | PATHOLOGICAL (ALWAYS) | Haemolysis (ABO/Rh incompatibility, G6PD deficiency), sepsis, congenital infection | URGENT investigation (bilirubin, FBC, blood group, Coombs test, septic screen), phototherapy, exchange transfusion if severe | | 24 hours - 14 days | Physiological (most common) OR pathological | Physiological jaundice (peaks day 3-5), breastfeeding jaundice, sepsis, haemolysis | Check bilirubin level, plot on nomogram, phototherapy if indicated | | >14 days (term) or >21 days (preterm) | PROLONGED | Breast milk jaundice (benign), biliary atresia (surgical ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY), hypothyroidism, UTI, haemolysis | Investigate: Split bilirubin (conjugated vs unconjugated), LFTs, TFTs, urine MCS, USS liver. If conjugated >20%: URGENT surgical referral for biliary atresia |

CAUSES OF NEONATAL JAUNDICE

Unconjugated Hyperbilirubinaemia (most common):
  • Physiological jaundice (peaks day 3-5, resolves by day 14)
  • Breastfeeding jaundice (inadequate intake, dehydration)
  • Breast milk jaundice (substances in breast milk inhibit conjugation, benign, can last weeks)
  • Haemolysis (ABO/Rh incompatibility, G6PD deficiency, spherocytosis)
  • Polycythaemia (twin-twin transfusion, maternal diabetes)
  • Cephalhaematoma (blood reabsorption)
  • Sepsis
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Crigler-Najjar syndrome (rare)

Conjugated Hyperbilirubinaemia (ALWAYS pathological):
  • Biliary atresia (surgical ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY - Kasai procedure before 8 weeks for best outcome)
  • Neonatal hepatitis
  • Choledochal cyst
  • Congenital infection (TORCH: Toxoplasmosis, Rubella, CMV, HSV)
  • Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency
  • Galactosaemia

üö® RED FLAGS in Neonatal Jaundice

URGENT investigation/referral if:
  • Jaundice in first 24 hours (haemolysis, sepsis)
  • Jaundice >14 days (term) or >21 days (preterm) - rule out biliary atresia
  • Pale stools + dark urine = Conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia (biliary obstruction) ‚Üí URGENT surgical referral
  • Unwell baby (poor feeding, lethargy, fever, vomiting)
  • Bilirubin level in phototherapy or exchange transfusion range (use nomogram)

INVESTIGATIONS

Initial:
  • Serum bilirubin: Total bilirubin (transcutaneous or serum)
  • Plot on nomogram (Bhutani chart): Age in hours vs bilirubin level ‚Üí Determines need for phototherapy
  • FBC: Haemolysis (anaemia, reticulocytosis), sepsis (WCC)
  • Blood group + Coombs test (baby and mother): ABO/Rh incompatibility
  • G6PD level: If jaundice severe or ethnic background (Mediterranean, African, Asian)

If prolonged jaundice (>14 days):
  • Split bilirubin: Conjugated vs unconjugated

- If conjugated >20% of total ‚Üí Conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia ‚Üí URGENT surgical referral (biliary atresia)

  • LFTs, TFTs (hypothyroidism), urine MCS (UTI)
  • USS liver (biliary tree, choledochal cyst)
  • Stool colour (use stool colour chart - pale = biliary obstruction)

MANAGEMENT

Phototherapy:
  • Mechanism: Blue light (wavelength 420-470 nm) converts unconjugated bilirubin to water-soluble isomers (excreted in urine/stool without conjugation)
  • Indications: Based on Bhutani nomogram (age in hours + bilirubin level)
  • Method: Double phototherapy (lights above and below), eye protection, maximize skin exposure
  • Monitoring: Bilirubin every 4-6 hours (should fall), hydration, temperature
  • Stop when: Bilirubin falls below phototherapy threshold

Exchange Transfusion:
  • Indications: Bilirubin in exchange transfusion range (nomogram), acute bilirubin encephalopathy, failed phototherapy
  • Mechanism: Removes bilirubin and maternal antibodies (if haemolysis)
  • Procedure: Performed in NICU

Supportive:
  • Adequate hydration: Encourage breastfeeding, consider supplementation if dehydrated
  • Treat underlying cause: Antibiotics if sepsis

Common IMG Mistakes Across Paediatric Presentations

Mistake 1: Not Assessing Severity FIRST

Problem: Taking detailed history before recognizing critically unwell child Why It's Wrong:
  • Delays life-saving interventions
  • Child can deteriorate rapidly in paediatrics

Correct Approach:
  • ABCDE assessment FIRST for any acutely unwell child
  • Recognize sick child, call for help, stabilize BEFORE detailed history

Mistake 2: Not Using Age-Appropriate Differentials

Problem: Applying adult differential to paediatric presentations Why It's Wrong:
  • Misses common paediatric diagnoses (e.g., bronchiolitis in infant, febrile convulsion in toddler)

Correct Approach:
  • Age-based differentials from the start
  • Know age-specific common presentations

Mistake 3: Over-investigating Simple Febrile Convulsion

Problem: Ordering LP, EEG, neuroimaging for every febrile convulsion Why It's Wrong:
  • Causes unnecessary distress, cost
  • Australian guideline: Simple febrile convulsion needs NO investigations

Correct Approach:
  • Simple febrile convulsion (generalized, <15 min, single) = NO investigations (unless meningism or age <18 months)

Mistake 4: Prescribing Antibiotics/Salbutamol for Bronchiolitis

Problem: "This infant has wheeze, I'll give salbutamol and antibiotics" Why It's Wrong:
  • No evidence of benefit
  • Australian guideline: Supportive care ONLY for bronchiolitis

Correct Approach:
  • Bronchiolitis = Viral ‚Üí No antibiotics, no salbutamol, no steroids
  • If improves with salbutamol ‚Üí Diagnosis is viral-induced wheeze, NOT bronchiolitis

Mistake 5: Not Using Ondansetron for Gastroenteritis

Problem: "Vomiting child, I'll just tell parents to give oral fluids" Why It's Wrong:
  • Ondansetron facilitates oral rehydration, reduces admission
  • Australian guideline (RCH): Ondansetron recommended for vomiting

Correct Approach:
  • Vomiting preventing oral intake ‚Üí Give ondansetron (weight-based dose)

1. Severity Assessment FIRST (ABCDE)

  • Before detailed history: Is this child critically unwell?
  • Recognize sick child ‚Üí Call for help ‚Üí Stabilize

2. Age-Based Differential Thinking

  • Same presentation = Different diagnoses at different ages
  • Neonate with fever = Sepsis. Infant with wheeze = Bronchiolitis. School-age with wheeze = Asthma.

3. Fever Management

  • Neonate <28 days with fever ‚â•38¬∞C ‚Üí Admit for IV antibiotics (NO exceptions)

4. Respiratory Distress

  • Bronchiolitis = Supportive care ONLY (no antibiotics, no salbutamol)

  • Asthma = Salbutamol + steroids early

  • Sunken eyes + reduced skin turgor = Moderate dehydration (3-5%)
