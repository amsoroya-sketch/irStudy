Gynaecological Examination

ICRP OSCE Preparation - Obstetrics & Gynaecology Module

Following Dr. Amir Soufi's Differential-Driven Approach (IMG4IMGS Methodology)

Target Audience

  • IMGs preparing for AMC Clinical OSCE
  • ICRP participants at NSW Young Hospital Program
  • Medical students/residents rotating through gynaecology
  • Focus: 8-minute OSCE stations with systematic examination

Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  1. Perform systematic abdominal examination for gynaecological conditions
  2. Perform speculum examination correctly and identify cervical pathology
  3. Perform bimanual pelvic examination and interpret findings
  4. Recognize normal and abnormal findings (masses, tenderness, cervical changes)
  5. Maintain patient dignity and comfort during intimate examination
  6. Obtain appropriate consent and explain procedure
  7. Integrate examination findings with history to generate differential diagnosis
  8. Communicate findings sensitively to patient

Dr. Amir Soufi's Core Principle Applied to Gynaecological Examination

The Differential-Driven Examination Approach

Traditional Approach (WRONG for OSCE):
  1. Perform examination mechanically
  2. Record findings without interpretation
  3. Don't relate findings to presenting complaint

Dr. Amir's Approach (CORRECT for OSCE):
  1. Know the presenting complaint BEFORE examination:

- "Pelvic pain" ‚Üí Look specifically for adnexal tenderness (PID, ovarian cyst), uterine tenderness (adenomyosis), cervical excitation (ectopic, PID) - "Heavy periods" ‚Üí Look for enlarged uterus (fibroids), bulky tender uterus (adenomyosis) - "Vaginal discharge" ‚Üí Look carefully at cervix for cervicitis, discharge characteristics

  1. Your examination is TARGETED:

- Not robotic, but intelligent - Know what you're looking for based on differential

  1. Interpret findings in context:

- "Enlarged uterus + heavy periods" ‚Üí Fibroids or adenomyosis - "Cervical excitation + unilateral adnexal tenderness + missed period" ‚Üí Ectopic pregnancy - "Friable cervix + post-coital bleeding" ‚Üí Cervical ectropion vs cervical cancer

This focused, differential-driven examination is essential for OSCE success.

Part 1: General Principles

Consent and Chaperone

CRITICAL - Non-negotiable: Consent:
  • Explain what you will do and why
  • Obtain verbal consent (document in notes)
  • "I'd like to examine your tummy and perform an internal examination. This involves inserting a speculum - an instrument to look at your cervix - and then an examination with my fingers. Is that alright with you?"
  • Patient can decline or stop at any time

Chaperone:
  • ALWAYS offer chaperone for intimate examination (regardless of examiner gender)
  • "Would you like a female nurse to be present during the examination?"
  • Document if chaperone present or declined
  • In OSCE: State clearly "I would offer a chaperone"

In Australia:
  • Medical Board guidelines: Chaperone should be offered for breast, pelvic, rectal examinations
  • Mandatory in some settings (especially male doctor examining female patient)

Preparation

Before Examination:
  1. Empty bladder: "Can you empty your bladder before we start?"

- Full bladder makes examination uncomfortable and findings inaccurate

  1. Privacy: Close curtains, lock door, sign on door
  2. Equipment ready:

- Gloves (non-sterile) - Lubricant (water-based) - Speculums (small, medium, large - choose appropriate size) - Swabs (if taking cervical screening or infection swabs) - Light source (examination light or torch) - Tissues (for patient to clean afterward)

Part 2: Abdominal Examination (Gynaecological)

Purpose

Why perform abdominal examination in gynae patients?
  • Detect pelvic masses arising from pelvis (large ovarian cyst, fibroid uterus)
  • Assess for tenderness (peritonism, guarding)
  • Exclude pregnancy (gravid uterus)
  • Detect ascites (ovarian cancer)
  • Assess surgical scars (previous operations)

Positioning and Exposure

Patient Position:
  • Supine with one pillow under head
  • Arms by sides (not folded over abdomen)
  • Expose from xiphisternum to pubic symphysis
  • Maintain dignity (cover chest, keep legs covered until pelvic examination)

Inspection

Stand at end of bed and observe: Abdominal Shape:
  • Distension: Generalized (ascites, large ovarian cyst, pregnancy) vs localized (mass)
  • Asymmetry: Unilateral mass (ovarian cyst)

Scars:
  • Pfannenstiel (transverse suprapubic): Previous caesarean section, hysterectomy, ovarian cyst removal
  • Midline laparotomy: Previous bowel surgery, large gynae operation
  • Laparoscopy ports: Small scars at umbilicus + lower quadrants (diagnostic laparoscopy, sterilization, ectopic surgery)

Skin Changes:
  • Striae (pregnancy, weight gain)
  • Bruising (trauma, bleeding disorders)

Masses:
  • Visible mass rising from pelvis (large ovarian cyst >12cm, fibroid uterus)

Movements:
  • Visible peristalsis (bowel obstruction - not gynae)

Palpation

Light Palpation (all 4 quadrants):
  • Feel for tenderness

- Generalized tenderness + guarding: Peritonitis (ruptured ectopic, ruptured ovarian cyst, PID with peritonitis) - Localized tenderness: RLQ (appendicitis, ovarian pathology), LLQ (ovarian pathology, diverticulitis)

  • Feel for superficial masses

Deep Palpation:
  • Palpate for pelvic masses:

- Start at umbilicus, palpate downward toward pelvis - Bimanually (one hand abdominal, one hand pelvic during bimanual examination later) to assess mass

Characteristics of Pelvic Mass on Abdominal Examination:

| Characteristic | Findings | Interpretation | |-------------------|-------------|-------------------| | "Can't get below it" | Mass continuous with pelvis, no lower border palpable | Pelvic origin (ovarian, uterine, bladder) | | Can get below it | Can palpate lower border | Abdominal origin (not gynae) | | Mobility | Fixed vs mobile | Mobile = ovarian cyst, Fixed = malignancy with adhesions | | Consistency | Cystic (fluctuant) vs solid (firm/hard) | Cystic = ovarian cyst, Solid = fibroid | | Tender | Painful on palpation | Torsion, infection, endometrioma |

Percussion

Why Percuss:
  • Detect ascites (ovarian cancer, liver disease)
  • Distinguish cystic (dull) from solid/gas (resonant)

Technique:
  • Percuss all 4 quadrants
  • Dull over mass (fluid-filled or solid)
  • Shifting dullness: Ascites (percuss from midline to flanks in supine position, then turn patient on side and repercuss - dullness shifts)

Auscultation

Bowel Sounds:
  • Usually not crucial for gynae examination
  • Absent bowel sounds ‚Üí Ileus (post-operative, peritonitis)

Part 3: Speculum Examination

Purpose

  • Visualize cervix and vaginal walls
  • Take cervical screening test (CST)
  • Take swabs (endocervical for gonorrhoea/chlamydia, high vaginal for BV/candida/trichomonas)
  • Assess for discharge, bleeding, masses, prolapse
  • Identify cervical pathology (ectropion, polyp, cancer)

Equipment

Speculum Types: Cusco Speculum (Bivalve):
  • Two blades that open like duck's bill
  • Most common for cervical screening and visualization
  • Self-retaining (stays open)
  • Sizes: Small, medium, large

Sims Speculum (Single blade):
  • Used to assess for prolapse
  • Requires assistant or patient to hold
  • Not self-retaining

Which Size Speculum?:
  • Nulliparous, young: Small or medium Cusco
  • Parous, older: Medium or large Cusco
  • Postmenopausal, atrophic vagina: Small (use lubricant, be gentle)

Preparation

Explain to Patient:

"I'm going to insert a speculum - it's a plastic/metal instrument that opens gently inside your vagina so I can see your cervix. You might feel some pressure but it shouldn't be painful. Please let me know if you're uncomfortable and I'll stop. Try to relax your muscles as much as possible - it will be more comfortable."

Patient Position:
  • Modified lithotomy:

- Supine - Hips and knees flexed - Thighs abducted (legs apart) - Feet together or on leg supports - Bottom at edge of examination couch (for adequate access)

  • Cover patient with sheet over knees (maintain dignity)

Warm Speculum:
  • Run under warm water (not hot)
  • Or warm in hands

Lubricant:
  • If taking CST: Use warm water ONLY (lubricant interferes with cytology)
  • If not taking CST: Can use small amount of water-based lubricant on outside of blades

Technique

Inspect External Genitalia First:
  • Part labia gently with non-dominant hand
  • Look for:

- Ulcers, warts, lumps (HSV, HPV, VIN, cancer) - Discharge at introitus (BV, candida, trichomonas) - Prolapse (bulge from anterior/posterior vaginal wall) - Atrophy (thin, pale, dry mucosa - postmenopausal)

Insert Speculum: Step 1:
  • Hold speculum in dominant hand
  • Blades closed and vertical (handle toward patient's side, not toward ceiling)
  • Part labia with non-dominant hand
  • Insert gently at 45¬∞ angle (pointing posteriorly toward sacrum, not straight in)

- Avoids urethra (anterior) - Follows natural angle of vagina

Step 2:
  • Advance speculum gently to full length (usually 6-8cm)
  • Rotate 90¬∞ clockwise as you advance (blades now horizontal)

Step 3:
  • Open blades slowly
  • Cervix should appear between blades
  • If not visible: Partially close, adjust angle (often need to angle more posteriorly), reopen
  • Lock speculum in open position (lock mechanism on handle)

Common Problem: "Can't see cervix"
  • Too anterior: Angle speculum more posteriorly
  • Too posterior: Angle speculum more anteriorly
  • Not deep enough: Advance further
  • Cervix to side: Redirect laterally

Inspect Cervix

Normal Cervix Appearance:
  • Nulliparous: Small, round external os (dimple)
  • Parous: Wider, slit-like external os
  • Color: Pink (premenopausal), pale (postmenopausal)
  • Surface: Smooth
  • Discharge: Clear or white mucus

Abnormal Findings:

| Finding | Appearance | Differential Diagnosis | |-------------|---------------|---------------------------| | Ectropion (Cervical erosion) | Red area around os (columnar epithelium visible) | Normal variant, common in pregnancy/COCP, causes discharge/PCB | | Nabothian cysts | Small white/yellow bumps on cervix | Benign retention cysts, normal variant | | Cervical polyp | Red, grape-like mass protruding from os | Benign polyp (causes IMB/PCB), rarely malignant | | Contact bleeding (bleeds when touched) | Cervix bleeds with gentle touch | Ectropion, cervicitis, cervical cancer | | Friable, irregular cervix | Bleeds easily, irregular surface, mass | Cervical cancer (URGENT colposcopy) | | Purulent discharge from os | Yellow/green pus | Cervicitis (chlamydia, gonorrhoea - take swabs) | | Strawberry cervix (petechiae) | Red pinpoint spots | Trichomoniasis | | Cervical stenosis | Very small os, scarred | Previous LLETZ, cervical surgery | | Cervicitis | Red, inflamed, discharge | Infection (STI), ectropion |

CRITICAL üö® RED FLAG:
  • Irregular, friable cervix with contact bleeding = Cervical cancer until proven otherwise ‚Üí URGENT referral for colposcopy and biopsy

Inspect Vaginal Walls

As You Withdraw Speculum:
  • Slowly close blades partially (maintain gentle opening)
  • Slowly withdraw while inspecting vaginal walls

Look For:
  • Discharge: Thick white (candida), grey thin fishy (BV), frothy yellow (trichomonas), blood
  • Inflammation: Red, inflamed (vaginitis)
  • Ulcers, warts: HSV, HPV
  • Prolapse: Bulging of anterior wall (cystocele), posterior wall (rectocele), cervix descending (uterine prolapse)
  • Vaginal septum (congenital anomaly)

Remove Speculum

Technique:
  • Unlock speculum
  • Begin to close blades BEFORE fully withdrawn (avoid pinching cervix)
  • Rotate back to vertical as you withdraw
  • Remove gently

After Removal:
  • Offer tissues for patient to clean
  • Allow patient to dress in privacy
  • Discard disposable speculum or place metal speculum in decontamination

Taking Cervical Screening Test (CST)

If Indicated (age 25-74, due for 5-yearly screening): Equipment:
  • Broom (plastic brush) or cytobrush + spatula
  • Specimen container with liquid preservative
  • Label with patient details

Technique:
  1. Visualize cervix with speculum
  2. Insert brush into cervical os (central bristles in os)
  3. Rotate 360¬∞ clockwise 5 times (ensures adequate sampling from transformation zone)
  4. Withdraw brush
  5. Rinse in specimen container (break off brush head into container, or swirl vigorously)
  6. Label and send to laboratory

If Taking Swabs (for infections):
  • Endocervical swab (for chlamydia/gonorrhoea NAAT): Insert swab into os, rotate, withdraw
  • High vaginal swab (for BV/candida/trichomonas): Swab posterior vaginal fornix
  • Place in appropriate transport medium

Part 4: Bimanual Pelvic Examination

Purpose

  • Palpate uterus: Size, position, mobility, tenderness
  • Palpate adnexa (ovaries and fallopian tubes): Masses, tenderness
  • Assess cervical excitation (tenderness on moving cervix - suggests pelvic pathology)
  • Detect masses (ovarian cyst, fibroid)
  • Assess for prolapse, pelvic floor tone**

Preparation

Explain:

"Now I'm going to examine you with my fingers. I'll insert two fingers into your vagina and press on your tummy with my other hand. This allows me to feel your womb and ovaries. You might feel some pressure, but it shouldn't be painful unless there's something tender. Please let me know if you're uncomfortable."

Gloves:
  • Non-sterile gloves on both hands
  • Lubricate index and middle fingers of dominant hand (water-based lubricant)

Patient Position:
  • Same as speculum (modified lithotomy)
  • Relaxed (ask patient to take deep breath and relax pelvic muscles)

Technique

Step 1: Inspect External Genitalia (if not done during speculum)

  • Part labia, inspect vulva

Step 2: Insert Fingers

Fingers to Use:
  • Index and middle fingers of dominant hand (usually right hand)
  • Palm facing upward (fingers curved anteriorly)

Insertion:
  • Part labia with non-dominant hand
  • Insert two fingers gently into vagina
  • Angle posteriorly initially (45¬∞), then advance horizontally
  • Advance to full length (8-10cm)

Step 3: Assess Vagina

As You Insert:
  • Vaginal walls: Smooth vs nodular (endometriosis nodules)
  • Pelvic floor tone: Tight vs lax
  • Prolapse: Bulging walls (ask patient to cough - accentuates prolapse)

Step 4: Palpate Cervix

Locate Cervix:
  • Firm, round structure at apex of vagina (feels like "tip of nose")
  • May be anterior, central, or posterior depending on uterine position

Assess:
  • Cervical os: Open (parous) vs closed (nulliparous)
  • Consistency: Firm (normal) vs soft (pregnant - "Hegar's sign")
  • Tenderness: Gently move cervix side to side

Cervical Excitation (Chandelier Sign):
  • Gently move cervix from side to side with internal fingers
  • Positive cervical excitation = Patient experiences pain

- Differential: PID, ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, appendicitis, endometriosis - Called "chandelier sign" (patient jumps up to touch the chandelier - hyperbole for severe pain)

OSCE Statement:

"I'm now going to assess for cervical excitation by gently moving the cervix." [Move cervix gently side to side] "There is cervical excitation - the patient experiences pain when I move the cervix. This suggests pelvic pathology such as PID, ectopic pregnancy, or ovarian pathology."

Step 5: Bimanual Palpation of Uterus

Technique:
  • Internal hand (vaginal fingers): Place in anterior fornix (in front of cervix), push cervix posteriorly
  • External hand (abdominal): Place suprapubically, press downward and posteriorly toward internal hand
  • "Trap" uterus between two hands (bimanual palpation)

Assess: 1. Size:
  • Normal: ~7-8cm long (size of small pear)

- Nulliparous: Smaller - Parous: Slightly larger

  • Enlarged:

- Weeks of pregnancy size (e.g., "12-week size") ‚Üí Pregnancy, fibroids - Bulky ‚Üí Fibroids, adenomyosis

| Uterine Size | Interpretation | |-----------------|-------------------| | Normal (7-8cm) | Normal | | 8-12 weeks size | Early pregnancy, small fibroids, adenomyosis | | 12-20 weeks size | Pregnancy second trimester, large fibroids | | >20 weeks size | Pregnancy late second/third trimester, very large fibroids, rarely sarcoma |

2. Position:
  • Anteverted (normal): Uterus tilted anteriorly (toward bladder)

- Cervix points posteriorly - Uterus palpable through anterior abdominal wall

  • Retroverted (15-20% women): Uterus tilted posteriorly (toward rectum)

- Cervix points anteriorly - Uterus palpable through posterior fornix (may need rectal examination) - Normal variant (not pathological)

  • Axial (midline): Uterus vertical

3. Mobility:
  • Mobile (normal): Can move uterus freely
  • Fixed ‚Üí Adhesions (endometriosis, PID, malignancy)

4. Consistency:
  • Firm (normal)
  • Soft (pregnancy - Hegar's sign)
  • Irregular, nodular (fibroids)

5. Tenderness:
  • Non-tender (normal)
  • Tender ‚Üí Adenomyosis, PID, endometriosis, degenerating fibroid

Step 6: Palpate Adnexa (Ovaries and Tubes)

Technique:
  • Internal fingers: Move to lateral fornix (side of cervix)
  • External hand: Move to corresponding iliac fossa
  • Press downward with external hand, try to trap adnexal structures between hands
  • Repeat on both sides (right and left adnexa)

Normal Ovaries:
  • Difficult to palpate (small, mobile)
  • If felt: Small (3x2x1cm), almond-shaped, slightly tender
  • Postmenopausal: Should NOT be palpable (atrophied)

Abnormal Findings:

| Finding | Interpretation | Differential Diagnosis | |-------------|-------------------|---------------------------| | Palpable mass | Ovarian cyst, ectopic, hydrosalpinx | Postmenopausal palpable ovary = ovarian cancer until proven otherwise | | Tender mass | Ovarian cyst (especially if torsion), ectopic pregnancy, tubo-ovarian abscess (PID) | Needs ultrasound URGENTly | | Fixed mass | Malignancy (ovarian cancer with adhesions), endometrioma | Needs imaging | | Bilateral tenderness | PID | Clinical diagnosis + swabs | | Unilateral tenderness + mass | Ovarian torsion, ectopic, hemorrhagic cyst | Surgical ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY if torsion |

CRITICAL:
  • Postmenopausal palpable ovary = Ovarian cancer until proven otherwise ‚Üí URGENT ultrasound + CA125

Step 7: Assess Pouch of Douglas (POD)

Pouch of Douglas = Space between uterus and rectum (rectouterine pouch) Technique:
  • Internal fingers: Place in posterior fornix (behind cervix)
  • Feel for:

- Nodularity (endometriosis deposits) - Mass (ovarian cyst, ectopic, abscess) - Tenderness (PID, endometriosis, ectopic)

Fullness or Mass in POD:
  • Ectopic pregnancy (blood in POD)
  • Pelvic abscess (tubo-ovarian abscess)
  • Endometriosis (nodules)
  • Ovarian cyst (posterior)

Withdraw Fingers

  • Gently withdraw fingers
  • Offer tissues for patient to clean
  • Allow patient to dress in privacy
  • Dispose of gloves, wash hands

Integrating Examination with History

Part 6: Common IMG Mistakes

Mistake 1: Not Offering Chaperone

What IMGs Do Wrong:
  • Forget to offer chaperone
  • Think it's not needed if examiner is female

Correct Approach:
  • Always offer chaperone for intimate examination (regardless of examiner gender)
  • Document if declined
  • In OSCE: State clearly "I would offer a chaperone" even if simulated patient/mannequin

Why It Matters:
  • Medicolegal protection
  • Patient comfort
  • Professional standards (AHPRA, Medical Board)

Mistake 2: Not Explaining Procedure Before Examination

What IMGs Do Wrong:
  • Start examination without explaining
  • Assume patient knows what you're doing

Correct Approach:
  • Explain speculum and bimanual examination before starting
  • Use simple language, not medical jargon
  • "I'll insert a speculum to see your cervix, then examine with my fingers"
  • Obtain verbal consent: "Is that alright with you?"

Mistake 3: Inserting Speculum Horizontally (Wrong Angle)

What IMGs Do Wrong:
  • Insert speculum straight in (horizontally) ‚Üí Hits anterior vaginal wall/urethra ‚Üí Painful

Correct Approach:
  • Insert at 45¬∞ angle (pointing posteriorly toward sacrum)
  • Follows natural angle of vagina
  • Rotate to horizontal once inserted

Mistake 4: Not Warming Speculum

What IMGs Do Wrong:
  • Use cold metal speculum ‚Üí Very uncomfortable for patient

Correct Approach:
  • Warm speculum under warm water or in hands
  • Or use disposable plastic speculum (warmer than metal)

Mistake 5: Using Lubricant When Taking Cervical Screening Test

What IMGs Do Wrong:
  • Apply lubricant to speculum before CST ‚Üí Interferes with cytology result

Correct Approach:
  • If taking CST: Use warm water only (no lubricant)
  • If NOT taking CST: Can use small amount of water-based lubricant

Mistake 6: Not Assessing for Cervical Excitation

What IMGs Do Wrong:
  • Perform bimanual without testing cervical excitation
  • Miss critical finding in acute pelvic pain

Correct Approach:
  • Always assess cervical excitation in patient with pelvic pain
  • Gently move cervix side to side with vaginal fingers
  • Positive = pain = suggests PID, ectopic, torsion, appendicitis

Mistake 7: Trying to Palpate Normal Ovaries in Postmenopausal Woman

What IMGs Do Wrong:
  • State "I palpated normal ovaries" in 65-year-old woman

Correct Approach:
  • Postmenopausal ovaries should NOT be palpable (atrophied)
  • If palpable = ovarian cancer until proven otherwise
  • Correct statement: "The adnexa were not palpable, which is normal for postmenopausal women"

Mistake 8: Not Recognizing üö® RED FLAGs

What IMGs Do Wrong:
  • See irregular cervix ‚Üí Don't recognize as potential cancer
  • Palpate postmenopausal ovary ‚Üí Don't recognize urgency

Correct Approach: üö® RED FLAGS - Recognize and Act:
  • Irregular, friable cervix with contact bleeding ‚Üí Cervical cancer ‚Üí URGENT colposcopy
  • Postmenopausal palpable ovary ‚Üí Ovarian cancer ‚Üí URGENT ultrasound + CA125
  • Cervical excitation + missed period ‚Üí Ectopic pregnancy ‚Üí URGENT Œ≤hCG + ultrasound
  • Unilateral tender mass ‚Üí Ovarian torsion ‚Üí Surgical ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY

Part 7: OSCE Practice Station

OSCE Station: Gynaecological Examination - Pelvic Pain

Candidate Instructions:

"Ms. Thompson is a 25-year-old woman presenting with lower abdominal pain for 3 days. She is sexually active. Perform an appropriate gynaecological examination. A mannequin will be used. You have 8 minutes."

Examiner Instructions (what you should know):
  • Patient has bilateral adnexal tenderness and cervical excitation
  • Diagnosis: Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID)

Approach (Model Performance): Introduction and Consent (1 minute):

"Hello Ms. Thompson, I'm Dr. [Name]. I understand you've been having lower tummy pain for 3 days. To find out what's causing this, I'd like to examine your tummy and perform an internal examination. The internal examination involves inserting a speculum to look at your cervix, and then examining with my fingers to feel your womb and ovaries. Is that alright with you?" [Patient agrees]

"Would you like a female nurse to be present as a chaperone during the examination?" [Patient declines] "That's fine. Can I ask you to empty your bladder first? The toilet is just outside." [Patient returns] "Thank you. I'm going to wash my hands now."

Positioning (30 seconds):

"Can I ask you to lie back on the examination couch? I'll need to examine your tummy first, so I'll need to expose from below your breasts to your pubic bone." [Position patient, expose abdomen appropriately]

Abdominal Examination (1.5 minutes): Inspection:

"On inspection, the abdomen appears flat with no visible masses or scars."

Palpation:

"I'm going to feel your tummy now. Please let me know if anywhere is tender." [Light palpation all 4 quadrants] "There is tenderness in both lower quadrants, more on the left than right. There is no guarding or rigidity, which is reassuring." [Deep palpation] "I don't palpate any masses arising from the pelvis."

Percussion:

[Percuss briefly] "Percussion is normal with no evidence of ascites."

Auscultation:

"Bowel sounds are present and normal."

Summary:

"The abdominal examination shows bilateral lower quadrant tenderness without peritonism."

Prepare for Pelvic Examination (1 minute):

"Thank you. Now I'd like to perform the internal examination. Can I ask you to remove your underwear and lie back with your knees bent and legs apart? I'll cover you with this sheet for your dignity. Your bottom needs to be right at the edge of the couch." [Position patient in modified lithotomy, cover with sheet]

"I'm going to warm the speculum under this warm water." [Warm speculum] "Put on gloves" [Don gloves]

Speculum Examination (2 minutes):

"I'm going to insert the speculum now. You'll feel some pressure but it shouldn't be painful. Please try to relax your muscles. Take a deep breath in and out for me." [Insert speculum - describe actions]

"The speculum is inserted. I'm now going to open it gently to visualize your cervix." [Open speculum]

Inspect Cervix:

"I can see the cervix. It appears normal in size and shape with a closed os, consistent with nulliparous status. The cervix is pink and smooth with no signs of ectropion, polyp, or malignancy. There is no contact bleeding. I can see a small amount of mucopurulent discharge at the os, which could indicate cervicitis."

Inspect Vagina:

[Begin to withdraw speculum slowly] "As I withdraw the speculum, I'm inspecting the vaginal walls. The vaginal walls appear normal with no ulceration, masses, or prolapse."

[Remove speculum, discard, remove gloves, don new gloves]

Bimanual Examination (2.5 minutes):

"Now I'm going to examine you with my fingers. I'll insert two fingers into your vagina and press on your tummy with my other hand. You'll feel some pressure." [Lubricate fingers, insert]

"The vaginal walls feel normal."

Palpate Cervix:

"I can feel the cervix. It's firm and closed." "I'm now going to assess for cervical excitation by gently moving the cervix." [Gently move cervix side to side] "There is cervical excitation - Ms. Thompson experiences pain when I move the cervix. This is a significant finding."

Bimanual - Uterus:

"I'm now placing my fingers in front of the cervix and pressing on the tummy with my other hand to feel the womb." [Bimanual palpation] "The uterus is normal size, anteverted, mobile, and non-tender."

Bimanual - Adnexa:

"I'm now going to examine each side to feel the ovaries." [Palpate right adnexa] "On the right side, I feel tenderness in the right adnexa. I don't palpate a discrete mass." [Palpate left adnexa] "On the left side, I also feel tenderness in the left adnexa, more marked than the right. I don't palpate a discrete mass."

Pouch of Douglas:

"I'm examining behind the womb now." [Palpate POD] "There is some fullness and tenderness in the Pouch of Douglas."

- Positive cervical excitation (pain on moving cervix) - Bilateral adnexal tenderness (left > right) - No palpable adnexal masses - Normal size, mobile uterus - Fullness and tenderness in Pouch of Douglas

Interpretation:

These findings are consistent with pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - likely sexually transmitted infection (chlamydia or gonorrhoea) causing cervicitis that has ascended to cause salpingitis and peritonitis.

Management:
  1. Swabs: Endocervical swabs for chlamydia and gonorrhoea NAAT, high vaginal swab
  2. Bloods: FBC, CRP, pregnancy test (exclude ectopic)
  3. Treatment: Empiric antibiotics (Australian guidelines - ceftriaxone 500mg IM stat + doxycycline 100mg BD 14 days + metronidazole 400mg BD 14 days)
  4. Contact tracing: Partner notification and treatment
  5. Screen for other STIs: HIV, hepatitis B and C, syphilis
  6. Advise: Abstain from intercourse until treatment completed and partner treated
  7. Follow-up: Review in 3 days to ensure responding to treatment, repeat swabs to ensure clearance

Differential diagnosis to consider:
  • Ectopic pregnancy (would need pregnancy test URGENTly - positive cervical excitation can also be seen in ectopic)
  • Appendicitis (but would expect more right-sided findings)
  • Ovarian cyst (torsion or rupture - but no discrete mass palpated)"

For Gynaecological Examination:

  1. Always offer chaperone (document if declined)
  2. Explain procedure and obtain consent before starting

  1. Warm speculum (water, not lubricant if taking CST)

For Bimanual Examination:

  1. Insert two fingers (index and middle), palm up
  2. Assess cervical excitation (gently move cervix side to side - pain = pathology)
  3. Bimanual palpation: Trap uterus/adnexa between internal and external hands
  4. Assess: Size, position, mobility, consistency, tenderness (uterus and adnexa)

Key Findings:

  1. Cervical excitation + adnexal tenderness = PID or ectopic pregnancy
  2. Enlarged irregular uterus = Fibroids
  3. Bulky tender uterus = Adenomyosis
  4. Postmenopausal palpable ovary = Ovarian cancer until proven otherwise
  5. Mucopurulent discharge from os = Cervicitis (chlamydia/gonorrhoea)
