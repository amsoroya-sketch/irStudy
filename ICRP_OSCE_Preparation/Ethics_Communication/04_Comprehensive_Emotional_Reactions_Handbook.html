INTRODUCTION

Why This Matters for AMC Clinical OSCE

Examiners assess how you respond to emotion,not just how you deliver information

Marks are awarded for:

  • Recognizing the emotional response
  • Naming the emotion (empathy statement)
  • Validating the emotion as normal
  • Responding appropriately with words and body language
  • Not rushing past the emotion
  • Sitting with discomfort

The Universal Truth

There is NO "correct" emotional response to bad news.

Every reaction listed below is normal and valid. Your job is not to "fix" the emotion or make the patient feel better (you can't), but to:

  1. Witness their pain
  2. Acknowledge it
  3. Sit with them in it
  4. Provide information and support when they're ready

FUNDAMENTAL RESPONSE PRINCIPLES

The 5 Core Skills

1. SILENCE
  • Most IMG candidates fear silence and rush to fill it
  • Silence after bad news is essential
  • Count to 10 slowly in your head before speaking again
  • Patients need time to process

2. NAMING THE EMOTION
  • "I can see you're feeling [angry/scared/overwhelmed]."
  • "This is [devastating/shocking/frightening]."
  • Naming validates

3. NORMALIZING
  • "This is a completely normal reaction."
  • "Anyone would feel this way."
  • "There's no right or wrong way to feel."

4. NON-VERBAL SUPPORT
  • Tissues within reach
  • Lean forward slightly (engagement)
  • Eye contact (but not staring)
  • Open body language (uncrossed arms)
  • Appropriate touch if culturally suitable (hand on shoulder - ask first: "May I?")

5. PATIENCE
  • Don't rush to "next steps"
  • Sit with the emotion first
  • Information can wait

REACTION-SPECIFIC GUIDES

REACTION 1: CRYING (Most Common)

Recognition:
  • Tears (may be silent or sobbing)
  • Voice breaking
  • Looking down
  • Reaching for tissues

Physiology: Natural grief response, stress hormone release Your Response - Step by Step:
  1. Stop talking immediately (0-5 seconds)

- Don't finish your sentence - Your words don't matter anymore - their emotion does

  1. Provide tissues (5-10 seconds)

- Place tissue box within reach or hand one tissue - If they're holding tissue already, just wait

  1. Silence (10-60 seconds)

- Allow crying without interruption - Don't say "don't cry" or "it's okay" - Your presence is the support

  1. Gentle validation (after crying subsides slightly - 60-90 seconds)

- "I can see this is very difficult for you." - "I'm so sorry." - "Take your time. There's no rush."

  1. Check readiness to continue (90-120 seconds)

- "Would you like a moment, or can I continue?" - "Is there anything I can get you? Some water?"

What to Say:
  • ‚úÖ "This is heartbreaking news." ‚úÖ "I'm very sorry."
  • ‚úÖ "Take all the time you need."
  • ‚úÖ Silence

What NOT to Say:
  • ‚ùå "Don't cry."
  • ‚ùå "You need to be strong."
  • ‚ùå "It's going to be okay." (You don't know that)
  • ‚ùå "At least..." (Never minimize)

Body Language:
  • Lean forward
  • Hand tissue box or tissues
  • If culturally appropriate and patient receptive: Hand on forearm (ask: "May I?")
  • Don't look away - witness their pain

Duration: 60-90 seconds minimum before moving on OSCE Tip: Examiners specifically watch for:
  • ‚úÖ Did candidate stop talking when patient cried?
  • ‚úÖ Did candidate provide tissues?
  • ‚úÖ Did candidate allow silence?
  • ‚úÖ Did candidate show empathy?

Common IMG Mistake:
  • Continuing to talk through patient's crying
  • Becoming visibly uncomfortable and rushing past the emotion
  • Not providing tissues

REACTION 2: SILENCE / SHOCK

Recognition:
  • Patient goes completely still
  • Blank stare
  • Not blinking
  • Pale
  • No verbal response
  • May look "frozen"

Physiology: Acute stress response, brain overwhelmed, emotional shutdown Your Response - Step by Step:
  1. Immediate silence (0-10 seconds)

- Don't repeat yourself - Don't ask "are you okay?" - Just wait

  1. Extended silence (10-30 seconds)

- Count to 20 slowly in your head - Maintain gentle eye contact - Don't fidget

  1. Gentle check-in (30-45 seconds)

- "I know this is an enormous shock." - Pause 5 seconds - "Take your time."

  1. Offer water (45-60 seconds)

- "Can I get you some water?" - Physical action can help patient "re-enter"

  1. Don't force conversation (60+ seconds)

- "There's no rush. We can sit here as long as you need." - Some patients need minutes of silence

  1. Check understanding when ready (variable)

- "When you're ready, would it help if I explain anything?"

What to Say:
  • ‚úÖ "I know this is a lot to take in."
  • ‚úÖ "This is shocking news."
  • ‚úÖ "Take all the time you need."
  • ‚úÖ Comfortable silence

What NOT to Say:
  • ‚ùå "Did you hear me?" (Yes, they heard - they're processing)
  • ‚ùå "Do you understand?" (Not the issue)
  • ‚ùå Repeating the diagnosis immediately

Body Language:
  • Still, calm presence
  • Gentle eye contact (but don't stare)
  • Slight forward lean showing you're present
  • Hands visible, relaxed

Duration: 30-60 seconds of silence; total 60-120 seconds before attempting to move forward OSCE Tip:
  • ‚úÖ Can you sit in silence without fidgeting?
  • ‚úÖ Do you look comfortable with discomfort?
  • ‚úÖ Do you rush to fill silence? (Don't)

Cultural Note:
  • Some cultures show emotion outwardly (crying, wailing)
  • Others show emotion inwardly (silence, stillness)
  • Both are equally valid grief responses

REACTION 3: ANGER

Recognition:
  • Raised voice
  • Accusatory statements: "How could you let this happen?"
  • Finger pointing
  • Standing up
  • Aggressive body language (arms crossed, leaning forward aggressively)
  • Swearing
  • "This is YOUR fault!"

Physiology: Anger is a secondary emotion - usually covering fear, helplessness, or grief Your Response - Step by Step:
  1. Stay calm (CRITICAL - 0-10 seconds)

- Don't mirror anger - Lower your own voice - Slow your speech - Take a breath

  1. Don't take it personally (0-60 seconds)

- Patient is not angry at YOU (even if they say they are) - They're angry at the situation - Remind yourself: This is displacement

  1. Validate the anger (10-30 seconds)

- "I can see you're very angry." - "You have every right to be angry." - "This is devastating news."

  1. Listen without defending (30-90 seconds)

- Let them vent - Don't interrupt - Don't explain or justify - Just listen

  1. Acknowledge feeling (90-120 seconds)

- "I hear your anger. This is incredibly unfair." - "Anyone would be furious in your position."

  1. Set boundaries if needed (only if abusive/threatening)

- "I understand you're upset, but I need you to please not shout at me/swear at me." - "I want to help you, but I need us to communicate respectfully."

  1. Refocus on helping (120+ seconds)

- "I know you're angry. What can I do right now to help you?"

What to Say:
  • ‚úÖ "I can see you're very angry, and that's completely understandable."
  • ‚úÖ "This is unfair. Anyone would be furious."
  • ‚úÖ "I hear you."
  • ‚úÖ "I know you're not angry at me - you're angry at this situation."

What NOT to Say:
  • ‚ùå "You need to calm down." (Will make them angrier)
  • ‚ùå "Don't be angry." (Invalidating)
  • ‚ùå "It's not that bad." (Minimizing)
  • ‚ùå "There's no need to shout." (Defensive)
  • ‚ùå Anything defensive about medical care

Body Language:
  • Stay seated (don't stand unless safety concern)
  • Open, non-defensive posture
  • Don't cross arms
  • Don't roll eyes or look exasperated
  • Maintain calm eye contact
  • Don't lean back (looks defensive)

Voice:
  • Lower and slower than patient's
  • Calm, steady tone
  • Don't match their volume

Duration: 90-120 seconds; let them vent fully When to Set Boundaries: If patient swears:
  • Mild swearing (e.g., "This is fucking unbelievable"): Allow it, ignore it
  • Swearing AT you (e.g., "You're a fucking idiot"): Set boundary

If patient threatens:
  • "I'm going to sue you/this hospital!"

- Don't respond to threat - "I understand you're very upset right now."

  • Physical threat: "I'm going to hit you!"

- Stand up, move toward door - "I don't feel safe continuing this conversation. I'm going to step out and come back when you're calmer." - Leave, inform senior, consider security

OSCE Tip:
  • ‚úÖ Did you stay calm?
  • ‚úÖ Did you validate the anger?
  • ‚úÖ Did you avoid being defensive?
  • ‚úÖ Did you set appropriate boundaries if needed?

Common IMG Mistake:
  • Becoming defensive: "I didn't cause this!"
  • Trying to "calm" the patient ("Just calm down")
  • Taking it personally and showing it
  • Matching patient's emotional intensity

REACTION 4: DENIAL

Recognition:
  • "You've made a mistake."
  • "The test must be wrong."
  • "I don't believe you."
  • "This can't be true."
  • Shaking head repeatedly
  • "I feel fine, there's nothing wrong with me."

Physiology: Psychological defense mechanism; brain can't process reality yet Your Response - Step by Step:
  1. Don't argue (CRITICAL - 0-30 seconds)

- You cannot logic someone out of denial - Arguing entrenches denial further - Denial is protective - respect it initially

  1. Acknowledge the disbelief (10-30 seconds)

- "I understand this is very hard to believe." - "This doesn't feel real."

  1. Gentle persistence (30-60 seconds)

- "I wish I had different news to give you." - "Unfortunately, the test results are definitive."

  1. Offer evidence calmly (60-90 seconds)

- "We did two separate tests to be certain." - "I've reviewed the results carefully."

  1. Don't insist they accept it NOW (90+ seconds)

- "I know this is overwhelming and hard to accept." - "It's okay to need time to process this."

  1. Offer support for when reality hits (120+ seconds)

- "When you're ready to talk about next steps, I'm here."

What to Say:
  • ‚úÖ "I understand you don't want this to be true."
  • ‚úÖ "I wish I were wrong, but unfortunately the results are clear."
  • ‚úÖ "It's normal to feel disbelief when you hear news like this."
  • ‚úÖ "Take your time processing this."

What NOT to Say:
  • ‚ùå "You need to accept this." (Pushy)
  • ‚ùå "It IS true whether you believe it or not." (Argumentative)
  • ‚ùå "You're in denial." (Labeling, unhelpful)

How to Handle Specific Denials: "The test must be wrong":
  • "I understand you want to be absolutely sure. We did [specific tests]. Both confirmed the diagnosis."
  • If they insist: "You have the right to a second opinion if you'd like. I can facilitate that."

"I feel fine, so I can't have [cancer/HIV/etc.]":
  • "Many serious conditions don't have symptoms in the early stages."
  • "That's actually why this test was important - to catch things before symptoms develop."

"This happens to other people, not me":
  • "I know this feels impossible to believe."
  • "Unfortunately, this diagnosis affects 1 in people. It can happen to anyone."

Duration: 60-90 seconds; don't force acceptance OSCE Tip:
  • ‚úÖ Did you avoid arguing?
  • ‚úÖ Did you stay calm and empathetic?
  • ‚úÖ Did you gently persist with truth?
  • ‚úÖ Did you offer second opinion option?

Follow-up:
  • Patients in denial often return 24-48 hours later, having processed
  • "I've been thinking about what you said..."
  • This is normal progression

REACTION 5: BARGAINING

Recognition:
  • "If I [diet/exercise/quit smoking/pray], will it go away?"
  • "Can I do anything to change this?"
  • "What if I try alternative medicine?"
  • "If I have the surgery, will I be completely cured?"
  • Searching for a way out
  • Desperate for control

Physiology: Attempt to regain control in an uncontrollable situation; part of grief process Your Response - Step by Step:
  1. Acknowledge desire for control (0-30 seconds)

- "I understand you want to do everything possible." - "It's natural to want to have some control over this."

  1. Validate the question (30-60 seconds)

- "That's a good question." - "I'm glad you're thinking about what you can do."

  1. Be honest about what WILL help (60-90 seconds)

- "While we can't make this go away, there are things that will help: - Treatment (specify) - Lifestyle changes (if applicable) - Support systems"

  1. Be honest about what WON'T help (90+ seconds)

- "Unfortunately, [dieting alone/exercise alone/alternative medicine instead of treatment] won't cure this." - "But [diet/exercise] can help you feel better and cope with treatment."

What to Say: For cancer: "If I change my diet, will the cancer go away?"
  • ‚úÖ "Diet alone can't cure cancer, but good nutrition helps your body cope with treatment and recover."
  • ‚úÖ "What WILL help is the chemotherapy/surgery we're planning."

For chronic disease (diabetes, heart disease): "If I exercise every day, can I reverse this?"
  • ‚úÖ "Exercise is really important and can improve your condition significantly."
  • ‚úÖ "Combined with medication and diet, we can control this well, though we can't completely reverse the damage already done."

For genetic/unchangeable conditions: "If I had done X differently, would this not have happened?"
  • ‚úÖ "No - this was not caused by anything you did or didn't do."
  • ‚úÖ "This was going to happen regardless."

What NOT to Say:
  • ‚ùå "Nothing will help." (Removes all hope)
  • ‚ùå "Prayer/diet/alternative medicine can cure you." (False hope)
  • ‚ùå "You should have done X earlier." (Blame)

Duration: 60 seconds OSCE Tip:
  • ‚úÖ Balance hope with honesty
  • ‚úÖ Empower patient with what they CAN control
  • ‚úÖ Be clear about what won't work

REACTION 6: IMMEDIATE CONCERN ABOUT FAMILY

Recognition:
  • "Who will look after my children?"
  • "What will happen to my wife/husband?"
  • "I can't leave my kids."
  • "My mother depends on me."
  • Focus immediately on dependents

Physiology: Protective instinct, responsibility, fear of abandoning loved ones Your Response - Step by Step:
  1. Validate the concern (0-30 seconds)

- "I can see your family is your first thought." - "That shows how much you care for them."

  1. Don't dismiss (30-60 seconds)

- "This is a completely understandable worry."

  1. Defer practical planning (60-90 seconds)

- "These are important practical concerns." - "But let's take this one step at a time." - "First, let's focus on your health and treatment." - "Then we'll work on support for your family."

  1. Offer resources (90+ seconds)

- "We have social workers who can help with family support." - "There are organizations that help families when a parent is ill."

  1. For terminal diagnosis (if applicable):

- "When you're ready, we can talk about planning for your family's future." - "But that doesn't need to happen right now."

What to Say:
  • ‚úÖ "Your family is clearly very important to you."
  • ‚úÖ "We'll make sure they're supported."
  • ‚úÖ "Let's focus on getting you the best treatment first."
  • ‚úÖ "Social work can help with practical support for your family."

What NOT to Say:
  • ‚ùå "Don't worry about your family now." (Their concern is valid)
  • ‚ùå "They'll be fine." (You don't know that)
  • ‚ùå "Let's not think about that." (Avoidant)

Duration: 30-60 seconds; acknowledge but defer OSCE Tip:
  • ‚úÖ Acknowledge concern as valid
  • ‚úÖ Offer social work/support resources
  • ‚úÖ Gently refocus on patient's treatment

REACTION 7: RELIEF

Recognition:
  • Smile or laugh
  • "At least I know what's wrong now."
  • "I'm almost relieved - I thought I was going crazy."
  • Visible relaxation in body language
  • Deep breath/sigh

Context: Common when patient has had unexplained symptoms for months/years Physiology: Relief at having an explanation; diagnosis provides direction forward Your Response - Step by Step:
  1. Don't judge (CRITICAL - 0-30 seconds)

- Relief is a valid reaction - It doesn't mean they don't understand severity - They're relieved to have answers, not happy about diagnosis

  1. Validate the complexity (30-60 seconds)

- "Some people feel relief to finally have an answer." - "It's okay to feel multiple things at once."

  1. Acknowledge the journey (60-90 seconds)

- "I know you've been struggling with symptoms for [months/years]." - "Having a diagnosis means we can start treating it."

  1. Gently ensure understanding of severity (90+ seconds)

- "I want to make sure you understand that this is serious, even though having a diagnosis is helpful."

What to Say:
  • ‚úÖ "It's completely understandable to feel relieved to finally know what's causing your symptoms."
  • ‚úÖ "Having a diagnosis means we can make a plan forward."
  • ‚úÖ "People can feel many different emotions at once - relief, fear, sadness - all at the same time."

What NOT to Say:
  • ‚ùå "You shouldn't feel relieved - this is serious!" (Judgmental)
  • ‚ùå Looking shocked at their relief

Duration: 30 seconds OSCE Tip:
  • ‚úÖ Normalize relief as valid emotion
  • ‚úÖ Don't judge
  • ‚úÖ Ensure they understand severity without shaming their reaction

REACTION 8: INAPPROPRIATE LAUGHTER / NERVOUS JOKING

Recognition:
  • Giggling
  • Making jokes ("Well, that's one way to lose weight!")
  • Seems like they're not taking it seriously
  • Laughter that doesn't match the situation

Physiology: Nervous system response to extreme stress; defense mechanism Your Response - Step by Step:
  1. Don't be offended (CRITICAL - 0-10 seconds)

- This is not disrespect - This is a coping mechanism - Very common reaction

  1. Don't laugh with them (10-30 seconds)

- Remain serious but kind - Don't mirror the laughter

  1. Normalize it (30-60 seconds)

- "Everyone reacts differently to difficult news." - "There's no right way to respond."

  1. Gently ground them (60+ seconds)

- "I know this is very difficult news, even if it doesn't feel real right now."

What to Say:
  • ‚úÖ "People react to shocking news in many different ways."
  • ‚úÖ "Sometimes laughter is a way our body copes with stress."
  • ‚úÖ "This doesn't mean you don't understand how serious this is."

What NOT to Say:
  • ‚ùå "This isn't funny." (Shaming)
  • ‚ùå "Do you understand how serious this is?" (Patronizing)
  • ‚ùå Laughing along with them

Duration: 30 seconds; normalize and move on OSCE Tip:
  • ‚úÖ Stay professional but kind
  • ‚úÖ Don't shame
  • ‚úÖ Normalize as stress response

REACTION 9: INTELLECTUALIZATION

Recognition:
  • Immediately asks detailed medical questions
  • "What's the pathophysiology?"
  • "What are the mutation rates?"
  • "I've read about this condition - is it the XYZ subtype?"
  • No visible emotion
  • Wants statistics, studies, technical detail

Physiology: Coping mechanism - focusing on facts to avoid feeling emotions Your Response - Step by Step:
  1. Don't judge (0-30 seconds)

- This is how some people process - Often healthcare workers, scientists, engineers - Knowledge gives them sense of control

  1. Answer their questions (30-120 seconds)

- Provide the information they're asking for - Be honest: "I don't know that detail, but the specialist will."

  1. Gently check in emotionally (120+ seconds)

- After answering several questions: "How are you feeling about all of this?" - Some will engage emotionally, others won't

  1. Don't force emotion (Throughout)

- If they want to stay in intellectual mode, that's okay - Emotions may come later

What to Say:
  • ‚úÖ [Answer their technical questions]
  • ‚úÖ "Those are good questions. Let me see what I can answer."
  • ‚úÖ "The specialist will be able to go into more technical detail."
  • ‚úÖ Gentle check-in: "I'm giving you a lot of information - how are you doing with all of this?"

What NOT to Say:
  • ‚ùå "Let's not worry about technical details right now." (Dismissive)
  • ‚ùå "You're avoiding your feelings." (Judging their coping mechanism)

Duration: Variable - can be entire consultation OSCE Tip:
  • ‚úÖ Respect their coping style
  • ‚úÖ Answer what you can
  • ‚úÖ Offer to connect them with specialist for detailed discussion

REACTION 10: GUILT / SELF-BLAME

Recognition:
  • "Is this my fault?"
  • "Did I cause this?"
  • "I should have..."
  • "If only I had..."
  • "I've been so stupid."
  • Self-directed anger

Context: Especially common in:
  • Lifestyle-related diseases (lung cancer in smoker)
  • Pregnancy loss
  • STIs
  • Preventable conditions

Physiology: Attempt to find meaning/cause in random events; need for control Your Response - Step by Step:
  1. Direct, firm reassurance (0-30 seconds)

- Be clear and unambiguous - "This is not your fault." - Don't soften it

  1. Address specific self-blame (30-90 seconds)

- Listen to what they think they did wrong - Correct misconceptions directly

  1. Explain actual causation (90+ seconds)

- Simple explanation of real cause - Remove blame

Context-Specific Responses: Miscarriage: "Did I cause this? I lifted something heavy / had a glass of wine / was stressed."
  • ‚úÖ "No. You absolutely did not cause this."
  • ‚úÖ "Lifting, one glass of wine before you knew, normal stress - none of these cause miscarriage."
  • ‚úÖ "Most miscarriages are due to random chromosomal abnormalities - nothing you did or didn't do."

Lung cancer in smoker: "This is my fault - I should have quit smoking."
  • ‚úÖ "Smoking is a risk factor, yes. But many smokers never get lung cancer, and some non-smokers do."
  • ‚úÖ "Blaming yourself doesn't help you now."
  • ‚úÖ "What matters is moving forward with treatment."

HIV: "I should have been more careful."
  • ‚úÖ "What matters now is your health and treatment."
  • ‚úÖ "Many people with HIV contracted it before they had full information about safe sex."
  • ‚úÖ "Let's focus on keeping you healthy going forward."

Child's cancer (parent guilt): "Did I do something wrong during pregnancy?"
  • ‚úÖ "No. Childhood cancer is not caused by anything parents do or don't do."
  • ‚úÖ "These are random genetic changes."
  • ‚úÖ "You are not responsible for this."

What to Say:
  • ‚úÖ "This is not your fault." (Firm, clear, direct)
  • ‚úÖ "You didn't cause this."
  • ‚úÖ "Even if you had [done X], this would have happened."

What NOT to Say:
  • ‚ùå "Well, smoking/drinking/etc. didn't help." (Blaming)
  • ‚ùå "You should have..." (Unhelpful)
  • ‚ùå Soft reassurance that leaves room for doubt: "It's probably not your fault."

Duration: 60 seconds; be firm and clear OSCE Tip:
  • ‚úÖ Clear, direct reassurance
  • ‚úÖ Don't leave room for ambiguity
  • ‚úÖ Firm "no" to self-blame

Common IMG Mistake:
  • Soft reassurance that doesn't land firmly enough
  • Focusing on "lifestyle factors" at this time (can come later)

REACTION 11: RELIGIOUS / SPIRITUAL RESPONSE

Recognition:
  • "Why is God doing this to me?"
  • "Is God punishing me?"
  • "This is God's will."
  • "I need to pray."
  • "Can I see a chaplain/priest/imam?"
  • Religious language or framework

Cultural Context: Very common in many cultures; faith is central to life and coping Your Response - Step by Step:
  1. Don't engage theologically (CRITICAL - Throughout)

- You're a doctor, not a spiritual leader - Don't answer "why" questions about God - Don't agree or disagree with religious interpretations

  1. Respect the faith (0-30 seconds)

- "I can see your faith is very important to you." - "Many people find their faith helpful during difficult times."

  1. Defer to appropriate person (30-60 seconds)

- "Would you like me to arrange for a [chaplain/priest/imam/rabbi] to visit you?" - "Would you like time to pray?"

  1. Focus on medical care (60+ seconds)

- "While you're thinking and praying about this, let me explain the medical treatment..." - Balance spiritual with practical

Context-Specific Responses: "Is God punishing me?"
  • ‚úÖ "I can't answer questions about God - that's beyond my expertise."
  • ‚úÖ "What I can tell you is that many good people get this diagnosis."
  • ‚úÖ "Would you like to speak with a chaplain about these spiritual questions?"
  • ‚ùå "God doesn't punish people this way." (Theological stance)

"This is God's will" (fatalistic acceptance)
  • ‚úÖ "I respect your faith."
  • ‚úÖ "I also believe God works through medicine and doctors."
  • ‚úÖ "Would you be willing to try treatment while you pray?"
  • ‚ùå "Don't be fatalistic." (Disrespectful)

"I need to pray before I decide about treatment"
  • ‚úÖ "Of course. Take the time you need."
  • ‚úÖ "Would you like to speak with a chaplain?"
  • ‚úÖ "We can meet again after you've had time to pray and think."

REACTION 12: EUTHANASIA / ASSISTED DYING REQUEST

Recognition:
  • "I want to die."
  • "Can you help me end my life?"
  • "I'd rather die than go through this."
  • "Why should I suffer?"
  • "Euthanasia is legal now, right?"

CRITICAL CONTEXT - Australian Law:
  • Legal in: Victoria (2019), Western Australia (2021), Tasmania (2022), Queensland (2023), South Australia (2023), Northern Territory (2023)
  • Illegal in: NSW, ACT
  • Requirements (where legal): Terminal illness, <12 months prognosis, decision-making capacity, residency requirements, multi-step approval process
  • NOT available for: Non-terminal chronic conditions, disability, depression, dementia

Risk Assessment: ALWAYS assess suicide risk Your Response - Step by Step:
  1. Don't be shocked (CRITICAL - 0-10 seconds)

- This is a common thought when hearing devastating news - Your reaction matters

  1. Acknowledge the feeling (10-30 seconds)

- "I hear that you're feeling this is too much to bear." - "That's an understandable reaction to such difficult news."

  1. Assess suicide risk (30-90 seconds)

- "When you say you want to die, are you having thoughts of hurting yourself?" - "Do you have a plan?" - If yes ‚Üí psychiatric assessment, safety plan

  1. Explore the statement (90-120 seconds)

- "Can you tell me more about what you're feeling?" - Often this is about: - Fear of suffering - Fear of loss of dignity/control - Fear of being a burden - Uncontrolled symptoms (pain, breathlessness)

  1. Address fears directly (120-180 seconds)

- "I hear that you're frightened of suffering." - "We have excellent pain and symptom management available." - "Palliative care focuses on keeping you comfortable."

  1. Legal position (180+ seconds)

  1. Emphasize palliative care (Throughout)

- "Our focus is on keeping you comfortable and maintaining your quality of life." - "Modern palliative care means people don't have to suffer."

  1. Hope within honesty (180+ seconds)

- "I know right now this feels unbearable." - "Let's take this one step at a time." - "Many people who have these thoughts initially find that treatment and support help enormously."

What to Say: If acute suicidal ideation:
  • ‚úÖ "I'm concerned about your safety right now."
  • ‚úÖ "I'd like to have a colleague from psychiatry come and talk with you."
  • ‚úÖ "We're going to keep you safe."

If expressing fear of future suffering:
  • ‚úÖ "I hear that you're frightened of suffering. That's a completely understandable fear."
  • ‚úÖ "We have excellent pain management and symptom control."
  • ‚úÖ "People don't have to suffer through terminal illness - that's what palliative care is for."

If specifically asking about VAD in legal state:
  • ‚úÖ "Voluntary assisted dying is legal in [state]. There's a formal process with multiple steps to ensure it's your informed, voluntary choice."
  • ‚úÖ "I can provide you with information about the process if you'd like."
  • ‚úÖ "I'd also like you to speak with palliative care, to understand all your options for staying comfortable."

  • ‚úÖ "Euthanasia is not legal in [NSW/ACT] currently."
  • ‚úÖ "What I can offer you is excellent symptom management through our palliative care team."

  • ‚ùå "Don't talk like that." (Dismissive)
  • ‚ùå "You have so much to live for." (Invalidating)
  • ‚ùå "Suicide is wrong." (Judgment)
  • ‚ùå Providing information on how to end life (unethical)
  • ‚ùå "Let's not think about that now." (Avoidant)

  • ‚úÖ Assess suicide risk
  • ‚úÖ Empathy for suffering
  • ‚úÖ Explain palliative care
  • ‚úÖ Know legal position in your state
  • ‚úÖ Psychiatric referral if acute risk

  • Palliative care referral (same day if possible)
  • Psychiatry/psychology referral
  • Safety plan if suicidal

  • ‚ùå "I'm the expert here." (Arrogant)

  • ‚úÖ Facilitate second opinion

Recognition:

  • Eyes roll back

REACTION 15: EXPLOSIVE GRIEF / WAILING

Recognition:
  • Loud crying / screaming
  • Wailing
  • "No, no, no, no!"
  • Rocking
  • Pulling hair
  • Dramatic physical expression of grief

Cultural Context:
  • Normal grief expression in many cultures (Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, some Asian, Pacific Islander, African)
  • NOT pathological
  • NOT "hysterical"
  • Healthy grief expression

Western medical culture often views this as "excessive" - it's not. It's cultural. Your Response - Step by Step:
  1. Allow it (CRITICAL - 0-60 seconds)

- Don't try to "calm them down" - Don't try to quiet them - Don't shush them - This is normal grief

  1. Ensure physical safety (0-60 seconds)

- If they're at risk of hurting themselves (hitting head, pulling hair out), gentle physical intervention - "I'm going to move this table so you don't hurt yourself."

  1. Stay present (60-180 seconds)

- Sit with them - Your presence is the support - Hand tissues - Don't leave them alone

  1. Gentle touch if appropriate (60+ seconds)

- If culturally appropriate: Hand on shoulder or back - Ask first if unsure: "May I?" [gesture to shoulder]

  1. Wait for wave to pass (Variable - can be 5-10 minutes)

- Grief comes in waves - Don't interrupt the wave - Just be there

  1. Soft validation when it subsides (After wave passes)

- "I'm so, so sorry." - "This is devastating."

What to Say:
  • ‚úÖ Silence (mostly)
  • ‚úÖ "I'm so sorry." (quietly)
  • ‚úÖ "Take your time."
  • ‚úÖ "I'm here." ‚úÖ [If they apologize]: "You don't need to apologize. This is a normal reaction to devastating news."

  • ‚ùå "Please calm down."
  • ‚ùå "Shhhh."
  • ‚ùå "You need to be quiet."
  • ‚ùå "Other patients can hear you."
  • ‚ùå Looking embarrassed or uncomfortable

  • Stay seated
  • Lean forward (engagement)
  • Open body language
  • Tissues ready
  • Calm presence
  • Don't look at watch/phone

  • ‚úÖ Comfort with intense emotion
  • ‚úÖ Don't try to suppress
  • ‚úÖ Cultural awareness
  • ‚úÖ Patient, calm presence

  • Protect patient's dignity

REACTION 16: NUMBNESS / DETACHMENT / FLAT AFFECT

Recognition:
  • "Okay."
  • No visible emotion
  • Flat voice
  • "I understand."
  • Looks calm, almost indifferent
  • No tears, no anger, no fear
  • May say they're "fine"

Physiology: Emotional shock, dissociation, protective numbness This is NOT the same as "handling it well" - emotions will likely hit later Your Response - Step by Step:
  1. Don't assume they're okay (CRITICAL - Throughout)

- Flat affect often means overwhelm, not acceptance - They're numb, not coping

  1. Acknowledge the news (0-30 seconds)

- Even if they look calm, name the severity - "I know this is very serious news."

  1. Normalize delayed reaction (30-60 seconds)

- "Sometimes when we hear shocking news, it doesn't feel real immediately." - "Emotions often come later - hours or days later." - "That's completely normal."

  1. Check in gently (60-90 seconds)

- "How are you feeling right now?" - They may say "I don't know" or "I feel numb" - both valid

  1. Warn about delayed reaction (90-120 seconds)

- "In the next few days, as this sinks in, you may experience a lot of emotions." - "That's normal and expected."

  1. Provide resources for later (120+ seconds)

- "Here's my number. When this hits you - and it will - please call." - "Counselling is available if you need it."

  1. Check safety (Before they leave)

- "Do you have someone at home with you?" - "Please don't be alone tonight."

What to Say:
  • ‚úÖ "This is very serious news, even if it doesn't feel real right now."
  • ‚úÖ "Sometimes our minds protect us from overwhelming news by numbing us."
  • ‚úÖ "Emotions often come in waves later."
  • ‚úÖ "Please don't be alone over the next few days."

What NOT to Say:
  • ‚ùå "You're handling this very well!" (Misinterpretation)
  • ‚ùå "You seem okay with this." (Assumption)
  • ‚ùå Moving too quickly to practical matters

Duration: 60-90 seconds; acknowledge and warn OSCE Tip:
  • ‚úÖ Don't mistake numbness for coping
  • ‚úÖ Warn about delayed reaction
  • ‚úÖ Provide resources for when emotions hit
  • ‚úÖ Check safety

  • Call patient next day to check in
  • Delayed grief reactions can be intense
  • Some patients need psychiatric support for complicated grief

WHAT NEVER TO SAY

Universal "Never Say" List

These phrases fail in ALL situations when breaking bad news:

‚ùå "I know how you feel."

  • You don't. Everyone's experience is unique.
  • ‚úÖ Instead: "I can't imagine how difficult this is for you."

‚ùå "Everything happens for a reason."

  • Meaningless platitude, often offensive
  • Implies there's a "good reason" for cancer/death/suffering
  • ‚úÖ Instead: Don't try to find meaning - just witness their pain

‚ùå "God doesn't give you more than you can handle."

  • Theological assumption (not all patients religious)
  • Factually wrong (people do get overwhelmed)
  • Implies if they're struggling, they're weak or lacking faith
  • ‚úÖ Instead: Don't invoke God unless patient does

‚ùå "You're so strong." / "You have to be strong."

  • Implies they can't show weakness or emotion
  • Invalidates their right to fall apart
  • ‚úÖ Instead: "It's okay to not be okay."

‚ùå "At least..."

  • "At least you caught it early."
  • "At least it's not [worse thing]."
  • "At least you have other children." (after child's death)
  • ALWAYS minimizes their pain
  • ‚úÖ Instead: Acknowledge the loss without comparison

‚ùå "Don't cry."

  • Invalidates healthy emotional expression
  • ‚úÖ Instead: Allow crying, provide tissues, sit quietly

‚ùå "It could be worse."

  • Minimizes their suffering
  • ‚úÖ Instead: Acknowledge that this IS bad

‚ùå "You'll be fine." / "It'll be okay."

  • False reassurance
  • You don't know if they'll be fine
  • ‚úÖ Instead: "We'll do everything we can."

‚ùå "Time heals all wounds."

  • Platitude, unhelpful
  • Not always true
  • ‚úÖ Instead: "This will be hard, but we'll support you."

‚ùå "Stay positive!"

  • Toxic positivity
  • Invalidates legitimate fear and sadness
  • ‚úÖ Instead: "It's okay to feel however you feel."

‚ùå "My [aunt/friend/cousin] had this and..."

  • If story ends badly: terrifying to patient
  • If story ends well: false hope, everyone's different
  • Makes conversation about YOU, not them
  • ‚úÖ Instead: Keep focus on THEIR situation

‚ùå "You're young, you'll have other children." (after miscarriage)

  • Invalidates grief for THIS baby
  • ‚úÖ Instead: "I'm so sorry for your loss."

‚ùå "At least you had X years together." (after death)

  • Minimizes grief
  • ‚úÖ Instead: "I'm so sorry for your loss."

‚ùå "Call me if you need anything."

  • Too vague, patient won't call
  • ‚úÖ Instead: "I'm going to call you in 2 days to check how you're doing."

BODY LANGUAGE AND NON-VERBAL COMMUNICATION

The Silent Messages You Send

Your body language is as important as your words.

Positive Non-Verbal Communication ‚úÖ

Seating:
  • Sit at same level as patient (never stand while they sit)
  • 45-degree angle, not directly opposite (less confrontational)
  • 3-4 feet distance (personal but not intimate space)
  • No desk between you (barrier)

Posture:
  • Slight forward lean (engagement)
  • Open posture (arms uncrossed)
  • Turned toward patient (not angled away)
  • Still, calm presence (not fidgeting)

Eye Contact:
  • Regular but not constant
  • Look at patient when they speak (active listening)
  • Look at patient when you speak (connection)
  • Okay to look away briefly (not staring)
  • If patient looks away, respect that

Hands:
  • Visible, relaxed
  • Not fidgeting with pen, phone, paper
  • Available for tissue-offering
  • Available for appropriate touch (if offered)

Facial Expression:
  • Serious but warm
  • Compassionate
  • Not smiling (inappropriate)
  • Not completely blank (seems cold)
  • Gentle, kind eyes

Voice:
  • Slower than normal conversation
  • Lower pitch than normal
  • Calm, steady
  • Quiet but audible
  • Pauses between sentences

Appropriate Touch (If culturally suitable and patient receptive):
  • Hand on forearm: Universal comfort gesture
  • Hand on shoulder: More personal, ask first
  • Holding hand: Very personal, only if patient initiates or if clearly appropriate (e.g., during death notification)
  • Ask permission: "May I?" [gesture to shoulder/hand]
  • Never: Hug (unless patient initiates and you're comfortable)

FINAL WORDS

Breaking bad news is one of the hardest things you'll do as a doctor.

No amount of reading prepares you fully. Experience teaches. But these principles will guide you:

  1. Slow down
  2. Sit with discomfort
  3. Witness their pain
  4. Validate every emotion
  5. Be human

You cannot make bad news "not bad." Your job is not to fix their pain. Your job is to deliver truth with compassion, sit with them in their worst moment, and help them find a path forward. That is enough.
