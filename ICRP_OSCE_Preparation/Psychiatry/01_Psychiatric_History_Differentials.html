Psychiatric History Taking with Differential Diagnosis

Target Audience: International Medical Graduates (IMGs) preparing for AMC Clinical OSCE and ICRP NSW Junior Hospital Program rotations in Psychiatry Learning Objectives: By the end of this document, you will be able to:
  1. Apply Dr. Amir Soufi's differential-driven methodology to psychiatric presentations
  2. Use the PSYCHIATRIC framework for systematic history-taking
  3. Formulate differentials from presenting complaints (low mood, anxiety, psychosis, mania)
  4. Conduct risk assessment integrated into history (suicide, violence, self-neglect)
  5. Gather collateral history appropriately
  6. Understand Australian mental health legislation and services (Mental Health Act, Medicare items, headspace, Beyond Blue)
  7. Avoid common IMG mistakes in psychiatric history-taking
  8. Complete psychiatric history OSCE stations confidently

Dr. Amir Soufi's Principle Applied to Psychiatry

"In psychiatry, your differential forms from the FIRST symptom: Low mood = Depression vs bipolar (depressed phase) vs adjustment disorder vs grief vs organic (hypothyroid, medication). Anxiety = GAD vs panic disorder vs PTSD vs substance withdrawal vs cardiac (MI, arrhythmia). Psychosis = Schizophrenia vs drug-induced vs affective psychosis (bipolar, depression with psychotic features) vs delirium vs organic (brain tumor, encephalitis). ALWAYS rule out ORGANIC causes and SUBSTANCE use FIRST - medical illnesses and drugs can mimic ANY psychiatric disorder."

The Psychiatric Differential Mindset

Traditional Approach:
  • Take full psychiatric history systematically
  • Perform mental state examination
  • At end, formulate diagnosis
  • Problem: Misses medical causes (hypothyroidism presenting as depression, delirium presenting as psychosis), delays treatment, fails to integrate risk assessment

Dr. Amir's Differential-Driven Approach:
  1. From First Symptom: Generate Differential

- Low mood ‚Üí Think immediately: Major depression, bipolar depression, adjustment disorder, grief, hypothyroidism, medications (beta-blockers, steroids), chronic illness - Anxiety ‚Üí Think immediately: GAD, panic disorder, PTSD, substance withdrawal (alcohol, benzodiazepines), hyperthyroidism, cardiac (MI, arrhythmia), caffeine excess - Psychosis (hallucinations, delusions) ‚Üí Think immediately: Schizophrenia, drug-induced (amphetamines, cannabis, steroids), affective psychosis (bipolar mania with psychosis, depression with psychotic features), delirium, organic (brain tumor, encephalitis, SLE) - Mania (elevated mood, increased energy) ‚Üí Think immediately: Bipolar disorder type I, drug-induced (amphetamines, steroids, antidepressants), hyperthyroidism, organic (frontal lobe tumor)

  1. Rule Out Organic FIRST

- Medical illnesses can present with psychiatric symptoms - ALWAYS ask: Physical health, medications, substance use, recent medical investigations - üö® RED FLAGs for organic cause: - New-onset psychiatric symptoms in older adult (>65 years) - Acute onset (hours-days) - Fluctuating course (delirium) - Visual hallucinations (organic until proven otherwise) - Neurological signs (confusion, disorientation, memory impairment, seizures) - No psychiatric history

  1. Integrate Risk Assessment Throughout

- Risk assessment is NOT a separate section at the end - Ask about suicidal thoughts when discussing low mood - Ask about violence/aggression when discussing anger/irritability - Ask about self-neglect when discussing functional impairment

  1. Collateral History is ESSENTIAL

- Patient may have poor insight (psychosis, mania, dementia) - Patient may minimize symptoms (substance use, eating disorder) - Patient may be unable to provide history (severe depression, psychosis, confusion) - ALWAYS seek collateral (family, friends, GP, previous treating team)

The PSYCHIATRIC Framework for History-Taking

P - Presenting complaint (in patient's own words) S - Symptom exploration (detailed, using screening tools) Y - Years of symptoms (onset, course, triggers) C - Collateral history (family, friends, previous medical records) H - History of psychiatric illness (diagnoses, hospitalizations, treatments) I - Impact on function (work, relationships, self-care, activities of daily living) A - Associated substance use (alcohol, illicit drugs, prescribed medications, caffeine, nicotine) T - Treatment history (medications, psychotherapy, ECT, admissions) R - Risk assessment (suicide, violence, self-neglect, vulnerability) I - Insight and understanding (patient's view of illness, willingness to engage with treatment) C - Co-morbid medical illness (physical health, medications that may affect mental health)

Opening the Psychiatric Consultation

Creating a Safe Environment

Setting:
  • Private room, door closed (but not locked - patient should feel they can leave)
  • Seating: Chairs at 90¬∞ angle (not directly opposite - less confrontational), equal height
  • No barriers: Don't sit behind desk
  • Safety: Sit nearest to door (your exit route), remove potential weapons from room
  • Time: Adequate time (minimum 30-45 minutes for initial assessment)

Introduction (1-2 minutes):
  • "Hello, I'm Dr [Name], one of the doctors here. What should I call you?" [Use patient's preferred name]
  • Explain purpose: "I'd like to talk with you today about how you've been feeling and what's been going on. This will take about 30-45 minutes. Is that okay?"
  • Confidentiality: "What we discuss is confidential, but I need to let you know that if I'm concerned about your safety or someone else's safety, I may need to share information with the team."
  • Rapport building: Open body language, empathic tone, active listening

Step 1: Presenting Complaint

Open question:
  • "What's brought you in today?"
  • "How can I help you?"
  • "Tell me what's been happening."

Listen without interrupting (1-2 minutes) - let patient tell their story Reflect back (show you're listening):
  • "So it sounds like you've been feeling very low in mood for the past few weeks?"
  • "You mentioned you've been hearing voices - tell me more about that."

Document in patient's own words:
  • PC: "I've been feeling really down and I can't see a way out. I just want it to stop." (45-year-old male)

Step 2: Symptom Exploration (Based on Presenting Complaint)

From the presenting complaint, generate differential and explore symptoms systematically

LOW MOOD / DEPRESSION

Immediate Differential:
  1. Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)
  2. Bipolar Disorder (depressive episode)
  3. Adjustment Disorder (in response to stressor within 3 months)
  4. Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia - chronic low mood >2 years)
  5. Grief / Bereavement
  6. Organic:

- Hypothyroidism - Medications (beta-blockers, steroids, isotretinoin, interferon) - Chronic illnesses (cancer, chronic pain, heart failure) - Neurological (stroke, Parkinson's disease, dementia)

  1. Substance-induced: Alcohol, cannabis withdrawal

Screening for Depression (DSM-5 Criteria)

Use the 9 symptoms of depression (mnemonic: SIG E CAPS + depressed mood)

Ask: "Over the past 2 weeks, have you noticed any of the following?"

S - Sleep disturbance
  • "How's your sleep?"
  • Insomnia: Difficulty falling asleep (initial insomnia), waking during night (middle insomnia), early morning waking (terminal insomnia - classic in depression)
  • Hypersomnia: Sleeping excessively (atypical depression)

I - Interest (loss of) / Anhedonia
  • "Have you lost interest in things you used to enjoy?"
  • "Do you still get pleasure from activities?"
  • Anhedonia = core symptom of depression

G - Guilt / Worthlessness
  • "Do you feel guilty about things?"
  • "How do you feel about yourself?"
  • "Do you feel you've let people down?"
  • Pathological guilt (out of proportion, delusional guilt in severe depression)

E - Energy (decreased) / Fatigue
  • "How are your energy levels?"
  • "Do you feel tired all the time, even after rest?"

C - Concentration (impaired)
  • "How's your concentration?"
  • "Can you focus when reading or watching TV?"
  • "Are you able to make decisions?"

A - Appetite / Weight change
  • "How's your appetite?"
  • "Have you lost or gained weight recently?" (>5% body weight in 1 month)
  • Decreased appetite (typical depression), increased appetite (atypical depression)

P - Psychomotor changes
  • Agitation: "Do you feel restless, unable to sit still?"
  • Retardation: Observe slowness of movement, speech, thought (ask: "Do you feel everything has slowed down?")

S - Suicidal thoughts (CRITICAL - ask EVERY patient with low mood)
  • See Risk Assessment section below

Plus: Depressed mood
  • "How would you describe your mood?"
  • "Do you feel sad, down, empty, hopeless most of the day, nearly every day?"

DSM-5 Diagnosis of Major Depressive Disorder:
  • 5 or more symptoms present during same 2-week period
  • Must include (1) depressed mood OR (2) loss of interest/pleasure (anhedonia)
  • Symptoms cause significant distress or functional impairment
  • Not attributable to substance or medical condition

Severity:
  • Mild: Few symptoms, minor functional impairment
  • Moderate: Symptoms/functional impairment between mild and severe
  • Severe: Most symptoms, marked functional impairment, may have psychotic features

Differentiating Diagnoses

Bipolar Depression vs Unipolar Depression:
  • ALWAYS ask about mania/hypomania in ANY patient presenting with depression
  • "Have you ever had periods where your mood was the opposite - where you felt very high, energized, needed less sleep?"
  • "Have you ever had periods where you felt 'too good', more confident than usual, spent more money than you should, or got into trouble?"
  • If yes ‚Üí Screen for mania (see below)
  • Why this matters: Antidepressants alone can trigger mania in bipolar disorder ‚Üí Need mood stabilizer

Adjustment Disorder:
  • Clear stressor within past 3 months (job loss, relationship breakdown, bereavement)
  • Symptoms out of proportion to stressor OR causing significant functional impairment
  • Duration: Symptoms resolve within 6 months of stressor ending
  • Doesn't meet criteria for MDD

Grief / Bereavement:
  • Recent loss (death of loved one)
  • Normal grief: Waves of sadness triggered by reminders, able to experience positive emotions, no pervasive worthlessness/suicidal ideation
  • Complicated grief / MDD: Persistent, pervasive low mood, anhedonia, guilt/worthlessness not related to deceased, suicidal ideation, functional impairment >2 months
  • Can have grief AND depression concurrently

ANXIETY

Immediate Differential:
  1. Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
  2. Panic Disorder
  3. Social Anxiety Disorder (Social Phobia)
  4. Specific Phobia
  5. Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)
  6. Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)
  7. Substance-induced: Caffeine excess, alcohol/benzodiazepine withdrawal, amphetamines, cannabis
  8. Organic:

- Hyperthyroidism (tremor, heat intolerance, weight loss, palpitations) - Cardiac: MI, arrhythmias, valvular disease - Respiratory: Asthma, COPD, pulmonary embolism - Endocrine: Pheochromocytoma (rare), hypoglycemia

Screening for Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

Core symptom: Excessive worry about multiple domains (work, health, family, finances) for ‚â•6 months, difficult to control GAD-7 Screening Questions:

"Over the past 2 weeks, how often have you been bothered by:"

  1. Feeling nervous, anxious, or on edge
  2. Not being able to stop or control worrying
  3. Worrying too much about different things
  4. Trouble relaxing
  5. Being so restless that it's hard to sit still
  6. Becoming easily annoyed or irritable
  7. Feeling afraid as if something awful might happen

Scoring: 0 (not at all), 1 (several days), 2 (more than half the days), 3 (nearly every day)
  • Total 0-4: Minimal anxiety
  • Total 5-9: Mild anxiety
  • Total 10-14: Moderate anxiety
  • Total 15-21: Severe anxiety

Physical symptoms of anxiety (ask specifically):
  • Palpitations, chest tightness
  • Shortness of breath, hyperventilation
  • Tremor, sweating
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness
  • Nausea, abdominal discomfort, diarrhea
  • Muscle tension, aches
  • Tingling/numbness (hyperventilation-induced)

Screening for Panic Disorder

Panic attack = Discrete episode of intense fear/discomfort, reaching peak within 10 minutes, with ‚â•4 of:
  • Palpitations, racing heart
  • Sweating
  • Trembling, shaking
  • Shortness of breath, feeling of choking
  • Chest pain/discomfort
  • Nausea, abdominal distress
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness, faint
  • Derealization (feelings of unreality) or depersonalization (feeling detached from self)
  • Fear of losing control or "going crazy"
  • Fear of dying
  • Numbness/tingling
  • Chills or hot flushes

Questions:
  • "Do you have sudden episodes of intense fear or anxiety that come on quickly?"
  • "During these episodes, do you have physical symptoms like racing heart, sweating, shortness of breath?"
  • "Do you worry about having another panic attack?"
  • "Do you avoid situations where panic attacks have occurred?"

Panic Disorder = Recurrent unexpected panic attacks + ‚â•1 month of:
  • Persistent worry about having another panic attack
  • Maladaptive change in behavior to avoid attacks (agoraphobia)

Screening for PTSD

Ask about trauma (be sensitive):
  • "Have you experienced or witnessed any traumatic events?" (life-threatening, serious injury, sexual violence)

If yes, screen for PTSD symptoms (‚â•1 month duration): 1. Intrusion symptoms (re-experiencing):
  • "Do you have distressing memories or nightmares about the event?"
  • "Do you feel like it's happening again (flashbacks)?"
  • "Do you get very upset when reminded of the event?"

2. Avoidance:
  • "Do you avoid thinking or talking about the event?"
  • "Do you avoid places, people, or activities that remind you of it?"

3. Negative alterations in cognition/mood:
  • "Do you have trouble remembering parts of the event?"
  • "Do you blame yourself or others for what happened?"
  • "Do you feel detached from others?"
  • "Have you lost interest in activities?"

4. Hyperarousal:
  • "Are you more irritable or angry than before?"
  • "Do you feel on guard or easily startled?"
  • "Do you have trouble concentrating or sleeping?"

Differentiating Anxiety Disorders

| Disorder | Key Discriminator | |----------|------------------| | GAD | Excessive worry about MULTIPLE domains, ‚â•6 months, difficult to control | | Panic Disorder | Recurrent UNEXPECTED panic attacks, worry about future attacks, avoidance | | Social Anxiety | Fear/anxiety specific to SOCIAL situations (public speaking, eating in public, meeting new people), fear of negative evaluation | | Specific Phobia | Fear/anxiety specific to ONE object/situation (heights, spiders, flying, blood/needles) | | PTSD | Trauma history, intrusion symptoms (flashbacks, nightmares), avoidance, hyperarousal ‚â•1 month | | OCD | Obsessions (intrusive thoughts) + Compulsions (repetitive behaviors to reduce anxiety) |

PSYCHOSIS (Hallucinations, Delusions, Disorganized Thinking)

Immediate Differential:
  1. Schizophrenia (chronic, >6 months)
  2. Schizophreniform Disorder (1-6 months)
  3. Brief Psychotic Disorder (<1 month)
  4. Drug-Induced Psychosis: Amphetamines, cannabis, cocaine, synthetic cannabinoids ("spice"), steroids, L-DOPA
  5. Affective Psychosis:

- Bipolar disorder with psychotic features (mania or depression) - Major depression with psychotic features

  1. Delirium (acute confusional state - fluctuating consciousness, inattention, disorientation)
  2. Organic:

- Brain tumor, stroke, encephalitis, SLE, HIV, syphilis (neurosyphilis) - Temporal lobe epilepsy (complex partial seizures - may have olfactory/gustatory hallucinations) - Dementia (Lewy body dementia - visual hallucinations)

Screening for Psychotic Symptoms

Hallucinations (perception without external stimulus): Auditory (most common in schizophrenia):
  • "Do you ever hear things that other people don't hear?"
  • "Do you hear voices when no one is around?"
  • If yes: "How many voices? Male or female? What do they say?"

- Running commentary: Voices commenting on patient's actions - Third-person: Voices talking about patient ("He's stupid", "She's going to hurt herself") - Command hallucinations: Voices telling patient to do things ‚Üí Risk assessment needed (What do they tell you to do? Do you feel compelled to obey?)

Visual (more common in organic psychosis, delirium, Lewy body dementia):
  • "Do you see things that others don't see?"
  • üö® RED FLAG: New-onset visual hallucinations ‚Üí Think organic cause, delirium

Olfactory/Gustatory (smell/taste - temporal lobe epilepsy, brain tumor):
  • "Do you smell or taste unusual things that others don't?"

Tactile (touch - formication in cocaine/amphetamine use, alcohol withdrawal):
  • "Do you feel sensations on your skin like bugs crawling?"

Delusions (fixed, false beliefs not shared by cultural/religious group): Screening questions:
  • "Do you have any beliefs that others find unusual?"
  • "Do you feel people are trying to harm you or plot against you?" (Persecutory delusion)
  • "Do you feel you have special powers or abilities?" (Grandiose delusion)
  • "Do you feel people are sending you special messages through TV/radio/internet?" (Ideas of reference)
  • "Do you feel your thoughts are being controlled by an outside force?" (Thought insertion, withdrawal, broadcasting - first-rank symptoms of schizophrenia)

Types of delusions:
  • Persecutory: Being followed, spied on, poisoned, plotted against (most common)
  • Grandiose: Special powers, wealth, identity (e.g., "I'm the son of God")
  • Referential: Events have special personal significance (e.g., "TV news is about me")
  • Erotomanic: False belief that someone (usually higher status) is in love with patient
  • Nihilistic: Belief that self, others, or world doesn't exist or is about to end
  • Somatic: Belief about body (e.g., "I have cancer", "Worms are eating my brain")

Disorganized thinking (assess through speech):
  • Tangential (goes off-topic, doesn't return)
  • Circumstantial (eventually gets to point, but very indirect)
  • Loose associations (knight's move thinking - jumps between unrelated topics)
  • Word salad (incomprehensible speech)
  • Neologisms (made-up words)

Differentiating Psychotic Disorders

Schizophrenia (DSM-5 criteria):
  • ‚â•2 symptoms (for significant portion of 1 month), at least one must be (1), (2), or (3):

1. Delusions 2. Hallucinations 3. Disorganized speech 4. Grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior 5. Negative symptoms (flat affect, avolition, alogia, anhedonia)

  • Duration: ‚â•6 months (including prodrome, residual symptoms)
  • Functional decline: Work, relationships, self-care
  • Rule out substance, medical condition

Drug-Induced Psychosis:
  • Clear temporal relationship to substance use (within hours-days of use)
  • Resolves within days-weeks of stopping substance
  • Common drugs: Amphetamines, cannabis (esp. high-THC strains), cocaine, synthetic cannabinoids, steroids

Delirium (vs psychosis):
  • Acute onset (hours-days)
  • Fluctuating consciousness, worse at night (sundowning)
  • Inattention (cannot focus, easily distracted)
  • Disorientation (time, place, person)
  • Reversible (treat underlying cause)

MANIA (Elevated Mood, Increased Energy)

Immediate Differential:
  1. Bipolar Disorder Type I (mania)
  2. Bipolar Disorder Type II (hypomania)
  3. Drug-Induced: Amphetamines, cocaine, steroids, antidepressants (can trigger mania in bipolar)
  4. Organic: Hyperthyroidism, frontal lobe tumor/stroke, SLE, HIV

Screening for Mania

Core symptoms: Elevated, expansive, or irritable mood + increased energy/activity for ‚â•1 week (mania) or ‚â•4 days (hypomania) DIG FAST mnemonic (‚â•3 needed): D - Distractibility
  • "Do you find your thoughts jumping from one thing to another?"

I - Irresponsibility / Indiscretion
  • Excessive involvement in pleasurable activities with potential for painful consequences
  • "Have you done things recently that are out of character? Spent money you don't have? Had risky sexual encounters?"

G - Grandiosity
  • "Do you feel more confident than usual?"
  • "Do you feel you have special powers or abilities?"

F - Flight of ideas
  • Racing thoughts
  • "Do your thoughts race? Do you have so many ideas you can't keep up?"

A - Activity increase / Agitation
  • Increased goal-directed activity (work, social, sexual)
  • Psychomotor agitation

S - Sleep (decreased need)
  • "How much sleep are you getting?"
  • "Do you feel rested after only a few hours of sleep?" (not insomnia - genuinely need less sleep)

T - Talkativeness
  • Pressured speech (rapid, difficult to interrupt)
  • Observe: Is patient talking excessively, rapidly, loudly?

Mania vs Hypomania:

| Feature | Mania | Hypomania | |---------|-------|-----------| | Duration | ‚â•7 days | ‚â•4 days | | Severity | Marked impairment in function, may require hospitalization, may have psychotic features | No marked impairment, no psychotic features, no hospitalization | | Diagnosis | Bipolar I | Bipolar II |

Mixed Episode: Features of both mania AND depression simultaneously (e.g., elevated mood + suicidal thoughts, or depressed mood + racing thoughts, increased energy)

Step 3: Years of Symptoms (Onset, Course, Triggers)

Onset:
  • "When did you first notice these symptoms?"
  • "Was there anything happening in your life at that time?" (Triggers: stressors, life events, trauma)

Course:
  • First episode vs Recurrent
  • Continuous vs Episodic (periods of wellness between episodes)
  • Getting better, worse, or staying the same?

Triggers:
  • Life events (job loss, relationship breakdown, bereavement, trauma)
  • Seasonal pattern (seasonal affective disorder - depression in winter)
  • Menstrual cycle (premenstrual dysphoric disorder)

Previous similar episodes:
  • "Have you felt like this before?"
  • If yes: "How many times? What helped? What didn't help?"

Step 4: Collateral History

Why Essential in Psychiatry:
  • Patient may lack insight (psychosis, mania, dementia)
  • Patient may minimize (substance use, eating disorder)
  • Patient may be unable to provide history (severe depression, psychosis, confusion)

Sources:
  • Family, friends, partner
  • GP (previous medical records, medications)
  • Previous treating psychiatrist/psychologist
  • Hospital records (previous admissions)

How to Obtain:
  • With patient's consent (if patient has capacity)
  • Without consent if patient lacks capacity OR safety concern (risk to self/others)

What to Ask Collateral:
  • "What changes have you noticed in [patient's] behavior/mood?"
  • "How long have you noticed these changes?"
  • "Have you noticed any triggers?"
  • "Any substance use you're aware of?"
  • "Any concerns about safety (suicide, violence, self-neglect)?"
  • "What was [patient] like before this episode?" (Baseline functioning)

Step 5: History of Psychiatric Illness

Previous diagnoses:
  • "Have you been diagnosed with any mental health conditions before?"
  • "Depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, PTSD, eating disorder?"

Previous hospitalizations:
  • "Have you been admitted to a psychiatric unit before?"
  • If yes: "How many times? When was the most recent? How long? Voluntary or involuntary?"

Previous treatments:
  • See Step 7 below

Step 6: Impact on Function (Work, Relationships, Self-care)

Work/Study:
  • "Are you working/studying?"
  • "How have your symptoms affected your work/study?"
  • "Have you had time off? Lost your job?"

Relationships:
  • "How are your relationships with family/friends/partner?"
  • "Have your symptoms affected these relationships?"

Self-care:
  • "How are you managing day-to-day tasks?"
  • "Are you eating regularly? Showering? Leaving the house?"
  • Severe depression: Patient may be bed-bound, not eating, not washing

Activities of Daily Living (ADLs):
  • "Can you cook, clean, manage finances, shop for groceries?"

Social activities:
  • "What do you do for fun?"
  • "Have you withdrawn from activities you used to enjoy?"

Step 7: Associated Substance Use

CRITICAL: Substance use can CAUSE or EXACERBATE psychiatric symptoms Alcohol:
  • "Do you drink alcohol? How much per week?"
  • AUDIT-C (quick screen):

1. "How often do you have a drink containing alcohol?" (0 = Never to 4 = 4+ times/week) 2. "How many standard drinks do you have on a typical day?" (0 = 1-2 to 4 = 10+) 3. "How often do you have 6 or more standard drinks on one occasion?" (0 = Never to 4 = Daily) - Score ‚â•4 (men) or ‚â•3 (women) = Possible alcohol use disorder

  • Standard drink in Australia: 10g alcohol = 100mL wine, 30mL spirits, 285mL beer

Illicit drugs:
  • "Do you use any recreational drugs?"
  • Non-judgmental tone, normalize: "Many people use substances to cope with stress. I need to ask to give you the best care."
  • Ask specifically: Cannabis, amphetamines (ice, speed), cocaine, MDMA (ecstasy), heroin, synthetic drugs

Prescription medications:
  • "Are you taking any medications prescribed by a doctor?"
  • Benzodiazepines (diazepam, alprazolam): Dependence, withdrawal
  • Opioids (oxycodone, codeine): Dependence, misuse
  • Stimulants (dexamphetamine for ADHD): Misuse potential

Caffeine:
  • "How much coffee/energy drinks do you have per day?"
  • Excessive caffeine (>400mg/day = 4 cups coffee) ‚Üí Anxiety, palpitations, insomnia

Nicotine:
  • "Do you smoke? How many per day?"
  • Smoking rates higher in psychiatric patients

Dependence screen (if substance use identified):
  • "Have you tried to cut down but couldn't?"
  • "Do you need more to get the same effect?" (Tolerance)
  • "Do you get withdrawal symptoms when you stop?" (Withdrawal)
  • "Does it interfere with your life?" (Work, relationships, legal problems)

Step 8: Treatment History

Medications:
  • "Have you tried any medications for your mental health?"
  • If yes: "Which ones? What dose? How long? Did they help? Any side effects? Why did you stop?"
  • Document: Previous antidepressants (SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs, MAOIs), antipsychotics, mood stabilizers, benzodiazepines

Psychotherapy:
  • "Have you seen a psychologist or counselor?"
  • If yes: "What type of therapy? (CBT, DBT, psychodynamic) How long? Was it helpful?"

ECT (Electroconvulsive Therapy):
  • "Have you ever had ECT?"
  • Used for: Severe depression (especially with psychotic features), catatonia, treatment-resistant mania

Hospitalizations:
  • Number, duration, voluntary vs involuntary (Mental Health Act)

Current treatment:
  • "Are you taking any medications currently?"
  • "Are you seeing a psychiatrist, psychologist, or counselor?"

Step 9: Risk Assessment (Integrated Throughout History)

CRITICAL: Risk assessment is NOT a separate section at the end - integrate throughout consultation

Suicide Risk

Screen ALL patients with depression, anxiety, psychosis, substance use Graduated approach (start gently, become more specific): Level 1: Hopelessness
  • "How do you see the future?"
  • "Do you feel hopeless?" (Strong predictor of suicide)

Level 2: Passive suicidal ideation
  • "Do you ever feel life isn't worth living?"
  • "Do you ever wish you could go to sleep and not wake up?"

Level 3: Active suicidal ideation
  • "Do you have thoughts of harming yourself or ending your life?"
  • If yes ‚Üí Assess severity:

- Frequency: "How often do you have these thoughts?" - Intent: "Do you intend to act on these thoughts?" - Plan: "Have you thought about how you would do it?" (Specific plan = higher risk) - Access to means: "Do you have access to [method]?" (e.g., tablets, rope, firearms) - Timeline: "When are you planning to do this?" - Protective factors: "What's stopped you so far?" (Family, pets, religious beliefs, fear of pain)

Level 4: Suicide attempts
  • "Have you ever tried to harm yourself or attempt suicide?"
  • If yes: "When? How? What happened? Did you receive medical treatment?"
  • Recent attempt = HIGH RISK

Level 5: Self-harm (non-suicidal)
  • "Do you harm yourself in other ways?" (Cutting, burning, head-banging)
  • Differentiate: Non-suicidal self-harm (to relieve emotional pain, no intent to die) vs Suicide attempt (intent to die)

Risk Factors for Suicide (SAD PERSONS): S - Sex (Male > Female for completed suicide, though females have higher attempt rate) A - Age (<25 or >65) D - Depression (especially severe, with hopelessness, psychotic features) P - Previous attempts (strongest predictor) E - Ethanol/substance use R - Rational thinking loss (psychosis, impulsivity) S - Social support lacking (isolated, divorced, bereaved) O - Organized plan (specific, lethal method, access to means) N - No spouse (single, divorced, widowed) S - Sickness (chronic illness, chronic pain, terminal illness) Immediate risk (requires URGENT intervention):
  • Current suicidal ideation + intent + plan + access to means
  • Recent suicide attempt
  • Command hallucinations telling patient to harm self
  • Severe hopelessness, agitation
  • Intoxication
  • No protective factors

Management of immediate risk:
  • Do not leave patient alone
  • Remove means (medications, sharps, ligature points)
  • Psychiatric assessment URGENTly (psychiatry team, Crisis Assessment Team)
  • Consider involuntary admission (Mental Health Act - see later)

Violence Risk

Screen if: History of violence, forensic history, current anger/irritability, paranoid delusions, command hallucinations Questions:
  • "Do you ever feel angry or aggressive?"
  • "Have you ever hurt someone else?"
  • "Do you have thoughts of harming anyone?"
  • If yes: "Who? Why? Do you have a plan? Access to weapons?"

Risk factors:
  • Previous violence (strongest predictor)
  • Substance use (alcohol, amphetamines)
  • Psychosis (especially paranoid delusions, command hallucinations)
  • Antisocial personality disorder
  • Male gender
  • Young age

Management:
  • Ensure safety (staff, other patients, self)
  • De-escalation (calm tone, non-threatening posture, offer food/drink, negotiate)
  • Medications (IM antipsychotic, benzodiazepine if needed)
  • Seclusion/restraint (last resort, documented, reviewed regularly)

Self-Neglect / Vulnerability

Screen if: Severe depression, psychosis, dementia, eating disorder, homelessness Questions:
  • "Are you eating regularly?"
  • "Are you taking care of yourself - showering, changing clothes?"
  • "Where are you living? Do you have stable housing?"

Risk factors:
  • Severe mental illness (catatonia, severe depression, dementia)
  • Homelessness
  • Financial exploitation
  • Physical health problems (diabetes, wounds)

Management:
  • Social work referral
  • Community mental health team (home visits, medication supervision)
  • Guardian (if lacks capacity for self-care)

Step 10: Insight and Understanding

Insight = Patient's awareness of having mental illness and need for treatment Ask:
  • "What do you think is causing how you're feeling?"
  • "Do you think you have a mental health condition?"
  • "Do you think you need treatment?"

Levels of insight:
  • Full insight: "I have depression and need treatment"
  • Partial insight: "I'm not well but I don't think it's mental illness / I don't think I need medication"
  • No insight: "There's nothing wrong with me, I don't need help" (common in psychosis, mania)

Capacity to consent to treatment:
  • Can patient understand information, retain it, weigh options, communicate decision?
  • If lacks capacity ‚Üí May need involuntary treatment (Mental Health Act)

Step 11: Co-morbid Medical Illness

ALWAYS ask about physical health:
  • "Do you have any medical conditions?"
  • Chronic illnesses: Diabetes, heart disease, cancer, chronic pain, HIV, hepatitis C
  • Bidirectional relationship: Physical illness ‚Üí depression; depression ‚Üí poor medical outcomes

Medications:
  • "What medications do you take?" (including over-the-counter, herbal)
  • Medications that can cause/worsen psychiatric symptoms:

- Depression: Beta-blockers, steroids, isotretinoin, interferon, some anticonvulsants - Anxiety: Stimulants, bronchodilators (salbutamol), thyroid hormones - Psychosis: Steroids, L-DOPA, high-dose steroids - Mania: Steroids, antidepressants (in bipolar)

Recent medical investigations:
  • "Have you had any blood tests or scans recently?"
  • Especially if new-onset psychiatric symptoms ‚Üí May need investigations to rule out organic cause

Organic Causes of Psychiatric Symptoms (üö® RED FLAGs)

When to suspect organic cause:
  • New-onset psychiatric symptoms in older adult (>65 years)
  • Acute onset (hours-days)
  • Fluctuating course
  • Visual hallucinations (more common in organic than functional psychosis)
  • Neurological signs (confusion, disorientation, memory impairment, seizures, focal neurology)
  • No psychiatric history
  • Treatment-resistant (doesn't respond to standard psychiatric treatment)

Investigations to Consider (if organic cause suspected): Bedside:
  • Blood glucose (hypoglycemia, hyperglycemia)
  • Urine drug screen (amphetamines, cannabis, benzodiazepines, opioids)

Blood tests:
  • FBC (anemia, infection)
  • U&E (hyponatremia ‚Üí confusion, psychosis)
  • LFTs (hepatic encephalopathy)
  • TFTs (hyperthyroidism ‚Üí anxiety, mania; hypothyroidism ‚Üí depression)
  • Calcium (hypercalcemia ‚Üí confusion, depression)
  • Vitamin B12, folate (deficiency ‚Üí depression, cognitive impairment)
  • CRP (infection, inflammation)
  • Syphilis serology (neurosyphilis)
  • HIV (HIV encephalopathy)

Imaging:
  • CT head (stroke, tumor, bleed)
  • MRI brain (more sensitive - encephalitis, dementia, MS, tumor)

Other:
  • EEG (seizures - temporal lobe epilepsy can present with psychosis)
  • Lumbar puncture (if encephalitis, meningitis suspected)

Common IMG Mistakes in Psychiatric History-Taking

Mistake 1: Not Ruling Out Organic Causes

Problem: Assuming all psychiatric symptoms are functional (primary psychiatric illness), not asking about physical health, medications, substance use Why It's Wrong:
  • Medical illnesses can present with psychiatric symptoms (hypothyroidism ‚Üí depression, hyperthyroidism ‚Üí anxiety, brain tumor ‚Üí psychosis)
  • Medications can cause symptoms (steroids ‚Üí psychosis/mania)
  • Substance use can mimic psychiatric disorders (amphetamines ‚Üí psychosis, alcohol withdrawal ‚Üí anxiety/seizures)
  • Missing organic cause ‚Üí Delays appropriate treatment, medicolegal risk

Correct Approach:
  • ALWAYS ask: "Do you have any medical conditions? What medications do you take? Do you use alcohol or drugs?"
  • üö® RED FLAGs for organic: New-onset in older adult, acute onset, visual hallucinations, neurological signs
  • Investigate if suspected: Bloods (FBC, U&E, TFTs, B12, glucose), imaging (CT/MRI brain)

Mistake 2: Not Asking About Suicidal Thoughts

Problem: Afraid to ask about suicide, worry it will "plant the idea" Why It's Wrong:
  • Asking about suicide does NOT increase risk - research shows it REDUCES distress
  • Suicide is leading cause of death in young adults in Australia
  • Failing to ask ‚Üí Miss high-risk patient ‚Üí Preventable death
  • Medicolegal risk: If patient dies by suicide and you didn't ask/document

Correct Approach:
  • ALWAYS screen for suicidal thoughts in patients with depression, anxiety, psychosis, substance use
  • Use graduated approach (hopelessness ‚Üí passive ‚Üí active ‚Üí plan ‚Üí attempt)
  • Document thoroughly: "Denies current suicidal ideation, no plan, no recent attempts. Identified protective factors: supportive family, religious beliefs."

Mistake 3: Not Seeking Collateral History

Problem: Relying solely on patient's account, not contacting family/GP/previous treating team Why It's Wrong:
  • Patient may lack insight (psychosis, mania), minimize (substance use), or be unable to provide history (severe depression, confusion)
  • Collateral provides crucial information (baseline functioning, triggers, previous treatments, substance use)
  • Missing information ‚Üí Inaccurate diagnosis, inappropriate treatment

Correct Approach:
  • ALWAYS seek collateral in psychiatric presentations
  • With patient's consent (or without if lacks capacity/safety concern)
  • Ask family: "What changes have you noticed? How long? Any substance use? Safety concerns?"

Mistake 4: Not Screening for Bipolar in Depression

Problem: Patient presents with depression, you diagnose MDD without asking about mania/hypomania Why It's Wrong:
  • ~20% of patients with depression have bipolar disorder
  • Antidepressants alone in bipolar ‚Üí Can trigger mania/rapid cycling
  • Need mood stabilizer (lithium, valproate, antipsychotic) not just antidepressant

Correct Approach:
  • ALWAYS ask about mania/hypomania in ANY patient with depression:

- "Have you ever had periods where your mood was the opposite - very high, energized, needed less sleep?"

  • Screen with DIG FAST if yes

Mistake 5: Using Medical Jargon

Problem: "You have anhedonia with neurovegetative symptoms and psychomotor retardation" Why It's Wrong:
  • Patient doesn't understand
  • Damages rapport, reduces engagement with treatment

Correct Approach:
  • Plain language: "You've lost interest in things you used to enjoy, you're sleeping poorly, and everything feels slowed down"
  • Check understanding: "Does that make sense?"

Mistake 6: Not Assessing Functional Impairment

Problem: Documenting symptoms without asking how they affect patient's life Why It's Wrong:
  • Functional impairment is KEY to diagnosis (DSM-5 requires symptoms cause "significant distress or impairment")
  • Severity assessment guides treatment (mild depression ‚Üí psychological therapy first; severe depression ‚Üí medication + therapy)
  • Monitoring: Functional improvement = treatment working

Correct Approach:
  • ALWAYS ask: "How have these symptoms affected your work/study/relationships/self-care?"
  • Document: "Patient reports symptoms significantly impair function - unable to work for past 4 weeks, withdrawn from friends, struggling with self-care."

Mistake 7: Being Judgmental About Substance Use

Problem: "Why are you using drugs?" (judgmental tone) Why It's Wrong:
  • Patient feels judged, shamed ‚Üí Stops disclosing ‚Üí Inaccurate history
  • Substance use is often coping mechanism for mental illness (self-medication)

Correct Approach:
  • Non-judgmental, normalize: "Many people use substances to cope with stress. I need to ask so I can give you the best care."
  • "Do you use any recreational drugs or alcohol? If so, what and how much?"
  • Harm minimization approach, not abstinence-only (meet patient where they are)
