Capacity Assessment and Legal Framework

ICRP OSCE Preparation - Psychiatry Module

Target Audience

This guide is for International Medical Graduates (IMGs) preparing for the AMC Clinical OSCE, specifically:

  • IMGs needing to understand Australian mental health legislation (Mental Health Act NSW 2007)
  • Candidates requiring frameworks for capacity assessment
  • Doctors preparing for ICRP placement in Australian hospitals
  • Medical professionals navigating consent in psychiatric contexts
  • Clinicians needing to understand involuntary treatment, guardianship, and advance care planning

Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  1. Assess mental capacity using the four-element framework (understand, retain, use/weigh, communicate)
  2. Apply Mental Health Act NSW 2007 criteria for involuntary treatment
  3. Distinguish between different Mental Health Act forms (Form 1, Schedule 1, Community Treatment Order)
  4. Navigate consent in psychiatric settings (voluntary vs involuntary, ECT, medication)
  5. Understand guardianship, advance care directives, and substitute decision-making
  6. Manage common scenarios (patient refusing treatment, discharge against medical advice)
  7. Avoid common IMG mistakes in capacity assessment and legal framework
  8. Perform confidently in OSCE stations involving capacity and consent

Dr. Amir Soufi's Approach Applied to Capacity Assessment

Core Principle: SYSTEMATIC ASSESSMENT with CLEAR DOCUMENTATION

Traditional Approach (Inadequate):
  • Vague statement: "Patient lacks capacity"
  • No structured assessment
  • Assumption that psychiatric diagnosis = lack of capacity
  • Poor documentation

Dr. Soufi's Systematic Approach:
  1. DECISION-SPECIFIC: Capacity is assessed for a SPECIFIC decision (not global)

- Patient may have capacity for some decisions but not others - Example: Patient may have capacity to consent to blood test but not to discharge against medical advice

  1. TIME-SPECIFIC: Capacity can fluctuate

- Patient may lack capacity now (acute psychosis, intoxication, delirium) but regain it later - Reassess as clinical state changes

  1. FOUR-ELEMENT FRAMEWORK (Structured Assessment):

- Understand information relevant to decision - Retain information long enough to make decision - Use and Weigh information to make decision - Communicate decision - Mnemonic: U-R-U-W-C

  1. ASSUME CAPACITY unless proven otherwise

- Everyone (including psychiatric patients) assumed to have capacity until assessment demonstrates otherwise - Mental illness ‚â† automatic lack of capacity

  1. MAXIMIZE CAPACITY: Support patient to have capacity

- Optimize timing (when patient not acutely unwell) - Optimize environment (quiet, supportive) - Optimize communication (simple language, visual aids, interpreter) - Optimize treatment (treat delirium, pain, anxiety)

  1. DOCUMENT THOROUGHLY: Medicolegal protection

- What decision is being assessed - All four elements assessed with specific evidence - Conclusion: Has capacity / Lacks capacity (with rationale)

Example:
  • Decision: Consent to voluntary psychiatric admission
  • Assessment:

- Understand: "Can you tell me why we've recommended admission?" ‚Üí Patient: "You think I'm depressed and might hurt myself"‚úì - Retain: Able to recall information after 5 minutes ‚úì - Use/Weigh: "What are the pros and cons of admission?" ‚Üí Patient: "Pros: Get help, be safe. Cons: Miss work, worried about stigma. But I understand I need help." ‚úì - Communicate: Verbally communicates decision ‚úì

  • Conclusion: Patient HAS capacity to consent to voluntary admission

1. MENTAL CAPACITY ASSESSMENT

What is Mental Capacity?

Capacity (Mental Capacity): The ability to make a decision Key Principles:
  1. Assume capacity unless proven otherwise
  2. Decision-specific: Assessed for each specific decision (not global)
  3. Time-specific: Can fluctuate (reassess as clinical state changes)
  4. Maximize capacity: Take steps to support patient to have capacity
  5. Mental illness ‚â† lack of capacity: Many patients with psychiatric illness have capacity

Four-Element Framework for Capacity Assessment

U-R-U-W-C Mnemonic:
  1. Understand
  2. Retain
  3. Use and Weigh
  4. Communicate

Element 1: UNDERSTAND

Can the patient understand information relevant to the decision? How to assess:
  • Explain the information (diagnosis, treatment options, risks/benefits, alternatives)
  • Ask patient to explain back in their own words

Questions:
  • "Can you tell me what I've just explained?"
  • "What is your understanding of your condition?"
  • "What is this treatment for?"

Example:
  • Doctor: "You have pneumonia, a lung infection. We recommend antibiotics."
  • Doctor: "Can you tell me what I've just said?"
  • Patient: "I have an infection in my lungs. You want to give me antibiotics to treat it." ‚úì Understands

Factors that impair understanding:
  • Cognitive impairment (dementia, intellectual disability, delirium)
  • Psychosis (if delusions interfere with understanding)
  • Language barrier (needs interpreter)
  • Poor explanation by doctor (use simple language, avoid jargon)

Element 2: RETAIN

Can the patient retain information long enough to make a decision? How to assess:
  • After explaining, wait a few minutes
  • Ask patient to recall the information

Questions:
  • "Can you tell me again what we discussed?"

Note:
  • Patient does NOT need to retain information indefinitely
  • Only needs to retain long enough to make the decision
  • Severe memory impairment (advanced dementia, Korsakoff syndrome) may prevent retention

Example:
  • Doctor explains treatment
  • 5 minutes later: "Can you tell me what we discussed about your treatment?"
  • Patient: "You said I need antibiotics for my lung infection." ‚úì Retains

Element 3: USE and WEIGH

Can the patient use and weigh information to make a decision? This is the most complex element:
  • Can patient appreciate how information applies to them?
  • Can patient weigh pros and cons?
  • Can patient arrive at a decision using reasoning?

How to assess:
  • Ask patient to discuss pros and cons of options
  • Ask how information applies to their situation
  • Observe reasoning process

Questions:
  • "What are the benefits of this treatment?"
  • "What are the risks or downsides?"
  • "What might happen if you don't have this treatment?"
  • "How do you feel about these options?"
  • "How did you arrive at your decision?"

Example:
  • Doctor: "What are the pros and cons of taking antidepressants?"
  • Patient: "Pros: Might help my depression, improve my mood. Cons: Side effects like nausea, sexual dysfunction. But I think the benefits outweigh the risks because my depression is really affecting my life." ‚úì Uses and weighs

Factors that impair use/weigh:
  • Severe depression (hopelessness prevents weighing benefits: "Nothing will help")
  • Delusions (patient bases decision on false belief)
  • Severe anxiety (unable to process information rationally)
  • Mania (impulsive, lacks consideration of consequences)

Element 4: COMMUNICATE

Can the patient communicate their decision? How to assess:
  • Can patient express choice by any means (verbal, written, gesture, communication aid)?

Note:
  • Communication difficulty (aphasia, intubation, locked-in syndrome) ‚â† lack of capacity
  • Must provide alternative means of communication (writing, picture boards, eye blinks for yes/no)

Example:
  • Patient with stroke and aphasia
  • Uses picture board to point to "YES" for surgery ‚úì Communicates

RARELY the issue in capacity assessment (unless severe impairment of consciousness)

Common Scenarios and Capacity

Scenario 1: Refusing Treatment in Depression

Case: 55-year-old woman with severe depression refuses antidepressants Capacity Assessment:
  • Understand: ‚úì "You think I have depression and antidepressants might help"
  • Retain: ‚úì Able to recall
  • Use/Weigh: ‚úó "Nothing will help, I'm beyond help, there's no point" (Hopelessness from depression prevents weighing potential benefits)
  • Communicate: ‚úì "I don't want medication"

Conclusion: LACKS capacity for this decision (unable to use and weigh due to severe depression with hopelessness) Plan: Treat in patient's best interests (start antidepressant), reassess capacity as depression improves

Scenario 2: Refusing Admission in Psychosis

Case: 28-year-old man with paranoid delusions refuses psychiatric admission Capacity Assessment:
  • Understand: ‚úó "You're part of the conspiracy to imprison me. There's nothing wrong with me." (Delusions interfere with understanding that he is unwell)
  • Retain: Cannot assess (doesn't understand information)
  • Use/Weigh: ‚úó Decision based on delusional beliefs (government conspiracy)
  • Communicate: ‚úì "I'm not staying here"

Conclusion: LACKS capacity for this decision (unable to understand due to delusions that he is unwell) Plan: Involuntary admission under Mental Health Act (Schedule 1)

Scenario 3: Discharge Against Medical Advice - Has Capacity

Case: 35-year-old woman with mild depression wants to discharge against medical advice Capacity Assessment:
  • Understand: ‚úì "I know you think I should stay, and I understand there's a risk I might get worse at home"
  • Retain: ‚úì Able to recall
  • Use/Weigh: ‚úì "I know the risks, but I have support at home from my husband. I'll see my GP tomorrow. I understand I can come back if I get worse. I just want to be at home with my kids."
  • Communicate: ‚úì "I want to leave"

Conclusion: HAS capacity for this decision (all four elements met) Plan: Respect patient's decision even though you disagree. Document thoroughly. Provide safety plan (GP appointment, crisis numbers, return if worsens). Document "patient has capacity, declines admission, risks explained, safety plan provided, patient agrees to follow-up."

Maximizing Capacity (Optimize Before Assessing)

Before concluding patient lacks capacity, maximize their ability to have capacity:
  1. Optimize timing:

- Avoid assessing when patient acutely unwell (delirium, intoxication, post-ictal) - Reassess when patient more settled - Consider time of day (some patients better in morning)

  1. Optimize environment:

- Quiet, private space (reduce distractions) - Familiar person present (family member for reassurance) - Remove stressors

  1. Optimize communication:

- Simple language (avoid medical jargon) - Visual aids (diagrams, pictures, written information) - Interpreter if language barrier - Communication aids if aphasia, hearing impairment - Check understanding regularly ("Can you tell me what I've just explained?")

  1. Optimize medical treatment:

- Treat delirium (common reversible cause of impaired capacity) - Treat pain (impairs concentration) - Treat acute psychosis/mania (if safe to do so, capacity may improve)

Example:
  • Patient with delirium (UTI, new confusion) ‚Üí Treat UTI, wait 24-48 hours ‚Üí Reassess capacity when delirium resolved

2. MENTAL HEALTH ACT NSW 2007

Overview

Mental Health Act NSW 2007: Legislation governing involuntary assessment and treatment of mental illness in New South Wales Key Principles:
  • Least restrictive care
  • Voluntary treatment preferred
  • Involuntary treatment only when criteria met
  • Patients' rights protected
  • Tribunal review

Criteria for Involuntary Treatment (Schedule 1)

ALL THREE criteria must be met:
  1. Mental illness (defined in Act)
  2. Risk of serious harm to self or others, OR serious deterioration
  3. Involuntary treatment necessary (no less restrictive alternative)

Let's explore each:

Criterion 1: Mental Illness (Defined in Mental Health Act)

Mental illness means:
  • Condition that seriously impairs mental functioning
  • Characterized by disturbance of:

- Thought - Mood - Perception - Memory - OR behavior

Includes:
  • Schizophrenia, psychotic disorders
  • Major depressive disorder
  • Bipolar disorder
  • Severe anxiety disorders (if seriously impairing)

EXCLUDES (cannot use Mental Health Act for):
  • Substance intoxication alone (cannot schedule someone purely for being intoxicated)

- Exception: If intoxication reveals underlying mental illness

  • Personality disorder alone (unless co-occurring mental illness)
  • Intellectual disability alone
  • Developmental disorder alone

Criterion 2: Risk of Serious Harm

A. Risk of serious harm to self OR others:
  • Suicide risk (active ideation with plan/intent, previous attempts)
  • Self-harm risk (severe, recurrent)
  • Violence risk (to others)
  • Self-neglect (not eating, drinking, placing self in danger)

OR B. Risk of serious deterioration in patient's condition if not treated "Serious" = significant, not trivial

Criterion 3: Involuntary Treatment Necessary

No less restrictive alternative available Considerations:
  • Voluntary admission not possible (patient refuses, lacks insight)
  • Community treatment insufficient (patient too unwell)
  • Risk cannot be managed otherwise

Mental Health Act Forms

Form 1 (Medical Practitioner - 12 hours)

Who can complete: ANY medical practitioner (GP, ED doctor, psychiatrist) Criteria: Reasonable grounds to believe person is mentally ill AND requires immediate assessment/treatment Duration: Up to 12 hours (patient can be detained for assessment) Purpose: Allows time for psychiatric assessment Process:
  1. Medical officer completes Form 1
  2. Patient detained in hospital (usually ED or psychiatric unit)
  3. Psychiatrist must assess within 12 hours
  4. Psychiatrist decides: Voluntary admission / Schedule 1 (involuntary) / Discharge

Documentation:
  • Mental state examination
  • Reason for Form 1 (mental illness, risk of serious harm)
  • "Form 1 completed due to [psychotic symptoms, suicide risk, etc.]. Patient detained for psychiatric assessment."

Schedule 1 (Psychiatrist - up to 3 months)

Who can complete: ONLY psychiatrist (not other medical practitioners) Criteria: ALL three criteria met (mental illness, risk of serious harm, involuntary treatment necessary) Duration: Up to 3 months initially (can be renewed) Process:
  1. Psychiatrist assesses patient (may be after Form 1, or directly)
  2. If criteria met, completes Schedule 1
  3. Patient becomes involuntary patient
  4. Mental Health Review Tribunal must review within 21 days

Involuntary patient:
  • Can be treated without consent (medication, ECT with Tribunal approval)
  • Cannot leave hospital (can be detained, brought back if absconds)
  • Rights: Access to legal representation, Tribunal review, second opinion

Community Treatment Order (CTO)

Purpose: Involuntary treatment in the community (not hospital) Who can make: Psychiatrist (authorized or staff specialist) Duration: Up to 6 months initially (renewable up to 12 months) Criteria: Same as Schedule 1, but can be managed in community with conditions Conditions (must be followed):
  • Attend appointments with psychiatrist, GP
  • Take prescribed medication
  • Reside at specified address
  • Abstain from drugs/alcohol (if relevant)

If patient breaches CTO: Can be brought to hospital for assessment, may be admitted involuntarily Benefit: Less restrictive than hospital admission, supports community living with treatment adherence

Mental Health Review Tribunal

Independent statutory body that reviews involuntary patients Reviews:
  • Every involuntary patient within 21 days of Schedule 1
  • Then at regular intervals (at least every 3 months)
  • Patient can request review

Tribunal panel:
  • Lawyer (presiding officer)
  • Psychiatrist
  • Lay person

Tribunal powers:
  • Affirm order (patient remains involuntary)
  • Revoke order (patient becomes voluntary or discharged)
  • Make CTO

Patient rights:
  • Legal representation
  • Present evidence, call witnesses
  • Appeal Tribunal decision

3. CONSENT IN PSYCHIATRY

Voluntary Patients

Voluntary patient = Patient admitted to psychiatric unit with consent Rights:
  • Can refuse treatment (if has capacity)
  • Can discharge themselves (unless converted to involuntary)
  • Same rights as any medical patient

Capacity assessment:
  • If patient has capacity ‚Üí Consent required for treatment
  • If patient lacks capacity ‚Üí Treat in best interests (or substitute decision-maker consents)

Can voluntary patient be converted to involuntary?
  • YES, if criteria met (mental illness, risk of serious harm, involuntary treatment necessary)
  • Patient wants to leave, but you assess they meet criteria ‚Üí Complete Form 1 (up to 12 hours), call psychiatry for Schedule 1 assessment

Involuntary Patients

Involuntary patient = Patient on Schedule 1 or CTO Treatment without consent:
  • Medication for mental illness can be given without consent
  • Restraint if necessary (physical, chemical) - must be proportionate, last resort
  • Seclusion if necessary (locked room) - must be reviewed regularly, documented

ECT (Electroconvulsive Therapy):
  • Requires Mental Health Review Tribunal approval (even for involuntary patients)
  • Exception: ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY (life-threatening, no time for Tribunal) - senior psychiatrist can authorize

Physical health treatment (non-psychiatric):
  • Still requires consent (or capacity assessment if patient lacks capacity)
  • Mental Health Act does NOT authorize treatment of physical illness without consent
  • Example: Involuntary psychiatric patient needs appendectomy ‚Üí Still need consent (or assess capacity)

Consent for ECT

ECT (Electroconvulsive Therapy): Treatment involving electrical stimulation of brain under general anesthesia Indications:
  • Severe depression (especially with psychotic features, catatonia, treatment-resistant)
  • Severe mania (treatment-resistant)
  • Catatonia
  • Schizophrenia (treatment-resistant)

Consent:
  • Voluntary patient with capacity ‚Üí Written informed consent required (same as for surgery)
  • Voluntary patient lacking capacity ‚Üí Substitute decision-maker can consent (or Tribunal if no substitute)
  • Involuntary patient ‚Üí Mental Health Review Tribunal approval required (even if patient consents)

- Exception: ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY (life-threatening) - senior psychiatrist can authorize without Tribunal

Information to provide (informed consent):
  • What ECT involves (general anesthesia, electrical stimulation)
  • Why recommended (indication)
  • Benefits (high efficacy for severe depression)
  • Risks: Memory loss (retrograde amnesia, usually temporary), confusion post-treatment, headache, rare risks (anesthesia complications)
  • Alternatives (medication, no treatment)
  • Can withdraw consent at any time

4. SUBSTITUTE DECISION-MAKING AND GUARDIANSHIP

Hierarchy of Decision-Makers (When Patient Lacks Capacity)

When patient lacks capacity and decision needs to be made: 1. Enduring Guardian (if appointed):
  • Person appointed by patient (when had capacity) to make decisions if they lose capacity
  • Appointed via legal document
  • Can make medical, lifestyle decisions (within scope of appointment)

2. Person Responsible (statutory hierarchy if no guardian appointed):
  • Spouse or de facto partner
  • Carer (unpaid)
  • Close friend or relative

3. NSW Civil and Administrative Tribunal (NCAT) (if no one above available, or disagreement):
  • Can appoint guardian
  • Can make specific decisions

If URGENT and no substitute decision-maker available:
  • Treat in patient's best interests (‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY doctrine)
  • Document: "‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY treatment provided in patient's best interests. Patient lacks capacity for decision. No substitute decision-maker immediately available."

Advance Care Directive (Advance Care Plan)

Advance Care Directive: Document prepared by person (when has capacity) stating treatment preferences if they lose capacity in future Not legally binding in NSW (unlike some jurisdictions), but should be strongly considered in best interests decision-making May state:
  • Treatments patient would want / not want
  • Values, beliefs (religious, cultural)
  • Preferred decision-maker

Example:
  • Patient with recurrent depression writes advance directive: "If I become severely depressed and lack capacity, I consent to ECT"
  • Patient becomes severely depressed, lacks capacity
  • Advance directive supports decision to proceed with ECT (with Tribunal approval if involuntary)

Guardianship Tribunal (NCAT Guardianship Division)

NSW Civil and Administrative Tribunal (NCAT) - Guardianship Division Functions:
  • Appoint guardians for adults who lack capacity (long-term)
  • Make specific medical decisions
  • Appoint financial managers

When to involve:
  • Patient lacks capacity for important decision
  • No substitute decision-maker available
  • Disagreement among family members about treatment
  • Concern about substitute decision-maker acting against patient's best interests

Applications:
  • Family, doctors, social workers can apply
  • Tribunal hears evidence, makes decision in patient's best interests

5. COMMON SCENARIOS IN OSCE

Scenario 1: Patient Refusing Medication (Voluntary Patient with Capacity)

Situation: Voluntary psychiatric patient with depression refuses antidepressant Approach:
  1. Explore reasons for refusal:

- "Can you tell me why you don't want to take the medication?" - Common reasons: Side effects, fear of addiction, stigma, prefer non-medication approach

  1. Assess capacity (U-R-U-W-C):

- Understand: "What is your understanding of why we've recommended medication?" - Use/weigh: "What are the pros and cons as you see them?"

  1. If HAS capacity:

- Respect decision (even if you disagree) - Explore alternatives: "I respect your decision. Would you consider psychological therapy? I can refer you to a psychologist through a Mental Health Plan." - Provide information: "If you change your mind, or if things get worse, please come back or call." - Document: "Patient has capacity, declines antidepressant, risks explained, psychological therapy offered, patient agrees to follow-up."

  1. If LACKS capacity (e.g., severe depression with hopelessness preventing weighing benefits):

- Treat in best interests - Involve substitute decision-maker if available - Document: "Patient lacks capacity for decision to refuse medication (unable to use and weigh information due to severe depression with hopelessness). Treatment commenced in patient's best interests."

KEY: Capacity determines whether you respect refusal
  • Has capacity ‚Üí Respect refusal
  • Lacks capacity ‚Üí Treat in best interests (may proceed despite refusal)

Scenario 2: Patient Wants to Leave (Discharge Against Medical Advice)

Situation: Voluntary patient with mental illness wants to leave hospital Approach:
  1. Explore reasons:

- "Can you tell me why you want to leave?" - Address concerns if possible (e.g., worried about children ‚Üí facilitate contact)

  1. Explain risks:

- "I'm concerned that leaving now might put you at risk because [specific risks]. Can we talk about this?"

  1. Assess capacity for decision to discharge:

- Understand: "What is your understanding of your condition and our recommendations?" - Use/weigh: "What are the risks of leaving now? What might happen if you leave?"

  1. Does patient meet criteria for involuntary treatment?

- Mental illness? (Yes - e.g., depression, psychosis) - Risk of serious harm / serious deterioration? (Assess suicide risk, self-neglect risk) - Involuntary treatment necessary?

  1. Decision tree:

A. Patient has capacity AND does NOT meet criteria for involuntary:

- Allow to leave (even if you disagree) - Provide safety plan: GP follow-up, crisis numbers (Lifeline 13 11 14), return if worsens - Document: "Patient has capacity, declines admission, does not meet criteria for involuntary treatment, risks explained, safety plan provided."

B. Patient has capacity BUT meets criteria for involuntary:

- Example: Patient with psychosis, high suicide risk, but can articulate decision - Detain involuntarily (Form 1 ‚Üí Psychiatry for Schedule 1) - Explain: "I understand you want to leave, but I'm very concerned about your safety. I need to keep you here for a psychiatric assessment." - Document: "Patient meets criteria for involuntary treatment (psychotic, high suicide risk). Form 1 completed. Psychiatry notified."

C. Patient lacks capacity:

- Detain (Form 1 if meets criteria, or ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY doctrine) - Treat in best interests - Document: "Patient lacks capacity for decision to self-discharge (unable to use and weigh due to psychosis). Meets criteria for involuntary treatment. Form 1 completed."

Common IMG Mistake: Allowing high-risk patient to leave because "they want to" without assessing capacity or involuntary criteria

Scenario 3: Family Requesting Involuntary Admission

Situation: Family brings patient to ED, requests psychiatric admission. Patient refuses. Approach:
  1. Thank family for bringing patient
  2. Assess patient (history, MSE, risk assessment)
  3. Determine if criteria for involuntary treatment met:

- Mental illness? - Risk of serious harm / serious deterioration? - Involuntary treatment necessary?

  1. Decision tree:

A. Criteria MET:

- Complete Form 1 - Psychiatry assessment for Schedule 1 - Explain to patient: "I understand you don't want to stay, but I'm concerned about your safety. I need to keep you here for assessment." - Explain to family: "I agree [patient] needs help. I'm going to arrange for assessment for admission."

B. Criteria NOT met:

- Cannot admit involuntarily (even if family requests) - Explain to patient: "You don't need to stay if you don't want to." - Explain to family: "I understand your concerns. However, [patient] does not meet the criteria for involuntary admission right now. This means they have the right to leave if they choose." - Offer alternatives: - Voluntary admission (if patient agrees) - Outpatient psychiatry/psychology referral - GP follow-up - Crisis team contact numbers - "If things worsen, please come back"

KEY: Family request does NOT determine involuntary admission - criteria must be met Common IMG Mistake: Admitting patient involuntarily because "family requested" without proper criteria assessment

Scenario 4: Assessing Capacity for Treatment Refusal in Psychosis

Situation: Patient with schizophrenia refuses antipsychotic medication Capacity Assessment: 1. Understand:
  • Doctor: "Can you tell me why we've recommended medication?"
  • Patient: "You think I have schizophrenia and the medication will help the voices."
  • Assessment: Patient can repeat information ‚úì

2. Retain:
  • After 5 minutes: "Can you remind me what we discussed?"
  • Patient: "You want me to take antipsychotic medication."
  • Assessment: Retains ‚úì

3. Use and Weigh (CRITICAL):
  • Doctor: "Do you think you have schizophrenia?"
  • Patient: "No, I'm not sick. The voices are real - they're the FBI communicating with me. I don't need medication." (Delusion)
  • Doctor: "What do you think will happen if you don't take medication?"
  • Patient: "Nothing, because I'm not sick. The medication is poison to stop me from hearing the truth."
  • Assessment: Patient's decision based on delusional belief that he is not ill, voices are real, medication is poison. UNABLE to use and weigh ‚úó

4. Communicate:
  • Patient clearly states: "I refuse medication"
  • Assessment: Communicates ‚úì

Conclusion: Patient LACKS capacity for decision to refuse medication (unable to use and weigh due to delusions) Management:
  • If voluntary patient: Treat in best interests (give medication), involve substitute decision-maker if available
  • If involuntary patient: Can give medication under Mental Health Act
  • Document: "Patient lacks capacity for decision re medication (unable to use and weigh - decision based on delusional belief that not ill, medication is poison). Medication commenced in patient's best interests / under Mental Health Act Schedule 1."

6. COMMON IMG MISTAKES - CAPACITY AND LEGAL FRAMEWORK

Mistake 1: Assuming Mental Illness = Lack of Capacity

Wrong: "Patient has schizophrenia, so lacks capacity" Correct: "Patient has schizophrenia. I will assess capacity for the specific decision using the four-element framework." Principle: Mental illness does NOT automatically mean lack of capacity. Many patients with psychiatric illness have capacity. Always assess.

Mistake 2: Global Capacity Assessment

Wrong: "Patient lacks capacity" (vague, global statement) Correct: "Patient lacks capacity for decision to self-discharge due to inability to use and weigh information (severe depression with hopelessness)." Principle: Capacity is decision-specific. Patient may have capacity for some decisions but not others.

Mistake 3: Poor Documentation

Wrong: "Patient lacks capacity. Treated." Correct:

"CAPACITY ASSESSMENT - Decision to consent to antidepressant

  • Understand: Patient able to explain back: 'You think I have depression, medication might help' ‚úì
  • Retain: Able to recall after 5 minutes ‚úì
  • Use/Weigh: Patient states 'Nothing will help, no point trying, I'm beyond help.' Unable to weigh potential benefits due to hopelessness (symptom of severe depression) ‚úó
  • Communicate: Verbally communicates refusal ‚úì
  • Conclusion: Patient LACKS capacity for this decision (unable to use and weigh)
  • Plan: Commenced sertraline 50mg in patient's best interests. Will reassess capacity as depression improves."

Principle: Document all four elements with specific evidence. Medicolegal protection.

Mistake 4: Not Maximizing Capacity

Wrong: Assessing capacity in patient with delirium, concluding lacks capacity, proceeding with treatment without attempting to optimize Correct: Treat delirium (UTI, hydration, avoid sedatives), reassess capacity in 24-48 hours when delirium resolving Principle: Maximize capacity before concluding patient lacks capacity (optimize timing, environment, communication, medical treatment)

Mistake 5: Using Mental Health Act for Substance Intoxication Alone

Wrong: Patient intoxicated with alcohol, aggressive. Complete Form 1 / Schedule 1 for "substance use." Correct:
  • Substance intoxication alone is NOT a mental illness under Mental Health Act
  • Manage intoxication medically (monitor, de-escalate, sedation if necessary for safety)
  • Reassess when sober - if underlying mental illness evident (depression, psychosis), then consider Mental Health Act

Exception: Intoxication reveals underlying mental illness (e.g., psychosis triggered by methamphetamine in person with schizophrenia) Principle: Cannot use Mental Health Act for substance intoxication alone

Mistake 6: Admitting Involuntarily Because "Family Requested"

Wrong: Family brings patient, requests admission. Doctor admits involuntarily without assessing criteria. Correct: Assess patient. Determine if criteria met (mental illness, risk of serious harm, involuntary treatment necessary). If criteria NOT met, cannot admit involuntarily (even if family requests). Principle: Criteria (not family request) determine involuntary admission

Mistake 7: Allowing High-Risk Patient to Leave Because "They Have Capacity"

Wrong: Patient with psychosis and high suicide risk wants to leave. Doctor assesses patient has capacity (can articulate decision), allows to leave. Correct: Having capacity does NOT prevent involuntary admission if criteria met.
  • Patient may have capacity BUT meet criteria for involuntary treatment (mental illness + risk of serious harm)
  • Complete Form 1, psychiatry for Schedule 1

Principle: Involuntary admission based on criteria (mental illness, risk, necessity), NOT on capacity. Patient can have capacity AND still be detained if criteria met.

  • Does NOT authorize treatment of physical illness
  • Must still obtain consent for appendectomy (or assess capacity if patient refuses)

SUMMARY: KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR ICRP

Capacity Assessment Framework

U-R-U-W-C Mnemonic:
  1. Understand information
  2. Retain information
  3. Use and Weigh information (most complex - can they appreciate consequences, weigh pros/cons?)
  4. Communicate decision

Principles:
  • Assume capacity unless proven otherwise
  • Decision-specific (not global)
  • Time-specific (can fluctuate - reassess)
  • Maximize capacity before concluding patient lacks it (optimize timing, environment, communication, treatment)
  • Document thoroughly (all four elements, specific evidence)

Key Point: Mental illness ‚â† lack of capacity (always assess)

Mental Health Act NSW 2007

Criteria for Involuntary Treatment (ALL THREE required):
  1. Mental illness (excludes substance intoxication alone, personality disorder alone)
  2. Risk of serious harm to self/others OR serious deterioration
  3. Involuntary treatment necessary (no less restrictive alternative)

Forms:
  • Form 1: Any doctor, 12 hours, allows detention for assessment
  • Schedule 1: Psychiatrist only, up to 3 months, involuntary admission
  • CTO: Community Treatment Order (involuntary treatment in community)

Key Points:
  • Mental Health Act authorizes treatment of mental illness (NOT physical illness)
  • ECT for involuntary patient requires Tribunal approval
  • Involuntary patients reviewed by Mental Health Review Tribunal within 21 days

Decision Trees for Common Scenarios

Patient Refusing Treatment:
  1. Assess capacity (U-R-U-W-C)
  2. If HAS capacity ‚Üí Respect decision (even if you disagree)
  3. If LACKS capacity ‚Üí Treat in best interests (involve substitute decision-maker if available)

Patient Wants to Leave:
  1. Explore reasons, explain risks
  2. Assess capacity for decision to self-discharge
  3. Assess if meets criteria for involuntary treatment (mental illness, risk, necessity)
  4. Decision:

- Has capacity + does NOT meet involuntary criteria ‚Üí Allow to leave (safety plan) - Has capacity + meets involuntary criteria ‚Üí Detain (Form 1 ‚Üí Schedule 1) - Lacks capacity ‚Üí Detain (treat in best interests, or involuntary if criteria met)

Key Point: Having capacity does NOT prevent involuntary admission if criteria met

Substitute Decision-Making

Hierarchy (when patient lacks capacity):
  1. Enduring Guardian (if appointed)
  2. Person Responsible: Spouse/partner ‚Üí Carer ‚Üí Close friend/relative
  3. NCAT (if no one above, or disagreement)
  4. ‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY: Treat in patient's best interests if URGENT and no substitute available

Advance Care Directive: Not legally binding in NSW, but strongly consider in best interests decision-making
